# Neurophysiology

## Overview

Neurophysiology is the study of the functional properties of nerve cells (neurons), neural circuits, and the nervous system. It encompasses the mechanisms by which neurons generate and transmit electrical signals, process information, and integrate with other body systems to control behavior and physiological functions.

### Key Components
- **Central Nervous System (CNS)**: Brain and spinal cord
- **Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)**: Cranial and spinal nerves
- **Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)**: Sympathetic, parasympathetic, and enteric divisions
- **Somatic Nervous System**: Motor and sensory pathways

## Neuron Structure and Function

### Neuron Components
1. **Cell body (soma)**: Contains nucleus and most organelles
2. **Dendrites**: Receive synaptic inputs from other neurons
3. **Axon**: Conducts action potentials away from cell body
4. **Axon terminals**: Release neurotransmitters at synapses
5. **Myelin sheath**: Insulates axons for faster conduction

### Types of Neurons
- **Sensory neurons**: Detect stimuli and transmit to CNS
- **Motor neurons**: Control muscle contraction and glandular secretion
- **Interneurons**: Process information within CNS

## Membrane Potential and Ion Channels

### Resting Potential
- **Typical value**: -70 mV (inside negative relative to outside)
- **Na+/K+ pump**: Maintains concentration gradients
- **Selective permeability**: K+ leak channels dominate resting conductance

### Ion Channels
1. **Voltage-gated channels**: Open/close based on membrane potential
2. **Ligand-gated channels**: Open/close based on neurotransmitter binding
3. **Mechanically-gated channels**: Respond to physical deformation

### Equilibrium Potentials
- **K+**: ~-90 mV
- **Na+**: ~+60 mV
- **Ca2+**: ~+120 mV
- **Cl-**: ~-70 mV

## Action Potential

### Phases
1. **Threshold**: ~-55 mV, trigger for action potential
2. **Depolarization**: Na+ channels open, membrane potential rises
3. **Repolarization**: K+ channels open, Na+ channels inactivate
4. **Hyperpolarization**: K+ channels remain open, potential undershoots
5. **Return to resting potential**: Ion pumps restore gradients

### Properties
- **All-or-nothing**: Action potentials have consistent amplitude
- **Refractory periods**: Absolute and relative refractory periods
- **Conduction velocity**: Depends on axon diameter and myelination

## Synaptic Transmission

### Chemical Synapses
1. **Action potential arrival**: Depolarizes presynaptic terminal
2. **Ca2+ influx**: Voltage-gated Ca2+ channels open
3. **Vesicle fusion**: Exocytosis releases neurotransmitters
4. **Receptor binding**: Neurotransmitters bind postsynaptic receptors
5. **Postsynaptic response**: Ion channels open/close, potential changes

### Electrical Synapses
- **Gap junctions**: Direct electrical coupling between neurons
- **Bidirectional**: Current flows in both directions
- **Fast transmission**: No synaptic delay

## Neurotransmitters

### Major Classes
1. **Acetylcholine**: Motor neurons, autonomic nervous system
2. **Monoamines**: Dopamine, norepinephrine, serotonin, histamine
3. **Amino acids**: Glutamate (excitatory), GABA and glycine (inhibitory)
4. **Neuropeptides**: Endorphins, substance P, neuropeptide Y

### Synaptic Integration
- **Temporal summation**: Multiple inputs over time
- **Spatial summation**: Multiple inputs at same time
- **EPSP**: Excitatory postsynaptic potentials
- **IPSP**: Inhibitory postsynaptic potentials

## Central Nervous System Organization

### Spinal Cord
- **Gray matter**: Cell bodies, interneurons
- **White matter**: Myelinated axons, ascending/descending tracts
- **Reflexes**: Monosynaptic and polysynaptic reflex arcs

### Brain Regions

#### Brainstem
- **Medulla**: Vital functions (breathing, heart rate, blood pressure)
- **Pons**: Sleep, arousal, facial sensation
- **Midbrain**: Eye movements, reflexes, dopamine neurons

#### Cerebellum
- **Motor learning**: Coordination, balance, motor memory
- **Cognitive functions**: Language, executive function

#### Diencephalon
- **Thalamus**: Sensory and motor relay, consciousness
- **Hypothalamus**: Homeostasis, endocrine control, behavior

#### Cerebral Cortex
- **Sensory areas**: Process sensory information
- **Motor areas**: Control voluntary movement
- **Association areas**: Integration, higher cognitive functions

## Motor Systems

### Motor Unit
- **Definition**: Motor neuron and all muscle fibers it innervates
- **Recruitment**: Small motor units recruited first
- **Force gradation**: Number and frequency of motor units

### Motor Pathways
1. **Corticospinal tract**: Voluntary movement, fine motor control
2. **Corticobulbar tract**: Cranial nerve motor control
3. **Extrapyramidal system**: Basal ganglia, brainstem nuclei

### Motor Control
- **Upper motor neurons**: Cortex and brainstem
- **Lower motor neurons**: Spinal cord and cranial nerve nuclei
- **Basal ganglia**: Movement initiation, habit formation
- **Cerebellum**: Movement coordination, error correction

## Sensory Systems

### General Principles
- **Transduction**: Conversion of stimulus energy to neural signals
- **Encoding**: Stimulus intensity, location, modality
- **Processing**: Feature detection, pattern recognition

### Somatosensation
- **Mechanoreceptors**: Touch, pressure, vibration, proprioception
- **Thermoreceptors**: Temperature detection
- **Nociceptors**: Pain detection
- **Pathways**: Dorsal column-medial lemniscal, spinothalamic tracts

### Special Senses
- **Vision**: Retinal processing, visual cortex organization
- **Hearing**: Cochlear mechanics, auditory pathways
- **Balance**: Vestibular system, spatial orientation
- **Taste and smell**: Chemical senses, limbic connections

## Autonomic Nervous System

### Sympathetic Division
- **Fight-or-flight response**: Increased heart rate, blood pressure
- **Neurotransmitters**: Acetylcholine (ganglia), norepinephrine (targets)
- **Origin**: Thoracolumbar spinal cord (T1-L2)

### Parasympathetic Division
- **Rest-and-digest response**: Decreased heart rate, increased digestion
- **Neurotransmitters**: Acetylcholine (ganglia and targets)
- **Origin**: Cranial nerves and sacral spinal cord (S2-S4)

### Enteric Division
- **"Second brain"**: Independent control of gastrointestinal function
- **Neurotransmitters**: Multiple types including ACh, NO, VIP
- **Integration**: With sympathetic and parasympathetic systems

## Neural Plasticity

### Types of Plasticity
1. **Synaptic plasticity**: Changes in synaptic strength
2. **Structural plasticity**: Changes in neural connections
3. **Homeostatic plasticity**: Maintains neural excitability

### Mechanisms
- **Long-term potentiation (LTP)**: Activity-dependent strengthening
- **Long-term depression (LTD)**: Activity-dependent weakening
- **Hebbian learning**: "Cells that fire together, wire together"

## Clinical Applications

### Neurological Assessment
- **Electrophysiology**: EEG, EMG, nerve conduction studies
- **Reflexes**: Assessment of neural pathway integrity
- **Sensory testing**: Modality-specific assessments

### Common Pathologies
- **Stroke**: Cerebrovascular accidents affecting brain function
- **Multiple sclerosis**: Demyelinating disease affecting conduction
- **Parkinson's disease**: Basal ganglia dysfunction
- **Epilepsy**: Abnormal neural synchronization
- **Neuropathies**: Peripheral nerve dysfunction

## Key Points

- Action potentials are all-or-nothing electrical signals that propagate along axons
- Synaptic transmission converts electrical signals to chemical signals and back
- Neural integration occurs through summation of excitatory and inhibitory inputs
- The nervous system is organized hierarchically with spinal cord, brainstem, and cortex
- Motor control involves upper and lower motor neurons with cerebellar and basal ganglia modulation
- Sensory systems transduce environmental stimuli into neural signals
- Autonomic nervous system controls involuntary physiological functions
- Neural plasticity allows adaptation and learning throughout life
- Understanding neurophysiology is essential for diagnosing and treating neurological disorders
