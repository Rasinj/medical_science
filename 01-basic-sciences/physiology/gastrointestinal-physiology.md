# Gastrointestinal Physiology

## Overview

The gastrointestinal (GI) system is responsible for the ingestion, digestion, absorption, and excretion of food and waste products. It consists of the alimentary canal and accessory organs that work together to break down food into nutrients that can be absorbed and utilized by the body.

### Components
- **Alimentary canal**: Mouth, esophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine
- **Accessory organs**: Salivary glands, liver, gallbladder, pancreas
- **Enteric nervous system**: "Second brain" controlling GI function
- **GI hormones**: Coordinate digestive processes

## General Principles

### Functions
1. **Motility**: Movement of food through the GI tract
2. **Secretion**: Production of digestive enzymes, acids, and bile
3. **Digestion**: Breakdown of macromolecules into absorbable units
4. **Absorption**: Uptake of nutrients, water, and electrolytes
5. **Excretion**: Elimination of waste products

### Regulation
- **Neural control**: Enteric, parasympathetic, and sympathetic nervous systems
- **Hormonal control**: GI hormones and paracrine factors
- **Local control**: Intrinsic reflexes and smooth muscle properties

## Oral Cavity and Swallowing

### Salivary Secretion
- **Daily production**: 1-1.5 L of saliva
- **Components**: Water, electrolytes, enzymes (amylase, lipase), mucins
- **Functions**: Lubrication, initial digestion, antimicrobial activity

### Swallowing (Deglutition)
1. **Oral phase**: Voluntary formation and propulsion of bolus
2. **Pharyngeal phase**: Involuntary, coordinated by swallowing center
3. **Esophageal phase**: Peristaltic waves propel bolus to stomach

## Esophageal Function

### Structure
- **Upper esophageal sphincter**: Prevents air entry
- **Body**: Smooth muscle with peristaltic activity
- **Lower esophageal sphincter (LES)**: Prevents gastroesophageal reflux

### Motility
- **Primary peristalsis**: Initiated by swallowing
- **Secondary peristalsis**: Triggered by esophageal distension
- **LES relaxation**: Allows food entry into stomach

## Gastric Function

### Gastric Secretion
- **Daily production**: 2-3 L of gastric juice
- **Parietal cells**: Hydrochloric acid (HCl) and intrinsic factor
- **Chief cells**: Pepsinogen (converted to pepsin by acid)
- **G cells**: Gastrin hormone
- **Mucous cells**: Protective mucus and bicarbonate

### Regulation of Acid Secretion
1. **Cephalic phase**: Sight, smell, taste of food (vagal stimulation)
2. **Gastric phase**: Food in stomach (gastrin release, distension)
3. **Intestinal phase**: Food in duodenum (inhibitory feedback)

### Gastric Motility
- **Receptive relaxation**: Accommodation of food volume
- **Grinding and mixing**: Antral contractions break down food
- **Gastric emptying**: Regulated by volume, osmolarity, and nutrient content

## Small Intestine

### Structure and Function
- **Duodenum**: Receives chyme, bile, and pancreatic juice
- **Jejunum**: Primary site of nutrient absorption
- **Ileum**: Bile acid and vitamin B12 absorption
- **Surface area**: Increased by villi and microvilli (600-fold increase)

### Pancreatic Secretion
- **Daily production**: 1.5-2 L of pancreatic juice
- **Enzymes**: Amylase, lipase, proteases (trypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase)
- **Bicarbonate**: Neutralizes gastric acid
- **Regulation**: Secretin and cholecystokinin (CCK)

### Bile Production and Secretion
- **Bile acids**: Emulsify lipids for digestion
- **Daily production**: 500-1000 mL
- **Enterohepatic circulation**: 95% of bile acids reabsorbed
- **Gallbladder**: Concentrates and stores bile

### Digestion and Absorption

#### Carbohydrates
1. **Starch digestion**: Amylase breaks down to oligosaccharides
2. **Brush border enzymes**: Disaccharidases (lactase, sucrase, maltase)
3. **Absorption**: Glucose and galactose via SGLT1, fructose via GLUT5

#### Proteins
1. **Gastric digestion**: Pepsin begins protein breakdown
2. **Pancreatic enzymes**: Trypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase, carboxypeptidases
3. **Brush border peptidases**: Complete breakdown to amino acids
4. **Absorption**: Various amino acid transporters

#### Lipids
1. **Emulsification**: Bile acids form micelles
2. **Pancreatic lipase**: Breaks down triglycerides
3. **Absorption**: Fatty acids and monoglycerides enter enterocytes
4. **Chylomicron formation**: Transport via lymphatic system

#### Water and Electrolytes
- **Daily fluid load**: 8-10 L (2 L ingested, 6-8 L secreted)
- **Small intestine absorption**: 7-8 L
- **Sodium absorption**: Coupled with glucose, amino acids, or chloride
- **Water absorption**: Follows osmotic gradients

### Small Intestinal Motility
- **Segmentation**: Mixing contractions during feeding
- **Peristalsis**: Propulsive waves during fasting
- **Migrating motor complexes**: Cleansing waves between meals

## Large Intestine (Colon)

### Functions
- **Water absorption**: Concentrates fecal material
- **Electrolyte absorption**: Sodium and chloride
- **Bacterial fermentation**: Short-chain fatty acid production
- **Feces formation and storage**

### Colonic Motility
- **Segmental contractions**: Mixing and slow transit
- **Mass movements**: Propel contents toward rectum
- **Defecation reflex**: Coordinated relaxation of anal sphincters

### Colonic Microbiome
- **Bacterial population**: 10^14 bacteria (greater than human cells)
- **Functions**: Vitamin synthesis, immune modulation, pathogen resistance
- **Metabolites**: Short-chain fatty acids, vitamins K and B12

## GI Hormones

### Major Hormones
1. **Gastrin**: Stimulates acid secretion, gastric motility
2. **Cholecystokinin (CCK)**: Stimulates pancreatic enzyme secretion, gallbladder contraction
3. **Secretin**: Stimulates pancreatic bicarbonate secretion
4. **Glucose-dependent insulinotropic peptide (GIP)**: Stimulates insulin release
5. **Glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1)**: Incretin hormone, delays gastric emptying

### Integration
- **Fed state**: Hormones coordinate digestion and absorption
- **Fasting state**: Migrating motor complexes clear debris
- **Postprandial response**: Coordinated secretion and motility changes

## Enteric Nervous System

### Components
- **Myenteric plexus**: Controls motility patterns
- **Submucosal plexus**: Controls secretion and blood flow
- **Neurotransmitters**: Acetylcholine, VIP, NO, substance P

### Integration
- **Intrinsic reflexes**: Local control of GI function
- **Extrinsic innervation**: Parasympathetic (stimulatory) and sympathetic (inhibitory)
- **Brain-gut axis**: Bidirectional communication

## Clinical Applications

### Common GI Disorders
- **Peptic ulcer disease**: H. pylori infection, NSAIDs
- **Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD)**: LES dysfunction
- **Inflammatory bowel disease**: Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis
- **Irritable bowel syndrome**: Functional motility disorder
- **Malabsorption syndromes**: Celiac disease, pancreatic insufficiency

### Diagnostic Tests
- **Gastric acid secretion**: Pentagastrin stimulation test
- **Pancreatic function**: Fecal elastase, secretin stimulation
- **Small bowel absorption**: D-xylose test, Schilling test
- **Motility studies**: Manometry, gastric emptying studies

## Key Points

- GI system processes 8-10 L of fluid daily with 99% absorption efficiency
- Digestion requires coordinated secretion of enzymes, acid, and bile
- Small intestine is the primary site of nutrient absorption
- Enteric nervous system provides autonomous control of GI functions
- GI hormones integrate digestive processes with metabolic needs
- Large intestine concentrates waste and houses beneficial microbiome
- Surface area amplification in small intestine maximizes absorption
- Neural and hormonal regulation coordinate motility and secretion
- Understanding GI physiology is essential for managing digestive disorders
