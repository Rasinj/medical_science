# Endocrine Physiology

## Overview

The endocrine system consists of glands that secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream to regulate various physiological processes. This system works through chemical messengers that coordinate functions across different organ systems to maintain homeostasis.

### Key Characteristics
- **Hormones**: Chemical messengers that travel through bloodstream
- **Target cells**: Cells with specific receptors for particular hormones
- **Feedback mechanisms**: Primarily negative feedback to maintain balance
- **Integration**: Coordinates with nervous system for body regulation

## Hormone Classifications

### Chemical Structure
1. **Peptide/Protein hormones**: Insulin, growth hormone, ADH
2. **Steroid hormones**: Cortisol, testosterone, estrogen, aldosterone
3. **Amino acid derivatives**: Thyroid hormones, epinephrine, norepinephrine

### Mechanism of Action
- **Hydrophilic hormones**: Bind to surface receptors, use second messengers
- **Lipophilic hormones**: Enter cells, bind to intracellular receptors, affect gene expression

## Major Endocrine Glands

### Hypothalamus
**Function**: Master regulator of endocrine system
**Hormones produced**:
- **Releasing hormones**: TRH, CRH, GnRH, GHRH, somatostatin
- **ADH (vasopressin)**: Water balance and blood pressure
- **Oxytocin**: Uterine contractions and milk ejection

### Anterior Pituitary (Adenohypophysis)
**Hormones produced**:
- **Growth hormone (GH)**: Promotes growth and metabolism
- **Thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH)**: Stimulates thyroid gland
- **Adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH)**: Stimulates adrenal cortex
- **Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH)**: Reproductive function
- **Luteinizing hormone (LH)**: Reproductive function
- **Prolactin**: Milk production

### Posterior Pituitary (Neurohypophysis)
**Hormones stored and released**:
- **ADH**: Increases water reabsorption in kidneys
- **Oxytocin**: Stimulates uterine contractions and milk ejection

### Thyroid Gland
**Hormones produced**:
- **Thyroid hormones (T3, T4)**: Regulate metabolism, growth, development
- **Calcitonin**: Lowers blood calcium levels

### Parathyroid Glands
**Hormone produced**:
- **Parathyroid hormone (PTH)**: Increases blood calcium levels

### Adrenal Glands

#### Adrenal Cortex
- **Cortisol**: Stress response, glucose metabolism, anti-inflammatory
- **Aldosterone**: Sodium and potassium balance
- **Androgens**: Secondary sex characteristics

#### Adrenal Medulla
- **Epinephrine**: Fight-or-flight response
- **Norepinephrine**: Sympathetic nervous system effects

### Pancreas (Islets of Langerhans)
**Hormones produced**:
- **Insulin**: Lowers blood glucose, promotes glucose uptake
- **Glucagon**: Raises blood glucose, promotes gluconeogenesis
- **Somatostatin**: Inhibits insulin and glucagon release

## Feedback Mechanisms

### Negative Feedback
**Most common mechanism**:
- Hormone levels increase → target organ responds → hormone production decreases
- Examples: Thyroid axis, adrenal axis, glucose regulation

### Positive Feedback
**Less common**:
- Output enhances the original stimulus
- Examples: Oxytocin during labor, LH surge during ovulation

### Feed-Forward Mechanisms
- Anticipatory responses to expected changes
- Example: Insulin release in response to food intake

## Hormone Regulation Systems

### Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid Axis
1. **TRH** (hypothalamus) → **TSH** (anterior pituitary) → **T3/T4** (thyroid)
2. **Negative feedback**: T3/T4 inhibit TRH and TSH release
3. **Functions**: Metabolism, growth, development

### Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal Axis
1. **CRH** (hypothalamus) → **ACTH** (anterior pituitary) → **Cortisol** (adrenal cortex)
2. **Negative feedback**: Cortisol inhibits CRH and ACTH release
3. **Functions**: Stress response, glucose metabolism

### Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System
1. **Low blood pressure/volume** → **Renin** release → **Angiotensin II** → **Aldosterone**
2. **Functions**: Blood pressure and volume regulation

### Growth Hormone Axis
1. **GHRH** (hypothalamus) → **GH** (anterior pituitary) → **IGF-1** (liver)
2. **Regulation**: Somatostatin inhibits GH release
3. **Functions**: Growth, metabolism

## Calcium Homeostasis

### PTH Actions
- Increases bone resorption
- Increases renal calcium reabsorption
- Stimulates calcitriol (active vitamin D) production

### Calcitonin Actions
- Decreases bone resorption
- Increases renal calcium excretion

### Vitamin D (Calcitriol)
- Increases intestinal calcium absorption
- Enhances PTH effects on bone and kidney

## Glucose Homeostasis

### Insulin Effects
- Promotes glucose uptake by muscle and adipose tissue
- Stimulates glycogen synthesis
- Inhibits gluconeogenesis
- Promotes lipogenesis

### Glucagon Effects
- Stimulates glycogenolysis
- Promotes gluconeogenesis
- Stimulates lipolysis

### Counter-regulatory Hormones
- **Cortisol**: Increases gluconeogenesis
- **Epinephrine**: Stimulates glycogenolysis
- **Growth hormone**: Reduces glucose uptake

## Reproductive Endocrinology

### Male
- **GnRH** → **LH/FSH** → **Testosterone** production
- **Functions**: Spermatogenesis, secondary sex characteristics

### Female
- **Follicular phase**: FSH stimulates follicle development, estrogen production
- **Ovulation**: LH surge triggers ovulation
- **Luteal phase**: LH maintains corpus luteum, progesterone production

## Clinical Applications

### Common Endocrine Disorders
- **Diabetes mellitus**: Insulin deficiency or resistance
- **Hypothyroidism**: Low thyroid hormone levels
- **Hyperthyroidism**: Excess thyroid hormone levels
- **Addison's disease**: Adrenal insufficiency
- **Cushing's syndrome**: Excess cortisol

### Diagnostic Testing
- **Stimulation tests**: Assess gland responsiveness
- **Suppression tests**: Evaluate feedback mechanisms
- **Hormone replacement therapy**: Treat deficiency states

## Key Points

- Endocrine system maintains homeostasis through hormone signaling
- Negative feedback is the primary regulatory mechanism
- Hypothalamus and pituitary gland control many endocrine functions
- Hormones can be peptides, steroids, or amino acid derivatives
- Multiple systems integrate to regulate metabolism, growth, and reproduction
- Calcium and glucose homeostasis involve complex multi-hormone systems
- Clinical disorders often result from hormone excess or deficiency
- Understanding feedback mechanisms is crucial for diagnosis and treatment
