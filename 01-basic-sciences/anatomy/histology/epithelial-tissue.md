# Epithelial Tissue

## Introduction

Epithelial tissue (epithelium) is one of the four basic tissue types, characterized by sheets of closely packed cells with minimal extracellular matrix. Epithelia cover body surfaces, line cavities and hollow organs, and form glands. Understanding epithelial classification and function is fundamental to recognizing normal tissue architecture and identifying pathological changes.

### Learning Objectives

1. Classify epithelia based on cell layers and shape
2. Identify different types of epithelium under microscopy
3. Understand specialized epithelial structures and their functions
4. Describe glandular epithelium and gland classification
5. Correlate epithelial structure with function
6. Recognize clinical manifestations of epithelial dysfunction

## General Characteristics of Epithelia

### Defining Features

**Cellularity:**
- Composed almost entirely of cells
- Minimal extracellular matrix (ECM)
- Cells closely apposed via cell junctions

**Polarity:**
- **Apical surface**: Faces lumen or external environment
  - May have specializations (microvilli, cilia, stereocilia)
- **Basal surface**: Faces underlying connective tissue
  - Attached to basement membrane
- **Lateral surfaces**: Face adjacent epithelial cells
  - Site of cell-cell junctions

**Basement Membrane:**
- Thin acellular layer between epithelium and connective tissue
- **Basal lamina** (epithelial contribution):
  - Lamina lucida (electron-lucent)
  - Lamina densa (type IV collagen, laminin, proteoglycans)
- **Reticular lamina** (connective tissue contribution):
  - Type III collagen (reticular fibers)
- **Functions**:
  - Structural support and anchorage
  - Filtration barrier (kidney glomerulus)
  - Cell migration during wound healing
  - Separates epithelium from connective tissue

**Avascularity:**
- No blood vessels within epithelium
- Nutrients diffuse from underlying connective tissue
- Exception: Some highly vascularized organs (liver sinusoids adjacent to hepatocytes)

**Innervation:**
- Supplied by nerve endings (sensation)
- No neuronal cell bodies in epithelium

**Regeneration:**
- High capacity for cell renewal
- Continuous mitosis in basal cells (stem cells)
- Varies by location (intestinal epithelium renewed every 3-5 days; skin every 2-4 weeks)

## Classification of Epithelia

Epithelia are classified by:
1. **Number of cell layers**: Simple or stratified
2. **Shape of cells** (in surface layer): Squamous, cuboidal, columnar

### Simple Epithelia

**Definition**: Single layer of cells, all cells rest on basement membrane.

#### Simple Squamous Epithelium

**Cell Shape**: Flat, scale-like cells; nuclei appear as bulges

**Characteristics:**
- Thin (facilitates diffusion/filtration)
- Nuclei are flattened, elliptical
- Cytoplasm barely visible (except around nucleus)

**Locations:**
- **Endothelium**: Lining of blood vessels and lymphatics
- **Mesothelium**: Lining of body cavities (pleura, pericardium, peritoneum)
- **Alveoli** of lungs (type I pneumocytes)
- **Parietal layer** of Bowman's capsule (kidney glomerulus)
- **Thin loop of Henle** (kidney)

**Function:**
- **Diffusion**: Gases (alveoli), nutrients (capillaries)
- **Filtration**: Glomerulus
- **Lubrication**: Mesothelium (serous fluid production)

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Mesothelioma**: Malignancy of mesothelium (asbestos exposure)
- **Endothelial dysfunction**: Atherosclerosis, hypertension
- **Pulmonary edema**: Damage to alveolar epithelium

#### Simple Cuboidal Epithelium

**Cell Shape**: Cube-shaped cells; round, centrally located nuclei

**Characteristics:**
- Single layer of cube-shaped cells
- Height ≈ width
- Central, spherical nuclei
- Often involved in secretion or absorption

**Locations:**
- **Kidney tubules**: Proximal and distal convoluted tubules, collecting ducts
- **Thyroid follicles**: Forms wall of thyroid follicles
- **Small ducts** of many glands
- **Surface of ovary**
- **Lens of eye** (lens epithelium)

**Functions:**
- **Secretion**: Thyroid hormone (thyroid follicles)
- **Absorption and secretion**: Kidney tubules
- **Protection**: Ovarian surface

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Acute tubular necrosis (ATN)**: Damage to tubular epithelium (kidney injury)
- **Polycystic kidney disease**: Abnormal proliferation of tubular epithelium

#### Simple Columnar Epithelium

**Cell Shape**: Tall, column-shaped cells; elongated, basally located nuclei

**Characteristics:**
- Single layer of tall cells (height > width)
- Oval nuclei oriented perpendicular to basement membrane
- Nuclei often near base (basal location)
- May have apical specializations (microvilli, cilia)

**Locations:**

**Non-ciliated:**
- **Stomach** lining (secretes mucus, HCl, pepsinogen)
- **Small intestine** (absorption; brush border with microvilli)
- **Colon** (absorption of water, mucus secretion; goblet cells)
- **Gallbladder**
- **Large ducts** of some glands

**Ciliated:**
- **Uterine tubes** (Fallopian tubes)
- **Small bronchi**
- **Efferent ductules** of testis
- **Central canal** of spinal cord
- **Ventricles** of brain (ependymal cells)

**Functions:**
- **Absorption**: Intestine (nutrients), kidney tubules
- **Secretion**: Stomach (gastric juice), intestine (mucus)
- **Movement**: Cilia propel mucus (respiratory tract), ovum (uterine tube)
- **Protection**: Mucus layer

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Barrett's esophagus**: Metaplasia (stratified squamous → simple columnar)
- **Inflammatory bowel disease**: Disrupted intestinal epithelium
- **Ectopic pregnancy**: Impaired ciliary function in uterine tube

### Pseudostratified Epithelium

**Definition**: Appears stratified but all cells rest on basement membrane (truly simple).

**Characteristics:**
- Cells of varying heights
- All cells contact basement membrane (simple)
- **Not all cells reach apical surface**
- Nuclei at different levels → "stratified" appearance
- Usually ciliated

**Pseudostratified Ciliated Columnar Epithelium:**
- **Respiratory epithelium**: Nasal cavity, trachea, bronchi
- Contains:
  - **Ciliated columnar cells**: Motile cilia (move mucus)
  - **Goblet cells**: Secrete mucus
  - **Basal cells**: Stem cells for regeneration

**Pseudostratified Columnar with Stereocilia:**
- **Epididymis** and **vas deferens** (male reproductive tract)
- **Stereocilia**: Long, branched microvilli (non-motile, absorption)

**Functions:**
- **Protection**: Traps particles in mucus
- **Movement**: Cilia beat to move mucus toward pharynx (mucociliary clearance)
- **Absorption**: Stereocilia in epididymis

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Chronic bronchitis**: Goblet cell hyperplasia, excessive mucus
- **Kartagener syndrome**: Primary ciliary dyskinesia (immotile cilia)
  - Chronic sinusitis, bronchiectasis, situs inversus, infertility
- **Smoker's lungs**: Squamous metaplasia (pseudostratified → stratified squamous)

### Stratified Epithelia

**Definition**: Multiple cell layers; only basal layer rests on basement membrane.

**Function**: Protection (mechanical stress, abrasion)

**Classification**: Based on shape of **surface** cells.

#### Stratified Squamous Epithelium

**Most common stratified epithelium.**

**Characteristics:**
- Multiple cell layers
- Basal cells: Cuboidal or columnar, mitotically active
- Surface cells: Flat, squamous
- Cell shape changes from basal (cuboidal) to superficial (squamous)

**Types:**

**Keratinized:**
- Surface cells filled with keratin, dead (anucleate)
- **Stratum corneum**: Outer layer of dead, keratinized cells
- **Location**: **Skin** (epidermis)
- **Function**: Protection from abrasion, water loss, pathogens

**Non-keratinized:**
- Surface cells retain nuclei, alive
- Moist surface
- **Locations**:
  - **Oral cavity** (mouth, tongue, oropharynx)
  - **Esophagus**
  - **Vagina**
  - **Anal canal** (upper part)
  - **Cornea**
- **Function**: Protection in moist environments

**Layers of Keratinized Stratified Squamous (Epidermis):**
From deep to superficial (mnemonic: **"Come Let's Get Sun Burned"**):
1. **Stratum Basale** (Germinativum): Single layer, mitotically active, melanocytes
2. **Stratum Spinosum**: Several layers, "prickle cells" (desmosomes), Langerhans cells
3. **Stratum Granulosum**: 3-5 layers, keratohyalin granules
4. **Stratum Lucidum**: Clear layer (only in thick skin: palms, soles)
5. **Stratum Corneum**: Many layers, dead keratinized cells

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Psoriasis**: Hyperproliferation of keratinocytes, increased stratum corneum
- **Squamous cell carcinoma**: Malignancy of stratified squamous epithelium
- **Leukoplakia**: White plaque (hyperkeratosis, premalignant)
- **Barrett's esophagus**: Metaplasia (stratified squamous → simple columnar)
  - Due to chronic acid reflux (GERD)
  - Increased risk of esophageal adenocarcinoma

#### Stratified Cuboidal Epithelium

**Rare.**

**Characteristics:**
- 2-3 cell layers
- Surface cells: Cuboidal

**Locations:**
- **Ducts of sweat glands**
- **Large ducts** of some exocrine glands (salivary, pancreas)
- **Follicles** of developing ovarian follicles

**Function**: Protection, secretion

#### Stratified Columnar Epithelium

**Very rare.**

**Characteristics:**
- Multiple cell layers
- Surface cells: Columnar

**Locations:**
- **Large excretory ducts**: Parotid, submandibular glands
- **Male urethra** (membranous and penile portions)
- **Conjunctiva** of eye
- **Transition areas** between other epithelial types

**Function**: Protection, secretion

#### Transitional Epithelium (Urothelium)

**Specialized stratified epithelium unique to urinary system.**

**Characteristics:**
- **Variable number of layers** (depends on distension):
  - Relaxed (empty): 4-5 layers, appears thick
  - Distended (full): 2-3 layers, appears thin
- **Surface cells**:
  - Large, dome-shaped (when relaxed)
  - Flattened (when stretched)
  - Often binucleate
- Allows distension without tearing

**Locations:**
- **Renal pelvis, calyces**
- **Ureters**
- **Bladder**
- **Proximal urethra** (prostatic urethra in males)

**Function:**
- **Accommodation**: Allows bladder to expand/contract
- **Impermeability**: Prevents urine from crossing epithelium (tight junctions)
- **Protection**: Barrier to toxic substances in urine

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Transitional cell carcinoma** (urothelial carcinoma): Most common bladder cancer
  - Risk factors: Smoking, aromatic amines, cyclophosphamide
  - Presentation: Painless hematuria
- **Bladder outlet obstruction**: Hypertrophy of transitional epithelium

## Specialized Epithelial Structures

### Apical Modifications

#### Microvilli

**Structure:**
- Small, finger-like projections (0.1 μm diameter, 1-2 μm tall)
- Actin filaments (core)
- Covered by plasma membrane

**Function**: **Increase surface area** for absorption

**Locations:**
- **Intestine**: Brush border (small intestine; very prominent)
- **Kidney**: Brush border (proximal tubule)
- **Gallbladder**
- **Placenta**

**Special Type:**
- **Stereocilia**: Very long, branched microvilli
  - Non-motile (despite name)
  - Epididymis, vas deferens
  - Function: Absorption

**Clinical Correlation:**
- **Celiac disease**: Villous atrophy (flattening of intestinal villi/microvilli)
  - Malabsorption
- **Fanconi syndrome**: Proximal tubule dysfunction (defective microvilli)

#### Cilia

**Structure:**
- Hair-like projections (0.25 μm diameter, 5-10 μm tall)
- **9+2 arrangement**: 9 outer doublet microtubules + 2 central microtubules
- **Dynein arms**: Motor proteins (generate movement)
- **Basal body**: Centriole at base (anchors cilium)

**Function**: **Movement** (propel mucus, fluids, cells)

**Locations:**
- **Respiratory tract**: Trachea, bronchi (mucociliary clearance)
- **Uterine tubes**: Propel ovum toward uterus
- **Efferent ductules**: Move sperm
- **Ventricles of brain**: Circulate CSF
- **Primary cilium**: Single, non-motile cilium on many cells (sensory function)

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Primary ciliary dyskinesia (Kartagener syndrome)**:
  - Absent/dysfunctional dynein arms
  - Immotile cilia
  - Chronic sinusitis, bronchiectasis, infertility
  - 50% have situs inversus (role of cilia in embryonic left-right determination)
- **Polycystic kidney disease**: Defective primary cilia (mechanosensors)

### Cell-Cell Junctions

Junctions connect adjacent epithelial cells, providing structural integrity and regulating permeability.

#### Tight Junctions (Zonula Occludens)

**Location**: Most apical junction (near lumen)

**Structure:**
- Transmembrane proteins (occludins, claudins) bind cells together
- Form continuous seal around cell

**Functions:**
- **Barrier**: Prevents passage of molecules between cells (paracellular pathway)
- **Fence**: Maintains apical-basal polarity (prevents mixing of membrane proteins)

**Permeability**: Varies by tissue
- Very tight: Blood-brain barrier, bladder
- Leaky: Intestine (allows some paracellular transport)

**Clinical Correlation:**
- **Cholera toxin**: Disrupts tight junctions → watery diarrhea

#### Adherens Junctions (Zonula Adherens)

**Location**: Below tight junctions

**Structure:**
- Transmembrane proteins: **Cadherins** (calcium-dependent)
- Intracellular: Link to **actin filaments**

**Function:**
- **Cell adhesion**
- **Mechanical stability**
- **Signal transduction**

**Clinical Correlation:**
- **Pemphigus**: Autoantibodies against desmoglein (cadherin in desmosomes)

#### Desmosomes (Macula Adherens)

**Location**: Scattered (spot-like junctions)

**Structure:**
- Transmembrane proteins: **Desmoglein, desmocollin** (cadherins)
- Intracellular plaques link to **intermediate filaments** (keratin)
- Dense plaques visible on EM

**Function:**
- **Strong mechanical adhesion**
- Resist shearing forces

**Locations:**
- Skin (epidermis): Abundant in stratum spinosum ("prickle cells")
- Cardiac muscle (**intercalated discs**)
- Other epithelia subject to stress

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Pemphigus vulgaris**: Autoantibodies against desmoglein 3
  - Skin blistering (intraepidermal)
  - Oral ulcers
- **Bullous pemphigoid**: Autoantibodies against hemidesmosomes
  - Subepidermal blistering

#### Gap Junctions (Nexus)

**Structure:**
- **Connexons** (6 connexin proteins) form channels
- Channels align between adjacent cells
- Allow passage of small molecules (< 1 kD)

**Function:**
- **Cell-cell communication**
- Allow passage of:
  - Ions (electrical coupling)
  - Second messengers (cAMP, Ca²⁺, IP₃)
  - Small metabolites

**Locations:**
- **Cardiac muscle**: Electrical coupling (intercalated discs)
- **Smooth muscle**: Coordinated contraction
- **Many epithelial tissues**

**Clinical Correlation:**
- **Connexin mutations**: Hearing loss, skin disorders, cataracts

#### Hemidesmosomes

**Location**: Basal surface (cell-basement membrane junction)

**Structure:**
- **Integrins** (α6β4): Bind basement membrane (laminin)
- Intracellular: Link to **intermediate filaments** (keratin)

**Function:**
- **Anchor epithelium to basement membrane**

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Bullous pemphigoid**: Autoantibodies against BP antigens (hemidesmosome components)
  - Subepidermal blistering
- **Epidermolysis bullosa**: Genetic defects in hemidesmosome components
  - Skin fragility, blistering

## Glandular Epithelium

**Glands**: Epithelial cells specialized for secretion.

**Formation**: Invagination of surface epithelium during development

### Classification

**By Destination of Secretion:**

#### Exocrine Glands

**Definition**: Secrete products onto epithelial surface (via ducts or directly)

**Components:**
- **Secretory portion**: Synthesizes and secretes product
- **Duct**: Delivers secretion to surface (except unicellular glands)

**Classification:**

**By Structure:**

*Unicellular:*
- **Goblet cells**: Single-celled mucus-secreting glands
  - Locations: Intestine, respiratory tract
  - Shape: Goblet (expanded apical mucus-filled region)
  - Mucus: PAS-positive (carbohydrate-rich)

*Multicellular:*

**By Duct Branching:**
- **Simple**: Unbranched duct
- **Compound**: Branched duct

**By Secretory Portion Shape:**
- **Tubular**: Tube-like
- **Acinar (Alveolar)**: Round, sac-like
- **Tubuloacinar**: Both tubular and acinar components

**Examples:**
- **Simple tubular**: Intestinal glands (crypts of Lieberkühn)
- **Simple coiled tubular**: Sweat glands
- **Simple branched tubular**: Gastric glands, uterine glands
- **Simple acinar**: Rare (sebaceous glands in some classifications)
- **Simple branched acinar**: Sebaceous glands
- **Compound tubular**: Bulbourethral glands, gastric cardiac glands
- **Compound acinar**: Parotid gland (serous)
- **Compound tubuloacinar**: Pancreas, submandibular gland, mammary gland

**By Secretion Type:**
- **Serous**: Watery, protein-rich (enzymes)
  - Example: Parotid gland, pancreatic acini
- **Mucous**: Thick, glycoprotein-rich (mucin)
  - Example: Sublingual gland (mainly mucous)
- **Mixed**: Both serous and mucous cells
  - Example: Submandibular gland

**By Secretion Mechanism:**

- **Merocrine (Eccrine)**: Exocytosis (most common)
  - Examples: Salivary glands, pancreas, sweat glands
  - No cell damage
- **Apocrine**: Apical cytoplasm pinched off with secretion
  - Examples: Mammary glands (lipids in milk), apocrine sweat glands
  - Partial cell loss
- **Holocrine**: Entire cell disintegrates, becomes secretion
  - Example: Sebaceous glands (sebum)
  - Cell dies and is replaced

#### Endocrine Glands

**Definition**: Ductless glands that secrete hormones into bloodstream

**Characteristics:**
- No ducts
- Secretions (hormones) enter capillaries
- Richly vascularized

**Organization:**

*Cords and Clusters:*
- **Pancreatic islets** (islets of Langerhans): Insulin, glucagon
- **Parathyroid glands**: PTH
- **Adrenal cortex**: Cortisol, aldosterone, androgens
- **Pituitary anterior lobe**: ACTH, TSH, FSH, LH, GH, prolactin

*Follicles:*
- **Thyroid follicles**: T3, T4
  - Colloid (thyroglobulin) stored in follicle lumen

## Clinical Correlations

### Metaplasia

**Definition**: Reversible change from one mature cell type to another.

**Examples:**

**Barrett's Esophagus:**
- **Change**: Stratified squamous → simple columnar (intestinal type)
- **Cause**: Chronic acid reflux (GERD)
- **Significance**: Premalignant (increased risk of adenocarcinoma)

**Smoker's Lung:**
- **Change**: Pseudostratified ciliated columnar → stratified squamous
- **Cause**: Chronic smoke exposure
- **Significance**: Loss of mucociliary clearance; premalignant

**Bladder:**
- **Change**: Transitional → stratified squamous (squamous metaplasia)
- **Cause**: Chronic irritation (stones, catheter, schistosomiasis)
- **Significance**: Increased risk of squamous cell carcinoma

### Dysplasia

**Definition**: Abnormal cell growth and organization (pre-cancerous)

**Features:**
- Loss of uniformity (pleomorphism)
- Disorganized architecture
- Increased mitoses
- Nuclear abnormalities

**Grading**: Mild, moderate, severe (high-grade dysplasia = carcinoma in situ)

**Reversible** if cause removed

### Carcinoma

**Definition**: Malignant tumor of epithelial origin

**Types:**
- **Adenocarcinoma**: From glandular epithelium (breast, colon, lung, prostate)
- **Squamous cell carcinoma**: From stratified squamous epithelium (skin, lung, esophagus)
- **Transitional cell carcinoma**: From transitional epithelium (bladder, renal pelvis)

**Most common cancers** are carcinomas

## Key Points

1. Epithelium is avascular, highly cellular, with minimal ECM; rests on basement membrane

2. Classification: Simple (1 layer) vs. stratified (multiple layers); squamous, cuboidal, columnar

3. Simple squamous: Thin, facilitates diffusion/filtration (endothelium, mesothelium, alveoli)

4. Simple columnar: Absorption/secretion (intestine, stomach)

5. Pseudostratified: Appears stratified but all cells touch basement membrane (respiratory tract)

6. Stratified squamous: Protection from abrasion (skin-keratinized; esophagus-non-keratinized)

7. Transitional epithelium: Unique to urinary tract; stretches without tearing

8. Microvilli: Increase surface area for absorption (intestine, kidney)

9. Cilia: Motile projections (9+2 structure); move mucus/fluids (respiratory tract, uterine tubes)

10. Tight junctions: Seal between cells, control permeability, maintain polarity

11. Desmosomes: Strong cell-cell adhesion (skin, cardiac muscle)

12. Gap junctions: Allow cell-cell communication (ions, small molecules)

13. Exocrine glands: Secrete via ducts (sweat, salivary); endocrine: Secrete hormones into blood

14. Goblet cells: Unicellular mucus-secreting glands (intestine, respiratory tract)

15. Metaplasia: Reversible change in cell type (Barrett's esophagus); can be premalignant
