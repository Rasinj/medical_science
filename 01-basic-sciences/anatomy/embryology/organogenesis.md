# Organogenesis

## Introduction

Organogenesis is the formation of organs during embryonic development, primarily occurring during weeks 3-8. Each organ system has specific developmental sequences and critical periods when teratogenic exposures can cause malformations. Understanding organogenesis is essential for recognizing normal development and identifying congenital abnormalities.

### Learning Objectives

1. Describe the embryological development of major organ systems
2. Identify critical periods for each organ system
3. Understand the embryological basis for common congenital anomalies
4. Correlate developmental sequences with anatomical structure
5. Recognize teratogenic vulnerabilities during organogenesis

## Cardiovascular System Development

The cardiovascular system is the first functional organ system, with the heart beginning to beat at day 22-23.

### Heart Development

**Timeline:**
- **Day 18-19**: Paired endocardial heart tubes form
- **Day 21**: Tubes fuse into single heart tube
- **Day 22-23**: Heart begins beating
- **Day 28**: Looping complete
- **Weeks 5-8**: Septation

**Primitive Heart Tube (Week 3):**

**Structure** (from caudal to cranial):
1. **Sinus venosus**: Receives venous blood
2. **Primitive atrium**
3. **Primitive ventricle**
4. **Bulbus cordis**: Outflow tract
5. **Truncus arteriosus**: Divides into aorta and pulmonary artery

**Cardiac Looping (Day 23-28):**
- Heart tube elongates and bends
- Bulbus cordis and ventricle bend right (dextral or D-loop)
- Atrium and sinus venosus fold dorsally and cranially
- Establishes left-right asymmetry

**Atrial Septation (Weeks 4-6):**

**Septum Primum:**
- Grows from roof of common atrium toward endocardial cushions
- **Ostium primum**: Initial opening (closes when septum reaches cushions)
- **Ostium secundum**: Fenestrations form in septum primum

**Septum Secundum:**
- Grows to right of septum primum
- Incomplete (leaves **foramen ovale**)
- Septum primum acts as valve for foramen ovale

**Result:**
- Right-to-left shunt through foramen ovale in fetus
- Closes at birth (increased left atrial pressure)

**Ventricular Septation (Weeks 4-7):**

**Muscular Portion:**
- Grows from floor of ventricle upward
- Leaves interventricular foramen

**Membranous Portion:**
- Forms from endocardial cushions and conotruncal ridges
- Closes interventricular foramen
- **Last part to close**

**Atrioventricular Canal Division:**
- **Endocardial cushions** (superior and inferior)
- Fuse to divide AV canal into:
  - Right AV canal (tricuspid)
  - Left AV canal (mitral)
- Also contribute to atrial and ventricular septa

**Outflow Tract Septation (Weeks 5-7):**

**Process:**
- **Aorticopulmonary septum** (neural crest cells):
  - Spirals as it divides truncus arteriosus
  - Creates aorta and pulmonary artery
  - Explains crossed positions (aorta anterior-right, PA posterior-left)
- **Conotruncal ridges**:
  - Divide bulbus cordis
  - Form outflow portions of ventricles

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Atrial septal defect (ASD)**: Ostium secundum (most common), ostium primum, sinus venosus types
- **Ventricular septal defect (VSD)**: Most common congenital heart defect; membranous > muscular
- **Tetralogy of Fallot**: Unequal division of outflow tract
  - VSD, pulmonary stenosis, overriding aorta, RV hypertrophy
- **Transposition of great arteries**: Failure of spiral septation
- **Persistent truncus arteriosus**: Complete failure of outflow septation
- **Endocardial cushion defect**: AV canal malformations (common in Down syndrome)

### Fetal Circulation Shunts (see overview.md)

**Ductus venosus**, **foramen ovale**, **ductus arteriosus**
- Close at birth
- Remnants: ligamentum venosum, fossa ovalis, ligamentum arteriosum

### Aortic Arch Development

**Pharyngeal Arch Arteries:**
- Six paired arteries (numbered 1, 2, 3, 4, 6; fifth regresses)
- Connect aortic sac to dorsal aortae

**Derivatives:**
- **Arch 1**: Maxillary artery (part)
- **Arch 2**: Stapedial artery
- **Arch 3**: Common carotid, proximal internal carotid
- **Arch 4**:
  - **Left**: Aortic arch (between left common carotid and left subclavian)
  - **Right**: Proximal right subclavian
- **Arch 6**:
  - **Left**: Ductus arteriosus, left pulmonary artery
  - **Right**: Right pulmonary artery

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Coarctation of aorta**: Narrowing, usually near ductus arteriosus
  - Preductal (infantile) vs. postductal (adult)
  - Associated with Turner syndrome
- **Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)**: Failure of closure
  - Continuous "machine-like" murmur
  - Treated with indomethacin or surgery
- **Double aortic arch**: Vascular ring around trachea/esophagus
- **Right aortic arch**: Mirror image; can be associated with congenital heart disease

## Respiratory System Development

### Timeline

- **Week 4**: Respiratory diverticulum (lung bud) appears
- **Weeks 5-16**: Branching (pseudoglandular stage)
- **Weeks 16-26**: Canalization (canalicular stage)
- **Weeks 26-birth**: Terminal sacs, surfactant production (saccular stage)
- **Birth-8 years**: Alveolar maturation

### Early Development (Week 4)

**Respiratory Diverticulum:**
- Ventral outgrowth from foregut (endoderm)
- At level of future larynx

**Tracheoesophageal Septum:**
- Separates respiratory diverticulum from esophagus
- Forms trachea (ventral) and esophagus (dorsal)

**Laryngotracheal Tube:**
- Divides into:
  - **Cranial**: Larynx
  - **Caudal**: Trachea, bronchial tree, lungs

### Lung Maturation Stages

**Pseudoglandular Stage (Weeks 5-16):**
- Branching of bronchial tree
- No respiratory function possible

**Canalicular Stage (Weeks 16-26):**
- Canalization of bronchioles
- Respiratory bronchioles and alveolar ducts form
- Some gas exchange possible (borderline viability at 24 weeks)

**Saccular Stage (Weeks 26-Birth):**
- Terminal sacs (primitive alveoli) develop
- **Type II pneumocytes** begin producing **surfactant** (~24 weeks)
- Surfactant reduces surface tension (prevents alveolar collapse)
- Viability increases with gestational age

**Alveolar Stage (Late Fetal to Childhood):**
- Mature alveoli form
- Continues until age ~8 years

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Tracheoesophageal fistula (TEF)**: Most common with esophageal atresia
  - **Type C** (85%): Proximal esophageal atresia + distal TEF
  - Presentation: Excessive drooling, choking with feeding, aspiration pneumonia
  - Associated with VACTERL syndrome
- **Congenital diaphragmatic hernia**: Posterolateral defect (foramen of Bochdalek)
  - Abdominal contents herniate into thorax
  - Pulmonary hypoplasia (most critical problem)
  - Poor prognosis if severe
- **Respiratory distress syndrome (RDS)**: Surfactant deficiency in premature infants
  - Treatment: Maternal corticosteroids (prenatal), surfactant replacement
- **Pulmonary hypoplasia**: Oligohydramnios (Potter sequence), diaphragmatic hernia

## Gastrointestinal System Development

### Foregut Derivatives

**Structures** (from pharynx to proximal duodenum, including liver/pancreas bud origins):
- Pharynx and derivatives
- Esophagus (lower 2/3)
- Stomach
- Duodenum (proximal to ampulla of Vater)
- Liver, gallbladder, bile ducts
- Pancreas

**Blood Supply**: Celiac artery

### Stomach Development

**Rotation:**
- 90° clockwise (viewed from above)
- Left side becomes anterior (greater curvature)
- Right side becomes posterior (lesser curvature)
- **Dorsal mesentery**: Forms greater omentum
- **Ventral mesentery**: Forms lesser omentum, falciform ligament

### Duodenal Development

**Atresia Formation:**
- Lumen obliterated by epithelial proliferation
- Normally recanalizes by week 9
- Failure → **duodenal atresia**

**Clinical Correlation:**
- **Duodenal atresia**: "Double bubble" sign on imaging
  - Associated with Down syndrome (30%)

### Liver and Pancreas Development

**Liver:**
- **Hepatic diverticulum** from foregut endoderm (week 3)
- Grows into **septum transversum** (mesoderm)
- **Hematopoiesis** begins week 6 (important fetal function)

**Pancreas:**
- **Dorsal pancreatic bud**: Forms body, tail, accessory duct
- **Ventral pancreatic bud**: Forms head, uncinate process, main duct
- Ventral bud rotates to fuse with dorsal bud

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Annular pancreas**: Ventral and dorsal buds encircle duodenum
- **Pancreas divisum**: Failure of fusion (ventral and dorsal ducts remain separate)

### Midgut Derivatives

**Structures** (from distal duodenum to proximal 2/3 of transverse colon):
- Distal duodenum
- Jejunum, ileum
- Cecum, appendix, ascending colon
- Proximal 2/3 transverse colon

**Blood Supply**: Superior mesenteric artery (SMA)

### Midgut Rotation

**Physiological Herniation (Weeks 6-10):**
- Rapid growth → midgut herniates into umbilical cord
- **Primary intestinal loop**:
  - Superior mesenteric artery (SMA) is axis
  - Cranial limb (jejunum/ileum)
  - Caudal limb (cecum/colon)

**Rotation (Total 270° counterclockwise):**
1. **90° rotation** while in umbilical cord
2. **Reduction** into abdomen (week 10)
3. **Additional 180° rotation** during reduction
4. **Result**: Cecum positioned in right lower quadrant

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Omphalocele**: Failure of gut to return to abdomen
  - Covered by peritoneum and amnion
  - Associated with chromosomal abnormalities
- **Gastroschisis**: Paraumbilical abdominal wall defect
  - Bowel exposed (not covered by membrane)
  - Not associated with other anomalies
- **Malrotation**: Incomplete or abnormal rotation
  - Risk of midgut volvulus (surgical emergency)
  - Presentation: Bilious vomiting in neonate
- **Volvulus**: Twisting around SMA → ischemia
- **Meckel's diverticulum**: Persistent vitelline (omphalomesenteric) duct remnant
  - "Rule of 2s": 2% of population, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2 inches long
  - May contain ectopic gastric mucosa → bleeding

### Hindgut Derivatives

**Structures** (from distal 1/3 transverse colon to upper anal canal):
- Distal 1/3 transverse colon
- Descending colon, sigmoid colon
- Rectum, upper anal canal

**Blood Supply**: Inferior mesenteric artery (IMA)

### Anal Development

**Cloacal Division (Weeks 4-7):**
- **Urorectal septum** divides cloaca into:
  - **Urogenital sinus** (ventral): Bladder, urethra
  - **Anorectal canal** (dorsal): Rectum, upper anal canal

**Anal Membrane:**
- Separates anorectal canal from exterior
- Ruptures week 8

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Imperforate anus**: Failure of anal membrane perforation
  - High (supralevator) vs. low (infralevator)
  - Associated with fistulas (rectovaginal, rectourethral, rectovesical)
- **Hirschsprung disease**: Absent enteric ganglia (neural crest cells)
  - Most commonly affects rectosigmoid
  - Presentation: Failure to pass meconium, constipation, megacolon
  - Associated with Down syndrome

## Urogenital System Development

### Kidney Development

**Three Sequential Systems:**

**1. Pronephros (Week 4):**
- Nonfunctional in humans
- Regresses completely

**2. Mesonephros (Weeks 4-8):**
- Transient function in embryo
- **Mesonephric (Wolffian) duct**: Drains mesonephros
  - Persists in males (epididymis, vas deferens)
  - Regresses in females (vestigial remnants)

**3. Metanephros (Week 5-Birth):**
- **Definitive kidney**
- Two components:
  - **Ureteric bud** (from mesonephric duct): Collecting system
    - Ureter, renal pelvis, calyces, collecting ducts
  - **Metanephric mesenchyme**: Nephrons
    - Glomeruli, proximal/distal tubules, loop of Henle

**Ascent of Kidneys:**
- Kidneys initially in pelvis
- "Ascend" to lumbar region (relative, due to body growth)
- Blood supply shifts from iliac to renal arteries

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Renal agenesis**:
  - **Bilateral**: Potter sequence (oligohydramnios → pulmonary hypoplasia, limb deformities, facial abnormalities); incompatible with life
  - **Unilateral**: Compatible with normal life (contralateral hypertrophy)
- **Horseshoe kidney**: Fusion of inferior poles (crosses midline)
  - Catches on inferior mesenteric artery during ascent
  - Associated with Turner syndrome
- **Duplicated ureter**: Bifid or complete duplication (early ureteric bud splitting)
- **Polycystic kidney disease**:
  - **Autosomal recessive** (ARPKD): Bilateral, infantile, poor prognosis
  - **Autosomal dominant** (ADPKD): Adult-onset, associated with berry aneurysms, hepatic cysts
- **Multicystic dysplastic kidney**: Nonfunctional, unilateral usually

### Bladder Development

**From Urogenital Sinus:**
- **Vesicourethral canal**: Bladder and pelvic urethra
- **Allantois**: Fibrous cord → **median umbilical ligament** (urachus)

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Exstrophy of bladder**: Abdominal wall and bladder defects
  - Bladder mucosa exposed
  - Epispadias common
- **Patent urachus**: Urachal remnant patent → urine leakage from umbilicus
- **Urachal cyst**: Fluid accumulation in urachus remnant

### Sex Determination and Differentiation

**Genetic Sex:**
- Determined at fertilization (XX or XY)

**Gonadal Sex (Week 6-7):**
- **Indifferent gonad** stage (weeks 5-6)
- **SRY gene** (on Y chromosome):
  - Present → Testes develop (week 7)
  - Absent → Ovaries develop (week 10-12)

**Phenotypic Sex (Weeks 8-12):**

**Male Development** (presence of testes):
- **Sertoli cells** → Anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH)
  - **Müllerian ducts regress**
- **Leydig cells** → Testosterone
  - **Mesonephric (Wolffian) ducts** persist and differentiate:
    - Epididymis
    - Vas deferens
    - Seminal vesicles
  - **Dihydrotestosterone (DHT)** (from testosterone by 5α-reductase):
    - Masculinizes external genitalia
    - Prostate development

**Female Development** (absence of testes):
- **No AMH**:
  - **Müllerian (paramesonephric) ducts** persist:
    - Fallopian tubes
    - Uterus
    - Upper vagina
- **No testosterone**:
  - **Mesonephric ducts regress**
- **External genitalia**:
  - Feminize without androgens

**Indifferent External Genitalia (Week 4-6):**
- **Genital tubercle**
- **Urogenital folds**
- **Labioscrotal swellings**

**Male External Genitalia (Weeks 8-12):**
- **Genital tubercle** → Penis (glans, corpora)
- **Urogenital folds** → Ventral penis, penile urethra
- **Labioscrotal swellings** → Scrotum

**Female External Genitalia:**
- **Genital tubercle** → Clitoris
- **Urogenital folds** → Labia minora
- **Labioscrotal swellings** → Labia majora

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Androgen insensitivity syndrome** (AIS):
  - XY genotype, female phenotype
  - Nonfunctional androgen receptors
  - Testes present, no uterus (AMH functional)
  - Blind vaginal pouch
- **5α-reductase deficiency**:
  - XY genotype
  - Ambiguous genitalia at birth
  - Virilization at puberty (testosterone increases)
- **Congenital adrenal hyperplasia** (CAH):
  - XX genotype with virilized external genitalia
  - Excess androgens (21-hydroxylase deficiency most common)
- **Hypospadias**: Abnormal ventral opening of urethra (most common male genital anomaly)
- **Epispadias**: Abnormal dorsal opening of urethra (rarer, often with bladder exstrophy)
- **Cryptorchidism**: Undescended testis
  - Increased risk of infertility and testicular cancer
- **Bicornuate uterus**: Incomplete fusion of Müllerian ducts
- **Mayer-Rokitansky-Küster-Hauser (MRKH) syndrome**: Müllerian agenesis (absent uterus/upper vagina)

### Testicular Descent

**Process:**
- Testes form in abdominal cavity
- Descend through inguinal canal into scrotum
- **Gubernaculum**: Guides descent
- **Processus vaginalis**: Peritoneal outpouching

**Timing:**
- Begins week 28
- Completes around birth (or shortly after)

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Cryptorchidism**: Testis fails to descend
  - Surgery (orchiopexy) recommended by age 1-2 years
- **Hydrocele**: Fluid in processus vaginalis
- **Indirect inguinal hernia**: Persistent processus vaginalis

## Nervous System Development

(See Neuroanatomy subsection for detailed coverage)

**Key Points:**
- **Neurulation**: Neural tube formation (days 18-28)
- **Brain vesicles**: Prosencephalon, mesencephalon, rhombencephalon
- **Neural crest**: PNS, melanocytes, craniofacial structures
- **Critical period**: Weeks 3-4 (neural tube closure)
- **Defects**: Anencephaly, spina bifida (folic acid preventable)

## Musculoskeletal System Development

### Bone Development

**Two Mechanisms:**

**1. Intramembranous Ossification:**
- Bone forms directly from mesenchyme
- **Flat bones**: Skull (calvaria), facial bones, clavicle
- No cartilage intermediate

**2. Endochondral Ossification:**
- Bone forms by replacing cartilage model
- **Long bones, vertebrae, most of skeleton**

**Process:**
1. Mesenchyme condenses
2. Chondrification (cartilage model)
3. Primary ossification center (diaphysis)
4. Secondary ossification centers (epiphyses)
5. Epiphyseal plates (growth plates)

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Achondroplasia**: FGFR3 mutation
  - Impaired endochondral ossification
  - Short limbs, normal trunk
  - Autosomal dominant
- **Craniosynostosis**: Premature fusion of skull sutures
  - Scaphocephaly (sagittal), brachycephaly (coronal), etc.

### Vertebral Column Development

**Somite Differentiation:**
- **Sclerotome**: Ventral portion → vertebrae, ribs
- **Myotome**: Dorsolateral → muscles
- **Dermatome**: Dorsal → dermis

**Vertebral Formation:**
- **Resegmentation**: Each vertebra forms from caudal half of one sclerotome + cranial half of adjacent sclerotome
- Allows intervertebral discs and muscle attachments

**Notochord Fate:**
- **Vertebral bodies**: Degenerates
- **Intervertebral discs**: Persists as **nucleus pulposus**

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Spina bifida occulta**: Incomplete posterior vertebral arch fusion
  - Usually asymptomatic
  - Skin dimple, hair tuft may be present
- **Klippel-Feil syndrome**: Fusion of cervical vertebrae
  - Short neck, limited movement
- **Hemivertebra**: Incomplete formation → scoliosis

### Limb Development

**Timeline:**
- **Week 4**: Limb buds appear (upper first)
- **Week 6**: Hand plates, foot plates
- **Week 7**: Digital rays, finger separation begins
- **Week 8**: Digits fully separated

**Organization:**

**Axes:**
- **Proximal-distal**: Apical ectodermal ridge (AER) signals
  - FGF signaling
- **Anterior-posterior**: Zone of polarizing activity (ZPA)
  - Sonic hedgehog (SHH) signaling
  - Thumb (anterior) to pinky (posterior)
- **Dorsal-ventral**: Ectoderm signals
  - Wnt signaling

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Syndactyly**: Fusion of digits (most common limb malformation)
  - Failure of apoptosis between digital rays
- **Polydactyly**: Extra digits
  - Postaxial (ulnar/fibular side) more common
- **Amelia**: Complete absence of limb
- **Phocomelia**: Proximal limb structures absent ("seal limbs")
  - Thalidomide exposure (historical)
- **Clubfoot (talipes equinovarus)**: Inverted, plantarflexed foot

## Face and Palate Development

### Facial Prominences (Weeks 4-10)

**Five Prominences:**
1. **Frontonasal prominence**: Forehead, nose bridge
2. **Maxillary prominences** (paired): Upper cheek, upper jaw, secondary palate
3. **Mandibular prominences** (paired): Lower jaw, lower lip

**Nasal Placodes:**
- Invaginate to form nasal pits
- Form **medial** and **lateral nasal prominences**

**Fusion:**
- **Medial nasal prominences** fuse → philtrum of upper lip, primary palate
- **Maxillary prominences** fuse with medial nasal → upper lip, secondary palate
- **Mandibular prominences** fuse → lower lip, mandible

### Palate Development (Weeks 6-9)

**Primary Palate:**
- From medial nasal prominences
- Anterior to incisive foramen
- Forms anterior hard palate

**Secondary Palate:**
- From maxillary prominences
- **Palatine shelves**: Initially vertical (beside tongue)
- Week 7: Tongue drops, shelves elevate and fuse
- Forms posterior hard palate and soft palate

**Critical Period**: Weeks 6-9

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Cleft lip**:
  - Failure of fusion (maxillary + medial nasal prominences)
  - **Unilateral** (left > right) or **bilateral**
  - May be isolated or with cleft palate
- **Cleft palate**:
  - Failure of palatine shelf fusion
  - Can involve hard palate, soft palate, or both
  - May occur with or without cleft lip
- **Cleft lip ± palate** vs. **cleft palate alone**:
  - Different embryological origin
  - Cleft palate alone more associated with syndromes
- **Associated factors**: Maternal smoking, anticonvulsants, genetics

## Key Points

1. Heart begins beating day 22-23; functional cardiovascular system required early

2. VSD is most common congenital heart defect; tetralogy of Fallot most common cyanotic defect

3. Fetal circulation includes ductus venosus, foramen ovale, ductus arteriosus; close at birth

4. Tracheoesophageal fistula most common with esophageal atresia (Type C)

5. Surfactant production begins week 24; deficiency causes RDS in premature infants

6. Midgut rotates 270° counterclockwise; malrotation risks volvulus

7. Meckel's diverticulum: persistent vitelline duct remnant ("rule of 2s")

8. Three kidney systems: pronephros (nonfunctional), mesonephros (temporary), metanephros (definitive)

9. SRY gene on Y chromosome determines male gonadal development

10. Male differentiation requires testosterone (Wolffian ducts) and AMH (Müllerian regression)

11. Female development is "default" (no AMH, no testosterone)

12. Limb development weeks 4-8; critical for teratogens (thalidomide caused phocomelia)

13. Cleft lip/palate: failure of fusion during weeks 6-9; most common craniofacial anomaly

14. Neural tube closes days 22-28; folic acid prevents defects

15. Somites give rise to vertebrae, muscles, and dermis through sclerotome, myotome, dermatome
