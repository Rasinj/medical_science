# Embryology

## Overview

Embryology is the study of prenatal development from fertilization through the eighth week of gestation (embryonic period), as well as fetal development from week 9 until birth. Understanding embryology is essential for:
- Recognizing normal developmental processes
- Understanding the basis of congenital abnormalities
- Timing critical periods of development
- Appreciating teratogenic risks
- Correlating anatomy with developmental origins

## Purpose and Scope

This subsection provides comprehensive coverage of:
- **Fundamental Concepts**: Fertilization, cleavage, gastrulation, neurulation
- **Germ Layer Derivatives**: Ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm development
- **Organogenesis**: Development of major organ systems
- **Congenital Abnormalities**: Common birth defects and their embryological basis
- **Clinical Correlations**: Teratogens, critical periods, prenatal diagnosis

## Key Developmental Milestones

### Embryonic Period (Weeks 1-8)
- **Week 1**: Fertilization, cleavage, blastocyst formation, implantation
- **Week 2**: Bilaminar disc, primitive streak (gastrulation begins)
- **Week 3**: Trilaminar disc (gastrulation), neurulation begins, primitive heart beats
- **Week 4**: Folding, neural tube closure begins, limb buds appear, heart chambers form
- **Week 5-8**: Organogenesis of all major systems, facial features develop

### Fetal Period (Weeks 9-Birth)
- **Weeks 9-12**: Sex determination, ossification begins, urine production
- **Weeks 13-16**: Coordinated limb movements, skeletal ossification continues
- **Weeks 17-20**: Fetal movements felt (quickening), lanugo appears
- **Weeks 21-24**: Surfactant production begins, viable with intensive care
- **Weeks 25-38**: Growth and maturation, surfactant increases, fat deposition

## Critical Periods and Teratogenicity

### Most Susceptible Period
- **Weeks 3-8** (embryonic period): Organogenesis
  - Highest risk for major structural abnormalities
  - Each organ has specific critical period

### Teratogen Principles
- **Timing**: Effect depends on developmental stage
- **Dose**: Threshold effect for most teratogens
- **Genetics**: Individual susceptibility varies
- **Examples**: Alcohol, isotretinoin, thalidomide, certain infections (TORCH)

## Common Congenital Abnormalities by Frequency

### Cardiovascular (Most Common)
- Ventricular septal defect (VSD)
- Atrial septal defect (ASD)
- Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)
- Tetralogy of Fallot

### Neural Tube Defects
- Spina bifida
- Anencephaly
- Prevention: Folic acid supplementation

### Facial
- Cleft lip/palate
- Most common craniofacial abnormality

### Gastrointestinal
- Tracheoesophageal fistula
- Intestinal atresias
- Omphalocele/gastroschisis

### Genitourinary
- Hypospadias
- Undescended testis
- Renal agenesis/dysplasia

## Germ Layer Derivatives (Summary)

### Ectoderm
- **Surface ectoderm**: Epidermis, hair, nails, glands
- **Neuroectoderm**: CNS, PNS
- **Neural crest**: Peripheral nerves, melanocytes, some facial structures

### Mesoderm
- **Paraxial**: Somites → muscles, vertebrae, dermis
- **Intermediate**: Urogenital system
- **Lateral plate**: Body wall, limbs, cardiovascular system

### Endoderm
- **Gut tube**: GI tract epithelium, liver, pancreas
- **Respiratory**: Lung epithelium
- **Other**: Thyroid, parathyroid, thymus

## Prenatal Diagnosis

### Screening Tests
- Maternal serum markers (AFP, hCG, estriol, inhibin A)
- Cell-free fetal DNA (noninvasive prenatal testing)
- Ultrasound (anatomy scan at 18-20 weeks)

### Diagnostic Tests
- Chorionic villus sampling (CVS): 10-13 weeks
- Amniocentesis: 15-20 weeks
- Cordocentesis: After 18 weeks

### Indications
- Advanced maternal age
- Abnormal screening results
- Family history of genetic disorder
- Previous child with abnormality
- Abnormal ultrasound findings
