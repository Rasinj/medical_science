# Overview of the Nervous System

## Introduction

The nervous system is the body's primary communication and control network, responsible for receiving, processing, and responding to internal and external stimuli. It is anatomically divided into the central nervous system (CNS) and peripheral nervous system (PNS), with functional divisions including the somatic and autonomic systems.

### Learning Objectives

1. Describe the organization and divisions of the nervous system
2. Understand the basic structure and function of neurons and glial cells
3. Differentiate between central and peripheral nervous systems
4. Explain the organization of the autonomic nervous system
5. Recognize the embryological development of the nervous system
6. Correlate neuroanatomical structures with clinical conditions

## Anatomical Organization of the Nervous System

### Major Divisions

The nervous system is divided into two main anatomical components:

#### 1. Central Nervous System (CNS)
- **Brain**: Contained within the cranial cavity
  - Cerebrum (telencephalon)
  - Diencephalon (thalamus, hypothalamus)
  - Brainstem (midbrain, pons, medulla oblongata)
  - Cerebellum
- **Spinal Cord**: Extends from the medulla to the conus medullaris (L1-L2)

#### 2. Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)
- **Cranial Nerves**: 12 pairs (CN I-XII)
- **Spinal Nerves**: 31 pairs
  - 8 cervical
  - 12 thoracic
  - 5 lumbar
  - 5 sacral
  - 1 coccygeal
- **Autonomic Ganglia**: Sympathetic and parasympathetic
- **Peripheral Nerves**: Motor and sensory nerves throughout the body

### Functional Divisions

#### Somatic Nervous System
- **Function**: Voluntary control of skeletal muscles and conscious sensation
- **Components**:
  - Somatic motor neurons (anterior horn cells)
  - Somatic sensory neurons (dorsal root ganglia)
- **Innervation**: Skeletal muscle, skin, joints

#### Autonomic Nervous System
- **Function**: Involuntary control of visceral functions
- **Components**:
  - Sympathetic division (thoracolumbar outflow)
  - Parasympathetic division (craniosacral outflow)
  - Enteric nervous system (intrinsic gut innervation)
- **Innervation**: Smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, glands

## Cellular Components of the Nervous System

### Neurons

Neurons are the functional units of the nervous system, specialized for receiving, processing, and transmitting information through electrical and chemical signals.

#### Neuronal Structure

**1. Cell Body (Soma)**
- Contains nucleus and organelles
- Site of protein synthesis
- Nissl substance (rough endoplasmic reticulum)
- Located in CNS gray matter or PNS ganglia

**2. Dendrites**
- Branching processes extending from cell body
- Receive synaptic inputs
- Contain receptors for neurotransmitters
- Increase surface area for communication

**3. Axon**
- Single elongated process
- Transmits action potentials
- Originates at axon hillock
- May be myelinated or unmyelinated
- Terminates at axon terminals (boutons)

**4. Synaptic Terminals**
- Specialized endings for neurotransmitter release
- Contain synaptic vesicles
- Form synapses with target cells
- Chemical or electrical synapses

#### Neuronal Classification

**By Structure:**
- **Unipolar**: Single process (pseudounipolar sensory neurons)
- **Bipolar**: Two processes (retinal, olfactory neurons)
- **Multipolar**: Multiple dendrites, one axon (motor neurons, interneurons)

**By Function:**
- **Sensory (Afferent)**: Convey information to CNS
- **Motor (Efferent)**: Convey commands from CNS
- **Interneurons**: Connect neurons within CNS

**By Neurotransmitter:**
- Cholinergic (acetylcholine)
- Adrenergic (norepinephrine)
- Dopaminergic (dopamine)
- Serotonergic (serotonin)
- GABAergic (GABA)
- Glutamatergic (glutamate)

### Glial Cells

Glial cells (neuroglia) outnumber neurons and provide essential support functions. They do not generate action potentials but are crucial for nervous system function.

#### CNS Glial Cells

**1. Astrocytes**
- Most abundant glial cells
- Star-shaped with multiple processes
- Functions:
  - Blood-brain barrier maintenance
  - Metabolic support for neurons
  - Neurotransmitter uptake and recycling
  - Regulation of extracellular ion concentrations
  - Synaptic modulation
  - Scar formation after injury (gliosis)
- Types:
  - Protoplasmic (gray matter)
  - Fibrous (white matter)

**2. Oligodendrocytes**
- Form myelin in CNS
- One oligodendrocyte myelinates multiple axons (up to 50)
- Functions:
  - Increase conduction velocity
  - Provide metabolic support
  - Organize axon domains
- Target in multiple sclerosis

**3. Microglia**
- CNS resident immune cells
- Mesodermal origin (unlike other glia)
- Functions:
  - Immune surveillance
  - Phagocytosis of debris
  - Antigen presentation
  - Cytokine secretion
- Activated in inflammation and disease

**4. Ependymal Cells**
- Line ventricles and central canal
- Ciliated columnar epithelium
- Functions:
  - Facilitate CSF movement
  - Barrier between CSF and brain tissue
  - Some regions produce CSF (choroid plexus)

#### PNS Glial Cells

**1. Schwann Cells**
- Form myelin in PNS
- One Schwann cell myelinates one axon segment
- Functions:
  - Increase conduction velocity
  - Provide neurotrophic support
  - Facilitate axon regeneration after injury
- Can also ensheath unmyelinated fibers (Remak bundles)

**2. Satellite Cells**
- Surround neuronal cell bodies in ganglia
- Functions:
  - Metabolic support
  - Regulate microenvironment
  - Similar to astrocytes in CNS

## Central Nervous System (CNS)

### Brain

The brain is the control center of the nervous system, responsible for consciousness, cognition, emotion, sensation, and motor control.

#### Major Regions

**1. Cerebrum**
- Largest part of the brain
- Divided into two hemispheres
- Surface features:
  - Gyri (ridges)
  - Sulci (grooves)
  - Fissures (deep grooves)
- Four lobes per hemisphere:
  - Frontal: Executive function, motor control
  - Parietal: Somatosensory processing, spatial awareness
  - Temporal: Auditory processing, memory
  - Occipital: Visual processing
- Deep structures:
  - Basal ganglia (motor control)
  - Limbic system (emotion, memory)
  - White matter tracts

**2. Diencephalon**
- Located between cerebrum and brainstem
- Components:
  - **Thalamus**: Sensory relay station
  - **Hypothalamus**: Homeostasis, endocrine control
  - **Epithalamus**: Pineal gland, habenula
  - **Subthalamus**: Motor control (subthalamic nucleus)

**3. Brainstem**
- Connects brain to spinal cord
- Contains cranial nerve nuclei (CN III-XII)
- Three divisions:
  - **Midbrain**: Eye movements, motor control
  - **Pons**: Respiratory control, facial sensation
  - **Medulla Oblongata**: Cardiovascular control, respiratory control

**4. Cerebellum**
- "Little brain" posterior to brainstem
- Functions:
  - Motor coordination
  - Balance and equilibrium
  - Motor learning
  - Cognitive functions
- Three lobes:
  - Anterior lobe
  - Posterior lobe
  - Flocculonodular lobe

### Gray Matter vs. White Matter

**Gray Matter**
- Composed of:
  - Neuronal cell bodies
  - Dendrites
  - Unmyelinated axons
  - Glial cells
  - Synapses
- Locations in CNS:
  - Cerebral cortex (outer layer)
  - Deep nuclei (basal ganglia, thalamus)
  - Spinal cord (central butterfly/H-shape)
  - Cerebellar cortex

**White Matter**
- Composed of:
  - Myelinated axons
  - Oligodendrocytes
  - Astrocytes
- Locations in CNS:
  - Deep to cerebral cortex
  - Corpus callosum
  - Internal capsule
  - Cerebellar white matter (arbor vitae)
  - Spinal cord (peripheral)

### Ventricular System

The brain contains a system of interconnected cavities filled with cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).

**Components:**
1. **Lateral Ventricles** (1st and 2nd)
   - Located in cerebral hemispheres
   - C-shaped
   - Horns: Frontal, temporal, occipital, atrial

2. **Third Ventricle**
   - Midline cavity in diencephalon
   - Connected to lateral ventricles via interventricular foramina (of Monro)

3. **Cerebral Aqueduct** (of Sylvius)
   - Narrow channel through midbrain
   - Connects 3rd and 4th ventricles

4. **Fourth Ventricle**
   - Between pons/medulla and cerebellum
   - Opens to subarachnoid space via:
     - Two lateral apertures (foramina of Luschka)
     - One median aperture (foramen of Magendie)

**CSF Production and Circulation:**
- Produced by choroid plexus (primarily in lateral ventricles)
- Rate: ~500 mL/day (total volume ~150 mL)
- Circulation pathway:
  - Lateral ventricles → Interventricular foramina → 3rd ventricle
  - 3rd ventricle → Cerebral aqueduct → 4th ventricle
  - 4th ventricle → Subarachnoid space → Arachnoid granulations
- Reabsorbed into venous system via arachnoid granulations

### Meninges

Three protective layers surround the CNS:

**1. Dura Mater** (outermost)
- Thick, tough fibrous membrane
- Two layers in cranium:
  - Periosteal layer (adheres to skull)
  - Meningeal layer
- Forms dural folds:
  - Falx cerebri (between hemispheres)
  - Tentorium cerebelli (separates cerebrum from cerebellum)
  - Falx cerebelli (between cerebellar hemispheres)
- Contains dural venous sinuses

**2. Arachnoid Mater** (middle)
- Delicate, avascular membrane
- Arachnoid granulations: CSF reabsorption sites
- Subarachnoid space contains:
  - CSF
  - Major arteries
  - Arachnoid trabeculae

**3. Pia Mater** (innermost)
- Thin, vascular membrane
- Adheres closely to brain surface
- Follows gyri and sulci
- Forms part of choroid plexus

**Clinical Significance:**
- **Epidural hematoma**: Between skull and dura (middle meningeal artery)
- **Subdural hematoma**: Between dura and arachnoid (bridging veins)
- **Subarachnoid hemorrhage**: In subarachnoid space (ruptured aneurysm)
- **Meningitis**: Inflammation of meninges

### Spinal Cord

The spinal cord extends from the medulla oblongata to the conus medullaris (typically L1-L2 vertebral level in adults).

#### Gross Anatomy

**External Features:**
- Length: ~45 cm in adults
- Diameter: ~1 cm (varies by level)
- Enlargements:
  - Cervical (C5-T1): Brachial plexus innervation
  - Lumbosacral (L1-S2): Lumbosacral plexus innervation
- Conus medullaris: Tapered end of spinal cord
- Cauda equina: Nerve roots below conus medullaris
- Filum terminale: Fibrous extension to coccyx

**Surface Landmarks:**
- Anterior median fissure (deep)
- Posterior median sulcus (shallow)
- Anterolateral sulcus (ventral root exit)
- Posterolateral sulcus (dorsal root entry)

#### Internal Organization

**Gray Matter** (H or butterfly shaped)
- **Anterior (Ventral) Horn**: Motor neurons
- **Posterior (Dorsal) Horn**: Sensory processing
- **Lateral Horn**: Autonomic neurons (T1-L2, S2-S4)
- **Gray Commissure**: Connects left and right sides

**White Matter** (Surrounding gray matter)
- **Anterior (Ventral) Column**: Descending motor tracts
- **Lateral Column**: Ascending sensory and descending motor tracts
- **Posterior (Dorsal) Column**: Ascending sensory tracts (touch, vibration)

**Spinal Segments:**
- 31 total segments
- Each segment gives rise to one pair of spinal nerves
- Segments do not align with vertebrae (especially lower)

## Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

### Spinal Nerves

**Formation and Structure:**
- Formed by union of dorsal and ventral roots
- Exit through intervertebral foramina
- Mixed nerves (motor and sensory)

**Components:**
- **Dorsal Root**: Sensory afferents
  - Dorsal root ganglion contains sensory neuron cell bodies
- **Ventral Root**: Motor efferents
  - Cell bodies in anterior horn
- **Spinal Nerve**: Mixed nerve after roots join
- **Rami**:
  - Dorsal ramus: Back muscles and skin
  - Ventral ramus: Limbs and trunk (forms plexuses)
  - Communicating rami: Autonomic connections (white and gray)

**Dermatomes:**
- Area of skin innervated by single spinal nerve
- Clinically important for:
  - Localizing spinal cord lesions
  - Assessing nerve root compression
  - Mapping sensory deficits
- Key landmarks:
  - C5: Lateral arm (deltoid region)
  - C6: Thumb
  - C7: Middle finger
  - C8: Little finger
  - T4: Nipple line
  - T10: Umbilicus
  - L4: Medial malleolus
  - L5: Dorsum of foot
  - S1: Lateral foot
  - S2-S4: Perineum

**Myotomes:**
- Group of muscles innervated by single spinal nerve
- Testing helps localize lesions
- Examples:
  - C5: Shoulder abduction (deltoid)
  - C6: Elbow flexion (biceps)
  - C7: Elbow extension (triceps)
  - C8: Finger flexion
  - L2-L4: Knee extension (quadriceps)
  - L5: Ankle dorsiflexion
  - S1: Ankle plantarflexion

### Nerve Plexuses

Complex networks of nerve fibers formed by ventral rami.

**1. Cervical Plexus (C1-C4)**
- **Motor branches**: Neck muscles, diaphragm (phrenic nerve, C3-C5)
- **Sensory branches**: Neck and shoulder skin
- **Key nerve**: Phrenic nerve (diaphragm innervation)

**2. Brachial Plexus (C5-T1)**
- **Organization**: Roots → Trunks → Divisions → Cords → Branches
- **Major terminal branches**:
  - Musculocutaneous (C5-C7): Arm flexors
  - Axillary (C5-C6): Deltoid, teres minor
  - Radial (C5-T1): Arm and forearm extensors
  - Median (C6-T1): Forearm flexors, hand muscles
  - Ulnar (C8-T1): Hand intrinsic muscles
- **Clinical**: Erb-Duchenne palsy (upper trunk), Klumpke palsy (lower trunk)

**3. Lumbar Plexus (L1-L4)**
- **Major branches**:
  - Iliohypogastric, ilioinguinal (L1)
  - Genitofemoral (L1-L2)
  - Lateral femoral cutaneous (L2-L3)
  - Femoral (L2-L4): Thigh extensors
  - Obturator (L2-L4): Thigh adductors

**4. Sacral Plexus (L4-S4)**
- **Major branches**:
  - Superior gluteal (L4-S1): Hip abductors
  - Inferior gluteal (L5-S2): Gluteus maximus
  - Sciatic (L4-S3): Largest nerve, divides into:
    - Tibial: Leg and foot flexors
    - Common peroneal: Leg and foot extensors
  - Pudendal (S2-S4): Perineum

### Cranial Nerves

Twelve pairs of nerves arising from brain (detailed in brain-anatomy.md).

**Classification:**
- Sensory: I, II, VIII
- Motor: III, IV, VI, XI, XII
- Mixed: V, VII, IX, X

### Autonomic Nervous System

The autonomic nervous system regulates involuntary functions of visceral organs.

#### Sympathetic Division (Thoracolumbar)

**Anatomy:**
- **Origin**: Lateral horn of spinal cord (T1-L2)
- **Preganglionic neurons**: Short
  - Cell bodies in lateral horn
  - Exit via ventral roots
  - Synapse in sympathetic ganglia
- **Sympathetic chain ganglia**: Paravertebral
  - Extend from cervical to sacral regions
  - Connected by sympathetic trunk
- **Prevertebral ganglia**: Around aorta
  - Celiac ganglion
  - Superior mesenteric ganglion
  - Inferior mesenteric ganglion
- **Postganglionic neurons**: Long
  - Extend to target organs
  - Use norepinephrine (usually)

**Functions** (Fight or Flight):
- Increased heart rate and contractility
- Bronchodilation
- Pupillary dilation (mydriasis)
- Decreased GI motility
- Sphincter contraction
- Piloerection
- Sweating
- Vasoconstriction (α-adrenergic)
- Vasodilation in skeletal muscle (β2-adrenergic)

#### Parasympathetic Division (Craniosacral)

**Anatomy:**
- **Cranial component**: CN III, VII, IX, X
  - CN III: Ciliary ganglion (pupillary constriction)
  - CN VII: Pterygopalatine and submandibular ganglia (secretion)
  - CN IX: Otic ganglion (parotid secretion)
  - CN X: Vagus (thoracic and abdominal viscera)
- **Sacral component**: S2-S4
  - Pelvic splanchnic nerves
  - Innervate pelvic viscera
- **Preganglionic neurons**: Long
  - Synapse near or in target organs
- **Postganglionic neurons**: Short
  - Use acetylcholine

**Functions** (Rest and Digest):
- Decreased heart rate
- Bronchoconstriction
- Pupillary constriction (miosis)
- Increased GI motility
- Sphincter relaxation
- Secretion (salivary, lacrimal, digestive)
- Bladder contraction
- Sexual arousal (erection)

#### Enteric Nervous System

- **Location**: GI tract wall
- **Components**:
  - Myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus: Between muscle layers
  - Submucosal (Meissner's) plexus: In submucosa
- **Function**: Intrinsic GI control (can function independently)
- **Modulation**: Autonomic input modulates activity

## Embryological Development

Understanding nervous system development helps explain anatomical organization and congenital anomalies.

### Early Neurulation (Weeks 3-4)

**1. Neural Plate Formation**
- Ectoderm thickens along midline
- Induced by notochord
- Day 18-19 post-fertilization

**2. Neural Fold Formation**
- Neural plate edges elevate
- Neural groove forms centrally
- Day 20-21

**3. Neural Tube Closure**
- Neural folds fuse in midline
- Begins at cervical region
- Proceeds cranially and caudally
- Anterior neuropore closes: Day 25
- Posterior neuropore closes: Day 27
- **Defects**: Spina bifida (posterior), anencephaly (anterior)

**4. Neural Crest Formation**
- Cells at neural fold edges
- Migrate throughout body
- Give rise to:
  - PNS neurons and glia
  - Autonomic ganglia
  - Melanocytes
  - Adrenal medulla
  - Some craniofacial structures

### Brain Vesicle Development (Weeks 4-8)

**Primary Vesicles (Week 4):**
1. **Prosencephalon** (Forebrain)
2. **Mesencephalon** (Midbrain)
3. **Rhombencephalon** (Hindbrain)

**Secondary Vesicles (Week 5):**
1. Prosencephalon divides into:
   - **Telencephalon** → Cerebral hemispheres
   - **Diencephalon** → Thalamus, hypothalamus
2. Mesencephalon remains:
   - **Mesencephalon** → Midbrain
3. Rhombencephalon divides into:
   - **Metencephalon** → Pons, cerebellum
   - **Myelencephalon** → Medulla oblongata

**Adult Derivatives:**
- **Telencephalon**: Cerebral cortex, basal ganglia, lateral ventricles
- **Diencephalon**: Thalamus, hypothalamus, third ventricle
- **Mesencephalon**: Midbrain, cerebral aqueduct
- **Metencephalon**: Pons, cerebellum, upper 4th ventricle
- **Myelencephalon**: Medulla, lower 4th ventricle

### Cellular Differentiation

**Neuronal Development:**
- Neuroepithelial cells proliferate in ventricular zone
- Migration to cortical plate (inside-out pattern)
- Differentiation into specific neuronal types
- Synapse formation and refinement
- Programmed cell death (apoptosis) of excess neurons

**Glial Development:**
- Radial glia guide neuronal migration
- Astrocyte and oligodendrocyte differentiation
- Myelination begins prenatally, continues postnatally
- Microglia migrate from mesoderm

## Vascular Supply to the Nervous System

### Cerebral Circulation

**Arterial Supply:**
- **Internal carotid arteries** (anterior circulation)
  - Anterior cerebral artery (ACA)
  - Middle cerebral artery (MCA)
- **Vertebral arteries** (posterior circulation)
  - Basilar artery
  - Posterior cerebral artery (PCA)
- **Circle of Willis**: Anastomotic ring at brain base
  - Provides collateral circulation
  - Anterior communicating artery
  - Posterior communicating arteries

**Venous Drainage:**
- Superficial and deep cerebral veins
- Drain into dural venous sinuses
- Superior sagittal sinus
- Transverse and sigmoid sinuses
- Internal jugular veins

### Spinal Cord Circulation

**Arterial Supply:**
- **Anterior spinal artery**: Anterior 2/3 of cord
- **Posterior spinal arteries** (paired): Posterior 1/3
- **Radicular arteries**: Segmental reinforcement
  - Artery of Adamkiewicz (largest, T9-L2 usually)

**Venous Drainage:**
- Anterior and posterior spinal veins
- Internal vertebral venous plexus
- Drain into segmental veins

## Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)

### Structure
- Formed by:
  - Tight junctions between endothelial cells
  - Basement membrane
  - Astrocyte end-feet
- Restricts paracellular movement of substances

### Function
- Protects brain from toxins and pathogens
- Maintains stable extracellular environment
- Regulates ion concentrations
- Excludes most drugs (must be lipophilic)

### Circumventricular Organs
Areas lacking BBB:
- Posterior pituitary
- Area postrema (vomiting center)
- Pineal gland
- Median eminence
- Allow hormone secretion or blood sampling

## Clinical Correlations

### Hydrocephalus
- **Definition**: Abnormal accumulation of CSF
- **Types**:
  - **Obstructive**: Blockage of CSF flow
    - Aqueductal stenosis
    - Tumors
    - Congenital malformations
  - **Communicating**: Impaired reabsorption
    - Post-hemorrhagic
    - Post-infectious
- **Presentation**: Increased intracranial pressure, headache, vomiting
- **Treatment**: Ventriculoperitoneal shunt

### Meningitis
- **Definition**: Inflammation of meninges
- **Etiology**: Bacterial, viral, fungal
- **Presentation**: Fever, headache, neck stiffness, altered mental status
- **Diagnosis**: Lumbar puncture (CSF analysis)
- **Treatment**: Antibiotics (bacterial), supportive (viral)

### Spinal Cord Injury
- **Complete**: Total loss of function below lesion
- **Incomplete**: Partial preservation of function
- **Syndromes**: See spinal-cord.md
- **Management**: Stabilization, rehabilitation

### Peripheral Neuropathy
- **Definition**: Damage to peripheral nerves
- **Causes**: Diabetes, toxins, hereditary, inflammatory
- **Presentation**: Weakness, numbness, pain
- **Distribution**: Stocking-glove pattern (length-dependent)

## Key Points

1. The nervous system is divided into CNS (brain and spinal cord) and PNS (cranial and spinal nerves)

2. Neurons are the functional units; glial cells provide essential support

3. Gray matter contains cell bodies; white matter contains myelinated axons

4. The ventricular system produces and circulates CSF

5. Three meningeal layers protect the CNS: dura, arachnoid, pia

6. The spinal cord is organized into segments with specific dermatomes and myotomes

7. The autonomic nervous system has sympathetic (thoracolumbar) and parasympathetic (craniosacral) divisions

8. Embryological development proceeds from neural tube to brain vesicles to adult structures

9. Cerebral circulation includes anterior (carotid) and posterior (vertebrobasilar) systems

10. The blood-brain barrier protects the CNS but also limits drug delivery
