# Metabolism

## Overview

Metabolism encompasses all chemical reactions within living organisms that maintain life. It consists of two major processes: catabolism (breakdown of molecules to release energy) and anabolism (synthesis of molecules using energy). These processes are tightly regulated to maintain cellular energy balance and provide building blocks for growth and repair.

### Key Concepts
- **Catabolism**: Breakdown pathways that release energy (ATP production)
- **Anabolism**: Synthetic pathways that consume energy (biosynthesis)
- **Energy currency**: ATP, NADH, FADH2, and other high-energy compounds
- **Metabolic regulation**: Control of pathway activity through enzymes and feedback

## Energy Metabolism

### ATP Structure and Function
- **Adenosine triphosphate**: Universal energy currency
- **High-energy bonds**: Phosphoanhydride bonds release ~30.5 kJ/mol
- **ATP cycle**: Hydrolysis to ADP + Pi provides energy for cellular work
- **Regeneration**: ADP + Pi → ATP through cellular respiration

### Energy Sources
1. **Carbohydrates**: Primary fuel, 4 kcal/g
2. **Lipids**: Energy storage, 9 kcal/g
3. **Proteins**: Structural components, 4 kcal/g (used when other sources depleted)

## Carbohydrate Metabolism

### Glycolysis
- **Location**: Cytoplasm
- **Pathway**: Glucose → 2 Pyruvate
- **Net yield**: 2 ATP, 2 NADH
- **Regulation**: Hexokinase, phosphofructokinase, pyruvate kinase
- **Anaerobic**: Can occur without oxygen

### Citric Acid Cycle (Krebs Cycle)
- **Location**: Mitochondrial matrix
- **Pathway**: Acetyl-CoA → CO2 + reduced coenzymes
- **Yield per cycle**: 3 NADH, 1 FADH2, 1 GTP
- **Regulation**: Citrate synthase, isocitrate dehydrogenase, α-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase

### Electron Transport Chain and Oxidative Phosphorylation
- **Location**: Inner mitochondrial membrane
- **Components**: Complexes I-IV, coenzyme Q, cytochrome c
- **Proton gradient**: Drives ATP synthesis via ATP synthase
- **Yield**: ~30-32 ATP per glucose (theoretical maximum)

### Gluconeogenesis
- **Function**: Glucose synthesis from non-carbohydrate sources
- **Substrates**: Lactate, glycerol, amino acids
- **Location**: Primarily liver, also kidneys, intestine
- **Regulation**: Reciprocal to glycolysis

### Glycogen Metabolism
- **Glycogenesis**: Glucose → glycogen (storage)
- **Glycogenolysis**: Glycogen → glucose (mobilization)
- **Regulation**: Insulin (storage), glucagon/epinephrine (mobilization)
- **Storage sites**: Liver (~100g), muscle (~400g)

## Lipid Metabolism

### Fatty Acid Oxidation (β-oxidation)
- **Location**: Mitochondria
- **Process**: Fatty acids → Acetyl-CoA
- **Yield**: High ATP production (e.g., palmitate yields 129 ATP)
- **Regulation**: Carnitine shuttle, malonyl-CoA inhibition

### Fatty Acid Synthesis
- **Location**: Cytoplasm
- **Precursor**: Acetyl-CoA (from citrate)
- **Key enzyme**: Acetyl-CoA carboxylase
- **Regulation**: Insulin (stimulates), glucagon (inhibits)

### Ketogenesis
- **Location**: Liver mitochondria
- **Substrates**: Acetyl-CoA from fatty acid oxidation
- **Products**: Acetoacetate, β-hydroxybutyrate, acetone
- **Function**: Alternative fuel during fasting/starvation

### Cholesterol Metabolism
- **Synthesis**: HMG-CoA reductase (rate-limiting enzyme)
- **Regulation**: Feedback inhibition by cholesterol
- **Functions**: Membrane component, steroid hormone precursor
- **Transport**: Lipoproteins (LDL, HDL, VLDL)

## Protein and Amino Acid Metabolism

### Protein Turnover
- **Synthesis**: Translation of mRNA into proteins
- **Degradation**: Proteasomal and lysosomal pathways
- **Balance**: Determines net protein content

### Amino Acid Catabolism
- **Deamination**: Removal of amino groups
- **Transamination**: Transfer of amino groups
- **Carbon skeleton disposal**: Glucogenic or ketogenic pathways

### Urea Cycle
- **Function**: Disposal of toxic ammonia
- **Location**: Liver (primarily)
- **Process**: NH3 + CO2 → Urea
- **Regulation**: N-acetylglutamate (allosteric activator)

## Integrated Metabolism

### Fed State (Anabolic)
- **Insulin dominant**: Promotes storage pathways
- **Glucose**: Uptake and storage as glycogen
- **Fatty acids**: Synthesis and storage as triglycerides
- **Proteins**: Synthesis from amino acids

### Fasting State (Catabolic)
- **Glucagon/cortisol dominant**: Promotes mobilization
- **Glucose**: Production via gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis
- **Fatty acids**: Mobilization from adipose tissue
- **Proteins**: Breakdown to provide amino acids

### Exercise Metabolism
- **Immediate**: Phosphocreatine system (0-10 seconds)
- **Short-term**: Glycolysis (10 seconds - 2 minutes)
- **Long-term**: Oxidative metabolism (>2 minutes)

## Metabolic Regulation

### Allosteric Regulation
- **Positive effectors**: Enhance enzyme activity
- **Negative effectors**: Inhibit enzyme activity
- **Examples**: PFK-1 (inhibited by ATP, activated by AMP)

### Covalent Modification
- **Phosphorylation**: Usually regulatory
- **Acetylation**: Histone modification, metabolic enzymes
- **Ubiquitination**: Protein degradation signals

### Hormonal Control
- **Insulin**: Anabolic hormone (fed state)
- **Glucagon**: Catabolic hormone (fasting state)
- **Cortisol**: Stress hormone (gluconeogenesis)
- **Thyroid hormones**: Metabolic rate regulation

## Clinical Applications

### Metabolic Disorders
- **Diabetes mellitus**: Glucose homeostasis disruption
- **Metabolic syndrome**: Insulin resistance cluster
- **Inborn errors**: Enzyme deficiencies affecting specific pathways
- **Mitochondrial diseases**: Oxidative phosphorylation defects

### Laboratory Assessment
- **Glucose**: Fasting, postprandial, HbA1c
- **Lipids**: Total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides
- **Proteins**: Albumin, total protein, nitrogen balance
- **Metabolites**: Lactate, ketones, uric acid

### Therapeutic Targets
- **Enzyme inhibitors**: Statins (HMG-CoA reductase)
- **Hormone replacement**: Insulin therapy
- **Dietary interventions**: Carbohydrate restriction, fasting
- **Exercise**: Enhances metabolic flexibility

## Key Points

- Metabolism maintains energy balance through coordinated catabolic and anabolic pathways
- ATP is the universal energy currency linking energy-releasing and energy-requiring processes
- Carbohydrate metabolism provides rapid energy through glycolysis and sustained energy through oxidation
- Lipid metabolism provides the most efficient energy storage and mobilization system
- Protein metabolism maintains structural integrity and provides metabolic flexibility
- Metabolic regulation occurs at multiple levels: allosteric, covalent modification, and hormonal
- Fed and fasting states require different metabolic strategies coordinated by hormones
- Understanding metabolism is crucial for managing diabetes, obesity, and cardiovascular disease
- Exercise and nutrition can modulate metabolic pathways for health benefits
