# Abdominal Surgery

## Overview

Abdominal surgery encompasses operative procedures on the organs and structures within the abdominal cavity. Common conditions requiring abdominal surgery include acute appendicitis, cholecystitis, bowel obstruction, and hernias. These procedures range from emergent operations for life-threatening conditions to elective repairs of anatomical defects.

## Acute Appendicitis

### Indications

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Right Lower Quadrant Pain**: Classic periumbilical pain migrating to McBurney's point
- **Anorexia**: Loss of appetite is nearly universal
- **Nausea and Vomiting**: Typically follows onset of pain
- **Fever**: Low-grade initially, higher with perforation
- **Rebound Tenderness**: Indicates peritoneal inflammation
- **Rovsing Sign**: RLQ pain with LLQ palpation

#### Diagnostic Criteria
- **Clinical Scoring**: Alvarado score ≥7 suggests appendicitis
- **Laboratory Findings**: Leukocytosis (10,000-18,000), left shift
- **Imaging Studies**:
  - **CT Scan**: Gold standard (sensitivity 95%, specificity 94%)
  - **Ultrasound**: Preferred in children and pregnancy (sensitivity 85%)
  - **MRI**: Reserved for pregnant patients when ultrasound inconclusive

#### Special Populations
- **Pediatric Patients**: Higher perforation rates due to delayed diagnosis
- **Elderly**: Atypical presentations, increased morbidity
- **Pregnancy**: Second trimester safest for surgery, appendix displaced superiorly
- **Immunocompromised**: Subtle presentations, rapid progression

### Preoperative Assessment

#### History and Physical Examination
- **Duration of Symptoms**: Perforation risk increases after 24-48 hours
- **Previous Abdominal Surgery**: May affect surgical approach
- **Comorbidities**: Cardiac, pulmonary, renal function assessment
- **Medications**: Anticoagulation, immunosuppression
- **Allergies**: Particularly antibiotics and anesthetic agents

#### Laboratory Studies
- **Complete Blood Count**: Baseline leukocyte count
- **Basic Metabolic Panel**: Electrolytes, renal function
- **Urinalysis**: Rule out urinary tract infection, pregnancy test in females
- **Coagulation Studies**: If on anticoagulation or bleeding history
- **Type and Screen**: For complex or high-risk cases

#### Imaging Protocol
- **CT Abdomen/Pelvis with IV Contrast**:
  - Appendiceal diameter >6 mm
  - Periappendiceal fat stranding
  - Appendiceal wall thickening
  - Presence of appendicolith (30% of cases)
  - Free fluid or abscess formation

#### Risk Stratification
- **ASA Classification**: Anesthetic risk assessment
- **Perforation Risk Factors**: Duration >48 hours, appendicolith, fever >38.5°C
- **Surgical Risk**: Age >65, multiple comorbidities, immunosuppression

### Surgical Techniques

#### Laparoscopic Appendectomy (Standard Approach)

**Patient Positioning**
- Supine position with left arm tucked
- Slight Trendelenburg and left lateral tilt
- Prepare abdomen from nipples to pubis

**Port Placement**
- **Umbilical Port (10-12 mm)**: Camera port, primary access
- **Left Lower Quadrant (5 mm)**: Working port
- **Suprapubic (5 mm)**: Retraction and working port

**Operative Steps**
1. **Pneumoperitoneum Establishment**: 15 mmHg CO2 insufflation
2. **Diagnostic Laparoscopy**: Systematic inspection of abdomen
3. **Appendix Identification**: Trace cecum along taenia coli
4. **Mesoappendix Division**: Harmonic scalpel or clips
5. **Base Control**: Endoloop, stapler, or clips
6. **Appendix Division**: 5 mm proximal to base
7. **Specimen Retrieval**: Endobag to prevent contamination
8. **Irrigation**: If perforation or contamination present
9. **Hemostasis Verification**: Inspect appendiceal base and mesentery
10. **Closure**: Fascial closure of 10 mm ports

**Technical Considerations**
- **Retrocecal Appendix**: Mobilize cecum for adequate exposure
- **Complicated Appendicitis**: Consider diagnostic laparoscopy first
- **Conversion Criteria**: Unable to identify appendix, bleeding, extensive adhesions

#### Open Appendectomy (McBurney Approach)

**Incision**
- **Location**: McBurney's point (1/3 distance from ASIS to umbilicus)
- **Type**: Oblique or transverse incision (5-7 cm)
- **Muscle Splitting**: Preserve muscle fibers

**Operative Steps**
1. **Skin and Subcutaneous Tissue Division**
2. **External Oblique Aponeurosis Incision**: Along fiber direction
3. **Muscle Splitting**: Blunt separation of internal oblique and transversus
4. **Peritoneum Entry**: Sharp incision with care for bowel
5. **Appendix Delivery**: Identify cecum, trace to appendix
6. **Mesoappendix Division**: Clamp and ligate
7. **Base Management**: Purse-string or simple ligation
8. **Appendix Division**: Between clamps
9. **Stump Inversion**: Optional, purse-string technique
10. **Closure**: Anatomic layer closure

**Indications for Open Approach**
- Hemodynamic instability
- Extensive adhesions from prior surgery
- Inability to tolerate pneumoperitoneum
- Surgeon preference or experience

#### Management of Complicated Appendicitis

**Perforated Appendicitis**
- Copious irrigation with warm saline
- Consider drain placement if abscess present
- Extended-spectrum antibiotic coverage
- Higher wound infection risk

**Appendiceal Abscess**
- **Initial Management**: CT-guided drainage if >3 cm
- **Antibiotic Therapy**: 7-10 days IV antibiotics
- **Interval Appendectomy**: 6-8 weeks after resolution
- **Controversial**: Immediate appendectomy vs. interval approach

### Postoperative Management

#### Immediate Postoperative Care
- **Recovery Room**: Monitor vital signs, pain control
- **Diet Advancement**: Clear liquids when alert, advance as tolerated
- **Early Mobilization**: Ambulate within 4-6 hours
- **Foley Catheter**: Remove when patient mobile
- **DVT Prophylaxis**: Sequential compression devices, heparin if indicated

#### Antibiotic Management
- **Uncomplicated Appendicitis**: Single preoperative dose
- **Gangrenous Appendicitis**: 24 hours postoperative
- **Perforated Appendicitis**: 4-7 days IV antibiotics
- **Typical Regimen**: Piperacillin-tazobactam or ceftriaxone + metronidazole

#### Pain Management
- **Multimodal Approach**: NSAIDs + acetaminophen
- **Opioid Minimization**: Use only for breakthrough pain
- **Local Anesthetic**: TAP block or port site infiltration
- **Patient-Controlled Analgesia**: Reserved for complex cases

#### Discharge Criteria
- Tolerating regular diet
- Pain controlled with oral medications
- Ambulating independently
- No fever >24 hours
- Normal vital signs
- Adequate home support

#### Discharge Instructions
- **Activity**: Light activity for 1 week, no heavy lifting for 2-3 weeks
- **Wound Care**: Keep incisions clean and dry, remove dressings in 48 hours
- **Diet**: No restrictions for uncomplicated appendicitis
- **Follow-up**: 2-3 weeks for wound check and pathology discussion
- **Warning Signs**: Fever, increasing abdominal pain, wound drainage, vomiting

### Complications

#### Intraoperative Complications
- **Bleeding** (1-2%): Mesoappendix vessel injury, port site bleeding
- **Bowel Injury** (0.5%): Recognition and repair essential
- **Conversion to Open** (5-10%): Not a complication but change in approach

#### Early Postoperative Complications (0-30 days)
- **Wound Infection** (5-10% perforated, 1% non-perforated):
  - **Presentation**: Erythema, drainage, pain
  - **Management**: Open wound, local care, antibiotics if cellulitis

- **Intra-abdominal Abscess** (2-6% perforated):
  - **Presentation**: Fever, leukocytosis, abdominal pain
  - **Diagnosis**: CT scan with oral and IV contrast
  - **Management**: CT-guided drainage, antibiotics

- **Small Bowel Obstruction** (1-2%):
  - **Early**: Postoperative ileus vs. mechanical obstruction
  - **Management**: NPO, NG decompression, IV fluids, imaging

- **Urinary Retention** (3-5%):
  - **Risk Factors**: Age, male gender, opioid use
  - **Management**: Bladder scan, catheterization if >400 mL

#### Late Complications (>30 days)
- **Incisional Hernia** (0.5-1%):
  - **Risk Factors**: Port site >10 mm without fascial closure
  - **Prevention**: Fascial closure of ports ≥10 mm

- **Adhesive Small Bowel Obstruction** (1-3%):
  - **Timeline**: Can occur months to years postoperatively
  - **Management**: Conservative initially, surgery if persistent

- **Chronic Pain** (<1%):
  - **Nerve Entrapment**: Iliohypogastric or ilioinguinal nerve
  - **Management**: Nerve blocks, physical therapy, rarely reoperation

### Outcomes

#### Success Rates
- **Operative Mortality**: <0.1% uncomplicated, 1-5% perforated
- **Negative Appendectomy Rate**: 10-15% (higher in women of childbearing age)
- **Perforation Rate**: 20-30% overall, higher in extremes of age
- **Hospital Stay**: 1-2 days uncomplicated, 3-7 days perforated

#### Quality Metrics
- **Time to Antibiotics**: Within 1 hour of diagnosis
- **Time to Surgery**: Within 24 hours for uncomplicated
- **Antibiotic Duration**: Appropriate for complexity
- **Readmission Rate**: <5% at 30 days

## Cholecystitis and Biliary Disease

### Indications

#### Acute Cholecystitis
- **Pathophysiology**: Cystic duct obstruction leading to gallbladder inflammation
- **Clinical Presentation**:
  - Right upper quadrant pain (constant, >6 hours)
  - Murphy's sign (inspiratory arrest with RUQ palpation)
  - Fever and leukocytosis
  - Nausea and vomiting

- **Diagnostic Criteria (Tokyo Guidelines)**:
  - **Local Signs**: Murphy's sign, RUQ mass/tenderness
  - **Systemic Signs**: Fever, elevated WBC, CRP
  - **Imaging**: Gallbladder wall thickening >3 mm, pericholecystic fluid

#### Symptomatic Cholelithiasis
- **Biliary Colic**: Recurrent RUQ pain after fatty meals
- **Complications Risk**: 1-3% per year develop complications
- **Quality of Life**: Significant impact on daily activities

#### Chronic Cholecystitis
- **Pathology**: Chronic inflammation, fibrosis, wall thickening
- **Presentation**: Recurrent biliary pain, dyspepsia
- **Porcelain Gallbladder**: 10-25% cancer risk, absolute indication

### Preoperative Assessment

#### Clinical Evaluation
- **Symptom Characterization**: Frequency, severity, triggers
- **Complication Assessment**: Jaundice, pancreatitis, sepsis
- **Fitness for Surgery**: Cardiopulmonary reserve
- **Previous Surgery**: Upper abdominal procedures affecting approach

#### Laboratory Studies
- **Liver Function Tests**: ALT, AST, alkaline phosphatase, bilirubin
- **Amylase/Lipase**: Rule out concurrent pancreatitis
- **Complete Blood Count**: Assess for leukocytosis, anemia
- **Coagulation Profile**: PT/INR if liver disease or anticoagulation

#### Imaging Studies
- **Ultrasound** (First-line):
  - Gallstones (sensitivity 95%)
  - Gallbladder wall thickening
  - Pericholecystic fluid
  - Sonographic Murphy's sign

- **HIDA Scan**:
  - Non-visualization suggests cystic duct obstruction
  - Useful when ultrasound equivocal

- **MRCP**:
  - Choledocholithiasis evaluation
  - Biliary anatomy delineation
  - Pre-ERCP planning

#### Risk Stratification
- **Severity Grading (Tokyo Guidelines)**:
  - **Grade I (Mild)**: No organ dysfunction
  - **Grade II (Moderate)**: WBC >18,000, palpable mass, >72 hours symptoms
  - **Grade III (Severe)**: Organ dysfunction (cardiovascular, renal, hepatic, etc.)

### Surgical Techniques

#### Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy (Gold Standard)

**Patient Positioning**
- Supine position with reverse Trendelenburg
- Left side elevated 15-30 degrees
- Legs together or split-leg position

**Port Placement (Standard 4-Port Technique)**
- **Umbilical (10-12 mm)**: Camera port
- **Epigastric (5-10 mm)**: Primary working port
- **Right subcostal MCL (5 mm)**: Retraction
- **Right subcostal AAL (5 mm)**: Retraction/working port

**Critical View of Safety (CVS)**
Essential for preventing bile duct injury:
1. **Hepatocystic Triangle Cleared**: All fat and fibrous tissue removed
2. **Two Structures Only**: Cystic duct and cystic artery identified
3. **Gallbladder Separated**: Lower 1/3 of gallbladder separated from liver

**Operative Steps**
1. **Pneumoperitoneum**: 15 mmHg CO2 insufflation
2. **Diagnostic Laparoscopy**: Assess for additional pathology
3. **Gallbladder Retraction**: Fundus cephalad, Hartmann's pouch lateral
4. **Peritoneum Opening**: Expose hepatocystic triangle
5. **Dissection**: Achieve critical view of safety
6. **Cystic Artery Division**: Clip and divide (2 clips proximally, 1 distally)
7. **Cystic Duct Division**: Clip and divide after confirming anatomy
8. **Gallbladder Dissection**: Separate from liver bed using electrocautery
9. **Hemostasis**: Ensure liver bed dry
10. **Specimen Retrieval**: Extract through umbilical port
11. **Irrigation**: Remove bile and blood
12. **Closure**: Fascial closure of ports ≥10 mm

**Intraoperative Cholangiography**
- **Indications**: Abnormal LFTs, dilated CBD, unclear anatomy
- **Technique**: Inject contrast through cystic duct
- **Findings**: Identify stones, anatomy, filling defects

#### Management of Difficult Cholecystectomy

**Acute Inflammation**
- **Early Cholecystectomy**: Within 72 hours of symptom onset (preferred)
- **Delayed Cholecystectomy**: 6-8 weeks after resolution
- **Adhesiolysis**: Careful dissection of omental/bowel adhesions

**Bailout Procedures**
- **Subtotal Cholecystectomy**: Leave posterior wall attached to liver
- **Fundus-First Approach**: When Calot's triangle obscured
- **Cholecystostomy Tube**: Critically ill patients, drain for interval surgery

**Conversion to Open**
- **Indications**: Unable to achieve CVS, bleeding, bile duct injury suspected
- **Incision**: Right subcostal (Kocher) or midline
- **Not a Failure**: Appropriate response to difficult anatomy

### Postoperative Management

#### Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS)
- **Preoperative**: No prolonged fasting, carbohydrate loading
- **Intraoperative**: Minimize opioids, multimodal analgesia
- **Postoperative**: Early feeding, early mobilization

#### Immediate Postoperative Care
- **Same-Day Discharge**: Majority of uncomplicated cases
- **Diet**: Regular diet as tolerated
- **Activity**: No restrictions, return to work 1-2 weeks
- **Pain Management**: Oral NSAIDs + acetaminophen

#### Discharge Criteria
- Pain controlled with oral medications
- Tolerating oral intake
- Ambulating
- Voiding spontaneously
- No complications identified
- Adequate home support

### Complications

#### Intraoperative Complications
- **Bile Duct Injury** (0.3-0.7%):
  - **Prevention**: Critical view of safety
  - **Recognition**: Intraoperative cholangiography if suspected
  - **Management**: Immediate repair vs. delayed reconstruction

- **Vascular Injury** (0.5%):
  - Right hepatic artery, portal vein
  - Recognition and control essential
  - May require conversion to open

- **Gallbladder Perforation** (10-30%):
  - Spillage of bile and stones
  - Copious irrigation, retrieve all stones
  - May increase infection risk

#### Postoperative Complications
- **Bile Leak** (0.5-1%):
  - **Presentation**: Abdominal pain, fever, drain output
  - **Diagnosis**: HIDA scan, MRCP
  - **Management**: ERCP with sphincterotomy and stent

- **Retained Common Bile Duct Stones** (3-5%):
  - **Presentation**: Jaundice, pancreatitis, cholangitis
  - **Management**: ERCP with stone extraction

- **Port Site Infection** (1-3%):
  - Increased risk with gallbladder perforation
  - Local wound care, antibiotics if cellulitis

- **Wound Hernia** (0.5%):
  - Umbilical port site most common
  - Prevention: Fascial closure

### Outcomes

#### Efficacy
- **Symptom Resolution**: 95-98% pain-free postoperatively
- **Recurrence**: Not applicable after cholecystectomy
- **Return to Normal Activity**: 1-2 weeks

#### Safety
- **Mortality**: <0.1% elective, 0.5-1% emergency
- **Morbidity**: 5-10% overall complication rate
- **Conversion Rate**: 5-10% overall, higher in acute cholecystitis

#### Quality of Life
- **Post-Cholecystectomy Syndrome** (10-15%):
  - Persistent dyspepsia, diarrhea
  - Often related to other pathology (IBS, reflux)
  - Sphincter of Oddi dysfunction in subset

## Bowel Obstruction

### Indications

#### Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)

**Etiologies**
- **Adhesions** (60-75%): Prior abdominal surgery
- **Hernias** (15-20%): Internal, external, incisional
- **Malignancy** (10%): Primary or metastatic
- **Inflammatory** (5%): Crohn's disease, radiation
- **Intussusception** (rare in adults)

**Clinical Presentation**
- **Abdominal Pain**: Crampy, colicky, periumbilical
- **Vomiting**: Bilious, occurs early
- **Distension**: Variable, depends on level
- **Obstipation**: Complete obstruction
- **High-Pitched Bowel Sounds**: Early, absent if strangulation

**Imaging Findings (CT Abdomen/Pelvis)**
- **Dilated Small Bowel**: >3 cm proximal to obstruction
- **Collapsed Distal Bowel**: Decompressed
- **Transition Point**: Identifies obstruction level
- **Strangulation Signs**: Bowel wall thickening, pneumatosis, portal venous gas

#### Large Bowel Obstruction (LBO)

**Etiologies**
- **Colorectal Cancer** (60%): Most common cause
- **Diverticular Stricture** (20%)
- **Volvulus** (10-15%): Sigmoid or cecal
- **Fecal Impaction** (5%)

**Clinical Presentation**
- **Abdominal Distension**: Prominent
- **Crampy Abdominal Pain**: Lower abdomen
- **Nausea/Vomiting**: Late finding (feculent)
- **Obstipation**: Earlier than SBO
- **Tympany**: On percussion

### Preoperative Assessment

#### Clinical Evaluation
- **Vital Signs**: Tachycardia, hypotension suggest strangulation
- **Abdominal Examination**: Distension, tenderness, peritoneal signs
- **Hernia Examination**: All potential hernia sites
- **Rectal Examination**: Masses, fecal impaction, occult blood

#### Laboratory Studies
- **Complete Blood Count**: Leukocytosis suggests ischemia
- **Basic Metabolic Panel**: Assess dehydration, electrolyte abnormalities
- **Lactate**: Elevated in strangulation or ischemia
- **Liver Function Tests**: Baseline

#### Imaging
- **CT Abdomen/Pelvis with IV Contrast** (Study of Choice):
  - Etiology identification
  - Complete vs. partial obstruction
  - Strangulation assessment
  - Surgical planning

- **Plain Radiographs** (Limited Use):
  - Dilated loops of bowel
  - Air-fluid levels
  - Free air if perforation

#### Indications for Surgery
- **Absolute**:
  - Peritonitis
  - Pneumoperitoneum
  - Strangulation signs
  - Complete obstruction without improvement

- **Relative**:
  - Failure of conservative management (48-72 hours)
  - Recurrent episodes
  - Malignant obstruction

### Surgical Techniques

#### Exploratory Laparotomy for SBO

**Approach**
- **Midline Incision**: Provides maximum exposure
- **Adhesiolysis**: Systematic, from point of least to most involvement
- **Run the Bowel**: From ligament of Treitz to cecum

**Management Options**
1. **Simple Adhesiolysis**: Single band causing obstruction
2. **Resection with Primary Anastomosis**: Viable bowel
3. **Resection with Ostomy**: Ischemic bowel, hemodynamic instability
4. **Bypass**: Unresectable malignancy

**Technical Considerations**
- **Handle Bowel Gently**: Minimize serosal tears
- **Decompression**: NG tube, enterotomy if needed
- **Viability Assessment**: Color, peristalsis, bleeding from cut edge
- **Second-Look Laparotomy**: If bowel viability questionable

#### Laparoscopic Approach
- **Selected Cases**: Single adhesive band suspected
- **Contraindications**: Multiple prior surgeries, diffuse distension
- **Conversion Threshold**: Low if difficult

#### Management of LBO

**Sigmoid Volvulus**
- **Initial**: Sigmoidoscopy decompression (70-80% success)
- **Definitive**: Sigmoid colectomy (elective after decompression)
- **Emergency**: If peritonitis or failed decompression

**Malignant Obstruction**
- **Resection with Anastomosis**: Right-sided lesions
- **Hartmann's Procedure**: Left-sided lesions (resection, end colostomy)
- **Diverting Colostomy**: Unresectable tumor
- **Stenting**: Bridge to surgery or palliation

### Postoperative Management

#### Initial Management
- **NPO Status**: Until bowel function returns
- **Nasogastric Decompression**: Output-dependent
- **Fluid Resuscitation**: Correct deficits and losses
- **Electrolyte Replacement**: Particularly potassium
- **Antibiotics**: If contamination or ischemic bowel

#### Return of Bowel Function
- **Signs**: Flatus, bowel movement, decreased NG output
- **Diet Advancement**: Clear liquids → low residue → regular
- **NG Tube Removal**: When output <500 mL/24h and tolerating diet

#### Enhanced Recovery
- **Early Mobilization**: Ambulate day of surgery if able
- **Gum Chewing**: May stimulate bowel function
- **Avoid Routine NG Tubes**: Unless high output or ileus

### Complications

#### Early Complications
- **Recurrent Obstruction** (10-15%):
  - Conservative management initially
  - Surgery if persistent

- **Anastomotic Leak** (2-5%):
  - Fever, tachycardia, peritonitis
  - CT with oral contrast
  - Reoperation often required

- **Intra-abdominal Abscess** (5-10%):
  - CT-guided drainage
  - Antibiotics

- **Wound Infection** (10-20%):
  - Higher in contaminated cases
  - Open and pack, antibiotics if cellulitis

#### Late Complications
- **Recurrent Adhesive SBO** (10-20%):
  - Managed conservatively when possible
  - Surgery if recurrent or complicated

- **Incisional Hernia** (10-20%):
  - Risk factors: infection, malnutrition, emergency surgery
  - Repair when symptomatic

### Outcomes

#### Mortality
- **Simple Obstruction**: 2-5%
- **Strangulated Bowel**: 10-25%
- **Factors**: Age, comorbidities, delay to surgery

#### Success of Conservative Management
- **First Episode**: 65-85% success
- **Recurrent Episodes**: Lower success rate
- **Malignant Obstruction**: Low success rate

## Hernias

### Indications

#### Inguinal Hernias

**Classification**
- **Indirect**: Through internal inguinal ring, lateral to inferior epigastric vessels
- **Direct**: Through Hesselbach's triangle, medial to inferior epigastric vessels
- **Pantaloon**: Combined indirect and direct components

**Clinical Presentation**
- **Groin Bulge**: Increases with Valsalva, standing
- **Reducible**: Can push contents back into abdomen
- **Discomfort**: Dull ache, worse with activity
- **Incarceration**: Irreducible, risk of strangulation
- **Strangulation**: Pain, erythema, systemic symptoms (surgical emergency)

**Indications for Repair**
- **Symptomatic Hernias**: Pain, discomfort affecting quality of life
- **Incarcerated/Strangulated**: Emergency surgery
- **Watchful Waiting**: Acceptable for asymptomatic, easily reducible in elderly

#### Femoral Hernias
- **Demographics**: More common in women
- **Location**: Below inguinal ligament, medial to femoral vessels
- **Strangulation Risk**: 40% (higher than inguinal)
- **Indication**: All femoral hernias should be repaired

#### Ventral/Incisional Hernias
- **Incisional**: Through prior surgical incision (10-20% of abdominal surgeries)
- **Primary Ventral**: Umbilical, epigastric, Spigelian
- **Indications**: Symptomatic, enlarging, strangulation risk

### Preoperative Assessment

#### Clinical Evaluation
- **Hernia Characteristics**: Size, reducibility, contents
- **Risk Factors**: Obesity, smoking, chronic cough, constipation
- **Fitness for Surgery**: Especially mesh repair consideration
- **Optimization**: Weight loss, smoking cessation, COPD management

#### Imaging
- **Usually Not Required**: Clinical diagnosis sufficient
- **CT Scan Indications**:
  - Uncertain diagnosis
  - Complex hernias
  - Surgical planning for large defects
- **Ultrasound**: Occult hernias, groin pain without bulge

### Surgical Techniques

#### Open Inguinal Hernia Repair

**Lichtenstein Tension-Free Mesh Repair**
- **Incision**: Oblique groin incision over inguinal canal
- **Steps**:
  1. Divide external oblique aponeurosis
  2. Identify and protect ilioinguinal nerve
  3. Dissect spermatic cord, identify hernia sac
  4. Reduce indirect sac or invert direct defect
  5. Place mesh over posterior wall
  6. Secure mesh: medial (pubic tubercle), lateral (internal ring)
  7. Reconstruct external oblique over cord

**Shouldice Repair (Tissue-Only)**
- Multi-layer tissue repair
- Reserved for young patients when mesh contraindicated
- Higher recurrence than mesh (2-5% vs. 1-2%)

#### Laparoscopic Inguinal Hernia Repair

**Transabdominal Preperitoneal (TAPP)**
- **Advantages**: Bilateral repair, examine contralateral side
- **Steps**:
  1. Three-port laparoscopic access
  2. Peritoneal flap creation
  3. Identify myopectineal orifice anatomy
  4. Reduce hernia sac
  5. Place large mesh covering all defects
  6. Close peritoneum over mesh

**Totally Extraperitoneal (TEP)**
- **Advantages**: No peritoneal entry, lower visceral injury risk
- **Disadvantages**: Steeper learning curve
- **Technique**: Dissect preperitoneal space, place mesh extraperitoneally

**Indications for Laparoscopic Approach**
- Bilateral hernias
- Recurrent hernias after open repair
- Quick return to work desired
- Patient preference

#### Ventral Hernia Repair

**Open Repair with Mesh**
- **Mesh Types**: Polypropylene, polyester, biologic (if contamination risk)
- **Mesh Position**:
  - **Onlay**: Above fascia
  - **Sublay/Retromuscular**: Below rectus, above peritoneum (preferred)
  - **Intraperitoneal**: Requires barrier coating

**Component Separation**
- For large defects with loss of domain
- Release external oblique aponeurosis
- Mobilize rectus muscles medially
- Achieve midline fascial closure

**Laparoscopic Ventral Hernia Repair**
- **Indications**: Small to moderate defects (<10 cm)
- **Technique**: Intraperitoneal mesh placement with wide overlap
- **Fixation**: Transfascial sutures and tacks

### Postoperative Management

#### Inguinal Hernia
- **Same-Day Discharge**: Majority of cases
- **Pain Management**: NSAIDs + acetaminophen, minimize opioids
- **Activity**: Light activity immediately, no heavy lifting 4-6 weeks
- **Return to Work**: 1-2 weeks (laparoscopic), 2-4 weeks (open)
- **Sports/Strenuous Activity**: 6 weeks

#### Ventral Hernia
- **Hospital Stay**: 1-3 days depending on size
- **Abdominal Binder**: Support for comfort
- **Activity Restrictions**: 6-8 weeks for heavy lifting
- **Wound Care**: Monitor for seroma, infection

### Complications

#### Early Complications
- **Seroma** (5-10%):
  - Fluid collection at surgical site
  - Usually self-limited, aspiration rarely needed

- **Hematoma** (1-4%):
  - Manage conservatively unless expanding
  - Evacuation if large or infected

- **Wound Infection** (1-5%):
  - Higher in ventral/incisional hernias
  - Superficial: antibiotics and local care
  - Deep/mesh: may require mesh removal

- **Urinary Retention** (2-10%):
  - More common in elderly males
  - Catheterization, alpha-blockers

#### Chronic Complications
- **Chronic Pain** (5-10%):
  - **Neuropathic**: Nerve injury (ilioinguinal, iliohypogastric)
  - **Mesh-Related**: Inflammation, migration
  - **Management**: Nerve blocks, medication, rarely mesh removal

- **Recurrence**:
  - **Inguinal**: 1-3% with mesh, 2-10% without
  - **Ventral**: 10-30% depending on size and technique
  - **Risk Factors**: Obesity, smoking, infection, tension repair

#### Rare but Serious
- **Visceral Injury** (<1%):
  - Bowel, bladder during laparoscopic approach
  - Recognition and repair essential

- **Vascular Injury** (<1%):
  - Femoral vessels, inferior epigastric artery
  - Immediate control and repair

### Outcomes

#### Success Rates
- **Inguinal Mesh Repair**: 97-99% success, <3% recurrence
- **Ventral Hernia Repair**: 70-90% success, recurrence varies by size
- **Patient Satisfaction**: >90% satisfied with mesh repair

#### Quality of Life
- **Pain Resolution**: 85-90% have significant improvement
- **Return to Activity**: Majority return to full activity
- **Chronic Pain Impact**: 5-10% have some degree of chronic pain

## Key Points

### Acute Appendicitis
- Clinical diagnosis supported by imaging (CT gold standard)
- Laparoscopic appendectomy is standard of care
- Critical view of safety prevents major complications
- Early surgery (within 24 hours) reduces perforation risk
- Antibiotics based on complexity: single dose vs. 4-7 days

### Cholecystectomy
- Critical view of safety is essential for preventing bile duct injury
- Same-day discharge for most uncomplicated cases
- ERCP for common bile duct stones and bile leaks
- Early cholecystectomy (within 72 hours) preferred for acute cholecystitis

### Bowel Obstruction
- Adhesions are most common cause of small bowel obstruction
- Conservative management initially unless signs of strangulation
- CT imaging identifies etiology, level, and complications
- Lactate elevation and peritonitis indicate need for urgent surgery
- Malignant large bowel obstruction often requires surgical intervention

### Hernias
- Mesh repair has lower recurrence than tissue-only repair
- All femoral hernias should be repaired due to high strangulation risk
- Laparoscopic approach ideal for bilateral and recurrent inguinal hernias
- Component separation for large ventral hernias
- Smoking cessation and weight loss reduce recurrence risk
