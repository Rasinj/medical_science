# Thoracic Trauma

## Overview

Thoracic trauma accounts for 25% of trauma deaths, ranking second only to head and spinal injuries. Injuries to the chest threaten oxygenation, ventilation, and circulation through compromise of the airway, lungs, heart, and major vessels. Most thoracic injuries (85-90%) can be managed non-operatively with tube thoracostomy, but life-threatening conditions require immediate recognition and intervention. Emergency department thoracotomy represents a last-resort procedure for penetrating cardiac injury. Understanding chest anatomy, injury patterns, and management algorithms is critical for trauma surgeons.

## Thoracic Anatomy

### Chest Wall

**Ribs**: 12 pairs
- **True ribs** (1-7): Attach to sternum via costal cartilage
- **False ribs** (8-10): Attach to costal cartilage above
- **Floating ribs** (11-12): No anterior attachment
- **Fractures**: Direct force, protect underlying structures (ribs 1-3 high force, ribs 9-12 associated with abdominal injury)

**Intercostal Structures**:
- **VAN**: Vein, Artery, Nerve (superior to inferior at inferior rib border)
- **Intercostal muscles**: External, internal, innermost

**Diaphragm**:
- Separates thorax from abdomen
- Insertion: Inferior border 6th rib anteriorly, 10th rib laterally, 12th rib posteriorly
- **Penetrating injury below nipples (anterior) or scapular tip (posterior)**: May have diaphragmatic + abdominal injury

### Pleural Space

**Visceral Pleura**: Covers lung surface
**Parietal Pleura**: Lines chest wall, diaphragm, mediastinum
**Pleural Space**: Potential space, normally 10-20mL fluid
- **Negative pressure** (-5cmH2O) keeps lung expanded

**Pathologic Collections**:
- **Pneumothorax**: Air in pleural space
- **Hemothorax**: Blood in pleural space
- **Hemopneumothorax**: Air + blood

### Mediastinum

**Anterior**: Thymus, great vessels (ascending aorta, SVC)
**Middle**: Heart, pericardium, phrenic nerves
**Posterior**: Descending aorta, esophagus, thoracic duct, azygos vein

**Boundaries**: Sternum (anterior), vertebral bodies (posterior), thoracic inlet (superior), diaphragm (inferior)

### Lungs

**Right Lung**: 3 lobes (upper, middle, lower)
**Left Lung**: 2 lobes (upper, lower), lingula

**Hilum**: Entry point for mainstem bronchus, pulmonary artery, pulmonary veins

**Trachea**: Bifurcates at carina (T4-5 level) into mainstem bronchi
- **Right mainstem**: More vertical (foreign body, endobronchial intubation preferentially enters right)
- **Left mainstem**: More horizontal

## Initial Assessment

### Primary Survey

**Airway**:
- Assess patency, secure if needed
- **Tracheobronchial injury**: Massive air leak, subcutaneous emphysema, pneumomediastinum

**Breathing**:
- **Inspection**: Chest wall movement, respiratory rate, work of breathing
- **Palpation**: Tenderness, crepitus (rib fractures, subcutaneous emphysema)
- **Percussion**: Hyperresonance (pneumothorax), dullness (hemothorax)
- **Auscultation**: Breath sounds (diminished/absent if pneumo/hemothorax)

**Circulation**:
- **Beck's triad** (cardiac tamponade): Hypotension, distended neck veins, muffled heart sounds
- **Pulsus paradoxus**: >10mmHg decrease in SBP with inspiration (tamponade)

### Imaging

**Chest X-Ray** (CXR):
- **Portable AP**: Initial in trauma bay
- **Findings**: Pneumothorax, hemothorax, rib fractures, widened mediastinum, pneumomediastinum, subcutaneous emphysema

**CT Chest with IV Contrast**:
- **When**: Hemodynamically stable
- **Assess**: Aortic injury, pulmonary contusion extent, occult pneumo/hemothorax, mediastinal hematoma
- **NOT for unstable patients** (go to OR, not CT)

**FAST Exam**:
- **Pericardial window**: Assess for pericardial effusion (tamponade)
- **eFAST**: Includes bilateral thoracic views (pneumothorax, hemothorax)

## Life-Threatening Injuries (Immediate Treatment)

### Tension Pneumothorax

**Pathophysiology**: Air enters pleural space (one-way valve), accumulates, collapses ipsilateral lung, shifts mediastinum to contralateral side, compresses heart/great vessels → decreased venous return → shock

**Presentation**:
- **Respiratory distress**: Tachypnea, dyspnea
- **Hypotension**, **tachycardia**
- **Absent breath sounds** ipsilaterally
- **Hyperresonance** to percussion
- **Tracheal deviation** away from affected side (late finding)
- **Distended neck veins** (may be absent if hypovolemic)

**Diagnosis**: **CLINICAL** (do NOT wait for CXR)

**Treatment**:
- **Needle decompression**: Immediate temporizing
  - **Site**: 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line (or 4th-5th ICS, anterior axillary line)
  - 14- or 16-gauge angiocatheter
  - Advance until rush of air (decompress), remove needle, leave catheter
- **Definitive**: Tube thoracostomy (chest tube)

### Open Pneumothorax ("Sucking Chest Wound")

**Pathophysiology**: Large chest wall defect, air preferentially enters pleural space through wound (rather than trachea) during inspiration → collapsed lung

**Presentation**:
- Open chest wound, air movement through wound
- Respiratory distress

**Treatment**:
- **Three-sided occlusive dressing**: Tape on three sides (allows air out during exhalation, prevents air in during inhalation)
- **Do NOT tape all four sides** (creates tension pneumothorax)
- **Tube thoracostomy**: Remote from wound
- **Definitive**: Surgical closure of chest wall defect

### Massive Hemothorax

**Definition**: Accumulation of blood in pleural space (>1500mL or ≥1/3 hemithorax on CXR)

**Causes**: Intercostal artery injury, internal mammary artery, pulmonary hilar vessels, great vessels

**Presentation**:
- **Shock**: Hypotension, tachycardia
- **Decreased breath sounds**
- **Dull percussion**

**Treatment**:
- **Resuscitation**: IV fluids, blood products (massive transfusion protocol)
- **Tube thoracostomy**: Large-bore (36-40 Fr), directed posteriorly and inferiorly
  - **Initial output >1500mL**: Likely ongoing bleeding → thoracotomy
  - **Continued output >200mL/hr for 2-4 hours**: Thoracotomy
  - **Autotransfusion**: Collect drained blood, retransfuse if <4 hours old, minimal contamination

**Thoracotomy Indications**: Immediate >1500mL or continued >200mL/hr

### Flail Chest

**Definition**: ≥3 consecutive ribs fractured in ≥2 places → free-floating chest wall segment

**Pathophysiology**:
- **Paradoxical movement**: Segment moves inward with inspiration (negative intrathoracic pressure), outward with exhalation
- **Underlying pulmonary contusion**: Impaired oxygenation (hypoxemia)
- **Pain**: Splinting → hypoventilation → atelectasis, pneumonia

**Presentation**:
- Paradoxical chest wall movement
- Respiratory distress, hypoxia
- Severe pain

**Treatment**:
- **Oxygen**: Maintain SpO2 >94%
- **Analgesia**: Critical
  - **Epidural**: Preferred (allows deep breathing, cough)
  - **Nerve blocks**: Intercostal, paravertebral
  - **Opioids**: If above not available
- **Pulmonary toilet**: Incentive spirometry, chest physiotherapy
- **Mechanical ventilation**: If severe hypoxemia, work of breathing unsustainable (avoid if possible - VILI risk)
- **Surgical rib fixation**: Selected cases (large flail, prolonged ventilation, symptomatic non-union)

### Cardiac Tamponade

**Pathophysiology**: Blood accumulates in pericardial sac (normally 15-50mL capacity), compresses heart → decreased diastolic filling → decreased cardiac output → shock

**Presentation**:
- **Beck's Triad**: Hypotension, distended neck veins (elevated JVP), muffled heart sounds
- **Pulsus paradoxus**: >10mmHg drop in SBP with inspiration
- **Kussmaul sign**: Paradoxical rise in JVP with inspiration
- **Electrical alternans** on ECG (rare)

**Diagnosis**:
- **FAST**: Pericardial effusion (anechoic fluid around heart)
- **CXR**: Enlarged cardiac silhouette (if chronic)

**Treatment**:

**Pericardiocentesis** (temporizing):
- **Indications**: Pre-arrest, temporizing measure before OR
- **Technique**: Subxiphoid approach, 16-18 gauge needle angled toward left shoulder, advance while aspirating
  - Aspirate 15-20mL often dramatically improves hemodynamics
- **Limitations**: Re-accumulation likely, clotted blood not aspirated
- **Complications**: Myocardial puncture, coronary laceration

**Definitive**: Operative repair (sternotomy or left anterolateral thoracotomy)

## Emergency Department Thoracotomy (EDT)

### Indications

**Penetrating Chest Trauma**:
- **Loss of vital signs** in ED with **recent signs of life** (pupillary response, cardiac activity, respiratory effort, extremity movement)
- **Cardiac arrest** in ED after witnessed arrest with **prehospital CPR <15 minutes**

**Blunt Trauma**: Very limited role
- **Cardiac arrest in ED** with **witnessed arrest** and **immediate CPR** - survival <2%

**Contraindications**:
- Prolonged CPR (>15 minutes) without response
- Blunt trauma with no signs of life on scene
- Severe non-survivable injuries (decapitation, incineration)

### Survival Rates

**Overall**: 5-10%
**Penetrating cardiac injury**: 20-35% (best outcomes)
**Penetrating thoracic (non-cardiac)**: 5-15%
**Blunt trauma**: <2%

### Technique

**Incision**:
- **Left anterolateral thoracotomy**: 4th-5th intercostal space (below nipple), anterior to mid-axillary line
- Incise skin, subcutaneous tissue, intercostal muscles
- Enter pleural space
- **Extension**: Can extend across sternum to right chest (bilateral "clamshell" thoracotomy) if needed

**Steps**:
1. **Evacuate blood/clots**: Visualize heart
2. **Pericardiotomy**: Open pericardium anterior to phrenic nerve (longitudinal incision)
3. **Identify cardiac injury**: Inspect all surfaces
4. **Control hemorrhage**:
   - **Digital pressure** (finger over hole)
   - **Suture repair**: Running or interrupted (3-0 or 4-0 polypropylene), horizontal mattress or simple
   - **Foley balloon tamponade**: Insert Foley through wound, inflate balloon, pull back (temporize)
   - **Skin stapler**: Rapidly close laceration (temporizing)
5. **Internal cardiac massage**: If asystole or inadequate cardiac output
6. **Cross-clamp descending aorta**: Reduce infradiaphragmatic bleeding, redirect blood to heart/brain
   - **Technique**: Palpate aorta against spine, apply vascular clamp (DeBakey or Satinsky)
7. **Resuscitation**: Volume, blood products
8. **Treat other injuries**: Pulmonary hilar injuries (clamp, repair, or pneumonectomy)

**Transport to OR** if successful (patient has pulse, BP)

### Complications

- **Phrenic nerve injury**: Pericardiotomy (avoid nerve)
- **Coronary artery injury**: Suturing myocardium
- **Air embolism**: Open cardiac chambers with resumption of circulation
- **Aortic injury**: Cross-clamp application

## Pulmonary Injuries

### Pulmonary Contusion

**Mechanism**: Blunt force → alveolar hemorrhage, edema → impaired gas exchange (V/Q mismatch)

**Presentation**:
- Hypoxemia (may worsen over 24-48 hours)
- Dyspnea
- **Hemoptysis**: Blood-tinged sputum

**Diagnosis**:
- **CXR**: Patchy infiltrates (may be delayed 6-12 hours)
- **CT**: More sensitive, ground-glass opacities

**Management**:
- **Supportive**: Oxygen, pulmonary toilet
- **Fluid restriction**: Avoid over-resuscitation (worsens edema)
- **Analgesia**: Adequate pain control (allows deep breathing)
- **Mechanical ventilation**: If severe hypoxemia

**Complications**: ARDS (acute respiratory distress syndrome), pneumonia

**Resolution**: Usually 5-7 days

### Pneumothorax

**Types**:
- **Simple**: Small, not under tension
- **Tension**: Progressive accumulation, mediastinal shift, hemodynamic compromise (see above)
- **Occult**: Not visible on supine CXR, seen on CT

**Management**:

**Simple Pneumothorax**:
- **Small (<2cm apex-to-cupola), asymptomatic, stable**: Observation (may resorb), supplemental oxygen
- **Symptomatic or large**: Tube thoracostomy

**Occult Pneumothorax**:
- **Positive pressure ventilation planned**: Tube thoracostomy (risk tension)
- **No ventilation, asymptomatic**: Observe

### Hemothorax

**Causes**: Intercostal artery, internal mammary artery, pulmonary parenchyma, hilar vessels

**Management**:
- **Tube thoracostomy**: 36-40 Fr, directed posteriorly/inferiorly
- **Thoracotomy indications**: >1500mL initial or >200mL/hr for 2-4 hours

**Retained Hemothorax**:
- **Diagnosis**: Persistent fluid on CXR post-chest tube
- **Management**: Video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery (VATS) evacuation (within 3-5 days, prevent empyema/fibrothorax)

### Tracheobronchial Injury

**Presentation**:
- **Massive air leak**: Chest tube output (persistent, large)
- **Subcutaneous emphysema**: Extensive
- **Pneumomediastinum**: Air in mediastinum
- **Persistent pneumothorax** despite chest tube
- **Hemoptysis**

**Diagnosis**:
- **Bronchoscopy**: Visualize injury

**Management**:
- **Airway**: Intubate distal to injury if possible (advance ETT past injury, or selective bronchial intubation)
- **Operative repair**: Thoracotomy, primary repair of trachea/bronchus

**Complications**: Stenosis, fistula

## Cardiovascular Injuries

### Blunt Cardiac Injury (BCI)

**Mechanism**: Direct sternal impact (steering wheel, dashboard)

**Spectrum**: Myocardial contusion → wall motion abnormality → arrhythmia → cardiac rupture

**Presentation**:
- **Arrhythmias**: Sinus tachycardia (most common), atrial fibrillation, ventricular ectopy, heart blocks
- **Chest pain**: Retrosternal
- **ECG changes**: Non-specific (ST changes, T-wave inversion)
- **Elevated troponin**: Myocardial injury

**Diagnosis**:
- **ECG**: Arrhythmias, ST changes
- **Troponin**: Elevated suggests injury
- **Echocardiography**: Wall motion abnormalities, valve injury

**Management**:
- **Monitoring**: Continuous cardiac monitoring if ECG abnormal or troponin elevated (24-48 hours)
- **Treat arrhythmias**: As per ACLS protocols
- **Supportive**: Usually resolves spontaneously

**Severe BCI** (rare): Cardiogenic shock → inotropes, ECMO, surgical repair if rupture

### Blunt Aortic Injury (BAI)

**Mechanism**: Rapid deceleration (high-speed MVC, fall from height) → shear forces at aortic isthmus (ligamentum arteriosum attachment, fixed point)

**Presentation**:
- Often **asymptomatic** initially (if contained)
- **Hypertension** (upper extremity), **pulse discrepancy** (decreased/absent lower extremity)
- **Interscapular back pain**

**Diagnosis**:
- **CXR**: Widened mediastinum (>8cm at T4), obscured aortic knob, left apical cap, deviation of trachea/NG tube
  - **Sensitivity low** (~60-70%) - not reliable to exclude
- **CT Angiography (CTA)**: GOLD STANDARD
  - Intimal flap, pseudoaneurysm, contrast extravasation
- **Aortography**: If CTA unavailable (rarely needed)

**Grading**:
- **Grade I**: Intimal tear
- **Grade II**: Intramural hematoma
- **Grade III**: Pseudoaneurysm
- **Grade IV**: Rupture (free contrast extravasation, usually fatal before arrival)

**Management**:

**Medical**:
- **Blood pressure control**: Aggressive (prevent rupture)
  - **Goal**: SBP 100-120, HR <100
  - **Beta-blockers**: First-line (esmolol, labetalol), reduce shear stress (decrease HR, contractility)

**Operative/Endovascular**:
- **Thoracic endovascular aortic repair (TEVAR)**: Preferred
  - Stent-graft placed endovascularly across injury
  - **Advantages**: Less invasive, lower mortality than open
  - **Timing**: Urgent (same day), or delayed if other life-threatening injuries take priority
- **Open repair**: Thoracotomy, primary repair or graft interposition
  - Reserved for TEVAR failure/contraindications
  - **Higher mortality** (~25-30%)

**Prognosis**:
- **Contained rupture**: 80-90% survival with treatment
- **Free rupture**: Usually fatal prehospital

## Diaphragmatic Injury

**Mechanism**:
- **Blunt**: Sudden increase in intra-abdominal pressure → diaphragm rupture (usually left, liver protects right)
- **Penetrating**: Below nipple anteriorly or scapular tip posteriorly

**Presentation**:
- Often **asymptomatic** initially
- **Herniation**: Abdominal contents into chest (bowel sounds in chest)
- **Respiratory distress** (if large herniation)

**Diagnosis**:
- **CXR**: Elevated hemidiaphragm, bowel gas in chest, NG tube in chest
- **CT**: Can miss small injuries
- **Laparoscopy/Laparotomy**: Direct visualization (if abdomen explored for other injuries)

**Management**:
- **Operative repair**: Laparotomy or thoracotomy
  - **Technique**: Primary repair (non-absorbable suture, horizontal mattress or interrupted)
  - **Approach**: Abdominal (if acute), thoracic (if chronic with adhesions)

**Complications**: If missed, delayed herniation (months to years later) → incarceration, strangulation

## Tube Thoracostomy (Chest Tube Insertion)

### Indications

- Pneumothorax (simple or tension after needle decompression)
- Hemothorax
- Hemopneumothorax
- Prophylactic (positive pressure ventilation with pneumothorax, transport with pneumothorax)

### Technique

**Site**: 4th-5th intercostal space, anterior to mid-axillary line ("safe triangle")
- **Safe triangle**: Bordered by lateral edge of pectoralis major (anterior), latissimus dorsi (posterior), 5th ICS (inferior), axilla (apex)

**Size**: 28-40 Fr (large-bore for hemothorax, smaller for pneumothorax)

**Procedure**:
1. **Position**: Supine or lateral decubitus, arm abducted
2. **Prep and drape**: Sterile technique
3. **Anesthesia**: Local (lidocaine 1%, infiltrate skin and down to pleura)
4. **Incision**: 2-3cm incision parallel to rib (5th ICS)
5. **Blunt dissection**: Kelly clamp through subcutaneous tissue, intercostal muscles
   - **Dissect OVER superior border of lower rib** (avoid neurovascular bundle at inferior border of upper rib)
6. **Enter pleural space**: Pop through pleura (rush of air/blood)
7. **Digital exploration**: Insert finger to confirm intrapleural position, break adhesions, palpate lung
8. **Insert tube**: Grasp tip with Kelly, direct posteriorly and superiorly (for pneumothorax) or posteriorly and inferiorly (for hemothorax)
   - Advance until all drainage holes inside chest
9. **Secure**: Suture to skin (0 or 1 silk), occlusive dressing
10. **Connect**: To drainage system (water seal, suction at -20cmH2O)
11. **CXR**: Confirm position, lung re-expansion

**Complications**:
- **Malposition**: Subcutaneous, intra-abdominal
- **Intercostal vessel injury**: Bleeding
- **Lung laceration**: Bleeding, persistent air leak
- **Infection**: Empyema

### Removal

**Criteria**: Lung expanded, <200mL drainage per day, no air leak for 24 hours

**Technique**: Deep inspiration or expiration (prevent air entry), pull tube while assistant applies occlusive dressing

## Key Points

- Thoracic trauma accounts for 25% of trauma deaths; most (85-90%) managed non-operatively with tube thoracostomy
- Tension pneumothorax clinical diagnosis: respiratory distress, hypotension, absent breath sounds, hyperresonance, tracheal deviation; treat immediately with needle decompression (2nd ICS MCL)
- Massive hemothorax (>1500mL initial or >200mL/hr for 2-4 hours) requires thoracotomy for hemorrhage control
- Cardiac tamponade presents with Beck's triad (hypotension, distended neck veins, muffled heart sounds); pericardiocentesis temporizing, operative repair definitive
- Emergency department thoracotomy indicated for penetrating chest trauma with recent loss of vital signs; best survival (20-35%) with isolated cardiac injury
- Flail chest (≥3 ribs fractured in ≥2 places) causes paradoxical chest movement; treat with analgesia (epidural preferred), pulmonary toilet, avoid mechanical ventilation if possible
- Blunt aortic injury occurs at ligamentum arteriosum (deceleration injury); CXR shows widened mediastinum, CTA gold standard; treat with TEVAR (endovascular stent-graft)
- Pulmonary contusion causes delayed hypoxemia (24-48 hours); treat with oxygen, fluid restriction, analgesia; resolves 5-7 days
- Tracheobronchial injury presents with massive air leak, persistent pneumothorax despite chest tube, extensive subcutaneous emphysema; diagnose with bronchoscopy, repair operatively
- Tube thoracostomy inserted at 4th-5th ICS anterior to mid-axillary line ("safe triangle"), directed posterosuperiorly (pneumothorax) or posteroinferiorly (hemothorax)
- Diaphragm injury suspect if penetrating wound below nipples anteriorly or scapular tip posteriorly; left side more common (liver protects right); can present late with herniation
- Blunt cardiac injury spectrum from contusion to rupture; monitor if abnormal ECG or elevated troponin; treat arrhythmias per ACLS
