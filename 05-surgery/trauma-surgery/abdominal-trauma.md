# Abdominal Trauma

## Overview

Abdominal trauma, resulting from blunt or penetrating mechanisms, poses diagnostic and therapeutic challenges due to the variety of organs at risk and potential for occult injury. The abdomen contains solid organs (liver, spleen, kidneys), hollow viscera (stomach, small and large bowel), major vessels, and retroperitoneal structures. Management decisions balance immediate operative intervention for unstable patients against selective non-operative management for stable patients with solid organ injuries. Timely recognition of injuries requiring surgery versus those amenable to observation distinguishes modern trauma care.

## Anatomy

### Abdominal Compartments

**Peritoneal Cavity**:
- Contains most intra-abdominal organs
- **Solid organs**: Liver, spleen, pancreas (partially)
- **Hollow viscera**: Stomach, small bowel, most of colon
- Peritoneal inflammation (blood, enteric contents) causes peritonitis

**Retroperitoneum**:
- **Zone I (Central)**: Aorta, IVC, pancreatic head/body, duodenum (portions 2-4)
- **Zone II (Lateral, bilateral)**: Kidneys, ureters, ascending/descending colon (portions)
- **Zone III (Pelvic)**: Rectum, iliac vessels, bladder
- Injuries can be occult (no peritoneal signs)

**Pelvis**:
- Bladder, rectum, pelvic vessels
- Pelvic fractures → massive retroperitoneal hemorrhage

### Organ-Specific Injury Patterns

**Liver** (right upper quadrant):
- Most frequently injured solid organ (blunt trauma)
- **Segments**: 8 functional segments (Couinaud classification)
- **Blood supply**: Hepatic artery (25%), portal vein (75%)
- **Venous drainage**: Hepatic veins to IVC

**Spleen** (left upper quadrant):
- Second most common solid organ injury
- Friable, highly vascular
- **Function**: Filtration, immune (antibody production, opsonization)

**Kidneys** (retroperitoneal, bilateral):
- Protected by rib cage posteriorly
- **Injury grades**: Contusion → laceration → vascular injury

**Pancreas** (retroperitoneal, epigastric):
- **Injury**: Often associated with duodenal injury (proximity)
- **Ductal injury**: Risk of leak, pseudocyst, fistula

**Small Bowel**:
- **Injury**: Blast injuries (handle bar, seat belt), penetrating
- **Diagnosis**: Can be subtle (delayed peritonitis)

**Colon**:
- **Intraperitoneal**: Transverse, sigmoid
- **Retroperitoneal**: Ascending, descending
- **Injury**: Penetrating most common

## Blunt Abdominal Trauma

### Mechanisms

**Motor vehicle collision** (most common):
- Compression (steering wheel, lap belt)
- Deceleration (shear injury to fixed organs - spleen, liver, kidney)
- **Seat belt sign**: Ecchymosis across abdomen (high risk bowel/mesenteric injury)

**Motorcycle collision**, **pedestrian struck**, **bicycle handlebar**: Direct impact

**Falls from height**: Deceleration

**Assault**: Direct blows

### Clinical Presentation

**Symptoms**:
- **Abdominal pain**: Not always present (distracting injuries, altered consciousness)
- **Referred pain**: Shoulder pain (Kehr sign - diaphragmatic irritation from blood)

**Signs**:
- **Tenderness, guarding, rigidity**: Peritoneal irritation
- **Distension**: Hemoperitoneum, ileus
- **Ecchymosis**: Flank (Grey-Turner sign - retroperitoneal bleeding), periumbilical (Cullen sign - retroperitoneal bleeding)
- **Seat belt sign**: Abdominal wall ecchymosis (bowel injury until proven otherwise)
- **Hemodynamic instability**: Shock from intra-abdominal hemorrhage

### Diagnostic Workup

**FAST (Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma)**:
- **Purpose**: Detect free intraperitoneal fluid (blood)
- **Four views**: Perihepatic, perisplenic, pelvis, pericardium
- **Positive FAST + unstable**: Immediate laparotomy
- **Positive FAST + stable**: CT scan to grade injuries
- **Negative FAST + stable**: Does NOT rule out injury (CT needed), especially bowel/pancreas
- **Sensitivity**: ~80% (better with >500mL free fluid)

**CT Abdomen/Pelvis with IV Contrast**:
- **When**: Hemodynamically stable patients
- **Provides**: Organ injury grading, active extravasation, free fluid, retroperitoneal injuries
- **Oral contrast**: NOT routinely given (delays imaging, aspiration risk)
- **Triple-contrast** (IV, oral, rectal): If concern for bowel injury (rarely used)

**Diagnostic Peritoneal Lavage (DPL)**: Largely replaced by FAST
- **Technique**: Infuse 1L saline into peritoneum, drain, analyze fluid
- **Positive**: Grossly bloody (>10mL), RBC >100,000/mm³, WBC >500/mm³, bile, bacteria, food particles
- **Indications**: Equivocal FAST, cannot obtain CT (going to OR for other injuries)
- **Limitations**: Does not identify source, cannot grade injuries, invasive

**Laboratory**:
- **Hemoglobin**: May be normal initially (takes time to equilibrate)
- **Lactate**: Elevated in shock (tissue hypoperfusion)
- **Liver enzymes (AST, ALT)**: Elevated suggests liver injury
- **Amylase/lipase**: Elevated suggests pancreatic injury (low sensitivity)
- **Urinalysis**: Hematuria suggests GU injury

### Indications for Laparotomy

**Absolute** (immediate surgery):
- **Hemodynamic instability** despite resuscitation with positive FAST or known intra-abdominal injury
- **Peritonitis** (diffuse tenderness, rigidity)
- **Evisceration** (bowel protruding through wound)
- **Free air** on imaging (hollow viscus perforation)
- **CT evidence** of hollow viscus injury (bowel wall discontinuity, extraluminal oral contrast)

**Relative**:
- **Unexplained shock**: Source unknown, abdomen suspected
- **Significant abdominal pain** or tenderness (concerning exam)

### Non-Operative Management (NOM)

**Criteria**:
- **Hemodynamically stable** (SBP >90, HR <120, responding to resuscitation)
- **No peritonitis**
- **No other indication for laparotomy**
- **Ability to monitor closely** (ICU or step-down unit)

**Appropriate for**:
- Solid organ injuries (liver, spleen, kidney) - most can be managed non-operatively
- **Serial exams**: Frequent abdominal exams (q4-6h initially)
- **Strict bed rest**: Initially (prevent rebleeding)
- **Serial hemoglobin**: Monitor for ongoing bleeding
- **Repeat imaging**: If clinical change

**Failure of NOM**: Hemodynamic instability, peritonitis, transfusion requirements (>4 units suggest ongoing bleeding)

**Success rates**: 90-95% for blunt solid organ injuries (liver, spleen)

## Liver Injury

### Grading (American Association for the Surgery of Trauma - AAST)

- **Grade I**: Subcapsular hematoma <10% surface area, laceration <1cm depth
- **Grade II**: Subcapsular hematoma 10-50%, laceration 1-3cm depth
- **Grade III**: Subcapsular hematoma >50%, laceration >3cm depth
- **Grade IV**: Parenchymal disruption 25-75% of lobe, vascular injury
- **Grade V**: Parenchymal disruption >75% of lobe, juxtahepatic venous injury (hepatic veins, retrohepatic IVC)
- **Grade VI**: Hepatic avulsion

### Management

**Non-Operative** (most common):
- **Indications**: Hemodynamically stable, any grade (even high-grade if stable)
- **Success**: >90% for grades I-III, 80-90% for grades IV-V
- **Monitoring**: ICU, serial exams, hemoglobin
- **Interventional radiology**: Angioembolization for active extravasation (blush on CT) or pseudoaneurysm

**Operative**:
- **Indications**: Hemodynamic instability despite resuscitation, peritonitis, failure of NOM

**Surgical Techniques**:
- **Manual compression**: Initial maneuver (pack liver, temporize)
- **Perihepatic packing**: Laparotomy pads around liver (damage control)
- **Pringle maneuver**: Clamp porta hepatis (hepatic artery + portal vein) - reduces inflow, controls hemorrhage (can tolerate 60-90 minutes intermittent clamping)
- **Hepatorrhaphy**: Suture lacerations (deep liver sutures, absorbable)
- **Resectional debridement**: Non-viable tissue (finger fracture technique)
- **Formal hepatectomy**: RARE (grade V-VI injuries, experienced surgeons only)

**Damage Control**: Pack, close temporarily, resuscitate ICU, return to OR for definitive repair/pack removal in 24-48 hours

### Complications

- **Hemorrhage**: Rebleeding (1-5%)
- **Bile leak**: Biloma, biliary fistula (managed with ERCP stenting or percutaneous drainage)
- **Abscess**: Intra-abdominal abscess (CT-guided drainage)
- **Hemobilia**: Bleeding into biliary tree (rare, treat with angioembolization)

## Splenic Injury

### Grading (AAST)

- **Grade I**: Subcapsular hematoma <10%, laceration <1cm depth
- **Grade II**: Subcapsular hematoma 10-50%, laceration 1-3cm depth, not involving trabecular vessels
- **Grade III**: Subcapsular hematoma >50%, laceration >3cm or involving trabecular vessels
- **Grade IV**: Laceration involving segmental/hilar vessels (>25% devascularization)
- **Grade V**: Completely shattered spleen, hilar vascular injury (devascularized spleen)

### Management

**Non-Operative**:
- **Success**: >90% grades I-III, 75-85% grades IV-V
- **Criteria**: Same as liver (hemodynamic stability, close monitoring)
- **Angioembolization**: Active extravasation on CT (especially grades III-V)

**Operative**:
- **Indications**: Hemodynamic instability, peritonitis, failure NOM

**Surgical Options**:

**Splenectomy** (most common in trauma):
- **Technique**: Mobilize spleen, divide splenic artery/vein, remove
- **Faster**, **definitive hemostasis** (damage control setting)
- **Complications**: Post-splenectomy sepsis (encapsulated bacteria: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Neisseria meningitidis)
  - **Vaccinations**: Pneumovax, Hib, Menactra (give 2 weeks post-op if elective, immediately if emergent)
  - **Lifetime risk**: 3-5% overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI)

**Splenorrhaphy** (splenic salvage):
- **Indications**: Low-grade injuries, hemodynamically stable, elective setting
- **Techniques**: Suture repair, topical hemostatic agents, mesh wrap
- **Rarely performed** in trauma (time-consuming, higher rebleeding risk)

**Partial splenectomy**: Remove devitalized portion, preserve viable spleen (rarely feasible in trauma)

### Complications

- **Rebleeding**: 1-5%
- **Post-splenectomy sepsis**: Lifelong risk (vaccinate)
- **Left subphrenic abscess**
- **Pancreatic tail injury**: Proximity (check pancreas during splenectomy)

## Pancreatic and Duodenal Injuries

### Challenges

- **Retroperitoneal location**: Can be missed initially
- **Associated injuries**: Often combined (85% pancreatic injuries have other injuries)
- **Delayed diagnosis**: Subtle initial presentation

### Diagnosis

**CT Scan**:
- Pancreatic edema, laceration, fluid around pancreas
- **Ductal injury**: Difficult to assess on CT

**Amylase/Lipase**: Elevated but low sensitivity (not reliable acutely)

**MRCP or ERCP**: If stable, concern for ductal injury (elective setting)

**Operative Exploration**: Often definitive diagnosis (visualize pancreas after Kocher maneuver)

### Pancreatic Injury Grading (AAST)

- **Grade I**: Superficial laceration, no duct injury
- **Grade II**: Laceration/contusion, no duct injury, body/tail involvement
- **Grade III**: Distal duct injury or parenchymal injury with duct disruption
- **Grade IV**: Proximal duct injury (head), major tissue loss
- **Grade V**: Massive head injury

### Management

**Non-Operative**: Grade I-II (no ductal injury), stable

**Operative**:

**Grade I-II (no duct injury)**:
- Hemostasis, drainage (closed-suction drain near pancreas)

**Grade III (distal duct injury, body/tail)**:
- **Distal pancreatectomy** (± splenectomy)
- Resect injured tail/body, oversew proximal pancreas, drain

**Grade IV-V (proximal duct, head injury)**:
- **Damage control**: Pack, drain, ICU resuscitation
- **Definitive**: Pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple) - RARE in trauma, high mortality
  - Usually drainage, damage control, manage fistula/leak post-op

### Duodenal Injury

**Diagnosis**: Free air, retroperitoneal air, CT oral contrast extravasation

**Management**:

**Grade I-II** (hematoma, partial-thickness laceration):
- **Primary repair**: Simple closure (2-layer)
- **Drainage**

**Grade III-IV** (full-thickness, >50% circumference):
- **Primary repair** if <24 hours old, minimal contamination
- **Pyloric exclusion**: Oversew pylorus, gastrojejunostomy (protect repair, divert gastric contents)
- **Duodenal diversion**: Duodenal repair + gastrojejunostomy + tube duodenostomy (damage control)

**Grade V** (devascularized, destroyed):
- **Pancreaticoduodenectomy** (Whipple) - rare, last resort

### Complications

- **Pancreatic fistula**: Drain pancreatic fluid (manage non-operatively with drainage, nutrition)
- **Abscess**
- **Pseudocyst**: Late (weeks), managed with drainage if symptomatic
- **Duodenal leak**: High morbidity (sepsis, fistula)

## Hollow Viscus Injury

### Small Bowel and Mesenteric Injury

**Mechanisms**:
- **Seat belt injury**: Compression, shear (handle bar)
- **Penetrating**

**Diagnosis**:
- **Seat belt sign**: High suspicion
- **CT findings**: Bowel wall thickening, free fluid without solid organ injury, mesenteric stranding/hematoma, free air (late)
- **Peritonitis**: Diffuse tenderness, rigidity (delayed 6-12 hours as enteric contents leak)

**Management**:
- **Operative**: Any peritonitis, free air, CT evidence perforation
- **Surgical repair**:
  - **Small perforation**: Primary repair (2-layer closure)
  - **Large injury, devitalized bowel**: Resection and anastomosis
  - **Multiple injuries**: Resection with anastomosis or damage control (staple ends, second-look)
  - **Mesenteric injury**: Resect devascularized bowel

### Colon Injury

**Management**:

**Operative**:

**Right colon**: Resection + primary anastomosis (usually safe)

**Left colon** (controversial):
- **Primary repair** or **resection with anastomosis**: If favorable conditions (minimal contamination, hemodynamically stable, <4 units transfused)
- **Damage control**: Resection, leave ends as ostomies (colostomy), restore continuity later

**Diverting colostomy**: If severe contamination, shock, extensive injuries

**Destructive colon injury**: **Hartmann's procedure** (resect, end colostomy, rectal stump)

## Retroperitoneal Injuries

### Zones

**Zone I (Central)**: Aorta, IVC, pancreas, duodenum
**Zone II (Lateral)**: Kidneys, ureters
**Zone III (Pelvic)**: Iliac vessels, bladder, rectum

### Management by Zone and Mechanism

**Blunt Trauma**:
- **Zone I**: Explore (aorta, IVC injuries life-threatening)
- **Zone II**: Do NOT explore (renal injuries managed non-operatively or via renal vascular control first)
- **Zone III**: Do NOT explore (pelvic fracture hematomas tamponade themselves, exploration worsens bleeding)

**Penetrating Trauma**:
- **All zones**: Explore (control vessels, repair injuries)

### Kidney Injury

**Grading (AAST)**: I (contusion), II (laceration <1cm), III (laceration >1cm), IV (vascular injury, shattered kidney), V (renal hilum avulsion, devascularized)

**Diagnosis**: Hematuria (gross or microscopic), CT with IV contrast

**Management**:
- **Non-operative**: Most (grades I-IV if stable)
- **Angioembolization**: Active extravasation
- **Operative**: Hemodynamic instability, expanding hematoma
  - **Nephrectomy**: If shattered, devascularized (grade V)
  - **Renorrhaphy**: Repair lacerations

## Pelvic Trauma

### Pelvic Fractures

**Classification** (Young-Burgess):
- **Lateral compression (LC)**: Side impact, pubic rami fractures (most common, lower bleeding risk)
- **Anteroposterior compression (APC)**: "Open book" fracture, symphysis pubis diastasis, high bleeding risk
- **Vertical shear (VS)**: Fall from height, hemipelvis displaced superiorly, high bleeding risk

**Hemorrhage Source**:
- **Venous** (85%): Pelvic venous plexus, cancellous bone
- **Arterial** (15%): Internal iliac artery branches

### Management

**Pelvic Binder**:
- **Apply immediately** if unstable pelvis (reduce pelvic volume, tamponade)
- Wrap at level of greater trochanters

**Resuscitation**: Massive transfusion protocol if hemodynamically unstable

**Angiography and Embolization**:
- **Indications**: Persistent hypotension despite resuscitation, contrast blush on CT
- **Embolize**: Internal iliac artery branches
- **Success**: 85-95% control arterial bleeding

**External Fixation**:
- Stabilize pelvic ring (APC, VS fractures)
- Reduce volume, aid tamponade
- **Timing**: Emergently if unstable, or in OR during damage control

**Preperitoneal Packing**:
- Direct packing of pelvic retroperitoneum (via laparotomy or extraperitoneal approach)
- **Indications**: Persistent hypotension despite embolization, or cannot access IR

**Definitive Fixation**: Open reduction internal fixation (ORIF) once stable (days to weeks later)

## Damage Control Laparotomy

**Indications**:
- Hemodynamic instability (SBP <90)
- Hypothermia (<35°C)
- Acidosis (pH <7.2)
- Coagulopathy (INR >1.5)
- Massive transfusion (>10 units)

**Principles**: See Damage Control Surgery file

## Key Points

- Blunt abdominal trauma most often injures solid organs (liver most common, then spleen); penetrating trauma often injures hollow viscus
- FAST exam detects free intraperitoneal fluid; positive FAST + hemodynamic instability → immediate laparotomy (don't wait for CT)
- Non-operative management (NOM) successful in 90-95% of blunt solid organ injuries (liver, spleen, kidney) if hemodynamically stable
- Absolute laparotomy indications: hemodynamic instability with intra-abdominal injury, peritonitis, evisceration, free air, hollow viscus injury on CT
- Liver injury managed non-operatively if stable (any grade); operative techniques include perihepatic packing, Pringle maneuver, hepatorrhaphy
- Splenic injury grades I-III have >90% NOM success; splenectomy in trauma requires post-op vaccinations (Pneumovax, Hib, Menactra) for OPSI prevention
- Seat belt sign (abdominal wall ecchymosis) high risk for bowel/mesenteric injury; requires high suspicion and CT evaluation
- Pancreatic ductal injuries: distal (body/tail) → distal pancreatectomy; proximal (head) → damage control, drainage (Whipple rare, high mortality)
- Retroperitoneal hematoma management: blunt Zone I explore, Zone II/III do NOT explore; penetrating all zones explore
- Pelvic fracture hemorrhage control: pelvic binder immediately, angioembolization for arterial bleeding (85-95% success), external fixation for mechanical stability
- Damage control laparotomy for "lethal triad" (hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy): control hemorrhage/contamination, pack, temporary closure, ICU resuscitation
- Free air on CT = hollow viscus perforation → laparotomy; bowel injuries may present with delayed peritonitis (6-12 hours post-injury)
