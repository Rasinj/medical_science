# Vascular Trauma

## Overview

Vascular injuries result from penetrating trauma (most common) or blunt mechanisms, threatening limb viability and life. Rapid recognition and treatment prevent exsanguination, limb loss, and organ ischemia. Hard signs of vascular injury mandate immediate operative exploration, while soft signs require further investigation with imaging or observation. Modern vascular trauma management emphasizes damage control principles, temporary shunting for severely injured patients, and definitive repair once stabilized. Endovascular techniques increasingly complement open surgical repair for select injuries.

## Vascular Anatomy

### Neck Vessels

**Zones** (for penetrating neck trauma):
- **Zone I**: Clavicle to cricoid cartilage (subclavian, innominate, carotid origin, vertebral artery)
- **Zone II**: Cricoid to angle of mandible (carotid, vertebral, jugular)
- **Zone III**: Angle of mandible to skull base (distal carotid, vertebral)

**Carotid Arteries**:
- **Common carotid**: Bifurcates at C4 into internal and external
- **Internal carotid**: No branches in neck, enters skull → brain
- **External carotid**: Multiple branches (superior thyroid, lingual, facial, etc.)

**Vertebral Arteries**: Originate from subclavian, enter transverse foramina C6, ascend → basilar artery

**Jugular Veins**: Internal (lateral to carotid), external (superficial)

### Thoracic Vessels

**Aorta**: Ascending, arch (3 branches: innominate, left common carotid, left subclavian), descending

**Innominate Artery** (brachiocephalic): First branch of arch → right subclavian and right common carotid

**Subclavian Arteries**: Become axillary arteries at lateral border of first rib

### Abdominal Vessels

**Aorta**: Bifurcates at L4 into common iliac arteries

**Major Branches**:
- Celiac axis (T12)
- Superior mesenteric artery (SMA, L1)
- Renal arteries (L1-L2)
- Inferior mesenteric artery (IMA, L3)

**IVC**: Formed by confluence of common iliac veins (L5)

### Extremity Vessels

**Upper Extremity**:
- **Axillary artery**: Subclavian at lateral 1st rib → brachial at teres major
- **Brachial artery**: Bifurcates at antecubital fossa → radial and ulnar
- **Radial/Ulnar arteries**: Form palmar arches (collateral circulation)

**Lower Extremity**:
- **Femoral artery**: External iliac becomes femoral at inguinal ligament
  - **Profunda femoris**: Major collateral (if superficial femoral occluded)
- **Superficial femoral artery (SFA)**: Becomes popliteal at adductor hiatus
- **Popliteal artery**: Trifurcates → anterior tibial, posterior tibial, peroneal
- **Dorsalis pedis**: Continuation of anterior tibial (palpable on dorsum of foot)
- **Posterior tibial**: Palpable posterior to medial malleolus

## Clinical Assessment

### Hard Signs of Vascular Injury

**Mandate immediate operative exploration** (>90% have vascular injury):
- **Active pulsatile bleeding**
- **Expanding or pulsatile hematoma**
- **Absent distal pulses** (with ischemic signs)
- **Audible bruit** or **palpable thrill** over injury site
- **Signs of distal ischemia** ("6 P's"):
  - Pain
  - Pallor
  - Pulselessness
  - Paresthesias
  - Poikilothermia (cool)
  - Paralysis (late, indicates irreversible ischemia)

### Soft Signs of Vascular Injury

**Require further investigation** (vascular injury 10-25%):
- **Diminished pulses** (asymmetric)
- **Nerve deficit** (adjacent to vascular structures)
- **Non-expanding hematoma**
- **Proximity** of penetrating wound to major vessel
- **History of significant bleeding** (prehospital)

**Evaluation**:
- **Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI)** or **Brachial-Brachial Index (BBI)**:
  - **ABI**: Ankle SBP / Brachial SBP (normal >0.9)
  - **<0.9**: Suggests arterial injury, obtain imaging
  - **>0.9**: Low likelihood of injury, observe
- **CT Angiography (CTA)**: If stable
- **Conventional angiography**: If unstable and going to OR, or if endovascular intervention planned

### Physical Examination

**Inspection**: Bleeding, expanding hematoma, penetrating wound proximity
**Palpation**: Pulses (radial, ulnar, femoral, popliteal, dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial), hematoma
**Auscultation**: Bruit, thrill
**Motor/Sensory**: Nerve injury often accompanies vascular injury
**Compartments**: Assess for compartment syndrome

## General Principles of Vascular Repair

### Damage Control Vascular Surgery

**Indications**:
- Hemodynamic instability
- Hypothermia (<35°C)
- Acidosis (pH <7.2)
- Coagulopathy (INR >1.5)
- Multiple injuries requiring staged management

**Technique**:
- **Temporary vascular shunt**: Restore blood flow temporarily (avoid prolonged ischemia)
  - **Shunt types**: Javid shunt, Pruitt-Inahara shunt, or chest tube segment
  - **Secure** with ties or vessel loops
  - **Allows**: Damage control of other injuries (orthopedic fixation, abdominal closure), resuscitation
- **Definitive repair**: Return to OR in 24-48 hours after resuscitation

### Vascular Control

**Proximal and distal control**: Clamp or compress vessels proximal and distal to injury before exploration

**Techniques**:
- **Direct pressure**: External bleeding
- **Intraluminal balloon**: Insert Foley catheter through wound, inflate, pull back (temporary)
- **Vascular clamps**: Atraumatic (Satinsky, DeBakey)
- **Tourniquet**: Extremity hemorrhage (pre-hospital or OR)

### Repair Techniques

**Primary Repair (Lateral Arteriorrhaphy)**:
- **Indications**: Small (<50% vessel circumference), clean laceration
- **Technique**: Suture repair with 4-0 or 5-0 polypropylene (running or interrupted)
- **Avoid narrowing** vessel

**Patch Angioplasty**:
- **Indications**: Repair would narrow vessel
- **Technique**: Vein patch (saphenous vein) to widen closure

**End-to-End Anastomosis**:
- **Indications**: Transection with minimal tissue loss
- **Technique**: Debride injured vessel ends, mobilize to approximate without tension, anastomose

**Interposition Graft**:
- **Indications**: Tissue loss, cannot approximate ends without tension
- **Graft types**:
  - **Autogenous vein** (preferred): Saphenous vein (reversed or non-reversed)
    - Lower infection risk, better patency
  - **Prosthetic**: PTFE, Dacron (if no autogenous vein, contamination low)
- **Technique**: Anastomose graft to proximal and distal vessel

**Ligation**:
- **Indications**: Damage control (temporary), venous injuries (select), non-essential arteries (ulnar, radial, single tibial vessel if others intact)
- **Not for essential arteries** (single vessel to limb)

**Endovascular Repair**:
- **Covered stent** (stent-graft): Exclude pseudoaneurysm, seal injury
- **Embolization**: Control bleeding (branch vessels, non-essential vessels)
- **Indications**: Stable patient, suitable anatomy, inaccessible or high-risk open surgery (e.g., subclavian, iliac, thoracic vessels)

## Specific Vascular Injuries

### Carotid Artery Injury

**Mechanisms**: Penetrating (most common), blunt (rare but high stroke risk)

**Presentation**:
- **Hard signs**: Pulsatile bleeding, expanding hematoma, neurologic deficit (stroke)
- **Soft signs**: Small hematoma, bruit

**Diagnosis**:
- **CTA**: If stable
- **Angiography**: If going to OR or endovascular intervention

**Management**:

**Penetrating Zone II** (cricoid to angle of mandible):
- **Hard signs**: Immediate operative exploration
- **Soft signs**: CTA → repair if injury, observe if normal

**Penetrating Zone I or III**:
- **Angiography** (operative access difficult) → endovascular repair if feasible, or open with vascular/thoracic surgery

**Blunt Carotid Injury**:
- **Dissection**: Antithrombotic therapy (aspirin or heparin), stenting if severe stenosis/occlusion
- **Stroke contraindication**: If already stroke with hemorrhagic transformation, avoid anticoagulation

**Operative Repair**:
- **Incision**: Along anterior sternocleidomastoid
- **Proximal/distal control**: Clamp common carotid (proximal), internal carotid (distal)
- **Repair**: Primary, patch, or interposition graft
- **Shunt**: Temporary internal shunt if repair prolonged (maintain cerebral perfusion)

**Ligation**:
- Last resort (if unrepairable, damage control)
- **Stroke risk**: 30-50% if ligated

### Subclavian/Axillary Artery Injury

**Mechanisms**: Penetrating, clavicle fracture (blunt)

**Presentation**: Hard signs (bleeding, ischemia), soft signs (diminished pulses)

**Diagnosis**: CTA (if stable)

**Management**:

**Operative**:
- **Proximal subclavian**: Median sternotomy or trap-door incision (combined sternotomy + supraclavicular)
- **Distal subclavian/axillary**: Infraclavicular incision, may need clavicle resection
- **Repair**: Primary, patch, interposition graft

**Endovascular**: Covered stent (if stable, suitable anatomy - increasingly used for proximal subclavian)

### Brachial Artery Injury

**Mechanisms**: Penetrating, supracondylar humerus fracture (blunt)

**Presentation**: Ischemic hand (absent radial/ulnar pulses)

**Diagnosis**: Hard signs → OR, soft signs → ABI/CTA

**Management**:

**Operative**:
- **Incision**: Medial upper arm (proximally), antecubital fossa (distally)
- **Repair**: Primary, patch, interposition graft (saphenous vein)

**Fasciotomy**: If prolonged ischemia (>6 hours), to prevent compartment syndrome

### Abdominal Aortic Injury

**Mechanisms**: Penetrating (most), blunt (deceleration)

**Presentation**: Exsanguinating hemorrhage, shock

**Management**:

**Zone I (Suprarenal)** or **Zone III (Infrarenal)**:
- **Immediate laparotomy**
- **Control**: Proximal aortic occlusion (supraceliac clamp if suprarenal)
- **Repair**: Primary, patch, interposition graft
- **Damage control**: Shunt if unstable, definitive repair later

**Endovascular**: Stent-graft (if stable, infrarenal)

### Inferior Vena Cava (IVC) Injury

**Management**:

**Operative**:
- **Infrarenal IVC**: Primary repair (lateral venorrhaphy)
- **Suprarenal IVC**: Repair if possible, ligation last resort (high morbidity - leg edema, renal dysfunction)
- **Hepatic veins/Retrohepatic IVC**: Very challenging, high mortality
  - Damage control: Pack, atriocaval shunt (rarely successful)

**Ligation**: Infrarenal IVC ligation tolerated (develops collaterals) but causes leg edema

### Iliac Artery Injury

**Mechanisms**: Penetrating, pelvic fracture (blunt)

**Management**:

**Operative**:
- **Common/External iliac**: Repair (primary or graft)
- **Internal iliac**: Ligation acceptable (collaterals from contralateral)

**Endovascular**: Covered stent (especially external iliac)

### Femoral Artery Injury

**Common** (superficial location)

**Management**:

**Operative**:
- **Incision**: Vertical, along vessel course
- **Repair**: Primary, patch, interposition graft (saphenous vein)
- **Profunda femoris**: Preserve if possible (major collateral), ligate if necessary

### Popliteal Artery Injury

**High amputation risk** if delay (poor collaterals, ischemia time critical)

**Mechanisms**: Posterior knee dislocation (blunt), penetrating

**Presentation**: Ischemic leg (6 P's)

**Diagnosis**: Hard signs → OR (do NOT delay for imaging), CTA if soft signs

**Management**:

**Operative**:
- **Approach**: Medial (above and below knee), avoid posterior (neurovascular structures)
- **Repair**: Interposition graft (saphenous vein preferred)
- **Orthopedic stabilization**: Stabilize knee fracture/dislocation BEFORE vascular repair (prevents re-injury), or temporary shunt → fix fracture → definitive vascular repair
- **Fasciotomy**: Prophylactic (high compartment syndrome risk)

**Amputation risk**: 10-15% (higher if delayed repair >6-8 hours)

## Venous Injuries

### Management Principles

**Repair vs. Ligation**:
- **Repair**: Major veins (IVC, iliac, femoral, popliteal) if patient stable, not coagulopathic
  - Better long-term outcomes (less edema)
- **Ligation**: Acceptable for most veins if patient unstable (damage control)
  - Leg edema common but improves with collateralization

**Repair Techniques**:
- **Lateral venorrhaphy**: Primary repair
- **Patch**: Vein patch if narrowing concern
- **Interposition graft**: If extensive injury (vein graft)

**Anticoagulation**: NOT routine post-repair (bleeding risk in trauma)

## Compartment Syndrome

### Pathophysiology

Increased pressure within fascial compartment → compromised perfusion → ischemia → necrosis (muscle, nerve)

**Causes**:
- **Vascular injury**: Ischemia-reperfusion injury (most common in trauma)
- **Fractures**: Tibia, forearm
- **Crush injury**, **prolonged compression**

**At-Risk Injuries**: Popliteal artery injury, brachial artery injury, prolonged ischemia (>6 hours)

### Clinical Presentation

**"6 P's"** (late findings - do NOT wait):
- Pain (out of proportion, especially with passive stretch)
- Pressure (tense compartment)
- Paresthesias
- Pallor
- Pulselessness (very late)
- Paralysis (very late)

**Early sign**: **Pain with passive stretch** of muscles in compartment

**Pulses often normal** (compartment pressure <systolic BP)

### Diagnosis

**Clinical** (primarily)

**Compartment Pressure Measurement** (if diagnosis uncertain):
- **Normal**: <10-15 mmHg
- **Compartment syndrome**: >30 mmHg absolute, or within 30 mmHg of diastolic BP (ΔP = Diastolic BP - Compartment Pressure <30)
- **Indications**: Equivocal exam, unconscious patient, unreliable exam

### Management

**Fasciotomy** (URGENT):

**Lower Leg** (4 compartments):
- **Anterior**: Tibialis anterior, extensor digitorum longus, extensor hallucis longus
- **Lateral**: Peroneus longus and brevis
- **Superficial posterior**: Gastrocnemius, soleus
- **Deep posterior**: Tibialis posterior, flexor digitorum longus, flexor hallucis longus

**Technique**:
- **Dual-incision**: Anterolateral (release anterior + lateral) and posteromedial (release superficial + deep posterior)
- **Anterolateral**: 2cm lateral to tibial crest, between tibia and fibula
- **Posteromedial**: 2cm posterior to medial tibia border
- **Incise fascia** of all compartments, inspect muscle
- **Leave open**: Cover with moist dressing, negative pressure wound therapy (wound VAC)
- **Delayed closure**: 3-7 days (may need skin graft)

**Forearm** (volar and dorsal):
- **Volar incision**: Curvilinear, release volar compartments, carpal tunnel
- **Dorsal incision**: If needed (less common)

**Complications of Delay**: Rhabdomyolysis, acute kidney injury, permanent nerve damage, contractures (Volkmann contracture in forearm)

## Endovascular Techniques

### Indications

- **Hemodynamically stable**
- **Suitable anatomy**
- **Injuries difficult to access surgically**: Subclavian, iliac, thoracic vessels, Zone I/III neck
- **Patient factors**: High surgical risk

### Techniques

**Covered Stent (Stent-Graft)**:
- Excludes injury (pseudoaneurysm, laceration)
- Restores flow

**Embolization**:
- Coils, gelfoam, particles
- Control bleeding from branch vessels (internal iliac, pelvic vessels)

**Balloon Occlusion**:
- Temporary control (REBOA - Resuscitative Endovascular Balloon Occlusion of Aorta)
- Bridge to OR

### Limitations

- Requires imaging (CTA or angiography)
- Not for unstable patients (go to OR)
- Risk of device-related complications (thrombosis, migration, endoleak)

## Outcomes

### Limb Salvage

**Factors Affecting Outcome**:
- **Ischemia time**: >6 hours poor prognosis
- **Soft tissue injury**: Mangled Extremity Severity Score (MESS)
  - ≥7: Amputation often required
  - Factors: Skeletal/soft tissue injury, limb ischemia, shock, age
- **Nerve injury**: Associated nerve injury worsens function even if limb salvaged

**Amputation Indications**:
- **Unsalvageable limb**: Prolonged ischemia (>6-8 hours), extensive soft tissue/nerve/bone injury
- **Life over limb**: Unstable patient (damage control - amputate quickly vs. prolonged repair)

### Mortality

**Highest risk**: Truncal vascular injuries (aorta, IVC, iliac)
- Aortic injury: 50-70% mortality (exsanguination)

**Extremity**: Low mortality (<5%) if isolated

## Key Points

- Hard signs of vascular injury (pulsatile bleeding, expanding hematoma, absent pulses, bruit/thrill, ischemia) mandate immediate operative exploration
- Soft signs (diminished pulses, nerve deficit, proximity) require further investigation with ABI (>0.9 low risk) or CTA
- Damage control vascular surgery uses temporary shunts to restore blood flow in unstable patients; definitive repair delayed until resuscitation complete
- Repair techniques: primary (small laceration), patch (avoid narrowing), interposition graft (tissue loss, autogenous vein preferred), ligation (damage control or non-essential vessels)
- Popliteal artery injury high amputation risk (10-15%) due to poor collaterals; ischemia time >6-8 hours worsens outcome; prophylactic fasciotomy indicated
- Compartment syndrome early sign is pain with passive muscle stretch; measure compartment pressure if uncertain (>30mmHg or ΔP <30mmHg = fasciotomy)
- Lower leg fasciotomy releases 4 compartments via dual incisions: anterolateral (anterior + lateral) and posteromedial (superficial + deep posterior)
- Carotid artery ligation stroke risk 30-50%; repair preferred with primary, patch, or graft; temporary shunt maintains cerebral perfusion during repair
- Endovascular techniques (covered stent, embolization) useful for stable patients with injuries difficult to access surgically (subclavian, iliac, Zone I/III neck)
- IVC ligation: infrarenal tolerated (leg edema, collaterals develop), suprarenal high morbidity (renal dysfunction, avoid if possible)
- Venous injuries can be ligated in unstable patients (damage control); repair preferred if stable (better long-term outcomes, less edema)
- Extremity vascular injury ischemia time critical: <6 hours good outcomes, >6-8 hours increased amputation risk, rhabdomyolysis, compartment syndrome
