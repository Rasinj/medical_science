# Initial Trauma Assessment and Resuscitation

## Overview

Systematic trauma assessment saves lives. The Advanced Trauma Life Support (ATLS) protocol provides a standardized approach to evaluate and resuscitate injured patients, prioritizing life-threatening injuries. The primary survey identifies and treats immediately fatal injuries using the ABCDE sequence: Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure. Simultaneous resuscitation occurs during assessment. The secondary survey provides comprehensive head-to-toe examination once the patient is stabilized. Effective trauma care requires coordinated team effort, rapid decision-making, and adherence to proven algorithms.

## Prehospital Care

### Emergency Medical Services (EMS)

**Goals**:
- Scene safety assessment
- Rapid assessment and stabilization
- Expedited transport to appropriate facility

**Interventions**:
- **Airway management**: Jaw thrust, oropharyngeal airway, endotracheal intubation
- **C-spine immobilization**: Cervical collar, backboard (if mechanism suggests injury)
- **Hemorrhage control**: Direct pressure, tourniquets (extremity)
- **IV access**: Large-bore peripheral lines
- **Fluid resuscitation**: Crystalloid (permissive hypotension for penetrating trauma)

**Transport Decisions**:
- **Trauma center** vs. nearest hospital
- **Air transport** if prolonged ground transport (>30-60 minutes), specialized care needed

**Communication**: Pre-arrival notification to receiving hospital (mechanism, injuries, vitals, ETA)

### Prehospital Trauma Triage

**Trauma Center Activation Criteria** (examples):
- **Physiologic**: Hypotension (SBP <90), respiratory compromise, GCS ≤13
- **Anatomic**: Penetrating injury to head/neck/torso/extremities proximal to elbow/knee, flail chest, open/depressed skull fracture, paralysis
- **Mechanism**: High-speed MVC, pedestrian struck, fall >20 feet, ejection from vehicle
- **Special**: Age >55, pregnancy, anticoagulation

## Trauma Team Activation

### Team Composition

- **Trauma surgeon** (team leader)
- **Emergency physician**
- **Anesthesiologist**
- **Nurses** (primary, recorder)
- **Respiratory therapist**
- **Radiology technician**
- **Laboratory personnel**
- **Consultants** (neurosurgery, orthopedics, etc. as needed)

### Roles

**Team Leader**: Coordinates resuscitation, assigns tasks, makes treatment decisions
**Airway Manager**: Anesthesiologist or emergency physician
**Circulation Manager**: Obtains vascular access, administers fluids/blood
**Recorder**: Documents vitals, interventions, timeline

## Primary Survey (ABCDE)

### A - Airway with C-Spine Protection

**Assessment**:
- **Can patient speak?**: Clear, full sentences → airway patent
- **Stridor, gurgling**: Partial obstruction
- **No air movement**: Complete obstruction

**Interventions**:
- **Jaw thrust**: Avoid chin lift (may worsen C-spine injury)
- **Suction**: Remove blood, secretions, foreign material
- **Oropharyngeal or nasopharyngeal airway**: Maintain patency
- **Definitive airway**: Endotracheal intubation or surgical airway if needed

**Definitive Airway Indications**:
- Unable to maintain airway (GCS ≤8, facial fractures, expanding neck hematoma)
- Inability to maintain oxygenation
- Risk of aspiration
- Anticipated need for prolonged ventilation

**Endotracheal Intubation**:
- **Rapid sequence intubation (RSI)**: Induction agent + paralytic
- **C-spine precautions**: Manual inline stabilization (remove anterior collar, maintain immobilization)
- **Confirm placement**: End-tidal CO2 (gold standard), chest rise, auscultation

**Surgical Airway** (if cannot intubate):
- **Cricothyroidotomy**: Emergency airway (cannot intubate, cannot oxygenate)
  - Contraindicated <12 years (risk subglottic stenosis) - needle cricothyroidotomy temporizing
- **Technique**: Horizontal incision through cricothyroid membrane, insert tube (tracheostomy tube, endotracheal tube 6.0-7.0)

**C-Spine Protection**:
- **Immobilization**: Cervical collar + backboard until spine cleared
- **Clearance criteria**:
  - **Clinical**: Alert, no neck pain, no neuro deficits, no distracting injuries, sober → remove collar
  - **Imaging**: CT C-spine if cannot clear clinically

### B - Breathing and Ventilation

**Assessment**:
- **Inspection**: Chest wall movement, respiratory rate, use of accessory muscles
- **Palpation**: Subcutaneous emphysema, tenderness, crepitus
- **Percussion**: Hyperresonance (pneumothorax), dullness (hemothorax)
- **Auscultation**: Breath sounds bilateral (absent/decreased if pneumo/hemothorax)

**Life-Threatening Conditions** (Identify and Treat Immediately):

**1. Tension Pneumothorax**:
- **Signs**: Respiratory distress, tracheal deviation (away), hyperresonance, absent breath sounds, distended neck veins, hypotension
- **Treatment**: **Needle decompression** (immediate)
  - 14- or 16-gauge catheter into 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line (or 4th-5th ICS, anterior axillary line)
  - Rush of air confirms diagnosis
  - Follow with tube thoracostomy

**2. Open Pneumothorax ("Sucking Chest Wound")**:
- **Treatment**: Three-sided occlusive dressing (allows air out but not in), tube thoracostomy (remote from wound)

**3. Massive Hemothorax**:
- **Signs**: Dull percussion, absent breath sounds, hypotension
- **Treatment**: Tube thoracostomy (large-bore 36-40 Fr), blood drainage >1500mL initial or >200mL/hr for 2-4 hours → thoracotomy

**4. Flail Chest**:
- Paradoxical chest wall movement (segment moves inward with inspiration)
- Usually underlying pulmonary contusion
- **Treatment**: Adequate analgesia (epidural preferred), pulmonary toilet, mechanical ventilation if severe

**Interventions**:
- **Supplemental oxygen**: 100% initially
- **Assisted ventilation**: Bag-valve mask if inadequate
- **Tube thoracostomy**: For pneumothorax, hemothorax

### C - Circulation with Hemorrhage Control

**Assessment**:
- **Blood pressure**: Hypotension (SBP <90) = shock until proven otherwise
- **Heart rate**: Tachycardia early shock sign
- **Pulse pressure**: Narrowed (SBP-DBP <25) suggests significant volume loss
- **Skin**: Pale, cool, mottled (poor perfusion)
- **Capillary refill**: >2 seconds abnormal
- **Mental status**: Agitation, confusion (cerebral hypoperfusion)

**Classes of Hemorrhagic Shock** (based on blood loss):
- **Class I** (<15% blood loss, <750mL): Minimal symptoms, normal vitals
- **Class II** (15-30%, 750-1500mL): Tachycardia, tachypnea, anxious, decreased pulse pressure
- **Class III** (30-40%, 1500-2000mL): Tachycardia >120, hypotension, confused
- **Class IV** (>40%, >2000mL): Profound hypotension, markedly decreased mentation, minimal urine output

**Sources of Hemorrhage**:
- **External**: Obvious wounds
- **Chest**: Hemothorax (up to 3L per hemithorax)
- **Abdomen**: Solid organs, vessels (can lose entire blood volume)
- **Pelvis**: Pelvic fractures (retroperitoneal bleeding, can lose 4-5L)
- **Long bones**: Femur (1-2L), tibia (500-1000mL)
- **"On the floor and four more"**: External, chest, abdomen, pelvis, long bones

**Interventions**:

**Hemorrhage Control**:
- **Direct pressure**: External bleeding
- **Tourniquets**: Extremity hemorrhage not controlled with pressure (pre-hospital or hospital)
  - Apply proximal to injury, tighten until bleeding stops
  - Document time applied
- **Pelvic binder**: Unstable pelvic fracture (reduce pelvic volume, tamponade bleeding)

**Vascular Access**:
- **Two large-bore peripheral IVs** (14- or 16-gauge)
- **Sites**: Antecubital fossa preferred
- **If unable**: Central line (femoral fastest), intraosseous (IO) access

**Resuscitation**:

**Permissive Hypotension** (Balanced Resuscitation):
- **Concept**: Avoid aggressive crystalloid in penetrating trauma (increases bleeding before surgical control)
- **Target**: SBP 80-90 mmHg (maintain perfusion, avoid exacerbating hemorrhage)
- **Exception**: Traumatic brain injury (TBI) - maintain SBP >90-110 (prevent secondary brain injury)

**Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP)**:
- **Definition**: Anticipated need for ≥10 units RBC in 24 hours or ≥4 units RBC in 1 hour
- **Activation criteria**: Shock + severe injury, active hemorrhage not controlled
- **Balanced resuscitation**: 1:1:1 ratio (RBC : FFP : Platelets)
  - Prevents dilutional coagulopathy, acidosis, hypothermia ("lethal triad")
- **Tranexamic acid (TXA)**: Antifibrinolytic, give within 3 hours of injury (1g IV over 10 minutes, then 1g over 8 hours)
  - CRASH-2 trial: Reduced mortality if given <3 hours

**Crystalloid**: Minimize (limit to 1-2L), then blood products

### D - Disability (Neurologic Assessment)

**Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)**:
- **Eye Opening** (1-4): Spontaneous (4), to voice (3), to pain (2), none (1)
- **Verbal Response** (1-5): Oriented (5), confused (4), inappropriate words (3), incomprehensible sounds (2), none (1)
- **Motor Response** (1-6): Obeys commands (6), localizes pain (5), withdraws from pain (4), flexion to pain (3), extension to pain (2), none (1)
- **Total**: 3-15 (3 worst, 15 best)
  - **Severe TBI**: GCS ≤8
  - **Moderate TBI**: GCS 9-12
  - **Mild TBI**: GCS 13-15

**Pupillary Exam**:
- Size, symmetry, reactivity to light
- **Unilateral dilated fixed pupil**: Herniation (ipsilateral), CN III compression
- **Bilateral dilated fixed pupils**: Severe brain injury, bilateral herniation, anoxia

**Motor Exam**: Move all extremities? Lateralizing weakness?

**Spinal Cord Injury**:
- **Complete**: No motor/sensory function below level
- **Incomplete**: Some preserved function below level

### E - Exposure and Environmental Control

**Exposure**:
- **Completely undress patient**: Remove all clothing (cut off)
- **Inspect entire body**: Front and back (log roll for back exam with C-spine precautions)
- Identify all injuries

**Environmental Control**:
- **Prevent hypothermia**: Major cause of trauma coagulopathy
  - **Warm room**, warm IV fluids, warm blankets, Bair Hugger (forced-air warming)
  - **Core temp <35°C**: Hypothermia (worsens coagulopathy)

## Adjuncts to Primary Survey

### FAST Exam (Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma)

**Purpose**: Detect free intraperitoneal or pericardial fluid (blood)

**Four Views**:
1. **Perihepatic** (Morrison's pouch): Hepatorenal recess (most dependent, fluid collects here supine)
2. **Perisplenic**: Splenorenal recess
3. **Pelvis**: Retrovesical (male), rectouterine pouch/Pouch of Douglas (female)
4. **Pericardial**: Subxiphoid view (pericardial effusion/tamponade)

**Positive FAST**: Free fluid (anechoic) in any view
**Indications for Laparotomy**: Positive FAST + hemodynamic instability

**Limitations**:
- **Operator-dependent**
- **Low sensitivity** for solid organ injury without free fluid, hollow viscus injury, retroperitoneal bleeding
- **Does not detect source** of bleeding

**Extended FAST (eFAST)**: Includes bilateral thoracic views (pneumothorax, hemothorax)

### Laboratory

**Initial Labs**:
- **Type and crossmatch**: For blood products
- **Hemoglobin/hematocrit**: Baseline (may be normal acutely, takes hours to equilibrate)
- **Coagulation**: PT/PTT, INR (baseline, assess coagulopathy)
- **Metabolic panel**: Assess acidosis (lactate, base deficit), renal function
- **Pregnancy test**: All females of childbearing age
- **Toxicology screen, blood alcohol**: If altered mental status

**Arterial Blood Gas**: Assess oxygenation, ventilation, acid-base (lactate, base deficit correlate with shock severity)

### Imaging

**Chest X-Ray**:
- **Indications**: All major trauma patients
- **Assess**: Pneumothorax, hemothorax, rib fractures, mediastinal widening (aortic injury), tube/line placement

**Pelvis X-Ray**:
- **Indications**: Major blunt trauma, mechanism concerning for pelvic fracture
- **Assess**: Pelvic ring disruption, acetabular fracture
- **FAST replacing pelvis XR** in some centers (if FAST positive, implies pelvic/intra-abdominal injury)

**CT Scan**:
- **When**: After stabilization (hemodynamically stable patients only)
- **Pan-scan**: Head, C-spine, chest, abdomen, pelvis (contrast-enhanced)
- **Benefit**: Detailed injury assessment, guide operative vs. non-operative management
- **Risk**: Delays definitive treatment if hemodynamically unstable

**NEVER go to CT if hemodynamically unstable** - go to OR

## Resuscitation

### Airway Management

See Primary Survey - Airway section

### Breathing Support

- **Oxygen**: Maintain SpO2 >94%
- **Mechanical ventilation**: If intubated
  - **Lung-protective ventilation**: Tidal volume 6-8mL/kg ideal body weight (prevent ARDS)

### Circulatory Support

**IV Fluids**: Balanced resuscitation (limit crystalloid, early blood products)

**Blood Products**:
- **Packed RBC**: O-negative (universal donor) if emergent, type-specific if time
- **Fresh frozen plasma (FFP)**: Replaces clotting factors
- **Platelets**: Goal >50,000-100,000
- **Cryoprecipitate**: Fibrinogen <100-150mg/dL

**Massive Transfusion**:
- **1:1:1 ratio**: 1 unit RBC : 1 unit FFP : 1 unit platelets (or 6-pack platelets per 6 units RBC)
- **Reassess**: After each round (typically 6 units RBC), recheck labs (Hgb, coags, fibrinogen, calcium)
- **Calcium**: Supplement (citrate in blood products chelates calcium → hypocalcemia → worsens coagulopathy)

**Vasopressors**: Only after volume resuscitation (vasopressors without volume = worse outcome)

**Tranexamic Acid**: Within 3 hours of injury

### Hypothermia Prevention

- **Warm room** (increase trauma bay temp)
- **Warm IV fluids**: Fluid warmer (37-40°C)
- **Warm blood products**
- **Forced-air warming** (Bair Hugger)
- **Minimize exposure**: Cover patient when not examining

### Lethal Triad

**Hypothermia + Acidosis + Coagulopathy** = High mortality
- **Prevention**: Damage control surgery (abbreviated operation, control hemorrhage, warm and resuscitate in ICU, then return for definitive repair)

## Secondary Survey

**When**: After primary survey complete, patient stabilized

**Goal**: Head-to-toe comprehensive examination, identify all injuries

### Components

**History** (AMPLE):
- **A**llergies
- **M**edications (especially anticoagulants)
- **P**ast medical history / **P**regnancy
- **L**ast meal (aspiration risk, anesthesia planning)
- **E**vents / **E**nvironment of injury

**Mechanism**: Details help predict injury pattern
- **MVC**: Speed, restraints, airbag deployment, steering wheel deformity, ejection, fatalities
- **Fall**: Height, landing surface
- **Penetrating**: Weapon type, number of wounds, estimated blood loss

**Physical Examination**:

**Head**:
- Scalp lacerations, skull fractures (palpable step-off, depression), periorbital ecchymosis ("raccoon eyes"), Battle's sign (mastoid ecchymosis)
- **Assess**: Pupils, extraocular movements, facial fractures

**Neck**:
- Cervical spine tenderness, step-offs
- **Penetrating**: Entry/exit wounds, expanding hematoma, absent pulses, tracheal deviation
- **Hard signs vascular injury**: Active bleeding, expanding hematoma, absent pulses, bruit/thrill

**Chest**:
- Inspection: Chest wall movement, seat belt sign
- Palpation: Rib tenderness/crepitus, subcutaneous emphysema
- Auscultation: Breath sounds, heart sounds (muffled in tamponade)

**Abdomen**:
- Inspection: Distension, ecchymosis, wounds
- Palpation: Tenderness, rigidity, guarding
- **Seat belt sign**: Associated with bowel/mesenteric injury
- **Unstable pelvis**: Do NOT repeatedly compress (dislodge clot, worsen bleeding)

**Pelvis/Genitourinary**:
- Pelvic stability (compress once gently)
- **Blood at meatus**: Urethral injury, do NOT place Foley (obtain retrograde urethrogram first)
- **High-riding prostate**: Urethral injury
- Vaginal/rectal exam: Blood (open pelvic fracture), rectal tone

**Extremities**:
- Deformities, open fractures
- Pulses, capillary refill
- Compartments: Tense, painful (compartment syndrome)
- Motor, sensory exam

**Back** (log roll):
- Spinal tenderness, step-offs
- Flank ecchymosis (Grey-Turner sign - retroperitoneal bleed)
- Penetrating wounds

### Adjuncts to Secondary Survey

**Imaging**:
- **CT head**: Any concerning mechanism, GCS <15, focal neuro deficits
- **CT C-spine**: Cannot clear clinically
- **CT chest/abdomen/pelvis**: Hemodynamically stable with concerning mechanism or exam
- **Extremity X-rays**: Suspected fractures

**Consultations**: Request specialty consults as needed (neurosurgery, orthopedics, etc.)

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Trauma

**Differences**:
- **Smaller blood volume**: 80mL/kg (vs. 70mL/kg adult), less reserve
- **Physiologic reserve**: Compensate longer (vital signs stable until sudden decompensation)
- **Airway**: Larger tongue, anterior larynx (more difficult intubation)
- **Skeleton**: More pliable (less fractures but more internal organ injury)
- **Thermoregulation**: Greater surface area to volume (lose heat faster)

**Resuscitation**:
- **Fluid bolus**: 20mL/kg crystalloid
- **Blood**: 10mL/kg
- **Broselow tape**: Estimate weight, equipment sizes, drug doses

### Geriatric Trauma

**Considerations**:
- **Physiologic reserve**: Decreased (less tolerance for shock, hypoxia)
- **Medications**: Anticoagulants, beta-blockers (blunt tachycardic response)
- **Frailty**: Minor trauma can cause severe injury
- **Comorbidities**: Cardiac, pulmonary, renal disease (complicate resuscitation)

**Lower threshold** for aggressive intervention, ICU admission

### Pregnancy

**Priorities**:
- **Mother first**: Best chance for fetal survival
- **Left lateral decubitus position**: After 20 weeks (prevent aorta-caval compression by gravid uterus)
- **Obstetric consult**: Assess fetal status (ultrasound, fetal monitoring)
- **Rh status**: If Rh-negative, give RhoGAM (prevent sensitization from fetal-maternal hemorrhage)

**Imaging**: Do NOT withhold necessary imaging (radiation risk to fetus < maternal death risk)

## Key Points

- ATLS primary survey follows ABCDE: Airway (with C-spine protection), Breathing, Circulation (with hemorrhage control), Disability, Exposure
- Tension pneumothorax clinical diagnosis: tracheal deviation, hyperresonance, absent breath sounds, distended neck veins, hypotension; treat with immediate needle decompression
- Hemorrhagic shock classes: I (<15% loss, minimal), II (15-30%, tachycardia), III (30-40%, hypotension), IV (>40%, profound shock)
- Sources of major hemorrhage: "on the floor and four more" - external, chest, abdomen, pelvis, long bones
- FAST exam detects free fluid in four areas: perihepatic (Morrison's pouch), perisplenic, pelvis, pericardium; positive FAST + unstable → laparotomy
- Massive transfusion protocol uses balanced 1:1:1 resuscitation (RBC:FFP:platelets) to prevent trauma coagulopathy
- Permissive hypotension (target SBP 80-90) in penetrating trauma before hemorrhage control; exception TBI (maintain SBP >90-110)
- Lethal triad: hypothermia + acidosis + coagulopathy = high mortality; prevent with damage control surgery and aggressive warming
- Tranexamic acid (TXA) reduces mortality if given within 3 hours of injury (CRASH-2 trial); 1g IV then 1g over 8 hours
- Glasgow Coma Scale: Eye (1-4) + Verbal (1-5) + Motor (1-6) = 3-15; severe TBI ≤8 (intubate), moderate 9-12, mild 13-15
- Surgical airway (cricothyroidotomy) indicated for "cannot intubate, cannot oxygenate"; contraindicated <12 years (needle cricothyroid temporizing)
- Secondary survey is head-to-toe comprehensive exam after primary survey; use AMPLE history (Allergies, Medications, Past history/Pregnancy, Last meal, Events)
