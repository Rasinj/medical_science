# Cosmetic Surgery

## Overview

Cosmetic (aesthetic) surgery aims to enhance appearance and improve patient self-esteem through elective surgical procedures. Unlike reconstructive surgery, cosmetic procedures are typically not medically necessary but address aesthetic concerns across the face, breast, and body. The field encompasses facial rejuvenation (facelift, blepharoplasty, rhinoplasty), breast surgery (augmentation, reduction, mastopexy), and body contouring (liposuction, abdominoplasty). Successful outcomes require artistic vision, surgical precision, realistic patient expectations, and thorough informed consent. Understanding anatomy, aging changes, and aesthetic ideals is fundamental.

## Principles of Aesthetic Surgery

### Patient Selection and Consultation

**Goals**
- Assess patient motivation, expectations
- Educate about procedure, risks, realistic outcomes
- Establish rapport, trust

**Psychological Screening**
- **Body dysmorphic disorder (BDD)**: Preoccupation with perceived defect, seeking multiple procedures, never satisfied
- **Unrealistic expectations**: "Make me look like [celebrity]"
- **External pressure**: Patient seeking surgery to please others
- **Recent major life event**: Divorce, death, job loss (emotional instability)
- **Contraindications**: Active BDD, unrealistic expectations, psychiatric instability

**Medical Assessment**
- Comorbidities: Diabetes, bleeding disorders, cardiovascular disease
- Medications: Anticoagulants, herbal supplements (may increase bleeding)
- Smoking: Cessation mandatory (4-6 weeks before/after surgery)
- Previous surgeries, outcomes

**Informed Consent**
- Detailed discussion of risks, benefits, alternatives
- Review photographs, before/after examples
- Realistic outcome expectations

### Aesthetic Analysis

**Facial Aesthetics**
- **Proportions**: Facial thirds (trichion to glabella, glabella to subnasale, subnasale to menton)
- **Symmetry**: Assess asymmetries (mild asymmetry is normal)
- **Angles**: Nasofacial angle (30-40°), nasolabial angle (90-120°)
- **Aging changes**: Skin laxity, fat atrophy/descent, volume loss

**Breast Aesthetics**
- **Ideal position**: Nipple at inframammary fold (IMF) or just above
- **Ptosis grading** (Regnault):
  - Grade I: Nipple at IMF level
  - Grade II: Nipple below IMF, above lower pole
  - Grade III: Nipple at lowest point (below IMF)
  - Pseudoptosis: Glandular tissue below IMF, nipple above

**Body Aesthetics**
- **Proportions**: Waist-to-hip ratio, body mass index (BMI)
- **Skin quality**: Elasticity, striae, scars

## Rhinoplasty

### Nasal Anatomy

**Structural Anatomy**
- **Nasal bones**: Upper third (bony vault)
- **Upper lateral cartilages**: Middle third (cartilaginous vault)
- **Lower lateral cartilages** (alar cartilages): Nasal tip (medial, intermediate, lateral crura)
- **Septum**: Cartilaginous septum (anteriorly), perpendicular plate ethmoid, vomer (posteriorly)

**External Aesthetic Units**
- Dorsum, tip, alae, columella, sidewalls

**Key Angles**
- **Nasofrontal angle**: 115-130°
- **Nasofacial angle**: 30-40°
- **Nasolabial angle**: 90-120° (males ~90-95°, females ~95-110°)
- **Tip projection**: 0.67× nasal length (Goode's ratio)

### Indications

- Dorsal hump
- Nasal deviation
- Wide nasal bones
- Bulbous tip, poorly defined tip
- Alar base width
- Functional issues (septal deviation, turbinate hypertrophy)

### Approaches

**Open (External) Rhinoplasty**
- **Incision**: Inverted-V columellar incision + bilateral marginal incisions
- **Advantages**: Excellent exposure, visualization of tip, teaching
- **Disadvantages**: External scar (usually imperceptible), longer surgery, more edema

**Closed (Endonasal) Rhinoplasty**
- **Incisions**: Intranasal (marginal, intercartilaginous, transfixion)
- **Advantages**: No external scar, less edema, shorter surgery
- **Disadvantages**: Limited exposure, technically challenging

### Techniques

**Dorsal Hump Reduction**
- **Rasp**: File down bony hump
- **Osteotome**: Remove cartilaginous hump
- **Create open roof**: After hump removal, dorsal width increased
- **Osteotomies**: Medial and lateral osteotomies to close open roof, narrow bony vault

**Osteotomies**
- **Medial osteotomy**: Paramedian cuts along dorsum
- **Lateral osteotomy**: Cuts along maxilla, mobilize nasal bones
- **Technique**: Low-to-high, continuous or perforating
- **Goal**: Mobilize, reposition, narrow nasal bones

**Tip Refinement**
- **Cephalic trim**: Excise cephalic portion of lateral crura (narrow tip)
- **Suture techniques**: Interdomal, transdomal sutures (shape, define tip)
- **Tip grafts**: Columellar strut, shield graft, cap graft (increase projection, refine contour)

**Alar Base Reduction**
- **Weir excisions**: Excise tissue at alar base to narrow nostrils
- **Indication**: Wide alar base, nostril flaring

**Septoplasty**
- Correct septal deviation
- Harvest cartilage for grafts

**Grafts**
- **Spreader grafts**: Between septum and upper laterals (widen middle vault, prevent collapse)
- **Tip grafts**: Shield, cap, onlay (refine tip)
- **Alar rim grafts**: Support alar rim, prevent retraction
- **Cartilage harvest**: Septum, ear, rib (if extensive grafting needed)

### Postoperative Management

**Immediate**
- External splint, internal splints/packing (if septoplasty)
- Splint removed 5-7 days, sutures removed 5-7 days (open)

**Swelling**
- Majority resolves 3-6 months, final result 12-18 months
- Tip swelling lasts longest

**Activity Restrictions**
- Avoid strenuous exercise × 2-4 weeks
- No glasses on nasal bridge × 6 weeks (use tape, contacts)

### Complications

- **Bleeding**: Epistaxis (common, usually minor)
- **Infection**: Rare (<1%)
- **Nasal obstruction**: Scar, valve collapse, turbinate hypertrophy
- **Asymmetry**: Residual or new asymmetry
- **Unsatisfactory result**: Undercorrection, overcorrection, persistent deformity (revision rate 5-15%)
- **Saddle nose deformity**: Excessive dorsal reduction, cartilage necrosis
- **Septal perforation**: Rare (<1%)

## Blepharoplasty (Eyelid Surgery)

### Indications

**Upper Blepharoplasty**
- Dermatochalasis (excess upper lid skin)
- Pseudoptosis (skin overhangs lash line)
- Visual field obstruction
- Aesthetic improvement

**Lower Blepharoplasty**
- Lower lid bags (orbital fat herniation)
- Festoons, malar mounds
- Skin laxity, wrinkles

### Upper Blepharoplasty

**Preoperative Assessment**
- Brow position (brow ptosis may need brow lift, not blepharoplasty)
- Eyelid ptosis (levator function, may need ptosis repair)
- Dry eyes (relative contraindication)

**Technique**
1. **Mark skin excision**: Natural supratarsal crease
2. **Incision**: Along marked crease
3. **Excise skin ± orbicularis muscle**: Conservative excision (avoid lagophthalmos)
4. **Fat removal** (if indicated): Medial and central fat pads (conservative to avoid hollowing)
5. **Closure**: Fine sutures (6-0 or 7-0)

**Postoperative**
- Cool compresses × 48 hours
- Sutures removed 5-7 days
- Minimal downtime (1-2 weeks)

### Lower Blepharoplasty

**Approaches**

**Transcutaneous (Skin Incision)**
- **Incision**: Subciliary (2 mm below lashes) or subtarsal
- **Technique**: Skin-muscle flap, fat excision or repositioning, skin excision
- **Indications**: Skin excess, fat herniation

**Transconjunctival (Inside Eyelid)**
- **Incision**: Inside lower lid (conjunctiva)
- **Technique**: Fat excision or repositioning, no skin excision
- **Indications**: Fat herniation without skin excess (younger patients)
- **Advantages**: No external scar, lower risk of ectropion

**Fat Management**
- **Fat excision**: Traditional (avoid excessive removal → hollowing)
- **Fat repositioning**: Transposition to tear trough, midface (more natural, preserves volume)

**Skin Resurfacing**
- Laser (CO2, erbium) or chemical peel to improve lower lid wrinkles, skin texture

### Complications

- **Hematoma**: Retrobulbar hematoma (vision-threatening emergency, evacuate immediately)
- **Ectropion**: Lower lid retraction, scleral show (from excessive skin excision, scarring)
- **Lagophthalmos**: Incomplete eye closure (excessive upper lid skin excision)
- **Dry eyes**: Temporary or permanent
- **Asymmetry, undercorrection, overcorrection**
- **Blindness**: Rare (<0.01%), from retrobulbar hematoma

## Facelift (Rhytidectomy)

### Aging Face Anatomy

**Aging Changes**
- **Skin**: Loss of elasticity, wrinkles, pigmentation
- **Fat**: Volume loss (temples, midface, periorbital), fat descent (jowls, nasolabial folds)
- **Muscle/Fascia**: SMAS (superficial musculoaponeurotic system) laxity, platysma banding
- **Bone**: Resorption (maxilla, mandible, orbit)

**SMAS Anatomy**
- Fibromuscular layer between skin and deep fascia
- Continuous with platysma (neck), frontalis (forehead)
- Suspensory ligaments: Zygomatic, masseteric

### Indications

- Midface descent (nasolabial folds, jowls)
- Neck laxity (platysmal banding, submental fullness)
- Jawline definition loss
- Skin laxity, rhytids

### Facelift Techniques

**Skin-Only Facelift**
- Historical, rarely performed
- High recurrence (skin stretches)

**SMAS Facelift** (Most Common)
- **SMAS plication**: Fold and suture SMAS
- **SMAS imbrication**: Overlap SMAS
- **SMAS elevation**: Elevate and reposition SMAS
- More durable results than skin-only

**Deep Plane Facelift**
- Dissection below SMAS, mobilize SMAS + overlying skin as single unit
- More extensive release, greater midface lift
- **Advantages**: More dramatic, longer-lasting
- **Disadvantages**: More edema, bruising, facial nerve risk

**Composite Facelift**
- SMAS + orbicularis oculi elevation (addresses lower lid, midface)

### Standard SMAS Facelift Technique

**Incisions**
- Temporal hairline or pretragal
- Around ear (pre/postauricular)
- Occipital hairline (posterior hairline)

**Steps**
1. **Skin flap elevation**: Undermine skin anterior to SMAS
2. **SMAS manipulation**: Plication, imbrication, or elevation
3. **SMAS suspension**: Suture SMAS to deep fascia (superolateral vector)
4. **Skin redraping**: Trim excess skin, close without tension
5. **Neck contouring**:
   - Platysmaplasty (midline plication of platysma if banding)
   - Submentoplasty (submental incision, liposuction, direct fat excision)

**Postoperative**
- Drains (24-48 hours)
- Head wrap dressing × 1-2 days
- Sutures removed 5-14 days (staged)
- Avoid strenuous activity × 2-4 weeks
- Swelling, bruising resolves 2-4 weeks

### Complications

- **Hematoma**: Most common (1-5%), usually first 24 hours, evacuate urgently
- **Facial nerve injury**: Temporary (5-15%), permanent (<1%)
  - Frontal branch (brow), marginal mandibular branch (lower lip) most at risk
- **Skin flap necrosis**: Smokers, tension, hematoma (1-3%)
- **Pixie ear deformity**: Earlobe pulled inferiorly (from excessive tension)
- **Hair loss**: Alopecia at incision sites (especially temporal)
- **Asymmetry, unfavorable scarring**
- **Nerve injury**: Great auricular nerve (numbness, usually temporary)

## Breast Augmentation

### Indications

- Hypoplasia (small breasts)
- Asymmetry
- Post-pregnancy volume loss
- Breast reconstruction (post-mastectomy)

### Preoperative Planning

**Implant Selection**

**Size**
- Volume (cc): 200-800 cc typical range
- Patient preference, tissue characteristics, chest wall dimensions

**Shape**
- **Round**: Most common, no orientation concern
- **Anatomic (teardrop)**: More natural (debatable), must maintain orientation

**Surface**
- **Smooth**: Lower capsular contracture risk (debatable), more rippling
- **Textured**: Lower rotation risk (anatomic implants), possibly lower capsular contracture (controversial)
- **FDA concerns**: Textured implants associated with BIA-ALCL (breast implant-associated anaplastic large cell lymphoma, rare)

**Filler**
- **Saline**: Lower cost, smaller incision, deflation obvious (rippling, less natural)
- **Silicone gel**: More natural feel, cohesive gel (reduced rupture risk), more expensive
- **Highly cohesive ("gummy bear")**: Firm, maintains shape

**Pocket Location**
- **Subglandular**: Above pectoralis muscle, more visible (thin patients), higher capsular contracture
- **Subpectoral** (dual-plane): Below pectoralis muscle, more natural upper pole, lower capsular contracture, animation deformity
- **Subfascial**: Below pectoralis fascia (uncommon)

**Incision Location**
- **Inframammary**: Most common, hidden in fold, direct access
- **Periareolar**: Around areola, risk of nipple sensation changes, capsular contracture
- **Transaxillary**: Armpit, endoscopic or blind, difficult revision
- **Transumbilical (TUBA)**: Navel, saline only, rarely performed

### Technique (Inframammary, Dual-Plane)

1. **Incision**: Inframammary fold (4-5 cm)
2. **Pocket dissection**:
   - Subglandular dissection to pectoralis
   - Release inferior pectoralis muscle (dual-plane: upper pole subpectoral, lower pole subglandular)
3. **Hemostasis**: Meticulous (prevent hematoma)
4. **Implant insertion**: Funnel or Keller funnel (reduce contamination)
5. **Closure**: Layered (muscle, Scarpa's fascia, dermis, skin)

**Postoperative**
- Surgical bra × 1-2 weeks
- Avoid strenuous activity × 4-6 weeks
- Breast massage (some surgeons recommend, others do not)

### Complications

**Capsular Contracture** (Baker Classification)
- **Grade I**: Soft, natural
- **Grade II**: Firm, natural appearance
- **Grade III**: Firm, abnormal appearance
- **Grade IV**: Hard, painful, abnormal appearance
- **Incidence**: 5-20% (higher with subglandular, textured implants controversial)
- **Treatment**: Capsulectomy, capsulotomy, implant exchange

**Implant Rupture**
- **Saline**: Obvious (deflation), implant removal/replacement
- **Silicone**: Often asymptomatic, detected on MRI, explantation recommended
- **Incidence**: 1-2% per year (cumulative risk)

**Other Complications**
- **Infection**: 1-2% (may require implant removal)
- **Hematoma/seroma**: 1-5%
- **Nipple sensation changes**: 15-20% (temporary or permanent)
- **Asymmetry, malposition**
- **Rippling, visible implant edges** (thin patients, saline)
- **BIA-ALCL**: Rare, associated with textured implants (especially Allergan Biocell), presents as late seroma

**Implant Longevity**
- Not lifetime devices
- Average lifespan 10-20 years (replacement often needed)

## Liposuction

### Indications

- Localized fat deposits resistant to diet/exercise
- Body contouring
- **Not weight loss surgery** (removes ~5-10 lbs maximum safely)

**Common Areas**
- Abdomen, flanks, thighs, arms, chin/neck, male chest (gynecomastia)

### Techniques

**Tumescent Technique** (Standard)
- Infiltrate subcutaneous tissue with tumescent solution (lidocaine, epinephrine, saline)
- **Advantages**: Anesthesia, hemostasis, hydrodissection
- **Ratios**: 1:1 or up to 3:1 (infiltrate:aspirate)

**Liposuction Methods**
- **Suction-assisted liposuction (SAL)**: Traditional, manual cannula + suction
- **Power-assisted liposuction (PAL)**: Vibrating cannula, easier in dense tissue
- **Ultrasound-assisted liposuction (UAL)**: Ultrasound energy liquefies fat, useful for fibrous areas, male chest
- **Laser-assisted liposuction (LAL)**: Laser energy (SmartLipo), skin tightening claims (limited evidence)

**Technique**
1. **Mark areas**: Standing position
2. **Tumescent infiltration**: 10-15 minutes for vasoconstriction
3. **Cannula insertion**: Small incisions (3-5 mm), strategic locations
4. **Fat aspiration**: Crisscross pattern, multiple depths, smooth transitions
5. **Skin assessment**: Pinch test (avoid over-resection)
6. **Closure**: Usually open (drainage), compression garment

**Volume Limits**
- **Safe limit**: 5 liters aspirate (large-volume liposuction >5 L increases risks)
- Fluid shifts, electrolyte imbalance risk

**Postoperative**
- Compression garment × 4-6 weeks
- Swelling, bruising resolves 4-8 weeks
- Final results 3-6 months

### Complications

- **Contour irregularities**: Divots, asymmetry (most common complaint)
- **Seroma**: Fluid collection
- **Infection**: <1%
- **Skin necrosis**: Over-aggressive liposuction, compromised vascularity
- **Fat embolism**: Rare, large-volume liposuction
- **Lidocaine toxicity**: Seizures, arrhythmias (respect maximum dose 35-55 mg/kg with tumescent)
- **Perforation**: Visceral (rare, catastrophic)

## Abdominoplasty (Tummy Tuck)

### Indications

- Excess abdominal skin (post-pregnancy, massive weight loss)
- Diastasis recti (separation of rectus muscles)
- Abdominal wall laxity

**Not for obesity** (BMI ideally <30)

### Technique

**Full Abdominoplasty**
1. **Incisions**: Low transverse (bikini line), periumbilical
2. **Flap elevation**: Undermine skin/fat to costal margins
3. **Umbilical preservation**: Incise around umbilicus, preserve stalk
4. **Diastasis recti repair**: Plication of rectus fascia (midline suture, tighten abdominal wall)
5. **Liposuction**: Flanks, epigastrium (if needed)
6. **Flap advancement**: Pull flap inferiorly, excise excess skin
7. **Umbilical transposition**: Create new umbilical opening, inset umbilicus
8. **Closure**: Layered, drains placed

**Mini-Abdominoplasty**
- Limited undermining (below umbilicus)
- Less skin excision, no umbilical transposition
- **Indications**: Infraumbilical excess only

**Postoperative**
- Drains × 7-14 days (until <30 mL/day)
- Compression garment × 6 weeks
- Flexed position initially (reduce tension)
- Avoid strenuous activity × 6 weeks

### Complications

- **Seroma**: Most common (15-30%), treated with drainage
- **Wound dehiscence**: Especially at T-junction (midline-transverse junction)
- **Skin flap necrosis**: Smokers, diabetes, tension (1-4%)
- **Infection**: 2-4%
- **Umbilical necrosis**: Compromised blood supply
- **Hematoma**: 1-3%
- **DVT/PE**: Mobilize early, compression stockings, consider prophylaxis
- **Persistent numbness**: Lateral femoral cutaneous nerve (common), resolves over months

## Key Points

1. **Body dysmorphic disorder (BDD)** is an absolute contraindication to cosmetic surgery; patients with unrealistic expectations, external pressure, or recent major life events should defer surgery.

2. **Open rhinoplasty** uses inverted-V columellar incision for excellent exposure; closed rhinoplasty avoids external scar but is technically challenging; dorsal hump reduction requires osteotomies to close open roof.

3. **Nasal tip refinement** techniques include cephalic trim, interdomal sutures, and tip grafts (shield, cap); spreader grafts between septum and upper laterals prevent middle vault collapse.

4. **Retrobulbar hematoma** after blepharoplasty is a vision-threatening emergency requiring immediate drainage; ectropion from excessive skin excision is a common complication of lower blepharoplasty.

5. **SMAS facelift** (superficial musculoaponeurotic system) provides more durable results than skin-only facelift; deep plane facelift offers greater midface lift but higher facial nerve injury risk.

6. **Hematoma** is the most common facelift complication (1-5%, usually first 24 hours); facial nerve injury is temporary in 5-15% (frontal and marginal mandibular branches most at risk), permanent <1%.

7. **Breast implants** are not lifetime devices (average lifespan 10-20 years); capsular contracture (Baker Grade III-IV) occurs in 5-20%; dual-plane (subpectoral) placement reduces contracture vs. subglandular.

8. **BIA-ALCL** (breast implant-associated anaplastic large cell lymphoma) is rare but associated with textured implants; presents as late seroma (>1 year post-op); requires explantation and capsulectomy.

9. **Tumescent liposuction** infiltrates lidocaine, epinephrine, and saline for anesthesia, hemostasis, and hydrodissection; safe limit is 5 liters aspirate; lidocaine toxicity risk at >35-55 mg/kg with tumescent technique.

10. **Seroma** is the most common abdominoplasty complication (15-30%); managed with drains (until <30 mL/day) or serial aspirations; skin flap necrosis risk increased in smokers and diabetics.
