# Craniofacial Surgery

## Overview

Craniofacial surgery addresses congenital and acquired deformities of the skull, face, and jaws. The field encompasses cleft lip and palate repair, craniosynostosis correction, orthognathic surgery for dentofacial deformities, craniofacial syndromes, and facial fracture management. Successful outcomes require multidisciplinary collaboration between plastic surgeons, oral-maxillofacial surgeons, neurosurgeons, orthodontists, speech therapists, and other specialists. Understanding embryology, growth and development, and three-dimensional surgical planning is essential for optimal functional and aesthetic results.

## Cleft Lip and Palate

### Embryology and Classification

**Embryology**
- **Primary palate** (lip, alveolus, anterior hard palate): Fusion of medial nasal and maxillary prominences (weeks 4-7)
- **Secondary palate** (hard and soft palate): Fusion of palatal shelves (weeks 8-12)

**Incidence**
- 1 in 700 live births
- Cleft lip ± cleft palate: More common in males, Asians, Native Americans
- Isolated cleft palate: More common in females, Caucasians

**Classification**

**Veau Classification** (Cleft Palate)
- **Class I**: Soft palate only
- **Class II**: Soft and hard palate (to incisive foramen)
- **Class III**: Unilateral complete cleft (lip, alveolus, palate)
- **Class IV**: Bilateral complete cleft

**Kernahan Classification** (Striped Y)
- Divides lip and palate into segments for documentation

**Unilateral vs. Bilateral, Complete vs. Incomplete**
- **Complete cleft lip**: Extends through entire lip to nostril
- **Incomplete cleft lip**: Partial thickness, intact Simonart's band
- **Complete cleft palate**: Involves entire hard and soft palate
- **Incomplete cleft palate**: Partial palate involvement

### Associated Anomalies and Syndromes

**Isolated Cleft** (70%)
- Non-syndromic, multifactorial inheritance

**Syndromic Cleft** (30%)
- **Pierre Robin sequence**: Micrognathia, glossoptosis, U-shaped cleft palate → airway obstruction
- **Van der Woude syndrome**: Cleft + lower lip pits
- **Stickler syndrome**: Cleft, myopia, arthropathy, hearing loss
- **Velocardiofacial syndrome (22q11 deletion)**: Cleft, cardiac defects, immune deficiency
- Many other syndromes (>400 associated with cleft)

### Cleft Lip Repair

**Timing**
- **Traditional Rule of 10s**: 10 weeks old, 10 pounds, hemoglobin 10 g/dL
- **Modern practice**: 3-6 months (allows growth, bonding)

**Goals**
- Restore normal lip anatomy: Cupid's bow, philtral columns, vermilion
- Achieve symmetry
- Minimize scar
- Normalize nasal architecture

**Unilateral Cleft Lip Repair**

**Millard Rotation-Advancement Technique** (Most Common)
- **Concept**: Rotate medial lip segment down, advance lateral segment medially
- **Steps**:
  1. **Mark landmarks**: Cupid's bow, philtral columns, nasal base
  2. **Rotation incision**: Medial lip, along philtral column, extends into columella
  3. **Advancement incision**: Lateral lip
  4. **Dissection**: Release lip muscles, nasal floor
  5. **Muscle repair**: Orbicularis oris muscle across cleft (critical for function)
  6. **Skin closure**: Three layers (mucosa, muscle, skin)
  7. **Nasal repair**: Reposition lower lateral cartilage, reconstruct nasal sill

**Alternatives**
- **Tennison-Randall (Triangular flap)**: Geometric design, lengthens lip
- **Mohler technique**

**Bilateral Cleft Lip Repair**
- More challenging (no normal side as template)
- **Prolabium**: Central lip segment (often deficient)
- Repair both sides (staged or simultaneous)
- Achieve Cupid's bow symmetry

**Primary Rhinoplasty**
- Nasal correction at time of lip repair (controversial)
- Reposition lower lateral cartilage, widen nostril
- Definitive rhinoplasty deferred until growth complete (adolescence)

### Cleft Palate Repair

**Timing**
- **9-18 months** (most common: 9-12 months)
- Earlier: Better speech (critical period for speech development <18 months)
- Later: Less maxillary growth restriction

**Goals**
- Close palatal defect
- Achieve velopharyngeal competence (normal speech, prevent nasal regurgitation)
- Minimize impact on maxillary growth

**Techniques**

**Two-Flap Palatoplasty** (Most Common)
- Elevate mucoperiosteal flaps from hard palate, bring to midline
- Close soft palate muscles (levator veli palatini, musculus uvulae)
- **Intravelar veloplasty**: Reorient levator muscles (critical for velopharyngeal function)

**Furlow Double-Opposing Z-Plasty**
- Z-plasty on oral and nasal layers (opposite orientations)
- Lengthens soft palate, repositions levator muscles
- Good velopharyngeal outcomes

**Vomer Flap**
- Nasal layer closure using vomer mucosa
- Reduces fistula risk

**Complications**
- **Velopharyngeal insufficiency (VPI)**: Incomplete closure during speech (hypernasal speech, nasal air emission)
  - Incidence: 10-30%
  - Treatment: Speech therapy, pharyngeal flap, sphincter pharyngoplasty
- **Oronasal fistula**: 5-15% (most common at hard-soft palate junction)
- **Maxillary growth restriction**: Inevitable to some degree (repair causes scar)
- **Bleeding, infection, airway obstruction**

### Secondary Procedures

**Alveolar Bone Grafting**
- **Timing**: Mixed dentition (age 7-10, before canine eruption)
- **Goal**: Provide bone for tooth eruption, stabilize maxillary arch, support nasal base
- **Graft**: Iliac crest cancellous bone (gold standard)

**Pharyngoplasty for VPI**
- **Indications**: Persistent VPI despite speech therapy
- **Techniques**:
  - **Pharyngeal flap**: Superiorly-based posterior pharyngeal wall flap attached to soft palate (creates lateral ports)
  - **Sphincter pharyngoplasty**: Bilateral palatopharyngeus muscle flaps create sphincter
- **Complications**: Sleep apnea (10-20%), hyponasality

**Definitive Rhinoplasty**
- After facial growth complete (age 16-18)
- Correct nasal asymmetry, septal deviation, alar base width

**Orthognathic Surgery**
- LeFort I osteotomy to advance hypoplastic maxilla (20-25% of cleft patients)

## Craniosynostosis

### Pathophysiology

**Definition**: Premature fusion of one or more cranial sutures

**Incidence**: 1 in 2000-2500 live births

**Virchow's Law**: Skull growth restricted perpendicular to fused suture, compensatory growth parallel to suture

**Etiology**
- **Non-syndromic** (80-90%): Isolated, sporadic
- **Syndromic** (10-20%): Crouzon, Apert, Pfeiffer, Saethre-Chotzen syndromes

### Types of Craniosynostosis

**Sagittal Synostosis** (Most Common, 40-60%)
- **Suture**: Sagittal suture
- **Skull shape**: Scaphocephaly (long, narrow skull)
- **Mechanism**: AP growth, restricted biparietal growth

**Metopic Synostosis** (10-20%)
- **Suture**: Metopic suture (frontal)
- **Skull shape**: Trigonocephaly (triangular forehead, midline ridge)

**Coronal Synostosis** (20-25%)
- **Unilateral**: Plagiocephaly (asymmetric, flattened forehead on affected side, elevated orbit)
- **Bilateral**: Brachycephaly (short, wide skull, flattened forehead)

**Lambdoid Synostosis** (Rare, <5%)
- **Suture**: Lambdoid suture
- **Skull shape**: Posterior plagiocephaly (asymmetric occiput)
- **Differentiate from positional plagiocephaly** (much more common, non-synostotic)

**Positional (Deformational) Plagiocephaly**
- **No synostosis**: Molding from positional preference, supine sleeping
- **Skull shape**: Occipital flattening, ipsilateral frontal bossing ("parallelogram head")
- **Treatment**: Repositioning, physical therapy, helmet orthosis (controversial efficacy)
- **Differentiate**: CT shows patent sutures (vs. fused in lambdoid synostosis)

### Craniofacial Syndromes

**Apert Syndrome**
- **Genetics**: FGFR2 mutation
- **Features**: Bicoronal synostosis, midface hypoplasia, syndactyly (mitten hands, sock feet)
- **Cognitive**: Variable (IQ often reduced)

**Crouzon Syndrome**
- **Genetics**: FGFR2 mutation
- **Features**: Bicoronal synostosis, midface hypoplasia, exorbitism, normal hands/feet
- **Cognitive**: Usually normal IQ

**Pfeiffer Syndrome**
- **Genetics**: FGFR1 or FGFR2 mutation
- **Features**: Bicoronal synostosis, broad thumbs/great toes, variable severity
- Type I: Mild, normal intelligence
- Type II-III: Severe, cloverleaf skull

**Saethre-Chotzen Syndrome**
- **Genetics**: TWIST1 mutation
- **Features**: Coronal synostosis, low-set hairline, ptosis, syndactyly (mild)

### Surgical Treatment

**Indications**
- **Functional**: Prevent elevated ICP, visual impairment
- **Aesthetic**: Normalize skull shape

**Timing**
- **Optimal**: 3-12 months (skull bones malleable, brain growth rapid)

**Techniques**

**Strip Craniectomy (Minimally Invasive)**
- **Technique**: Endoscopic or open removal of fused suture
- **Timing**: Early (2-6 months)
- **Adjunct**: Postoperative helmet molding (3-6 months)
- **Advantages**: Shorter surgery, less blood loss, faster recovery
- **Disadvantages**: Requires helmet compliance, limited correction for severe cases
- **Indications**: Sagittal, metopic, unilateral coronal (age <6 months)

**Cranial Vault Remodeling (Open)**
- **Technique**: Frontal-orbital advancement, cranial vault reshaping with bone grafts, plates
- **Timing**: 6-12 months (or later)
- **Advantages**: Definitive correction, more dramatic improvement
- **Disadvantages**: Longer surgery (4-6 hours), higher blood loss (often require transfusion), longer recovery
- **Indications**: Syndromic craniosynostosis, older children, severe deformities

**Specific Procedures**

**Sagittal Synostosis**
- Strip craniectomy (early) or cranial vault remodeling (barrel stave osteotomies, reshape parietal bones)

**Metopic Synostosis**
- Frontal-orbital advancement, widen bitemporal distance, correct trigonocephaly

**Unicoronal Synostosis**
- Unilateral frontal-orbital advancement, correct orbital dystopia

**Bicoronal Synostosis**
- Bilateral frontal-orbital advancement, advance supraorbital bar

**Complications**
- **Intraoperative**: Blood loss (may require transfusion), dural tear, air embolus
- **Postoperative**: Infection, CSF leak, hematoma, resynostosis (5-10%)
- **Long-term**: Residual deformity, need for revision surgery (10-30%)

### Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP)

**Incidence**: 10-15% (non-syndromic), 40-60% (syndromic, especially multi-suture)

**Signs**
- Papilledema, headache, developmental delay, thumb-printing on skull X-ray (beaten copper appearance)

**Monitoring**
- Fundoscopy, CT/MRI (small ventricles, increased digital markings)

**Treatment**
- Surgical decompression (cranial vault expansion)

## Orthognathic Surgery

### Indications

**Dentofacial Deformities**
- **Maxillary hypoplasia**: Midface retrusion, Class III malocclusion (underbite)
- **Maxillary hyperplasia**: Vertical maxillary excess, gummy smile
- **Mandibular prognathism**: Jaw protrusion, Class III malocclusion
- **Mandibular retrognathia**: Weak chin, Class II malocclusion (overbite)
- **Facial asymmetry**

**Obstructive Sleep Apnea** (OSA)
- Maxillomandibular advancement (MMA) for severe OSA

**Cleft Lip/Palate Sequelae**
- Maxillary hypoplasia from cleft repair

### Timing

**After growth complete**
- **Females**: Age 14-16
- **Males**: Age 17-21
- Hand-wrist radiographs (bone age) confirm skeletal maturity

### Preoperative Planning

**Orthodontic Preparation**
- **Decompensation**: Align teeth in preparation for surgery (12-18 months)
- Remove dental compensations (e.g., proclination of lower incisors in Class II)

**Cephalometric Analysis**
- Lateral cephalogram (skull X-ray)
- Measure SNA (maxilla-cranial base), SNB (mandible-cranial base), ANB (maxilla-mandible relationship)

**Model Surgery**
- Mount dental models on articulator
- Simulate surgical movements, fabricate surgical splints

**3D Virtual Surgical Planning** (Modern)
- CT-based planning, custom surgical guides/plates

### Surgical Procedures

**Le Fort I Osteotomy** (Maxilla)

**Indications**
- Maxillary hypoplasia, hyperplasia, asymmetry, vertical excess

**Technique**
1. **Incision**: Maxillary vestibule (intraoral)
2. **Osteotomy**: Horizontal cuts through maxilla above tooth apices (bilateral lateral nasal walls, pterygoid plates, midline nasal septum)
3. **Down-fracture**: Mobilize maxilla
4. **Reposition**: Move maxilla (anteriorly, posteriorly, vertically, rotate) per surgical plan
5. **Fixation**: Miniplate and screw fixation
6. **Splint**: Intermediate splint wired to teeth (guide occlusion)

**Movements**
- **Advancement**: Class III correction, midface augmentation
- **Impaction**: Reduce gummy smile, vertical maxillary excess
- **Widening**: Narrow maxilla (segmental osteotomy)

**Bilateral Sagittal Split Osteotomy (BSSO)** (Mandible)

**Indications**
- Mandibular prognathism, retrognathia, asymmetry

**Technique**
1. **Incision**: Mandibular vestibule (intraoral), expose ramus
2. **Osteotomy**: Sagittal split through ramus (lingual, buccal, inferior border cuts)
3. **Split**: Separate proximal (condyle, ramus) and distal (body with teeth) segments
4. **Reposition**: Advance or setback distal segment
5. **Fixation**: Bicortical screws or plates

**Movements**
- **Advancement**: Class II correction (weak chin)
- **Setback**: Class III correction (prognathism)

**Genioplasty** (Chin)

**Indications**
- Chin asymmetry, microgenia (small chin), macrogenia (large chin)

**Technique**
1. **Incision**: Lower labial sulcus (intraoral)
2. **Osteotomy**: Horizontal cut below tooth roots
3. **Reposition**: Advance, setback, vertically shorten/lengthen, shift laterally
4. **Fixation**: Plate and screws

**Combined Procedures**
- **Bimaxillary surgery**: Le Fort I + BSSO (most common for severe deformities)
- **MMA** (Maxillomandibular advancement): Le Fort I advancement + BSSO advancement (for OSA)

### Postoperative Management

**Immediate**
- ICU or step-down unit (airway monitoring)
- Soft diet × 6 weeks
- Oral hygiene (chlorhexidine rinses)
- **Maxillomandibular fixation (MMF)**: Wires or elastics securing upper/lower teeth together
  - Rigid MMF: 6 weeks (older technique)
  - Guiding elastics: 2-6 weeks (modern, allows jaw opening)

**Orthodontics**
- **Postoperative orthodontics**: Fine-tune occlusion (6-12 months)

**Complications**
- **Intraoperative**: Bleeding, unfavorable split (BSSO), maxillary devitalization (rare)
- **Nerve injury**:
  - **Inferior alveolar nerve** (BSSO): Temporary numbness (50-100%), permanent (5-10%)
  - **Infraorbital nerve** (Le Fort I): Temporary numbness (10-20%)
- **Malocclusion**: Relapse, unfavorable healing
- **Infection, nonunion**: Rare
- **TMJ dysfunction**: Condylar resorption (1-5%), especially in young females, high mandibular plane angle

### Outcomes

- Excellent functional and aesthetic results in most patients (90-95% satisfaction)
- Improved occlusion, facial balance, airway (if OSA)

## Facial Fractures

### Mandible Fractures

**Classification by Location**
- Condyle (30%), angle (25%), body (20%), symphysis/parasymphysis (15%), ramus (5%), coronoid (1%)

**Treatment Principles**
- **Restore occlusion**: Primary goal
- **ORIF** (open reduction internal fixation): Miniplates, bicortical screws
  - Indications: Displaced, unfavorable fractures (muscle forces distract fragments)
- **Closed reduction + MMF**: Non-displaced, favorable fractures (rare in adults, more common in children)

**Condylar Fractures**
- **Non-operative**: Most condylar fractures (maintain function, soft diet, early ROM)
- **Operative**: Bilateral condyle fractures with no posterior occlusal contact, severe displacement

### Midface Fractures

**Le Fort Classification** (Maxillary Fractures)

**Le Fort I**
- **Fracture line**: Horizontal, separates maxillary alveolus from midface
- **Mobile segment**: Maxillary teeth

**Le Fort II**
- **Fracture line**: Pyramidal, through nasal bones, inferior orbital rims, maxilla
- **Mobile segment**: Nose, maxilla

**Le Fort III**
- **Fracture line**: Craniofacial disjunction (entire midface separates from cranial base)
- **Mobile segment**: Entire midface

**Treatment**
- ORIF with miniplates (Le Fort I-III)
- Restore projection, vertical height, occlusion

**Zygomaticomaxillary Complex (ZMC) Fractures**
- **"Tripod fracture"**: Fracture of zygomatic bone at frontozygomatic, zygomaticomaxillary, zygomatic arch
- **Indications for surgery**: Displacement, enophthalmos, limited mouth opening (arch impinging on coronoid)
- **Treatment**: ORIF (1-3 points of fixation)

**Orbital Fractures**
- **Blowout fracture**: Isolated orbital floor or medial wall fracture (orbital contents herniate into maxillary or ethmoid sinus)
- **Indications for surgery**:
  - Enophthalmos (>2 mm)
  - Diplopia with positive forced duction test (muscle entrapment)
  - Large floor defect (>50%)
- **Treatment**: Orbital floor reconstruction (titanium mesh, porous polyethylene implant)

### Nasal Fractures

- **Most common** facial fracture
- **Treatment**: Closed reduction (within 7-10 days), septoplasty if septal hematoma/deviation
- **Definitive rhinoplasty**: If persistent deformity after 6-12 months

## Key Points

1. **Cleft lip repair** (Millard rotation-advancement) performed at 3-6 months; key step is orbicularis oris muscle repair for normal function; primary rhinoplasty corrects nasal deformity.

2. **Cleft palate repair** performed at 9-12 months to optimize speech (critical period <18 months); intravelar veloplasty repositions levator muscles for velopharyngeal competence.

3. **Velopharyngeal insufficiency (VPI)** complicates 10-30% of cleft palate repairs; presents as hypernasal speech and nasal air emission; treated with pharyngeal flap or sphincter pharyngoplasty.

4. **Alveolar bone grafting** performed at age 7-10 (mixed dentition, before canine eruption) using iliac crest bone; provides support for tooth eruption and stabilizes maxillary arch.

5. **Sagittal craniosynostosis** (most common, 40-60%) causes scaphocephaly (long, narrow skull); treated with strip craniectomy (early) or cranial vault remodeling; follows Virchow's law (growth perpendicular to suture restricted).

6. **Apert syndrome** (FGFR2 mutation) presents with bicoronal synostosis, midface hypoplasia, and syndactyly (mitten hands, sock feet); ICP monitoring critical (40-60% develop elevated ICP).

7. **Le Fort I osteotomy** repositions maxilla for Class III malocclusion, vertical maxillary excess, or midface hypoplasia; combined with BSSO (bilateral sagittal split osteotomy) for bimaxillary surgery in severe deformities.

8. **Inferior alveolar nerve injury** after BSSO causes temporary numbness in 50-100% and permanent numbness in 5-10%; risk reduced by careful osteotomy technique and nerve visualization.

9. **Maxillomandibular advancement (MMA)** (Le Fort I advancement + BSSO advancement) is surgical treatment for severe obstructive sleep apnea; advances both jaws, enlarges airway.

10. **Orbital blowout fracture** requires surgery if enophthalmos >2 mm, diplopia with muscle entrapment, or large floor defect (>50%); reconstructed with titanium mesh or porous polyethylene implant.
