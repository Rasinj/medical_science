# Hand Surgery

## Overview

Hand surgery encompasses the diagnosis and treatment of conditions affecting the hand, wrist, and forearm, including traumatic injuries, congenital anomalies, degenerative conditions, nerve compression syndromes, and tumors. The hand's intricate anatomy—bones, joints, tendons, nerves, vessels, and specialized skin—requires precise surgical technique and detailed knowledge of functional anatomy. Successful outcomes depend on meticulous repair, appropriate rehabilitation, and restoration of both form and function.

## Hand Anatomy

### Bones and Joints

**Carpal Bones** (Proximal to Distal)
- **Proximal row**: Scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum, pisiform
- **Distal row**: Trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, hamate
- **Mnemonic**: "Some Lovers Try Positions That They Can't Handle"

**Metacarpals**
- Five metacarpals (I-V)
- **CMC joints**: Carpometacarpal (thumb CMC highly mobile)
- **MCP joints**: Metacarpophalangeal

**Phalanges**
- **Thumb**: Proximal, distal phalanges
- **Fingers**: Proximal, middle, distal phalanges
- **IP joints**: Interphalangeal (thumb: 1 IP joint, fingers: PIP and DIP joints)

### Intrinsic Muscles

**Thenar Muscles** (Median nerve)
- Abductor pollicis brevis, flexor pollicis brevis, opponens pollicis
- Opposition, thumb flexion, abduction

**Hypothenar Muscles** (Ulnar nerve)
- Abductor digiti minimi, flexor digiti minimi, opponens digiti minimi

**Adductor Pollicis** (Ulnar nerve)
- Thumb adduction

**Interossei**
- **Dorsal interossei** (4): Abduct fingers (DAB)
- **Palmar interossei** (3): Adduct fingers (PAD)
- Ulnar nerve innervation

**Lumbricals**
- Flex MCP, extend PIP/DIP joints
- **Radial two**: Median nerve
- **Ulnar two**: Ulnar nerve

### Extrinsic Tendons

**Flexor Tendons**
- **Flexor digitorum profundus (FDP)**: Flexes DIP joint
- **Flexor digitorum superficialis (FDS)**: Flexes PIP joint
- **Flexor pollicis longus (FPL)**: Flexes thumb IP joint
- **Flexor zones**:
  - **Zone I**: Distal to FDS insertion (DIP to PIP)
  - **Zone II** ("No man's land"): A1 pulley to FDS insertion (PIP to distal palmar crease)
  - **Zone III**: Distal palmar crease to carpal tunnel
  - **Zone IV**: Carpal tunnel
  - **Zone V**: Proximal to carpal tunnel (forearm)

**Extensor Tendons**
- **Extensor digitorum communis (EDC)**: Extends MCP, PIP, DIP
- **Extensor indicis proprius (EIP)**: Independent index extension
- **Extensor digiti minimi (EDM)**: Independent small finger extension
- **Extensor pollicis longus (EPL), extensor pollicis brevis (EPB)**: Thumb extension
- **Abductor pollicis longus (APL)**: Thumb abduction
- **Extensor zones**:
  - **Zone I**: DIP joint (mallet finger)
  - **Zone II**: Middle phalanx
  - **Zone III**: PIP joint (central slip)
  - **Zone IV**: Proximal phalanx
  - **Zone V**: MCP joint
  - **Zone VI**: Metacarpal (dorsal hand)
  - **Zone VII**: Wrist (extensor retinaculum)
  - **Zone VIII**: Distal forearm

### Nerves

**Median Nerve**
- **Innervation**:
  - Motor: Thenar muscles (APB, FPB, opponens), radial lumbricals (1-2)
  - Sensory: Thumb, index, middle, radial half of ring finger (palmar and dorsal tips)
- **Carpal tunnel syndrome**: Median nerve compression at wrist

**Ulnar Nerve**
- **Innervation**:
  - Motor: Hypothenar muscles, interossei, ulnar lumbricals (3-4), adductor pollicis
  - Sensory: Small finger, ulnar half of ring finger
- **Froment sign**: Thumb IP flexion compensating for weak adductor pollicis
- **Guyon canal syndrome**: Ulnar nerve compression at wrist

**Radial Nerve**
- **Innervation**:
  - Motor: Wrist, finger, thumb extensors
  - Sensory: Dorsal first web space
- **Posterior interosseous nerve (PIN)**: Motor branch
- **Radial tunnel syndrome**: PIN compression

### Blood Supply

**Radial Artery**
- Supplies lateral hand
- Forms deep palmar arch (dominant)
- **Anatomic snuffbox**: Palpable dorsally

**Ulnar Artery**
- Supplies medial hand
- Forms superficial palmar arch (dominant)

**Allen Test**
- Assess collateral circulation (radial + ulnar arteries)
- Occlude both arteries, release one, observe capillary refill

## Hand Fractures

### Distal Radius Fractures

**Classification**

**Colles Fracture**
- **Most common**: Distal radius fracture with dorsal angulation, dorsal displacement
- **Mechanism**: Fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH)
- **"Dinner fork deformity"**

**Smith Fracture** (Reverse Colles)
- Distal radius fracture with **volar angulation**, volar displacement
- **Mechanism**: Fall on flexed wrist

**Barton Fracture**
- Intra-articular fracture with radiocarpal dislocation
- **Dorsal or volar Barton**

**Chauffeur Fracture** (Radial Styloid)
- Fracture of radial styloid

**Indications for Surgery**
- **>20 degrees dorsal angulation**
- **>2 mm articular step-off**
- **>5 mm radial shortening**
- **Intra-articular displacement**
- **Unstable fractures**: Comminution, dorsal comminution

**Treatment**

**Closed Reduction and Casting**
- Acceptable alignment, stable fracture
- **Acceptable parameters**: <10-15 degrees dorsal angulation, <2 mm articular step-off, <3 mm radial shortening
- **Casting**: 6 weeks (long-arm cast × 3 weeks → short-arm cast × 3 weeks)

**Percutaneous Pinning**
- K-wires across fracture site
- **Indications**: Extra-articular, unstable after reduction

**Open Reduction Internal Fixation (ORIF)**
- **Volar plating**: Most common (buttress volar cortex, early ROM)
- **Dorsal plating**: Less common (extensor tendon irritation)
- **Indications**: Intra-articular, unstable, comminuted

**External Fixation**
- Spanning fixator (radius to metacarpal)
- **Indications**: Highly comminuted, soft tissue injury, damage control

**Complications**
- **Median nerve compression**: Acute carpal tunnel syndrome (decompress urgently)
- **Malunion**: Dorsal angulation, radial shortening (corrective osteotomy)
- **Arthritis**: Post-traumatic radiocarpal arthritis
- **Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)**: Pain, swelling, stiffness

### Scaphoid Fractures

**Anatomy**
- **Blood supply**: Retrograde from distal to proximal (dorsal branch of radial artery)
- **Proximal pole**: Watershed area, high risk of avascular necrosis (AVN)

**Mechanism**
- Fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH)

**Diagnosis**
- **Clinical**: Anatomic snuffbox tenderness, scaphoid tubercle tenderness
- **X-ray**: PA, lateral, scaphoid view (may be negative initially)
- **MRI or CT**: If X-ray negative but high clinical suspicion (occult fracture)

**Classification**
- **Location**: Distal pole (10%), waist (70%), proximal pole (20%)
- **Displacement**: Non-displaced vs. displaced (>1 mm)

**Treatment**

**Non-Displaced Fractures**
- **Distal pole, waist**: Thumb spica cast × 6-12 weeks
- **Proximal pole**: Higher AVN risk, longer immobilization or surgery

**Displaced Fractures** (>1 mm displacement)
- **ORIF**: Headless compression screw (Herbert, Acutrak screw)
- **Approach**: Volar (distal, waist) or dorsal (proximal pole)

**Complications**
- **Nonunion**: 5-10% (higher for proximal pole)
  - Treatment: Bone grafting + screw fixation
- **Avascular necrosis (AVN)**: 10-40% (proximal pole)
  - Treatment: Vascularized bone graft
- **Scaphoid nonunion advanced collapse (SNAC wrist)**: Post-traumatic arthritis
  - Treatment: Wrist fusion, proximal row carpectomy

### Metacarpal Fractures

**Boxer's Fracture** (5th Metacarpal Neck)
- **Mechanism**: Punching injury
- **Deformity**: Volar angulation, loss of knuckle prominence

**Treatment**
- **Non-operative**: <40 degrees angulation (4th), <60 degrees angulation (5th)
  - Ulnar gutter splint × 3-4 weeks
- **Operative**: Excessive angulation, rotational deformity, multiple fractures
  - K-wire fixation, ORIF with plate

**Metacarpal Shaft Fractures**
- **Acceptable angulation**: Varies by digit (less tolerance for index/middle vs. ring/small)
- **Rotational deformity**: Not acceptable (causes finger overlap)
- **Treatment**: Splinting vs. ORIF (plate, K-wires)

**Metacarpal Base Fractures**
- **Thumb CMC (Bennett, Rolando)**:
  - **Bennett**: Intra-articular fracture-dislocation (volar-ulnar fragment)
  - **Rolando**: Comminuted intra-articular fracture (Y or T pattern)
  - Treatment: ORIF (K-wires, screw, plate)

### Phalangeal Fractures

**Indications for Surgery**
- **Angulation**: >10 degrees in any plane
- **Rotational deformity**: Any rotation (causes scissoring)
- **Intra-articular**: >1-2 mm step-off
- **Open fractures**

**Treatment**
- **Closed reduction and splinting**: Stable, minimally displaced
  - Buddy taping to adjacent finger
- **K-wire fixation**: Simple oblique, spiral fractures
- **ORIF**: Comminuted, intra-articular, unstable

## Tendon Injuries

### Flexor Tendon Injuries

**Flexor Zones**
- **Zone II** ("No man's land"): Most challenging (FDP and FDS in fibro-osseous sheath, poor healing, adhesions)

**Primary Repair** (Preferred)
- **Timing**: Ideally <24 hours (up to 10-14 days acceptable)
- **Technique**:
  - **Core suture**: 4-strand or 6-strand (stronger, allow early mobilization)
  - **Epitendinous suture**: Running peripheral suture
  - **Repair strength**: 4-strand ~40 N, 6-strand ~60 N (resist early motion forces)

**Zone II Repair**
- Repair both FDP and FDS (or FDP only if FDS severely damaged)
- Avoid bulky repair (prevent pulley impingement)

**Postoperative Rehabilitation**
- **Early passive motion** (Kleinert, Duran protocols): Reduce adhesions
  - Dorsal blocking splint (wrist 20-30° flexion, MCP 50-70° flexion, IP extended)
  - Passive flexion exercises
  - Start POD #1-3
- **Early active motion** (place-and-hold): Controversial (higher rupture risk but better outcomes)
- **Immobilization**: Obsolete (increases adhesions, stiffness)

**Complications**
- **Adhesions**: Most common (prevent full flexion)
  - Tenolysis if no progress after 3-6 months of therapy
- **Rupture**: 5-10% (especially with early active motion)
- **Flexion contracture**: PIP joint contracture

### Extensor Tendon Injuries

**Mallet Finger (Zone I)**
- **Mechanism**: DIP forced flexion (ball striking fingertip)
- **Pathology**: Extensor tendon avulsion from distal phalanx ± bony avulsion

**Classification**
- **Type I**: Closed tendon disruption
- **Type II**: Laceration
- **Type III**: Deep abrasion with tendon loss
- **Type IV**: Pediatric physeal fracture
- **Bony mallet**: Avulsion fracture (>30% articular surface or volar subluxation requires surgery)

**Treatment**
- **Non-operative** (most cases): Continuous DIP extension splint × 6-8 weeks (full time) + 4-6 weeks (night)
  - Stack splint, aluminum foam splint
- **Operative**: Large bony avulsion (>30% articular), volar subluxation
  - K-wire fixation, ORIF with screw or suture anchor

**Boutonniere Deformity (Zone III)**
- **Deformity**: PIP flexion, DIP hyperextension
- **Pathology**: Central slip disruption → lateral bands displace volarly → act as PIP flexors

**Mechanism**
- Trauma (laceration, forced PIP flexion), rheumatoid arthritis

**Treatment**
- **Acute**: Splint PIP in extension × 6 weeks (allow DIP flexion to prevent lateral band tightness)
- **Chronic**: Surgical reconstruction (restore central slip, release lateral bands)

**Extensor Tendon Laceration (Zones IV-VIII)**
- **Primary repair**: 4-0 or 5-0 non-absorbable suture (simple, figure-of-eight, Bunnell)
- **Postoperative**: Splint 3-4 weeks (MCP/wrist extension, allow controlled flexion)
- **Rehabilitation**: Easier than flexor tendons (less adhesion risk)

## Nerve Injuries

### Classification (Seddon, Sunderland)

**Seddon Classification**
- **Neurapraxia**: Conduction block, myelin injury, intact axon (recovers fully, weeks-months)
- **Axonotmesis**: Axonal disruption, intact endoneurium (regenerates 1 mm/day, Wallerian degeneration)
- **Neurotmesis**: Complete nerve transection (requires surgical repair)

**Sunderland Classification** (Grades I-V)
- Grade I: Neurapraxia
- Grade II: Axonotmesis, intact endoneurium
- Grade III: Axon + endoneurium disruption, intact perineurium
- Grade IV: Axon + endoneurium + perineurium disruption, intact epineurium
- Grade V: Neurotmesis (complete transection)

### Nerve Repair Techniques

**Primary Repair** (Direct Coaptation)
- **Indications**: Clean laceration, minimal gap (<5 mm), no tension
- **Timing**: Ideally <72 hours (primary), up to 3 weeks (delayed primary)
- **Technique**:
  - **Epineural repair**: Suture epineurium (9-0, 10-0 nylon)
  - **Group fascicular repair**: Match fascicular groups (intraneural dissection)
  - Tension-free, alignment (match nerve size, fascicular pattern, blood vessels)
- **Postoperative**: Splint 3-4 weeks (protect repair), then gradual mobilization

**Nerve Grafting**
- **Indications**: Gap >5 mm, excessive tension with primary repair
- **Graft types**:
  - **Autograft** (gold standard): Sural nerve (lower leg), medial/lateral antebrachial cutaneous nerve
  - **Allograft**: Cadaveric nerve (Avance)
  - **Conduits**: Synthetic (polyglycolic acid), biologic (collagen, vein)
- **Technique**: Interposition graft, tension-free coaptation
- **Outcomes**: Inferior to primary repair (two coaptation sites, graft must be revascularized)

**Nerve Transfer (Neurotization)**
- **Indications**: Proximal nerve injury, long regeneration distance, no distal stump (avulsion)
- **Concept**: Sacrifice less important donor nerve to reinnervate critical muscle
- **Examples**:
  - Oberlin transfer: Ulnar nerve fascicle → musculocutaneous nerve (restore elbow flexion)
  - Double fascicular transfer: Median + ulnar fascicles → musculocutaneous + axillary nerves

### Nerve Recovery

**Regeneration Rate**
- **1 mm/day** (or 1 inch/month)
- Calculate expected recovery time based on distance

**Tinel Sign**
- Percussion over regenerating nerve → distal tingling
- Advances distally as nerve regenerates

**Motor Recovery Grading** (Medical Research Council, MRC)
- **M0**: No contraction
- **M1**: Flicker of contraction
- **M2**: Movement with gravity eliminated
- **M3**: Movement against gravity
- **M4**: Movement against some resistance
- **M5**: Normal strength

**Sensory Recovery Grading**
- **S0**: No sensation
- **S1**: Deep pain
- **S2**: Superficial pain, some touch
- **S3**: Pain, touch, 2-point discrimination >15 mm
- **S3+**: 2-point discrimination 7-15 mm
- **S4**: 2-point discrimination <7 mm

## Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

### Pathophysiology

**Definition**: Median nerve compression at carpal tunnel (wrist)

**Anatomy**
- **Boundaries**: Carpal bones (floor, walls), transverse carpal ligament (roof)
- **Contents**: Median nerve, 9 flexor tendons (FDS × 4, FDP × 4, FPL)

**Etiology**
- **Idiopathic** (most common)
- **Increased pressure**: Tenosynovitis, fluid retention (pregnancy, hypothyroidism), trauma (distal radius fracture)
- **Decreased space**: Wrist fracture malunion, masses (ganglion, lipoma)

**Risk Factors**
- Female, age >40, obesity, diabetes, pregnancy, hypothyroidism, rheumatoid arthritis

### Clinical Presentation

**Symptoms**
- **Paresthesias**: Thumb, index, middle, radial half of ring finger (median nerve distribution)
  - **Nocturnal symptoms** (classic): Awaken with numbness, shake hand for relief
- **Weakness**: Thenar atrophy (severe, late), weak thumb opposition/abduction
- **Pain**: Wrist, hand, may radiate to forearm

**Physical Examination**
- **Tinel sign**: Percussion over median nerve at wrist → tingling in median distribution (60% sensitivity)
- **Phalen test**: Wrist flexion × 60 seconds → paresthesias (75% sensitivity)
- **Durkan test**: Direct compression over carpal tunnel → symptoms
- **Thenar atrophy**: Wasting of thenar eminence (severe cases)
- **Weak APB**: Abductor pollicis brevis weakness (thumb palmar abduction)

**Electrodiagnostic Studies**
- **Nerve conduction studies (NCS)**: Gold standard
  - Prolonged distal motor latency (>4.5 ms), decreased sensory conduction velocity
- **Electromyography (EMG)**: Assess for denervation (fibrillations, positive sharp waves) in severe cases
- **Indications**: Confirm diagnosis, assess severity, rule out cervical radiculopathy, guide treatment

### Treatment

**Non-Operative**
- **Wrist splinting**: Neutral position, nighttime (± daytime), 6-12 weeks
- **NSAIDs**: Reduce inflammation
- **Corticosteroid injection**: Median nerve sheath injection (temporary relief, 50-70% recurrence)
- **Activity modification**: Avoid repetitive wrist flexion/extension

**Indications for Surgery**
- **Failure of conservative management** (>3-6 months)
- **Severe disease**: Thenar atrophy, constant numbness, abnormal EMG (denervation)
- **Progressive symptoms**

**Surgical Technique** (Carpal Tunnel Release)

**Open Release**
- **Incision**: Longitudinal incision in palm (align with radial border of ring finger, avoid crossing wrist crease perpendicularly)
- **Divide transverse carpal ligament**: Release entire ligament under direct vision
- **Inspect median nerve**: Assess for mass, tenosynovitis, hourglass deformity
- **Closure**: Skin only (leave ligament open)

**Endoscopic Release**
- Single or dual portal technique
- **Advantages**: Smaller incision, faster recovery, less pillar pain
- **Disadvantages**: Higher risk of incomplete release, nerve/artery injury, requires expertise
- **Contraindications**: Severe tenosynovitis, masses, anomalous anatomy

**Postoperative Management**
- Soft dressing, early finger ROM
- Sutures removed 10-14 days
- Gradual return to activities (full recovery 6-12 weeks)

**Outcomes**
- **Excellent** in most patients (90-95% satisfaction)
- Sensory symptoms improve first (weeks), motor recovery slower (months)
- **Recurrence**: <5%

**Complications**
- **Incomplete release**: Persistent symptoms (reoperation)
- **Pillar pain**: Thenar/hypothenar tenderness (resolves over months)
- **Nerve injury**: Median nerve, palmar cutaneous branch, recurrent motor branch (<1%)
- **Vascular injury**: Superficial palmar arch, median artery (rare)
- **Infection, hematoma, scar tenderness**

## Dupuytren's Contracture

### Pathophysiology

**Definition**: Progressive fibroproliferative disease of palmar fascia → nodules, cords, finger contracture

**Pathology**
- Myofibroblast proliferation, collagen deposition in palmar fascia
- Cords form along pre-existing fascial bands

**Epidemiology**
- **Northern European descent** (Viking disease)
- Male > female
- Age >50

**Risk Factors**
- Family history, alcohol use, smoking, diabetes, epilepsy, HIV

### Clinical Presentation

**Stages**
1. **Nodules**: Firm lumps in palm (usually painless)
2. **Cords**: Thickened bands extending from palm to finger
3. **Contracture**: MCP and/or PIP joint flexion deformity

**Hueston Tabletop Test**
- Patient unable to place hand flat on table → indicates need for treatment

**Common Locations**
- **Ring and small fingers** (most common)
- **Thumb web** (first web space contracture)

### Treatment

**Indications for Intervention**
- **MCP contracture ≥30 degrees**
- **Any PIP contracture**
- **Functional impairment**

**Non-Operative**

**Observation**
- Nodules without contracture, mild contracture (<30 degrees MCP)

**Collagenase Injection (Xiaflex)**
- **Enzyme**: Clostridium histolyticum collagenase, digests collagen
- **Technique**: Inject into cord, manipulation 24-48 hours later (rupture cord)
- **Indications**: Isolated MCP or PIP contracture, palpable cord
- **Outcomes**: 60-70% correction, 50% recurrence at 5 years
- **Complications**: Skin tears, flexor tendon rupture (<1%), allergic reaction

**Operative**

**Percutaneous Needle Fasciotomy (Needle Aponeurotomy)**
- **Technique**: Multiple percutaneous cord cuts with needle (25-gauge)
- **Indications**: MCP contracture, elderly/poor surgical candidates
- **Outcomes**: Good short-term correction, high recurrence (50-70% at 5 years)
- **Complications**: Nerve/tendon injury (rare), infection

**Fasciectomy**
- **Technique**: Open excision of diseased fascia
  - **Selective (regional) fasciectomy**: Remove cords and involved fascia
  - **Radical fasciectomy**: Remove all palmar fascia (rarely done)
- **Indications**: Moderate-severe contracture, recurrent disease
- **Outcomes**: Excellent correction (90-95% MCP, 60-80% PIP), recurrence 20-30% at 10 years
- **Complications**:
  - Nerve injury (digital nerves displaced by cords): 2-5%
  - Vascular injury, hematoma
  - Wound healing problems, skin loss (especially PIP contracture)
  - Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)
  - Recurrence

**Dermofasciectomy**
- Fasciectomy + excision of overlying skin, skin graft
- **Indications**: Severe recurrent disease, aggressive Dupuytren's
- **Outcomes**: Lower recurrence than fasciectomy

**Postoperative Management**
- Hand therapy, splinting (extension splints for nighttime)
- Gradual return to activities

## Key Points

1. **Scaphoid fractures** have retrograde blood supply (distal to proximal); proximal pole fractures have 10-40% AVN risk and require surgical fixation or prolonged immobilization.

2. **Boxer's fracture** (5th metacarpal neck) accepts up to 60 degrees angulation due to CMC mobility; rotational deformity is never acceptable and causes finger overlap.

3. **Flexor tendon Zone II** ("no man's land") injuries require meticulous repair with 4-strand or 6-strand core sutures and early passive motion (Kleinert protocol) to prevent adhesions.

4. **Mallet finger** is treated non-operatively with continuous DIP extension splinting × 6-8 weeks; operative treatment indicated only for large bony avulsions (>30% articular surface) or volar subluxation.

5. **Nerve regeneration** occurs at 1 mm/day (1 inch/month); Tinel sign advances distally as nerve regenerates; primary repair preferred if gap <5 mm and tension-free.

6. **Carpal tunnel syndrome** classic presentation includes nocturnal paresthesias in median nerve distribution (thumb, index, middle, radial ring); surgical release indicated for thenar atrophy, constant numbness, or failed conservative management.

7. **Phalen test** (wrist flexion × 60 seconds reproducing symptoms) has 75% sensitivity for carpal tunnel syndrome; nerve conduction studies are gold standard with prolonged distal motor latency >4.5 ms.

8. **Dupuytren's contracture** treatment indicated for MCP contracture ≥30 degrees or any PIP contracture; fasciectomy provides best long-term outcomes with 20-30% recurrence at 10 years.

9. **Boutonniere deformity** (PIP flexion, DIP hyperextension) results from central slip disruption; acute injuries treated with PIP extension splinting × 6 weeks while allowing DIP flexion.

10. **Colles fracture** (distal radius with dorsal angulation) requires surgical fixation if >20 degrees dorsal angulation, >2 mm articular step-off, or >5 mm radial shortening; volar plating is most common approach.
