# Spine Surgery

## Overview

Spine surgery encompasses the operative management of degenerative, traumatic, infectious, neoplastic, and deformity conditions affecting the vertebral column and spinal cord. The goal of spine surgery is to decompress neural elements, stabilize the spine, correct deformity, and restore function while minimizing morbidity. Understanding spinal anatomy, biomechanics, and surgical approaches is essential for successful outcomes.

## Spinal Anatomy and Biomechanics

### Vertebral Column Structure

**Regions**
- **Cervical**: C1-C7 (7 vertebrae, 8 nerve roots)
- **Thoracic**: T1-T12 (12 vertebrae, relatively rigid)
- **Lumbar**: L1-L5 (5 vertebrae, mobile segment)
- **Sacral**: S1-S5 (fused)
- **Coccygeal**: 3-5 fused segments

**Typical Vertebra Components**
- **Vertebral body**: Anterior weight-bearing structure
- **Pedicles**: Connect body to posterior elements
- **Lamina**: Posterior arch
- **Spinous process**: Posterior projection
- **Transverse processes**: Lateral projections
- **Facet joints**: Zygapophyseal joints, guide motion

**Intervertebral Disc**
- **Nucleus pulposus**: Central gelatinous core (70-90% water)
- **Annulus fibrosus**: Concentric collagen rings
- **Endplates**: Cartilaginous interface with vertebral body

### Spinal Cord and Nerve Roots

**Spinal Cord Levels**
- Terminates at L1-L2 (conus medullaris)
- **Cauda equina**: Nerve roots below conus
- **Cervical enlargement**: C4-T1 (upper extremity innervation)
- **Lumbar enlargement**: L1-S3 (lower extremity innervation)

**Nerve Root Anatomy**
- Exit above corresponding vertebra in cervical spine (C5 exits C4-C5)
- Exit below in thoracic/lumbar spine (L4 exits L4-L5)
- **Dorsal root**: Sensory fibers
- **Ventral root**: Motor fibers
- **Dorsal root ganglion**: Sensory neuron cell bodies

**Spinal Cord Blood Supply**
- **Anterior spinal artery**: Anterior 2/3 of cord
- **Paired posterior spinal arteries**: Posterior 1/3
- **Artery of Adamkiewicz**: Major radicular artery (T9-L2), critical for cord perfusion

### Biomechanics

**Spinal Stability**
- **Three-column concept** (Denis):
  - **Anterior column**: Anterior longitudinal ligament, anterior half of vertebral body
  - **Middle column**: Posterior half of vertebral body, posterior longitudinal ligament
  - **Posterior column**: Pedicles, facets, lamina, spinous process, interspinous ligaments
  - **Instability**: Disruption of ≥2 columns

**Motion Segments**
- Flexion-extension, lateral bending, axial rotation
- **Most mobile**: Cervical spine, lumbar spine
- **Least mobile**: Thoracic spine (ribcage)

## Lumbar Disc Herniation

### Pathophysiology

**Disc Degeneration**
- Loss of nucleus pulposus hydration
- Annular fissures and tears
- Disc height loss
- Altered biomechanics and load distribution

**Herniation Types**
- **Protrusion**: Nucleus bulges through annular tear, annulus intact
- **Extrusion**: Nucleus breaches annulus, remains contiguous
- **Sequestration**: Free fragment, disc material separated

**Location**
- **Central**: Midline, may affect cauda equina
- **Paracentral**: Most common, affects traversing nerve root
- **Foraminal**: Affects exiting nerve root
- **Far lateral**: Beyond foramen, affects exiting root

### Clinical Presentation

**Radiculopathy Patterns**
- **L4**: Anterior thigh pain, knee weakness (quadriceps), decreased patellar reflex
- **L5**: Lateral leg, dorsal foot pain, foot drop (EHL, tibialis anterior), no reflex change
- **S1**: Posterior leg, plantar foot pain, weakness plantarflexion (gastrocnemius), decreased Achilles reflex

**Physical Examination**
- **Straight leg raise (SLR)**: Positive if radicular pain <70° (L4-S1)
- **Crossed SLR**: Pain in contralateral leg (more specific)
- **Femoral stretch test**: L2-L4 radiculopathy
- **Motor examination**: Strength grading (0-5)
- **Sensory examination**: Dermatomal distribution
- **Reflexes**: Patellar (L4), Achilles (S1)

**Red Flags**
- **Cauda equina syndrome**: Saddle anesthesia, bowel/bladder dysfunction, bilateral symptoms
- **Progressive motor deficit**: Foot drop, quadriceps weakness
- **Severe/intractable pain**: Despite conservative management

### Diagnostic Evaluation

**Imaging**
- **MRI**: Gold standard, disc herniation, nerve root compression, spinal stenosis
- **CT myelography**: Alternative if MRI contraindicated (pacemaker, claustrophobia)
- **Radiographs**: Lumbar AP/lateral, assess alignment, instability

**Electrodiagnostics**
- **EMG/NCS**: Confirm radiculopathy, localize level, assess chronicity
- Useful if imaging-clinical correlation unclear

### Non-Operative Management

**Conservative Treatment (6-12 weeks)**
- Activity modification, avoid aggravating positions
- Physical therapy: Core strengthening, McKenzie exercises
- NSAIDs, muscle relaxants
- Epidural steroid injections: Transforaminal vs. interlaminar
- **Natural history**: 80-90% improve with conservative management

**Indications for Surgery**
- Failed conservative management (6-12 weeks)
- Progressive neurological deficit
- Cauda equina syndrome (emergency)
- Severe, disabling pain

### Surgical Technique: Microdiscectomy

**Approach**
- Patient prone on Jackson table or Wilson frame
- Midline incision, centered over affected level
- Subperiosteal muscle dissection
- Identify level with fluoroscopy

**Decompression**
- Partial laminotomy/flavectomy
- Identify dura and nerve root
- Retract nerve root medially
- Identify and remove disc herniation
- Decompress nerve root foramen if stenotic

**Closure**
- Meticulous hemostasis
- Layered closure
- No drain typically needed

**Minimally Invasive Approaches**
- Tubular retractors, endoscopic techniques
- Smaller incision, less muscle dissection
- Similar outcomes to open microdiscectomy

### Postoperative Management

**Immediate Care**
- Early mobilization: Same day or POD #1
- Pain control: Multimodal analgesia
- No brace required

**Activity Restrictions**
- Avoid bending, lifting, twisting for 6 weeks
- Gradual return to activities
- Physical therapy for core strengthening

**Return to Work**
- Sedentary: 2-4 weeks
- Manual labor: 6-12 weeks

### Complications

**Intraoperative**
- **Dural tear** (1-5%): Primary repair, bed rest 24-48 hours
- **Nerve root injury**: Retraction injury, avulsion (rare)
- **Vascular injury**: Aorta, vena cava (anterior perforation)

**Postoperative**
- **Recurrent disc herniation** (5-10%): Same level, typically within 6 months
- **Wrong level surgery**: Prevention with intraoperative imaging
- **Infection** (1-2%): Discitis, epidural abscess
- **Chronic pain**: Failed back surgery syndrome

## Lumbar Spinal Stenosis

### Pathophysiology

**Degenerative Changes**
- Disc height loss and bulging
- Facet joint hypertrophy and arthritis
- Ligamentum flavum hypertrophy
- Spondylolisthesis
- **Result**: Narrowing of central canal, lateral recess, or foramen

**Types**
- **Central stenosis**: Narrowing of central spinal canal
- **Lateral recess stenosis**: Subarticular zone
- **Foraminal stenosis**: Nerve root exit zone

### Clinical Presentation

**Neurogenic Claudication**
- Bilateral leg pain, weakness, numbness with walking
- Improved with sitting, forward flexion ("shopping cart sign")
- Worse with standing, lumbar extension
- Variable distance tolerance

**Physical Examination**
- Often normal at rest
- Symptoms provoked by walking or extension
- Wide-based gait
- May have reduced lumbar lordosis

**Differential Diagnosis**
- **Vascular claudication**: Leg pain with walking, relieved by standing still, diminished pulses
- **Hip arthritis**: Groin pain, limited hip range of motion

### Diagnostic Evaluation

**Imaging**
- **MRI**: Assess canal diameter, nerve root compression
- **CT**: Bony stenosis, facet hypertrophy
- **Standing radiographs**: Assess alignment, spondylolisthesis, instability
- **Flexion-extension films**: Evaluate dynamic instability

**Severity Grading**
- **Mild**: CSF visible around nerve roots
- **Moderate**: Nerve roots crowded, CSF reduced
- **Severe**: No CSF, nerve roots compressed

### Non-Operative Management

**Conservative Treatment**
- Physical therapy: Flexion-based exercises, core strengthening
- Epidural steroid injections
- NSAIDs, gabapentin for neuropathic pain
- Activity modification
- Weight loss if obese

### Surgical Indications

**Criteria**
- Failed conservative management (3-6 months)
- Significant functional impairment
- Progressive neurological deficit
- MRI confirmation of stenosis correlating with symptoms

### Surgical Technique: Decompressive Laminectomy

**Standard Laminectomy**
- Midline approach, expose spinous processes and lamina
- Remove spinous processes, lamina, and ligamentum flavum
- **Bilateral decompression**: Ensure adequate lateral recess decompression
- Preserve facet joints (medial 50%) to maintain stability
- Undercutting facetectomy for foraminal stenosis

**Minimally Invasive Techniques**
- Tubular retractors, microscopic or endoscopic
- Unilateral approach for bilateral decompression
- Less muscle dissection, faster recovery

**Laminectomy vs. Laminotomy**
- **Laminectomy**: Complete removal of lamina
- **Laminotomy**: Partial removal, preserves midline structures

### Fusion Considerations

**Indications for Fusion with Decompression**
- Spondylolisthesis (Grade II or higher)
- Scoliosis >20°
- Iatrogenic instability (>50% facetectomy)
- Recurrent stenosis
- Dynamic instability on flexion-extension films

**Fusion Techniques**
- **Posterolateral fusion**: Bone graft over transverse processes
- **Interbody fusion**: PLIF (posterior), TLIF (transforaminal), ALIF (anterior)
  - Restores disc height and foraminal space
  - Higher fusion rates than posterolateral alone
- **Instrumentation**: Pedicle screws and rods for stability

### Postoperative Management

**Laminectomy Alone**
- Early mobilization
- No brace required
- Return to activities: 6-12 weeks

**Laminectomy with Fusion**
- Early mobilization, brace optional (surgeon-dependent)
- Bone healing: 3-6 months
- Gradual return to activities
- Fusion assessment: CT scan at 6-12 months

### Complications

- **Dural tear** (5-15%): Higher in revision surgery
- **Instability**: May require subsequent fusion
- **Adjacent segment disease**: Accelerated degeneration at adjacent levels
- **Epidural hematoma**: Rare, may cause neurological deficit
- **Pseudarthrosis (nonunion)**: If fusion performed (5-10%)

### Outcomes

- 70-80% good to excellent outcomes
- Improvement in walking distance and function
- Pain relief variable
- Reoperation rate: 10-15% at 10 years

## Spondylolisthesis

### Classification

**Wiltse-Newman Classification**
- **Type I (Dysplastic)**: Congenital facet dysplasia
- **Type II (Isthmic)**: Pars interarticularis defect (spondylolysis)
  - Most common at L5-S1
  - Repetitive stress in adolescents (gymnastics, football)
- **Type III (Degenerative)**: Facet arthropathy, most common L4-L5
- **Type IV (Traumatic)**: Acute fracture
- **Type V (Pathologic)**: Bone disease (tumor, infection)

**Meyerding Grade (Slip Percentage)**
- **Grade I**: 0-25%
- **Grade II**: 25-50%
- **Grade III**: 50-75%
- **Grade IV**: 75-100%
- **Grade V (Spondyloptosis)**: >100%

### Clinical Presentation

**Symptoms**
- Low back pain, worse with extension
- Radiculopathy: L5 nerve root (degenerative L4-L5), L5 or S1 (isthmic L5-S1)
- Hamstring tightness
- Neurogenic claudication (degenerative)

**Physical Examination**
- Palpable step-off (high-grade slips)
- Reduced lumbar lordosis
- Tight hamstrings
- Neurological examination: Motor, sensory, reflexes

### Diagnostic Evaluation

**Imaging**
- **Standing lateral radiograph**: Measure slip percentage, slip angle
- **Flexion-extension films**: Assess dynamic instability (>4 mm motion)
- **MRI**: Neural compression, disc degeneration
- **CT**: Pars defect evaluation, surgical planning

### Non-Operative Management

**Conservative Treatment**
- Physical therapy: Core strengthening, avoid hyperextension
- Bracing: For acute spondylolysis in adolescents
- Activity modification
- NSAIDs

**Indications for Surgery**
- Failed conservative management
- Progressive slip
- Neurological deficit
- High-grade slip (Grade III-V) in adolescents

### Surgical Technique

**Decompression Alone**
- Rarely indicated
- Risk of progressive slip

**Decompression and Fusion**
- **Standard**: L4-L5 or L5-S1 (single level)
- **Instrumented fusion**: Pedicle screws and rods
- **Interbody fusion**: TLIF, PLIF, or ALIF
  - Restores disc height, reduces slip, increases fusion rate
- **Reduction**: Controversial, risk of nerve root injury
  - Generally avoid in degenerative, consider in high-grade isthmic

**High-Grade Slips**
- **In situ fusion**: Fusion without reduction (safer)
- **Reduction and fusion**: Higher risk, experienced surgeons only
- L5-S1 or L4-S1 depending on anatomy

### Outcomes

- Fusion rates: 85-95% with instrumentation and interbody fusion
- Symptom improvement: 70-85%
- Return to sports: Variable, depends on activity level

## Cervical Disc Herniation and Radiculopathy

### Anatomy

**Cervical Nerve Roots**
- Exit above corresponding vertebra (C5 exits C4-C5)
- **Unique**: 8 cervical roots, 7 vertebrae

**Common Herniation Levels**
- **C5-C6**: C6 radiculopathy (most common)
- **C6-C7**: C7 radiculopathy (second most common)
- **C4-C5**: C5 radiculopathy

### Clinical Presentation

**Radiculopathy Patterns**
- **C5**: Deltoid weakness, lateral arm pain, no reflex change
- **C6**: Biceps/wrist extensor weakness, lateral forearm pain, decreased brachioradialis reflex
- **C7**: Triceps/wrist flexor weakness, middle finger pain, decreased triceps reflex
- **C8**: Hand intrinsic weakness, medial forearm/hand pain, no reflex change

**Physical Examination**
- **Spurling's test**: Neck extension + rotation + axial compression → radicular pain (specific)
- **Motor examination**: Strength testing
- **Sensory examination**: Dermatomal distribution
- **Reflexes**: Biceps (C5-C6), brachioradialis (C6), triceps (C7)

### Diagnostic Evaluation

**Imaging**
- **MRI cervical spine**: Disc herniation, cord compression
- **Radiographs**: AP/lateral, flexion-extension (instability)
- **CT myelography**: Alternative if MRI contraindicated

**Electrodiagnostics**
- EMG/NCS: Localize level, confirm radiculopathy, rule out peripheral neuropathy

### Non-Operative Management

**Conservative Treatment (6-12 weeks)**
- Soft cervical collar (short-term use only)
- Physical therapy
- NSAIDs, muscle relaxants
- Selective nerve root injections

**Natural History**
- 75-90% improve with conservative management

### Surgical Indications

- Failed conservative management (6-12 weeks)
- Progressive motor deficit
- Cervical myelopathy
- Severe, disabling pain

### Surgical Technique

**Anterior Cervical Discectomy and Fusion (ACDF)**
- **Approach**: Anterior, Smith-Robinson approach (medial to SCM)
- **Technique**:
  - Diskectomy and decompression
  - Osteophyte removal, foraminotomy
  - Interbody spacer (allograft, PEEK cage, autograft)
  - Plate fixation (rigid vs. dynamic)
- **Advantages**: Direct decompression, excellent fusion rates, no posterior muscle disruption
- **Disadvantages**: Dysphagia (10-20%), recurrent laryngeal nerve injury (<1%), adjacent segment disease

**Posterior Cervical Foraminotomy**
- **Approach**: Posterior, laminoforaminotomy
- **Technique**:
  - Remove medial facet and ligamentum flavum
  - Decompress nerve root
  - No fusion required
- **Advantages**: Motion preservation, no dysphagia, no graft needed
- **Disadvantages**: Soft disc only (not hard disc/osteophyte), neck pain, limited access to central disc

**Cervical Disc Arthroplasty (CDA)**
- **Indication**: Single-level soft disc, no facet arthropathy, no instability
- **Advantages**: Motion preservation, lower adjacent segment disease
- **Disadvantages**: Cost, device failure, contraindicated in ossification

### Postoperative Management

**ACDF**
- Collar: 2-6 weeks (surgeon-dependent)
- Fusion assessment: Radiographs at 3, 6, 12 months
- Return to work: 4-6 weeks (sedentary), 3 months (manual labor)
- Driving: When off narcotics and collar removed

**Posterior Foraminotomy**
- No collar required
- Return to work: 2-4 weeks
- Faster recovery than ACDF

### Complications

**ACDF**
- **Dysphagia**: 10-20% transient, 1-2% persistent
- **Recurrent laryngeal nerve injury**: <1% (hoarseness)
- **Horner's syndrome**: Sympathetic chain injury (rare)
- **Vertebral artery injury**: Rare, avoid lateral dissection
- **Pseudarthrosis**: 5-10% single level, higher multilevel
- **Adjacent segment disease**: 2-3% per year

**Posterior Foraminotomy**
- **Neck pain**: Myofascial pain
- **Instability**: Rare if <50% facet preserved
- **C5 palsy**: Transient deltoid weakness (1-5%)

### Outcomes

- Radiculopathy relief: 85-95%
- Fusion rates: 95-98% single level (ACDF)
- Return to work: 80-90%

## Cervical Myelopathy

### Pathophysiology

**Causes**
- Degenerative stenosis (disc, ligamentum flavum, facets)
- Ossification of posterior longitudinal ligament (OPLL)
- Congenital stenosis
- Trauma

**Mechanism of Injury**
- Chronic cord compression
- Ischemia and demyelination
- Gray and white matter damage
- Irreversible if prolonged

### Clinical Presentation

**Symptoms**
- **Hand clumsiness**: Difficulty with fine motor tasks (buttons, writing)
- **Gait instability**: Wide-based, unsteady gait
- **Bowel/bladder dysfunction**: Urgency, retention (late finding)
- Neck pain (variable)

**Physical Examination**
- **Hoffmann's sign**: Flick middle finger DIP → thumb and index flexion (upper motor neuron)
- **Babinski sign**: Plantar stimulation → great toe extension (upper motor neuron)
- **Clonus**: Sustained rhythmic contractions
- **Hyperreflexia**: Brisk reflexes, spread
- **Lhermitte's sign**: Neck flexion → electric shock sensation down spine

**Nurick Grading**
- **0**: Signs only, no symptoms
- **1**: Mild symptoms, normal gait
- **2**: Mild gait abnormality, can work
- **3**: Gait abnormality prevents work
- **4**: Needs assistance to walk
- **5**: Wheelchair-bound or bedridden

### Diagnostic Evaluation

**MRI**
- Cord compression and signal change
- T2 hyperintensity: Edema, myelomalacia, gliosis
- T1 hypointensity: Severe, chronic injury (poor prognosis)

**CT Myelography**
- Alternative if MRI contraindicated
- Assess degree of compression

**Radiographs**
- Alignment, instability
- Developmental stenosis: Canal diameter <13 mm

### Natural History

- Progressive without treatment
- Stepwise deterioration with periods of stability
- Recovery limited if severe or prolonged compression

### Surgical Indications

**Absolute**
- Progressive myelopathy
- Moderate to severe myelopathy

**Relative**
- Mild myelopathy (consider early surgery to prevent progression)

### Surgical Approach Selection

**Anterior Approach**
- **Indications**: 1-3 levels, anterior compression (disc, osteophyte), kyphosis
- **Techniques**: ACDF, corpectomy
- **Advantages**: Direct decompression, kyphosis correction
- **Disadvantages**: Dysphagia, pseudarthrosis (multilevel)

**Posterior Approach**
- **Indications**: >3 levels, posterior compression (ligamentum flavum, OPLL), lordosis
- **Techniques**: Laminectomy, laminoplasty
- **Advantages**: Multilevel decompression, no fusion (laminoplasty)
- **Disadvantages**: Kyphosis risk (laminectomy), C5 palsy, neck pain

**Combined Approach**
- Severe disease, circumferential compression

### Surgical Techniques

**Laminectomy with Fusion**
- Remove lamina and ligamentum flavum
- Lateral mass or pedicle screw fixation
- Prevents kyphosis

**Laminoplasty**
- Hinged expansion of spinal canal
- Preserves posterior elements
- No fusion, motion preserved
- Lower complication rate than laminectomy-fusion

**Anterior Corpectomy and Fusion**
- Remove vertebral body and discs
- Reconstruct with cage and plate
- For severe anterior compression or ossification

### Postoperative Management

- Collar: 6-12 weeks (approach-dependent)
- Early mobilization
- Physical therapy for gait and strength
- Gradual return to activities

### Outcomes

- Improvement: 50-80% (depends on severity, duration)
- Mild myelopathy: Better outcomes
- Severe, chronic myelopathy: Limited recovery
- Stabilization of symptoms (prevent progression) is success

### Complications

- **C5 palsy** (5-10% posterior approach): Transient deltoid weakness, usually recovers
- **Kyphosis**: Laminectomy without fusion, especially in young patients
- **Pseudarthrosis**: Multilevel anterior fusion
- **Infection, hematoma, CSF leak**

## Scoliosis

### Classification

**Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS)**
- Age 10-18 years
- Most common type (80%)
- Female predominance (8:1 for curves >30°)
- Unknown etiology

**Other Types**
- **Congenital**: Vertebral anomalies (hemivertebra, bar)
- **Neuromuscular**: Cerebral palsy, muscular dystrophy
- **Syndromic**: Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos
- **Adult degenerative**: Asymmetric disc degeneration

### Curve Patterns

**Lenke Classification (AIS)**
- Type 1: Main thoracic
- Type 2: Double thoracic
- Type 3: Double major (thoracic + lumbar)
- Type 4: Triple major
- Type 5: Thoracolumbar
- Type 6: Main lumbar

### Clinical Presentation

**Screening**
- **Adams forward bend test**: Rib hump or lumbar prominence
- Shoulder height asymmetry
- Waist asymmetry
- Trunk shift

**Risk Factors for Progression**
- Skeletal immaturity (Risser 0-2)
- Female sex
- Larger curve magnitude
- Thoracic curve pattern
- Premenarchal status

### Diagnostic Evaluation

**Radiographs**
- Standing PA and lateral 36-inch spine films
- **Cobb angle**: Measure curve magnitude
- **Risser sign**: Iliac apophysis ossification (0-5, skeletal maturity)
- **Skeletal maturity**: Triradiate cartilage closure, Risser grade

**MRI Indications**
- Atypical curve (left thoracic, rapidly progressive)
- Neurological signs
- Age <10 years
- Pain (rule out tumor, syrinx)

### Non-Operative Management

**Observation**
- Curves <25° in skeletally immature
- Curves <40-50° in skeletally mature
- Serial radiographs every 4-6 months

**Bracing**
- **Indications**: Skeletally immature, curves 25-40°
- **Types**: TLSO (thoracolumbar sacral orthosis), Boston brace
- **Goal**: Prevent progression, not correction
- **Compliance**: 18-23 hours/day until skeletal maturity
- **Efficacy**: 70-80% prevent progression

### Surgical Indications

**Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis**
- Curves >45-50° (risk of progression even after maturity)
- Progressive curves despite bracing
- Unacceptable deformity

**Adult Degenerative Scoliosis**
- Failed conservative management
- Progressive deformity
- Neurological deficit

### Surgical Technique: Posterior Spinal Fusion

**Approach**
- Midline incision, subperiosteal exposure
- Pedicle screw instrumentation
- Osteotomies (if needed): Smith-Petersen, Ponte, pedicle subtraction
- Deformity correction: Rod contouring, translation, derotation
- Fusion: Autograft (local bone), allograft, bone graft substitutes
- Neuromonitoring: Somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEP), motor evoked potentials (MEP)

**Fusion Levels**
- Selective fusion: Preserve motion segments
- Lenke classification guides fusion levels
- Goal: Balanced, coronal and sagittal alignment

**Correction Techniques**
- Concave compression, convex distraction
- Direct vertebral rotation
- In situ bending

### Postoperative Management

- ICU monitoring (first 24 hours)
- Early mobilization (POD #1-2)
- Brace: Surgeon-dependent (typically not required)
- Return to school: 4-6 weeks
- Return to sports: 6-12 months
- Fusion assessment: Radiographs at intervals

### Complications

**Intraoperative**
- **Neurological injury** (<1%): Cord injury, nerve root injury
  - Prevention: Neuromonitoring, wake-up test
  - Management: Immediate correction reversal, decompression
- **Vascular injury**: Aorta, vena cava (anterior procedures)

**Postoperative**
- **Infection** (2-5%): Superficial or deep, I&D ± implant retention/removal
- **Pseudarthrosis** (5-10%): May require revision fusion
- **Implant failure**: Rod fracture, screw pullout (usually with pseudarthrosis)
- **Proximal junctional kyphosis**: Kyphosis above fusion (10-20%)
- **Neurological deficit**: Delayed (rare)

### Outcomes

- Curve correction: 50-70%
- Fusion rates: 90-95%
- Patient satisfaction: High
- Complications: 10-20% overall
- Revision rate: 5-10%

## Key Points

1. **Lumbar disc herniation** typically improves with conservative management (80-90%); surgery indicated for cauda equina syndrome (emergency), progressive motor deficit, or failed conservative treatment (6-12 weeks).

2. **Microdiscectomy** is the gold standard for symptomatic disc herniation; minimally invasive approaches offer similar outcomes with less tissue disruption.

3. **Lumbar spinal stenosis** presents with neurogenic claudication (leg pain with walking, improved with sitting/forward flexion); decompressive laminectomy provides good outcomes (70-80%).

4. **Fusion is indicated** with stenosis when there is spondylolisthesis, scoliosis, instability, or iatrogenic instability from extensive facetectomy (>50%).

5. **Spondylolisthesis** is classified by etiology (Wiltse) and severity (Meyerding); degenerative type most common at L4-L5, isthmic type at L5-S1.

6. **Cervical disc herniation** most commonly affects C5-C6 (C6 radiculopathy) and C6-C7 (C7 radiculopathy); 75-90% improve with conservative management.

7. **ACDF** is the gold standard for cervical disc herniation requiring surgery; posterior foraminotomy is an alternative for soft lateral disc herniations in young patients.

8. **Cervical myelopathy** is a progressive condition requiring surgical decompression; severity and duration of symptoms affect recovery potential.

9. **Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis** curves >45-50° require surgery due to risk of progression; bracing (18-23 hours/day) prevents progression in skeletally immature patients with curves 25-40°.

10. **Neuromonitoring** (SSEP, MEP) is essential during spine surgery to detect and prevent neurological injury; changes require immediate attention and possible intervention reversal.
