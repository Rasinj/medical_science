# Fractures

## Overview

Fracture management is fundamental to orthopedic surgery. Understanding fracture patterns, biomechanics, and treatment options is essential for optimal outcomes. Treatment goals include anatomic reduction, stable fixation, preservation of blood supply, and early mobilization to restore function.

## Fracture Classification

### Descriptive Classification

**Location**
- **Epiphyseal**: Through growth plate (pediatric)
- **Metaphyseal**: Flared region between shaft and epiphysis
- **Diaphyseal**: Shaft/mid-portion of long bone

**Pattern**
- **Transverse**: Perpendicular to long axis
- **Oblique**: Angled across bone
- **Spiral**: Twisting injury, circumferential
- **Comminuted**: Multiple fragments (>2)
- **Segmental**: Bone fractured in two separate locations
- **Impacted**: Bone ends driven into each other

**Displacement**
- **Non-displaced**: <1 mm separation, alignment maintained
- **Displaced**: Fragment separation, loss of alignment
- **Angulation**: Degrees of angular deformity
- **Rotation**: Rotational deformity
- **Shortening**: Overlap of fragments

**Open vs. Closed**
- **Closed**: Skin intact
- **Open** (Compound): Communication with external environment

### Gustilo-Anderson Classification (Open Fractures)

**Type I**
- Clean wound <1 cm
- Low-energy injury
- Minimal soft tissue damage

**Type II**
- Wound 1-10 cm
- Moderate soft tissue damage
- No extensive stripping

**Type IIIA**
- Wound >10 cm or high-energy injury
- Adequate soft tissue coverage despite extensive laceration
- Includes segmental/comminuted fractures

**Type IIIB**
- Extensive soft tissue loss
- Periosteal stripping
- Requires flap coverage

**Type IIIC**
- Vascular injury requiring repair

### AO/OTA Classification

**Alphanumeric System**
- **Bone**: 1=Humerus, 2=Radius/Ulna, 3=Femur, 4=Tibia/Fibula
- **Segment**: 1=Proximal, 2=Diaphysis, 3=Distal
- **Type**: A=Simple, B=Wedge, C=Complex
- **Group and Subgroup**: Further detail

**Example**: 42-A3 = Distal femur, simple, specific pattern

### Salter-Harris Classification (Pediatric Physeal Fractures)

**Type I**: Through physis only (SLIP)
**Type II**: Through physis and metaphysis (ABOVE) - Most common
**Type III**: Through physis and epiphysis (LOWER)
**Type IV**: Through metaphysis, physis, and epiphysis (THROUGH)
**Type V**: Crush injury to physis (CRUSH/RAMMED)

**Mnemonic**: SALTR (Slip, Above, Lower, Through, Rammed)

**Prognosis**: Types I-II: Good; Types III-V: Risk of growth arrest

## Indications for Operative vs. Non-Operative Management

### Non-Operative Management Indications

**Criteria**
- Acceptable alignment and minimal displacement
- Stable fracture pattern
- Intact soft tissue envelope
- Patient compliance anticipated
- Medical contraindications to surgery

**Methods**
- **Casting/Splinting**: Immobilization in acceptable position
- **Functional Bracing**: Allows some motion while maintaining alignment
- **Traction**: Rarely used, except in specific pediatric fractures

**Common Fractures Treated Non-Operatively**
- Non-displaced or minimally displaced fractures
- Pediatric buckle/torus fractures
- Clavicle fractures (most)
- Rib fractures
- Stable compression fractures of spine

### Operative Management Indications

**Absolute Indications**
- **Open Fractures**: Require irrigation, debridement, stabilization
- **Vascular Injury**: Requires stabilization for vascular repair
- **Compartment Syndrome**: Fasciotomy + stabilization
- **Intra-articular Displacement >2mm**: Restore joint congruity
- **Floating Knee**: Ipsilateral femur and tibia fractures

**Relative Indications**
- **Polytrauma**: Facilitate mobilization, pulmonary toilet
- **Shortening**: Unacceptable overlap
- **Angulation**: Beyond acceptable limits for age/location
- **Failed Closed Reduction**: Cannot achieve/maintain alignment
- **Pathologic Fracture**: Underlying bone lesion
- **Non-Union**: Failed healing with conservative treatment

## Preoperative Assessment

### Clinical Evaluation

**History**
- Mechanism of injury (high vs. low energy)
- Time since injury
- Previous injuries/surgeries to affected limb
- Comorbidities (diabetes, smoking, osteoporosis)
- Functional status before injury

**Physical Examination**
- **Inspection**: Deformity, swelling, ecchymosis, wounds
- **Palpation**: Tenderness, crepitus, abnormal mobility
- **Neurovascular Assessment** (CRITICAL):
  - **Pulses**: Distal to fracture (palpate, Doppler if needed)
  - **Capillary Refill**: <2 seconds normal
  - **Motor Function**: Test distal muscles
  - **Sensation**: Test dermatomes distal to injury
  - **Document**: Before and after any manipulation

**Compartment Assessment**
- **Pain out of Proportion**: Classic sign
- **Pain with Passive Stretch**: Most sensitive early sign
- **5 P's**: Pain, Pressure, Paresthesias, Pallor, Pulselessness (late)
- **Compartment Pressure Measurement**: If concern (>30 mmHg or within 30 mmHg of diastolic pressure)

### Imaging Studies

**Plain Radiographs** (Standard)
- **Two Views Minimum**: AP and lateral (orthogonal)
- **Include Joints Above and Below**: Assess for associated injuries
- **Contralateral Views**: Comparison in complex cases (pediatrics)

**CT Scan**
- **Intra-articular Fractures**: Assess displacement, comminution, fragment position
- **Complex Fractures**: Pelvis, acetabulum, tibial plateau, pilon
- **Preoperative Planning**: 3D reconstructions

**MRI**
- **Occult Fractures**: Not visible on X-ray (e.g., hip, scaphoid)
- **Soft Tissue Injury**: Ligaments, tendons, menisci
- **Pathologic Fractures**: Underlying lesion characterization

**Vascular Studies**
- **Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI)**: <0.9 suggests vascular injury
- **CT Angiography**: If vascular injury suspected, preoperative planning
- **Arteriography**: Diagnostic and therapeutic (rare)

### Laboratory Studies
- **Complete Blood Count**: Baseline hemoglobin
- **Type and Screen/Cross-Match**: Anticipated blood loss
- **Coagulation Studies**: If anticoagulated or bleeding disorder
- **Metabolic Panel**: Renal function (contrast studies)

## Surgical Techniques

### Open Reduction Internal Fixation (ORIF)

#### Principles

**Reduction**
- **Anatomic**: Restore normal alignment (especially intra-articular)
- **Relative Stability**: Acceptable alignment for fracture healing (some motion)

**Fixation**
- **Absolute Stability**: Rigid fixation, no motion (lag screw, compression plate)
- **Relative Stability**: Some motion allowed (bridge plating, IM nail)

**Soft Tissue Preservation**
- **Minimize Stripping**: Preserve blood supply
- **Indirect Reduction**: Avoid extensive exposure

**Implant Selection**
- Appropriate strength and biomechanics for fracture and patient

#### Surgical Approaches

**Upper Extremity Examples**
- **Proximal Humerus**: Deltopectoral approach
- **Humeral Shaft**: Anterolateral or posterior approach
- **Distal Radius**: Volar (Henry) approach
- **Olecranon**: Direct posterior approach

**Lower Extremity Examples**
- **Femoral Neck/Intertrochanteric**: Lateral or anterolateral
- **Femoral Shaft**: Lateral for plating
- **Tibial Plateau**: Anterolateral, posteromedial
- **Ankle**: Anterolateral, posteromedial, posterior

#### Implant Types

**Screws**
- **Lag Screw Principle**: Compression across fracture
- **Types**: Cortical, cancellous, cannulated
- **Uses**: Interfragmentary compression, plate fixation

**Plates**
- **Compression Plate**: Dynamic compression via screw design
- **Neutralization Plate**: Protects lag screws, prevents rotation/shear
- **Bridge Plate**: Spans comminution, maintains length/alignment
- **Locking Plate**: Fixed-angle construct, acts as internal fixator
  - **Advantages**: Less soft tissue stripping, better in osteoporotic bone

**Intramedullary Nails**
- **Load-Sharing Device**: Central location in medullary canal
- **Interlocking Screws**: Proximal and distal (prevent rotation, shortening)
- **Reaming**: Controversial (destroys endosteal blood supply but allows larger nail)
- **Indications**: Femoral shaft, tibial shaft, humerus

**Operative Steps (Example: Distal Radius ORIF)**
1. **Positioning**: Supine, arm on hand table
2. **Approach**: Volar (Henry) approach between FCR and radial artery
3. **Exposure**: Retract FPL laterally, expose fracture
4. **Reduction**: Restore radial height, inclination, volar tilt
5. **Provisional Fixation**: K-wires
6. **Plate Application**: Volar locking plate
7. **Screw Insertion**: Proximal cortical or locking, distal locking (subchondral support)
8. **Fluoroscopy**: Confirm reduction and hardware position
9. **Closure**: Layered, splint

### Intramedullary Nailing

**Indications**
- Femoral shaft fractures
- Tibial shaft fractures
- Selected humerus, forearm fractures

**Advantages**
- Minimally invasive
- Load-sharing (vs. plates which are load-bearing laterally)
- Early weight-bearing
- Preserves fracture hematoma

**Technique (Example: Femoral Shaft)**
1. **Entry Point**: Piriformis fossa or greater trochanter (antegrade) vs. supracondylar (retrograde)
2. **Guide Wire**: Across fracture into distal fragment
3. **Reaming**: Sequential reamers (1-2 mm larger than planned nail)
4. **Nail Insertion**: Over guide wire, ensure nail crosses fracture
5. **Proximal Interlocking**: Screws through jig
6. **Distal Interlocking**: Screws via freehand or electromagnetic guidance
7. **Final Images**: Confirm reduction, nail position, screw placement

**Complications**
- **Malreduction**: Rotational, angular
- **Iatrogenic Fracture**: Comminution during insertion
- **Nail Too Short/Long**: Inadequate fixation or impingement

### External Fixation

**Indications**
- **Damage Control**: Polytrauma, hemodynamic instability
- **Severe Soft Tissue Injury**: Open fractures (Gustilo IIIB/IIIC)
- **Pelvic Fractures**: Emergent stabilization
- **Definitive Treatment**: Selected cases (distal radius in elderly)

**Principles**
- **Pin Placement**: Safe zones (avoid neurovascular structures)
- **Frame Configuration**: Unilateral, bilateral, circular (Ilizarov)
- **Spanning vs. Periarticular**: Bridge joint vs. directly at fracture

**Technique (Example: Tibia)**
1. **Proximal Pins**: Anteromedial tibia (safe zone)
2. **Distal Pins**: Anterior tibia or medial malleolus
3. **Frame Assembly**: Connect pins to bars
4. **Reduction**: Manipulate and tighten frame
5. **Final Adjustment**: Confirm alignment radiographically

**Complications**
- **Pin Tract Infection**: Most common (10-30%)
- **Loss of Reduction**: Inadequate fixation
- **Neurovascular Injury**: Poor pin placement
- **Joint Stiffness**: If crosses joint

### Percutaneous/Minimally Invasive Techniques

**Indications**
- Fractures amenable to closed or limited open reduction
- Preserve soft tissue envelope and blood supply

**Examples**
- **Percutaneous Pinning**: Pediatric supracondylar humerus, distal radius, scaphoid
- **MIPO (Minimally Invasive Plate Osteosynthesis)**: Humeral shaft, tibial shaft
- **Balloon Kyphoplasty/Vertebroplasty**: Compression fractures

## Postoperative Management

### Immediate Postoperative Care

**Immobilization**
- **Splint vs. Cast**: Splint initially (allows for swelling)
- **Convert to Cast**: 7-14 days when swelling subsides
- **Duration**: Depends on fracture location and fixation stability

**Weight-Bearing Status**
- **Non-Weight-Bearing (NWB)**: No weight on affected limb
- **Touch-Down Weight-Bearing (TDWB)**: Foot contact for balance only
- **Partial Weight-Bearing (PWB)**: 25-50% body weight
- **Weight-Bearing as Tolerated (WBAT)**: Progress as comfortable
- **Full Weight-Bearing (FWB)**: No restrictions

**Pain Management**
- Multimodal analgesia (NSAIDs, acetaminophen, regional blocks)
- Minimize opioids (risk of dependence, constipation)
- Ice, elevation

**Neurovascular Monitoring**
- Frequent checks first 24-72 hours
- Watch for compartment syndrome (especially after reduction/fixation)

### Follow-up and Rehabilitation

**Radiographic Follow-up**
- **Initial**: 7-14 days (assess reduction maintenance)
- **Serial**: Every 4-6 weeks until healed
- **Union Assessment**: Bridging callus, trabeculae crossing fracture site

**Physical Therapy**
- **Early Range of Motion**: Prevent stiffness (when fixation allows)
- **Strengthening**: Progress as healing occurs
- **Functional Training**: Return to activities of daily living

**Return to Activity**
- **Light Activities**: 6-12 weeks (most fractures)
- **Full Activity/Sports**: 3-6 months (depends on fracture, healing, patient)

### Hardware Removal

**Indications**
- **Symptomatic**: Pain, irritation, prominence
- **Pediatric**: Risk of physeal injury from crossing screws/plates
- **Infection**: Remove hardware after fracture healed

**Timing**
- **Minimum**: 12-18 months (allow full remodeling)
- **Risk**: Re-fracture if removed too early (3-5% risk)

## Complications

### Immediate/Early Complications (0-6 weeks)

**Compartment Syndrome**
- **Incidence**: 1-10% (varies by fracture type)
- **Pathophysiology**: Increased pressure in closed fascial compartment → ischemia
- **High-Risk Fractures**: Tibial shaft, forearm, supracondylar humerus
- **Presentation**:
  - **Pain**: Out of proportion, worse with passive stretch (earliest, most sensitive)
  - **Pressure**: Tense, swollen compartment
  - **Paresthesias**: Nerve ischemia
  - **Pallor, Pulselessness, Paralysis**: LATE findings (irreversible damage)
- **Diagnosis**:
  - **Clinical**: High index of suspicion
  - **Compartment Pressure**: >30 mmHg or delta pressure <30 mmHg (diastolic BP - compartment pressure)
- **Management**:
  - **Emergent Fasciotomy**: Four-compartment (leg) or volar/dorsal (forearm)
  - **Delayed Closure**: 3-7 days, skin graft if needed
  - **Complications of Delay**: Volkmann contracture, rhabdomyolysis, renal failure

**Vascular Injury**
- **High-Risk Fractures**: Supracondylar humerus, knee dislocation, tibial plateau
- **Assessment**: Pulses, ABI, CTA if indicated
- **Management**: Orthopedic stabilization + vascular repair (within 6-8 hours for limb salvage)

**Nerve Injury**
- **Radial Nerve**: Humeral shaft fractures (Holstein-Lewis)
- **Peroneal Nerve**: Proximal fibula, knee dislocation
- **Ulnar Nerve**: Medial epicondyle fractures
- **Types**:
  - **Neurapraxia** (Most common): Temporary, resolves 6-12 weeks
  - **Axonotmesis**: Axon damage, recovery months to year
  - **Neurotmesis**: Complete transection, requires repair
- **Management**: Observation (most), surgical exploration if open injury or penetrating trauma

**Fat Embolism Syndrome**
- **Incidence**: 1-3% (long bone fractures, especially femur)
- **Presentation**: Hypoxia, confusion, petechial rash (24-72 hours post-injury)
- **Management**: Supportive, early fixation of fractures reduces risk

**Thromboembolic Disease**
- **Risk**: Varies by fracture (hip, pelvis, multiple fractures highest)
- **Prevention**: Mechanical (SCDs), chemical (LMWH, factor Xa inhibitors)
- **Treatment**: Anticoagulation if DVT/PE diagnosed

### Delayed Complications (6 weeks - 6 months)

**Malunion**
- **Definition**: Fracture healed in unacceptable position (angulation, rotation, shortening)
- **Consequences**: Deformity, pain, arthritis, functional limitation
- **Prevention**: Adequate reduction and fixation
- **Treatment**: Osteotomy and re-fixation if significant functional impairment

**Delayed Union**
- **Definition**: Slower healing than expected (no specific timeline)
- **Risk Factors**: Smoking, NSAIDs, infection, poor blood supply, inadequate stability
- **Treatment**: Continue protected weight-bearing, bone stimulator, consider bone graft

**Nonunion**
- **Definition**: Failure of fracture to heal (no progression in 3-6 months)
- **Types**:
  - **Hypertrophic** (Elephant foot): Abundant callus, inadequate stability → improve fixation
  - **Atrophic**: Minimal callus, poor biology → bone graft, improve blood supply
- **Risk Factors**: Smoking (most important modifiable), infection, gap, poor fixation, avascular bone
- **Treatment**:
  - **Hypertrophic**: Improved stability (compression plate, IM nail)
  - **Atrophic**: Debridement, bone graft (iliac crest), plate or nail fixation, BMP (off-label)

**Infection**
- **Superficial**: 1-5% (wound infection)
- **Deep/Osteomyelitis**: <2% (higher in open fractures)
- **Presentation**: Fever, wound drainage, pain, elevated inflammatory markers
- **Diagnosis**: Wound culture, ESR/CRP, bone biopsy/culture
- **Treatment**:
  - **Acute**: Irrigation/debridement, culture-directed antibiotics (6-12 weeks)
  - **Chronic**: Debridement, remove hardware if possible after union, long-term antibiotics

### Late Complications (>6 months)

**Post-Traumatic Arthritis**
- **Incidence**: 30-50% (intra-articular fractures)
- **Risk Factors**: Residual step-off >2 mm, cartilage damage
- **Presentation**: Pain, stiffness, crepitus months to years later
- **Treatment**: NSAIDs, injections, activity modification, eventual arthroplasty

**Avascular Necrosis (AVN)**
- **High-Risk Fractures**: Femoral neck, scaphoid, talus (precarious blood supply)
- **Presentation**: Pain, collapse months to years after fracture
- **Diagnosis**: MRI (most sensitive), X-ray (late changes)
- **Treatment**: Core decompression (early), osteotomy, arthroplasty (late)

**Heterotopic Ossification (HO)**
- **Definition**: Bone formation in soft tissues
- **High-Risk**: Hip dislocation, acetabular fractures, elbow injuries
- **Prevention**: Indomethacin or radiation (limited)
- **Treatment**: Excision after maturation (12-18 months) if symptomatic

**Complex Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS)**
- **Incidence**: 1-2%
- **Presentation**: Severe pain, swelling, color/temperature changes, allodynia
- **Diagnosis**: Clinical (Budapest criteria)
- **Treatment**: Physical therapy (mainstay), medications (gabapentin, bisphosphonates), sympathetic nerve block

## Outcomes

### Fracture Healing Timeline (Approximate)

**Upper Extremity**
- **Clavicle**: 6-8 weeks
- **Humerus**: 8-12 weeks
- **Forearm**: 8-12 weeks
- **Distal Radius**: 6-8 weeks

**Lower Extremity**
- **Femoral Neck**: 12-16 weeks
- **Femoral Shaft**: 12-16 weeks
- **Tibial Shaft**: 12-20 weeks (slower than femur)
- **Ankle**: 6-12 weeks

**Factors Affecting Healing**
- **Bone**: Cancellous heals faster than cortical
- **Age**: Children heal faster than adults
- **Location**: Good blood supply (femur) heals faster than poor (scaphoid)
- **Fixation**: Stable fixation promotes healing

### Functional Outcomes

**Return to Work**
- **Sedentary**: 6-12 weeks
- **Manual Labor**: 3-6 months
- **Overhead/Heavy**: 4-8 months

**Return to Sports**
- **Low Impact**: 3-4 months
- **High Impact/Contact**: 6-9 months
- **Criteria**: Radiographic union, pain-free ROM, strength >80% contralateral

### Success Rates

**Union Rates** (With Appropriate Treatment)
- **Most Fractures**: 95-98%
- **Femoral Neck (Young)**: 95%
- **Scaphoid**: 90-95% (85% displaced if ORIF)
- **Tibia**: 90-95%

## Key Points

- **Open fractures** are orthopedic emergencies requiring urgent irrigation, debridement, and antibiotics within 6-8 hours
- **Compartment syndrome** is a clinical diagnosis; pain with passive stretch is earliest sign; emergent fasciotomy is treatment
- **Neurovascular assessment** before and after reduction/manipulation is mandatory and must be documented
- **Absolute indications for surgery**: Open fractures, vascular injury, irreducible fractures, intra-articular displacement >2 mm
- **Intramedullary nailing** is standard for femoral and tibial shaft fractures (load-sharing, early weight-bearing)
- **External fixation** is ideal for damage control and severe soft tissue injury
- **Nonunion risk factors**: Smoking (most important modifiable), infection, inadequate stability, poor blood supply
- **Hypertrophic nonunion** needs better stability; **atrophic nonunion** needs bone graft
- **Post-traumatic arthritis** occurs in 30-50% of intra-articular fractures despite anatomic reduction
