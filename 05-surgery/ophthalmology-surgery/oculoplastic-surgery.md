# Oculoplastic and Orbital Surgery

## Overview

Oculoplastic surgery addresses functional and cosmetic disorders of the eyelids, lacrimal system, and orbit. This subspecialty combines ophthalmology and plastic surgery principles to manage conditions affecting periocular structures. Common procedures include ptosis repair, entropion/ectropion correction, blepharoplasty, orbital decompression, enucleation/evisceration, and dacryocystorhinostomy. Successful outcomes require detailed anatomic knowledge, precise surgical technique, and appreciation for both functional and aesthetic considerations.

## Eyelid Anatomy

### Layers (Anterior to Posterior)

**1. Skin**: Thinnest in body (0.5mm), minimal subcutaneous fat

**2. Orbicularis Oculi Muscle**:
- **Orbital portion**: Sphincter around orbit (forceful closure)
- **Preseptal portion**: Over tarsal plates
- **Pretarsal portion**: Over tarsus
- **Innervation**: Facial nerve (CN VII) - temporal and zygomatic branches

**3. Orbital Septum**:
- Fibrous membrane from orbital rim to tarsal plates
- Separates preseptal from postseptal (orbital) spaces
- **Importance**: Barrier to infection spread (preseptal vs orbital cellulitis)

**4. Orbital Fat**:
- **Upper lid**: Two fat pads (nasal, central)
- **Lower lid**: Three fat pads (nasal, central, lateral)
- Prolapse causes "bags" (dermatochalasis)

**5. Levator Palpebrae Superioris** (upper lid only):
- **Function**: Primary upper lid elevator
- **Origin**: Lesser wing of sphenoid (orbital apex)
- **Insertion**: Anterior tarsal surface, skin (creates upper lid crease)
- **Innervation**: Oculomotor nerve (CN III) - superior division

**Müller's Muscle**:
- Sympathetically innervated (contributes 2mm lid elevation)
- Originates from levator, inserts on superior tarsal border

**6. Tarsal Plates**:
- **Upper tarsus**: 10-12mm height
- **Lower tarsus**: 4-5mm height
- Dense connective tissue, contains meibomian glands
- Structural support for lid

**7. Conjunctiva**: Mucous membrane lining posterior lid surface

### Eyelid Margin

**Gray Line**: Junction between anterior (skin, muscle) and posterior (tarsus, conjunctiva) lamellae
**Lash Line**: Eyelashes emerge anterior to gray line
**Meibomian Gland Orifices**: Posterior to gray line (30-40 glands)

### Lower Lid Retractors

**Capsulopalpebral Fascia**: Analogous to levator (lower lid)
**Inferior Tarsal Muscle**: Analogous to Müller's muscle

### Canthal Tendons

**Medial Canthal Tendon**: Attaches tarsal plates to maxillary bone (anterior and posterior limbs)
**Lateral Canthal Tendon**: Attaches to Whitnall's tubercle on lateral orbital wall

## Lacrimal Anatomy

### Lacrimal Gland

- **Location**: Superotemporal orbit (lacrimal fossa)
- **Function**: Tear production (basal and reflex tearing)
- **Innervation**: Parasympathetic (facial nerve via greater superficial petrosal nerve)

### Lacrimal Drainage System

**Puncta**: Openings on medial upper and lower lid margins (1.5mm from medial canthus)
**Canaliculi**: Channels from puncta to common canaliculus (8-10mm length)
**Common Canaliculus**: Confluence, enters lacrimal sac
**Lacrimal Sac**: Dilated portion in lacrimal fossa (medial orbital wall)
**Nasolacrimal Duct**: Drains sac to inferior meatus of nose (under inferior turbinate)

**Valve of Hasner**: At nasolacrimal duct opening (prevents reflux)

## Ptosis

### Definition

Abnormally low position of upper eyelid (droopy lid)

### Classification by Etiology

**Aponeurotic** (most common in adults):
- **Cause**: Levator aponeurosis dehiscence/stretching (age, trauma, chronic eye rubbing, contact lens wear)
- **Features**: Good levator function, high or absent lid crease, thinned upper lid

**Myogenic**:
- **Congenital**: Levator muscle dysgenesis (poor levator function)
- **Acquired**: Myasthenia gravis, chronic progressive external ophthalmoplegia (CPEO), oculopharyngeal muscular dystrophy

**Neurogenic**:
- **CN III palsy**: Poor levator function, dilated pupil (if complete), ophthalmoplegia
- **Horner's syndrome**: Sympathetic denervation, mild ptosis (2mm), miosis, anhidrosis

**Mechanical**: Mass effect (tumor, edema, dermatochalasis)

**Traumatic**: Levator laceration, CN III injury

### Measurements

**Margin-Reflex Distance 1 (MRD1)**:
- Distance from upper lid margin to corneal light reflex (pupil center)
- **Normal**: 4-5mm
- **Ptosis**: <3mm (mild), <2mm (moderate), <1mm or negative (severe)

**Levator Function**:
- Excursion of lid from downgaze to upgaze (measure with frontalis blocked)
- **Normal**: ≥15mm
- **Good**: 10-14mm
- **Fair**: 5-9mm
- **Poor**: <5mm

**Marginal Reflex Distance 2 (MRD2)**: Lower lid margin to reflex (assess lower lid retraction)

**Palpebral Fissure Height**: Distance from upper to lower lid margin (normal 9-11mm)

### Surgical Options

**Levator Advancement** (External Approach):
- **Indications**: Aponeurotic ptosis, good levator function (>10mm)
- **Technique**:
  1. Eyelid crease incision
  2. Dissect to identify levator aponeurosis
  3. Disinser from tarsus, advance, reattach to anterior tarsus
  4. Adjust height intraoperatively (patient seated, assess symmetry)
  5. Re-form lid crease
- **Advantages**: Precise, good long-term results
- **Disadvantages**: External scar (hidden in crease)

**Internal Levator Advancement** (Müllerectomy):
- **Indications**: Mild ptosis (1-2mm), good levator function, positive phenylephrine test (lid elevates 1-2mm with topical phenylephrine)
- **Technique**: Conjunctival approach, resect Müller's muscle and conjunctiva
- **Advantages**: No external scar
- **Disadvantages**: Limited correction (1-3mm)

**Frontalis Sling**:
- **Indications**: Poor levator function (<5mm), congenital ptosis
- **Technique**: Suspend lid to frontalis muscle (using silicone rod, autogenous fascia lata, or allograft)
- **Mechanism**: Patient uses frontalis (forehead) to elevate lid
- **Disadvantages**: Lagophthalmos (incomplete closure) - corneal exposure risk, need for lubrication

### Complications

- **Undercorrection/overcorrection**: Reoperation may be needed
- **Asymmetry**: Especially if bilateral surgery
- **Lagophthalmos**: Incomplete closure (exposure keratopathy)
- **Lid crease abnormality**: Too high, absent, asymmetric
- **Hemorrhage**, **infection** (rare)

### Outcomes

- **Success**: 85-95% achieve satisfactory height and symmetry
- **Reoperation rate**: 5-15%

## Entropion

### Definition

Inward rotation of eyelid margin (lashes rub cornea - trichiasis)

### Classification

**Involutional** (most common):
- **Causes**: Horizontal lid laxity, lower lid retractor dehiscence, overriding orbicularis
- **Age-related**: Elderly patients

**Cicatricial**:
- **Cause**: Scarring of palpebral conjunctiva (trachoma, ocular cicatricial pemphigoid, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, chemical injury)
- Posterior lamella contracts, pulls lid inward

**Congenital**: Rare

**Acute Spastic**: Blepharospasm, ocular inflammation (transient)

### Symptoms

- Ocular irritation, foreign body sensation
- **Corneal abrasion**: From inturned lashes
- Tearing, redness

### Surgical Treatment

**Involutional Entropion**:
- **Lateral tarsal strip**: Shorten and reattach lateral lower lid to periosteum (addresses horizontal laxity)
- **Lower lid retractor reinsertion**: Reattach retractors to tarsus
- **Everting sutures**: Horizontal mattress sutures to evert lid (adjunct)

**Cicatricial Entropion**:
- **Posterior lamellar graft**: Mucous membrane (oral, nasal) or hard palate graft to lengthen posterior lamella
- **Tarsal fracture**: Break tarsus to allow eversion (if mild)

### Complications

- **Recurrence**: 5-10%
- **Ectropion**: Overcorrection
- **Lid retraction**, **asymmetry**

## Ectropion

### Definition

Outward rotation of eyelid margin (everted lid)

### Classification

**Involutional** (most common):
- Horizontal lid laxity (medial/lateral canthal tendon laxity)
- Lower lid retractor dehiscence

**Cicatricial**:
- Scarring/contracture of anterior lamella (skin, muscle)
- **Causes**: Trauma, burns, prior surgery, chronic sun damage

**Paralytic**:
- Facial nerve (CN VII) palsy
- Orbicularis paralysis → lid sags, no blink → exposure

**Mechanical**: Tumor, edema

**Congenital**: Rare

### Symptoms

- **Epiphora** (tearing): Punctum everted away from globe (tears don't drain)
- **Exposure keratopathy**: Corneal dryness, abrasion (if severe)
- **Cosmesis**

### Surgical Treatment

**Involutional Ectropion**:
- **Lateral tarsal strip**: Tighten horizontal lid, most common procedure
  - Technique: Shorten lateral lower lid, reattach to lateral orbital rim periosteum
- **Medial spindle**: If medial laxity (excise horizontal ellipse of lid, tighten)

**Cicatricial Ectropion**:
- **Skin graft**: Full-thickness skin graft (FTSG) to lengthen anterior lamella
- **Z-plasty**, **V-Y advancement**: For localized scarring

**Paralytic Ectropion**:
- **Temporary**: Taping, moisture chamber, lubrication
- **Permanent**: Gold weight implant in upper lid (gravity closes lid), lateral tarsal strip (lower lid)

### Complications

- **Recurrence**: 5-10%
- **Entropion**: Overcorrection
- **Eyelid retraction**, **notching**

## Blepharoplasty

### Indications

**Upper Lid**:
- **Dermatochalasis**: Excess skin (cosmetic or visual field obstruction)
- **Pseudoptosis**: Appearance of ptosis from overhanging skin

**Lower Lid**:
- **Dermatochalasis**, **fat prolapse** ("bags")
- **Tear trough deformity**: Junction of lid-cheek

**Functional**: Visual field obstruction (insurance covered if documented)

### Surgical Technique

**Upper Blepharoplasty**:
1. Mark excess skin (pinch test - ensure no lagophthalmos post-op)
2. Eyelid crease incision
3. Excise skin (± orbicularis muscle)
4. **Fat removal**: Conservative (nasal and central fat pads) - avoid over-resection (hollowing)
5. Hemostasis
6. Close skin

**Lower Blepharoplasty**:
- **Transcutaneous**: Skin incision below lash line, excise skin/fat
- **Transconjunctival**: Conjunctival incision (inside lid), remove fat, no skin excision (younger patients, no excess skin)
  - Advantage: No external scar
- **Fat repositioning**: Mobilize fat to fill tear trough (instead of excision)

### Complications

- **Hemorrhage**: Retrobulbar hemorrhage (RARE but vision-threatening - emergent lateral canthotomy/cantholysis)
- **Lagophthalmos**: Excessive skin removal
- **Ectropion**: Lower lid laxity, scar contracture
- **Hollowing**: Over-resection of fat ("sunken" appearance)
- **Asymmetry**, **unsatisfactory cosmesis**
- **Dry eyes**: Exacerbation (lacrimal gland manipulation)

### Outcomes

- **High patient satisfaction**: 85-95%
- **Revision rate**: 5-10%

## Dacryocystorhinostomy (DCR)

### Indications

- **Nasolacrimal duct obstruction** (NLDO): Chronic epiphora
- **Dacryocystitis**: Recurrent infections of lacrimal sac

### Pathophysiology

NLDO causes tear stasis in sac → overflow tearing (epiphora) → infection risk (dacryocystitis)

### Diagnosis

**Symptoms**: Tearing, discharge, recurrent infections
**Dye disappearance test**: Fluorescein instilled, delayed disappearance (>5 minutes)
**Irrigation**: Attempt to irrigate canaliculi, regurgitation from opposite punctum (obstruction below common canaliculus)
**Dacryocystography** (DCG): Imaging with contrast (rarely needed)

### Surgical Options

**External DCR**:
- **Principle**: Create bony ostium between lacrimal sac and nasal cavity (bypass nasolacrimal duct obstruction)
- **Technique**:
  1. Skin incision medial to medial canthus (10-12mm)
  2. Dissect to lacrimal fossa
  3. **Osteotomy**: Remove bone to create opening into nose
  4. Open lacrimal sac and nasal mucosa
  5. **Anastomose**: Suture sac and nasal mucosa (create epithelialized fistula)
  6. **Stent**: Silicone tube through canaliculi and ostium into nose (removed 6-12 weeks)
- **Success**: 85-95%

**Endoscopic DCR**:
- **Approach**: Endonasal (through nose), endoscopic visualization
- **Advantages**: No external scar
- **Disadvantages**: Technically challenging, lower success (80-90%)
- Gaining popularity with experience

### Postoperative Care

- Nasal decongestants, saline spray
- Avoid nose-blowing (2 weeks)
- Antibiotics (1 week)
- Stent removal: 6-12 weeks

### Complications

- **Failure**: 5-15% (scar closure of ostium)
  - Revision DCR or Jones tube (conjunctivodacryocystorhinostomy)
- **Hemorrhage**: Intraoperative/postoperative
- **Infection**, **granulation tissue**
- **Canalicular laceration** (during stent placement)

## Orbital Decompression

### Indications

- **Graves' ophthalmopathy (thyroid eye disease)**: Compressive optic neuropathy, severe proptosis, exposure keratopathy
- **Orbital tumors**: Decompression for optic nerve compression
- **Orbital fractures**: Decompression of entrapped tissues

### Graves' Ophthalmopathy

**Pathophysiology**: Autoimmune inflammation of extraocular muscles and orbital fat → enlargement → proptosis, restrictive strabismus, optic neuropathy (crowded apex)

**Clinical Phases**:
- **Active (inflammatory)**: Eyelid retraction, proptosis, diplopia, optic neuropathy
- **Inactive (fibrotic)**: Stable, fibrosed muscles

**Medical Management**: Corticosteroids, radiation, immunosuppression (active phase)

**Surgical Indications**:
- **Urgent**: Dysthyroid optic neuropathy (compressive, vision-threatening)
- **Elective**: Severe proptosis, exposure keratopathy, cosmesis

### Surgical Technique

**Principle**: Remove orbital bone walls (expand orbital volume, allow fat/muscle herniation) → reduce proptosis

**Approaches**:
- **Medial wall**: Ethmoid bone removal (endoscopic or external)
- **Floor**: Maxillary bone removal
- **Lateral wall**: Lateral orbital rim and wall
- **Combination**: Two or three walls (balanced decompression)

**Most Common**: **Balanced medial wall + floor decompression** (endoscopic approach)

**Steps**:
1. Endoscopic or external approach
2. Remove bone (thin with drill or punch)
3. Open periorbita (allow fat/muscle herniation into sinuses)

### Complications

- **Diplopia**: New or worsened (20-30%) - imbalanced decompression, muscle entrapment
  - May need strabismus surgery later
- **Infraorbital nerve hypoesthesia**: Floor decompression (numbness upper lip, cheek)
- **Sinusitis**, **CSF leak** (rare)
- **Blindness** (very rare - hemorrhage, optic nerve injury)

### Outcomes

- **Proptosis reduction**: 3-6mm (depends on extent of decompression)
- **Vision preservation**: >90% in optic neuropathy cases (if timely)

## Enucleation vs. Evisceration

### Indications

- **Enucleation**: Remove entire globe
  - **Indications**: Blind painful eye, intraocular tumor (uveal melanoma, retinoblastoma), severe trauma, endophthalmitis (rare)
- **Evisceration**: Remove intraocular contents, preserve scleral shell
  - **Indications**: Blind painful eye (NO malignancy, NO risk of sympathetic ophthalmia)
  - **Advantage**: Simpler, better motility (extraocular muscles remain attached to sclera), lower risk of infection spread

### Surgical Technique

**Enucleation**:
1. **360° peritomy**: Incise conjunctiva around limbus
2. **Disinsertion of extraocular muscles**: Cut and tag muscles (4 recti, 2 obliques)
3. **Optic nerve transection**: Clamp and cut posteriorly
4. **Orbital implant**: Porous (hydroxyapatite, porous polyethylene) or non-porous (silicone, polymethylmethacrylate)
   - **Porous implants**: Allow fibrovascular ingrowth (peg placement for better motility)
5. **Muscle reattachment**: Suture rectus muscles to implant (restore motility)
6. **Tenon's and conjunctival closure**

**Evisceration**:
1. Circumferential incision at limbus
2. Scoop out intraocular contents (uvea, retina, lens, vitreous)
3. Insert implant into scleral shell
4. Close sclera, Tenon's, conjunctiva

### Orbital Implant

**Integrated (porous)**: Hydroxyapatite, porous polyethylene
- Fibrovascular ingrowth over 3-6 months
- **Peg placement**: After vascularization, drill hole, insert peg (connects to prosthesis - improved motility)

**Non-integrated**: Silicone, PMMA (no ingrowth)

**Size**: 18-22mm diameter (fills orbital volume)

### Prosthesis (Artificial Eye)

- Custom-painted acrylic shell
- Fitted by ocularist 6-8 weeks post-op (after swelling resolves)
- **Motility**: Implant moves, prosthesis follows (best with integrated implant + peg)

### Complications

- **Implant exposure**: Inadequate closure, infection (may need patch graft, implant removal)
- **Ptosis**: Superior rectus or levator damage
- **Enophthalmos**: Implant too small or migration
- **Discharge**, **socket contracture**
- **Sympathetic ophthalmia**: Rare (enucleation performed for trauma); bilateral granulomatous uveitis

## Orbital Fractures

### Types

**Orbital Floor ("Blowout Fracture")**:
- Most common
- **Mechanism**: Blunt trauma (fist, ball), increased intraorbital pressure fractures floor into maxillary sinus
- **Complications**: Inferior rectus entrapment (restrictive strabismus, diplopia), infraorbital nerve injury (hypoesthesia)

**Medial Wall**: Ethmoid bone fracture

**Roof**: Frontal bone fracture (high-impact trauma)

**Zygomatic (Tripod) Fracture**: Lateral orbital rim + floor + zygomatic arch

### Clinical Presentation

- **Periorbital ecchymosis**, **edema**
- **Enophthalmos**: Sunken eye (orbital volume expansion)
- **Diplopia**: Vertical (floor fracture with IR entrapment), horizontal (medial wall with MR entrapment)
- **Infraorbital hypoesthesia**: V2 nerve injury
- **Subcutaneous emphysema**: Air from sinus into soft tissue (nose-blowing)

### Imaging

**CT orbits** (axial and coronal): Gold standard
- Fracture location, size
- Extraocular muscle entrapment (muscle herniation into fracture, "trapdoor" fracture)
- Orbital emphysema, foreign body

### Indications for Surgery

**Urgent** (within 24-48 hours):
- **"White-eyed" blowout**: Minimal external signs, severe entrapment (trapdoor fracture), oculocardiac reflex (bradycardia with eye movement)
  - Common in children/young adults
  - Urgent repair prevents ischemic muscle damage

**Early** (within 1-2 weeks):
- **Large fracture** (>50% floor)
- **Significant enophthalmos** (>2mm)
- **Persistent diplopia** in primary or reading gaze with entrapment on forced duction testing

**Observation**:
- Small fracture (<50% floor), no entrapment, minimal symptoms
- Diplopia may resolve (edema, hematoma)

### Surgical Technique

**Approach**:
- **Transconjunctival**: Through lower lid conjunctiva (no external scar)
- **Subciliary**: Below lashes
- **Transcaruncular** (medial wall)

**Steps**:
1. Expose fracture site
2. **Free entrapped tissue**: Careful release (avoid damaging muscle)
3. **Implant**: Cover defect (medpor, titanium mesh, porous polyethylene)
4. Secure implant
5. Close

### Complications

- **Persistent diplopia**: Muscle damage, inadequate release
- **Enophthalmos**: Inadequate reconstruction, implant malposition
- **Infraorbital hypoesthesia**: Nerve injury (may persist)
- **Blindness** (rare): Retrobulbar hemorrhage, optic nerve injury
- **Implant infection/extrusion**

## Key Points

- Levator palpebrae superioris (CN III innervation) primary upper lid elevator; Müller's muscle (sympathetic) contributes 2mm elevation
- Aponeurotic ptosis most common adult type (levator dehiscence); treat with external levator advancement if levator function >10mm
- Frontalis sling indicated for ptosis with poor levator function (<5mm); suspends lid to frontalis muscle, risk of lagophthalmos/exposure keratopathy
- Entropion (inward lid rotation) causes corneal abrasion from lashes; involutional type treated with lateral tarsal strip + retractor reinsertion
- Ectropion (outward lid rotation) causes epiphora (punctum everted); lateral tarsal strip most common treatment for involutional type
- Blepharoplasty removes excess upper lid skin (dermatochalasis) and/or prolapsed orbital fat; retrobulbar hemorrhage rare but vision-threatening complication
- Dacryocystorhinostomy (DCR) creates fistula between lacrimal sac and nasal cavity to bypass nasolacrimal duct obstruction; 85-95% success rate
- Graves' ophthalmopathy orbital decompression indicated for dysthyroid optic neuropathy (urgent) or severe proptosis; removes medial wall/floor bone to expand orbital volume
- Enucleation removes entire globe (for malignancy); evisceration removes intraocular contents only (preserves sclera, better motility, no malignancy)
- Orbital floor blowout fracture can entrap inferior rectus causing vertical diplopia; "white-eyed blowout" with trapdoor fracture requires urgent repair (within 24-48h) to prevent ischemic muscle damage
- Porous orbital implants (hydroxyapatite, porous polyethylene) allow fibrovascular ingrowth and peg placement for improved prosthetic motility
- Orbital fracture repair indications: urgent for trapdoor fracture, early (1-2 weeks) for large fracture (>50% floor), significant enophthalmos (>2mm), or persistent diplopia with entrapment
