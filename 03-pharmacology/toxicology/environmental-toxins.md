# Environmental Toxins

## Introduction

Environmental toxins are substances in the environment that can cause adverse health effects through occupational, accidental, or intentional exposure. Understanding these toxins is essential for diagnosis, treatment, and prevention of poisoning.

## Heavy Metals

### Lead (Pb)

**Sources**:
- **Old paint** (pre-1978 houses) - most common in children
- **Contaminated water** (lead pipes, solder)
- **Occupational**: Battery manufacturing, smelting, ammunition
- **Traditional remedies**, imported pottery (lead glaze)
- **Gasoline** (phased out)

**Toxicokinetics**:
- **Absorption**: GI (10-15% adults, 40-50% children), inhalation
- **Distribution**: Blood (95% RBC-bound) → soft tissues → bone (95% body burden, half-life years)
- **Excretion**: Renal, fecal, minimal

**Mechanisms**:
- **Inhibits δ-aminolevulinic acid dehydratase, ferrochelatase** → ↓heme synthesis
- **Binds sulfhydryl groups** → enzyme inhibition
- **Displaces calcium** → neurologic effects
- **Oxidative stress**

**Clinical Features**:

**Children** (More Susceptible):
- **Neurodevelopmental toxicity** (most concerning):
  - ↓IQ, learning disabilities, behavioral problems
  - **No safe level** (effects at <5 mcg/dL)
- **Acute encephalopathy** (BLL >70 mcg/dL):
  - Seizures, coma, cerebral edema
  - **Medical emergency**
- **Anemia** (microcytic, basophilic stippling)
- **Abdominal pain** ("lead colic")
- **Gingival lead line** (rare)

**Adults**:
- **Peripheral neuropathy** (wrist/foot drop - motor > sensory)
- **Abdominal pain**, constipation
- **Hypertension**, nephropathy (chronic)
- **Reproductive**: ↓Fertility, miscarriage
- **Anemia**

**Diagnosis**:
- **Blood lead level (BLL)**:
  - Children: **≥5 mcg/dL** concerning (reference <5 mcg/dL)
  - Adults: **≥10 mcg/dL** concerning
  - **Symptomatic**: Usually >40-50 mcg/dL
- **Free erythrocyte protoporphyrin (FEP)**: ↑(heme synthesis inhibition)
- **Anemia**: Microcytic, basophilic stippling
- **Abdominal X-ray**: Lead particles (if ingested paint chips)

**Treatment**:

**Remove Source** (Essential):
- Identify and remediate lead exposure

**Chelation** (If Indicated):

**Children**:
- **BLL 45-69 mcg/dL**: **Succimer** (DMSA) 10 mg/kg PO q8h × 5d, then q12h × 14d
- **BLL ≥70 mcg/dL or encephalopathy**: **Dimercaprol** (BAL) + **EDTA** (hospitalize)

**Adults**:
- **Symptomatic or BLL >80 mcg/dL**: Succimer or EDTA

**Supportive**: Iron, calcium (if deficient)

**Prevention**:
- **Universal screening** (children 1-2 years in high-risk areas)
- Lead paint abatement, water testing

**Clinical Pearl**: **Permanent neurodevelopmental effects in children** - prevention crucial

### Mercury (Hg)

**Forms**:
1. **Elemental mercury** (Hg0): Liquid metal, thermometers
2. **Inorganic mercury** (Hg2+): Mercury salts
3. **Organic mercury**: Methylmercury (fish), ethylmercury (thimerosal in vaccines)

**Toxicokinetics**:
- **Elemental**: Vapor inhalation → absorbed lungs → oxidized to Hg2+ → CNS, kidneys
- **Methylmercury**: Oral → crosses BBB, placenta → CNS

**Clinical Features**:

**Acute Inhalation** (Elemental):
- **Pneumonitis**, pulmonary edema (high dose)
- Tremor, erethism (psychological changes)

**Chronic Inhalation**:
- **CNS**: Tremor, ataxia, cognitive impairment ("mad hatter" - felt hat makers)
- **Renal**: Tubular necrosis

**Methylmercury** (Chronic, Fish):
- **CNS toxicity** (lipophilic):
  - Paresthesias, ataxia, vision/hearing loss
  - **Developmental neurotoxicity** (fetus most sensitive - Minamata disease)
- **Cardiovascular** (high doses)

**Inorganic Mercury** (Ingestion):
- **GI**: Hemorrhagic gastroenteritis
- **Renal**: Acute tubular necrosis

**Diagnosis**:
- **Blood mercury**: Reflects recent exposure (methylmercury)
- **Urine mercury**: Reflects inorganic/elemental exposure
- **Hair mercury**: Chronic methylmercury exposure

**Treatment**:
- **Remove exposure**
- **Chelation**: **Succimer** (DMSA) or dimercaprol (BAL) for symptomatic inorganic/elemental
- **Methylmercury**: Supportive (chelation ineffective once in CNS)

**Prevention**:
- **Limit fish consumption** (pregnant women, children): Avoid shark, swordfish, king mackerel (high methylmercury)
- Proper handling/disposal of mercury-containing devices

**Clinical Pearl**: **Fetal neurotoxicity** - pregnant women limit high-mercury fish

### Arsenic (As)

**Sources**:
- **Contaminated water** (natural, industrial)
- **Occupational**: Smelting, pesticides, wood preservatives
- **Homicidal poisoning** (historical)
- **Traditional medicine**

**Forms**:
- **Inorganic** (more toxic): Arsenic trioxide (As2O3)
- **Organic** (less toxic): Seafood (arsenobetaine - nontoxic)

**Mechanisms**:
- **Binds sulfhydryl groups** → enzyme inhibition (pyruvate dehydrogenase, α-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase)
- **Uncouples oxidative phosphorylation**
- **Disrupts DNA repair**

**Clinical Features**:

**Acute** (Hours-Days):
- **GI**: Severe, "rice-water" diarrhea, abdominal pain, vomiting
- **Cardiovascular**: Hypotension, arrhythmias (QT prolongation)
- **CNS**: Encephalopathy, seizures
- **Hematologic**: Pancytopenia (days-weeks)

**Subacute/Chronic**:
- **Peripheral neuropathy** (sensory > motor, "stocking-glove")
- **Skin**:
  - **Mees' lines** (transverse white lines on nails)
  - **Hyperkeratosis** (palms, soles)
  - Hyperpigmentation ("raindrop" pattern)
- **GI**: Hepatitis
- **Hematologic**: Anemia, leukopenia
- **Carcinogenic**: Skin, lung, bladder cancer (chronic)

**Diagnosis**:
- **24-hour urine arsenic**: >50 mcg (specify inorganic + metabolites, not total)
- **Blood arsenic**: Reflects recent exposure only
- **Hair/nails**: Chronic exposure (beware external contamination)

**Treatment**:
- **Remove exposure**
- **Chelation** (if acute or symptomatic):
  - **Dimercaprol** (BAL): 3-5 mg/kg IM q4h
  - **Succimer** (DMSA): 10 mg/kg PO q8h (alternative, oral)
- **Supportive**: Fluids, electrolytes, cardiac monitoring

**Clinical Pearl**: **"Rice-water" diarrhea + peripheral neuropathy** classic for arsenic

## Pesticides

### Organophosphates

**Sources**: Insecticides (malathion, parathion, diazinon), nerve agents (sarin, VX)

**Mechanism**: **Irreversible acetylcholinesterase inhibition** → ↑acetylcholine

**Clinical Features** (**Cholinergic Toxidrome**):

**Muscarinic** (SLUDGE):
- **S**alivation, **L**acrimation, **U**rination, **D**efecation, **G**I upset, **E**mesis
- **Miosis** (pinpoint pupils)
- **Bronchospasm**, **bronchorrhea**
- **Bradycardia**

**Nicotinic**:
- **Muscle fasciculations**, weakness, paralysis (respiratory failure)
- **Tachycardia**, hypertension (sympathetic ganglia)

**CNS**:
- Confusion, seizures, coma

**Diagnosis**:
- **Clinical** (cholinergic toxidrome)
- **RBC acetylcholinesterase activity**: ↓(<50% baseline confirms)
- **Plasma cholinesterase**: ↓(less specific)

**Treatment**:
1. **Decontamination**: Remove clothing, wash skin (protect providers)
2. **Atropine** (muscarinic antagonist):
   - **1-2 mg IV q5min** (double dose) until drying of secretions
   - **May require massive doses** (hundreds of mg)
   - **Endpoint**: Dry lungs (not pupil size)
3. **Pralidoxime (2-PAM)** (reactivates enzyme):
   - **1-2 g IV** loading, then 500 mg/h infusion
   - **Must give before "aging"** (24-48 hours)
4. **Supportive**: Airway, ventilation, benzodiazepines (seizures)

**Clinical Pearl**: **Atropine + pralidoxime** both essential

### Carbamates

**Similar to Organophosphates** but:
- **Reversible** acetylcholinesterase inhibition
- **Shorter duration** (hours)
- **No aging** (spontaneous reactivation)

**Treatment**: **Atropine** (usually sufficient)
- **Pralidoxime NOT recommended** (theoretical concerns, not needed)

### Pyrethroids

**Mechanism**: Sodium channel dysfunction (prolonged opening)

**Clinical Features**:
- **Paresthesias** (especially face, tongue)
- Tremor, seizures (high dose)
- Generally less toxic than organophosphates

**Treatment**: Supportive

### Paraquat

**Mechanism**: **Redox cycling** → superoxide → pulmonary fibrosis

**Clinical Features**:
- **GI**: Oral/esophageal burns (if ingested)
- **Pulmonary**: Progressive pulmonary fibrosis (1-2 weeks) - **fatal**
- **Multi-organ failure**

**Treatment**:
- **No effective antidote**
- **Avoid oxygen** (worsens toxicity - controversial)
- Fuller's earth, activated charcoal (if early)
- **High mortality**

**Clinical Pearl**: **Paraquat ingestion usually fatal** (pulmonary fibrosis)

## Gases

### Carbon Monoxide (CO)

**Sources**:
- **Fires** (most common)
- **Improperly vented heaters**, generators
- **Car exhaust** (suicide/homicide)
- **Methylene chloride** (metabolized to CO)

**Mechanism**:
- **Binds hemoglobin** (200x affinity vs. O2) → **carboxyhemoglobin (COHb)** → ↓O2 delivery
- **Leftward shift** of O2-Hb dissociation curve → ↓O2 release
- **Binds myoglobin** (cardiac/skeletal muscle dysfunction)
- **Inhibits cytochrome c oxidase** (cellular respiration)

**Clinical Features**:

**Acute**:
- **Headache** (most common)
- **Nausea, vomiting**
- **Dizziness, confusion**
- **Syncope, seizures, coma** (severe)
- **Myocardial ischemia** (chest pain, arrhythmias)
- **"Cherry-red" skin** (rare, late finding)

**Chronic** (Low-level):
- Headache, fatigue, cognitive impairment (nonspecific)

**Delayed Neurologic Sequelae** (10-30%):
- **Cognitive impairment**, personality changes, parkinsonism
- Onset days to weeks after exposure

**Diagnosis**:
- **COHb level** (venous or arterial):
  - **Normal**: <3% (smokers <10%)
  - **Symptomatic**: Usually >15-20%
  - **Level does NOT correlate well with severity** (timing, duration)
- **Arterial blood gas**: **PaO2 normal** (measures dissolved O2), but ↓O2 content
- **Pulse oximetry**: **Falsely normal** (cannot distinguish COHb from oxyHb)
- **Co-oximeter**: Required for COHb measurement

**Treatment**:
1. **100% oxygen** (normobaric):
   - **First-line**
   - ↓COHb half-life: 5-6 hours (room air) → 60-90 minutes (100% O2)
   - **Continue until**: COHb <10% AND asymptomatic

2. **Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO)**:
   - **Controversial indications**:
     - Loss of consciousness
     - COHb >25%
     - Pregnancy
     - Cardiac ischemia
     - Severe acidosis
     - Neurologic symptoms
   - ↓COHb half-life to 20-30 minutes
   - **May reduce delayed neurologic sequelae** (evidence mixed)

**Clinical Pearls**:
- **High index of suspicion** (multiple victims, winter heating)
- **Pulse oximetry falsely normal** (co-oximetry required)
- **100% O2 essential**, HBO if available/severe

### Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S)

**Sources**: Sewers, petroleum/natural gas, volcanic gases, industrial

**Mechanism**: **Inhibits cytochrome c oxidase** (like cyanide) → cellular hypoxia

**Clinical Features**:
- **"Rotten egg" smell** (low concentrations)
- **Anosmia** (olfactory paralysis at high concentrations - dangerous)
- **Pulmonary**: Irritation, pulmonary edema
- **CNS**: Headache, confusion, seizures, coma
- **Rapid onset** ("knockdown gas")

**Treatment**:
- **Remove from exposure**
- **100% oxygen**
- **Nitrites** (if severe - induce methemoglobinemia, like cyanide antidote)
- **Supportive**

### Ammonia (NH3)

**Sources**: Fertilizers, refrigerants, cleaning products, industrial

**Clinical Features**:
- **Irritant gas** (eyes, respiratory tract)
- **Laryngospasm**, pulmonary edema
- **Alkali burns** (if liquid)

**Treatment**:
- **Remove from exposure**
- **Humidified oxygen**, bronchodilators
- **Supportive**

## Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs)

### Benzene

**Sources**: Gasoline, industrial solvent, cigarette smoke

**Toxicity**:
- **Acute**: CNS depression, arrhythmias
- **Chronic**: **Bone marrow toxicity** (aplastic anemia, leukemia)

**Clinical Pearl**: **Carcinogenic** (leukemia) - chronic exposure

### Toluene

**Sources**: Paint thinners, glues (inhalant abuse)

**Clinical Features**:
- **Acute**: Euphoria, CNS depression
- **Chronic**:
  - **Renal**: Renal tubular acidosis (Type 1 - distal)
  - **Hypokalemia**
  - **CNS**: Cognitive impairment, ataxia
  - **Peripheral neuropathy**

**Treatment**: Supportive, correct acidosis/hypokalemia

### Methylene Chloride (Dichloromethane)

**Sources**: Paint strippers

**Toxicity**:
- **Metabolized to carbon monoxide** → COHb
- **Prolonged toxicity** (slow metabolism)

**Treatment**: Same as CO poisoning (100% O2)

## Air Pollutants

### Ozone (O3)

**Sources**: Photochemical smog

**Effects**:
- **Respiratory irritation**, ↓lung function
- **Exacerbates asthma**, COPD

### Particulate Matter (PM2.5, PM10)

**Effects**:
- **Cardiovascular**: ↑MI, stroke
- **Respiratory**: ↑Asthma, COPD exacerbations
- **Mortality**: ↑With chronic exposure

## Clinical Pearls

1. **Lead**: Neurodevelopmental toxicity (children), no safe level, chelation if BLL ≥45 mcg/dL
2. **Mercury**: Methylmercury (fish) → fetal neurotoxicity, limit high-mercury fish in pregnancy
3. **Arsenic**: "Rice-water" diarrhea, peripheral neuropathy, Mees' lines
4. **Organophosphates**: Cholinergic toxidrome (SLUDGE), atropine + pralidoxime
5. **Carbon monoxide**: Headache, pulse ox falsely normal (need co-oximetry), 100% O2 treatment
6. **Paraquat**: Pulmonary fibrosis, usually fatal
7. **Hydrogen sulfide**: "Rotten egg" smell, inhibits cytochrome (like cyanide)
8. **Toluene**: Renal tubular acidosis, hypokalemia (inhalant abuse)

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Lead**: Neurodevelopmental effects (children <5 mcg/dL), chelation (succimer, EDTA)
2. **Mercury**: Forms (elemental, inorganic, methylmercury), methylmercury → fetal neurotoxicity
3. **Arsenic**: "Rice-water" diarrhea, peripheral neuropathy, Mees' lines, chelation (BAL, succimer)
4. **Organophosphates**: Acetylcholinesterase inhibition, cholinergic toxidrome, atropine + pralidoxime
5. **Carbon monoxide**: COHb (binds Hb 200x O2), pulse ox falsely normal, 100% O2 treatment
6. **Paraquat**: Redox cycling, pulmonary fibrosis (fatal)
7. **Pesticides**: Organophosphates (irreversible), carbamates (reversible)
8. **Heavy metal chelation**: Succimer (oral, less toxic), EDTA/BAL (severe)
9. **Environmental exposure**: Occupational, contaminated water, air pollution
10. **Prevention**: Remove source, screening (lead), limit exposure (mercury fish)
