# Dermatology

## Overview

Dermatology is the medical specialty focused on the diagnosis and treatment of diseases affecting the skin, hair, nails, and mucous membranes. The skin is the largest organ of the body, serving as a protective barrier against environmental threats while also playing crucial roles in thermoregulation, sensation, immune function, and vitamin D synthesis. Dermatological conditions range from common benign disorders to life-threatening malignancies and systemic diseases with cutaneous manifestations.

### Scope of Dermatology
- **Medical dermatology**: Inflammatory, infectious, autoimmune, and neoplastic skin diseases
- **Pediatric dermatology**: Congenital and developmental skin disorders
- **Dermatopathology**: Microscopic examination of skin biopsies
- **Cosmetic dermatology**: Aesthetic procedures and anti-aging treatments
- **Dermatologic surgery**: Excision of skin lesions, Mohs surgery, reconstructive procedures

### Epidemiology
- **Prevalence**: Skin diseases affect approximately 30-70% of the population at any given time
- **Primary care burden**: Dermatologic complaints account for 5-10% of primary care visits
- **Economic impact**: Skin diseases cost healthcare systems billions annually
- **Global variation**: Disease patterns vary by geography, climate, and genetic factors

## Skin Anatomy and Physiology

### Layers of the Skin

#### Epidermis
- **Stratum corneum**: Outermost layer of dead keratinized cells
- **Stratum granulosum**: Granular layer with keratohyalin granules
- **Stratum spinosum**: Prickle cell layer with abundant desmosomes
- **Stratum basale**: Basal layer containing stem cells and melanocytes
- **Turnover time**: Approximately 28 days from basal layer to surface

#### Dermis
- **Papillary dermis**: Upper layer with loose connective tissue and capillaries
- **Reticular dermis**: Deeper layer with dense collagen and elastic fibers
- **Contents**: Blood vessels, nerves, hair follicles, sebaceous glands, sweat glands
- **Structural proteins**: Type I and III collagen, elastin

#### Subcutaneous Tissue (Hypodermis)
- **Composition**: Adipose tissue and connective tissue
- **Functions**: Insulation, energy storage, cushioning
- **Thickness**: Varies by body location and individual factors

### Skin Appendages

#### Hair Follicles
- **Structure**: Dermal invagination extending into subcutis
- **Cycle**: Anagen (growth), catagen (regression), telogen (rest)
- **Distribution**: Varies across body regions
- **Sebaceous glands**: Attached to hair follicles, produce sebum

#### Sweat Glands
- **Eccrine glands**: Distributed throughout body, produce watery sweat
- **Apocrine glands**: Axillae and groin, produce protein-rich sweat
- **Thermoregulation**: Evaporative cooling mechanism
- **Density**: 2-5 million glands throughout body

#### Nails
- **Structure**: Nail plate, nail bed, nail matrix, cuticle
- **Growth rate**: Fingernails 3mm/month, toenails 1mm/month
- **Composition**: Keratin proteins
- **Function**: Protection, fine motor manipulation

### Cellular Components

#### Keratinocytes
- **Predominant cell**: 90-95% of epidermal cells
- **Function**: Physical barrier, antimicrobial peptide production
- **Differentiation**: Progressive keratinization as cells migrate upward
- **Intercellular connections**: Desmosomes, tight junctions

#### Melanocytes
- **Location**: Basal layer of epidermis
- **Function**: Melanin production for photoprotection
- **Distribution**: 1 melanocyte per 10 basal keratinocytes
- **Regulation**: UV exposure, hormones, genetic factors

#### Langerhans Cells
- **Type**: Dendritic antigen-presenting cells
- **Function**: Immune surveillance and response
- **Location**: Suprabasal epidermis
- **Origin**: Bone marrow-derived

#### Merkel Cells
- **Function**: Mechanoreception (touch sensation)
- **Location**: Basal epidermis, hair follicles
- **Innervation**: Associated with sensory nerve endings
- **Distribution**: High density in fingertips, lips

## Dermatologic Examination

### History Taking

#### Chief Complaint
- **Onset**: Acute vs. gradual
- **Duration**: Days, weeks, months, years
- **Location**: Initial site and spread pattern
- **Evolution**: Changes in appearance over time

#### Associated Symptoms
- **Pruritus**: Intensity, timing, triggers
- **Pain**: Character, severity, pattern
- **Burning**: Localized or generalized
- **Systemic symptoms**: Fever, malaise, weight loss

#### Aggravating and Relieving Factors
- **Environmental**: Sun exposure, heat, cold, humidity
- **Medications**: New drugs, topicals, systemic agents
- **Activities**: Occupation, hobbies, travel
- **Treatments tried**: Effectiveness and side effects

#### Past Medical History
- **Atopy**: Asthma, allergic rhinitis, atopic dermatitis
- **Autoimmune diseases**: Lupus, dermatomyositis, psoriatic arthritis
- **Immunosuppression**: HIV, chemotherapy, organ transplant
- **Previous skin conditions**: Chronic diseases, cancer history

#### Family History
- **Hereditary conditions**: Psoriasis, atopic dermatitis, melanoma
- **Autoimmune diseases**: Familial clustering patterns
- **Skin cancer**: First-degree relatives with melanoma or other skin cancers

### Physical Examination

#### Inspection
- **Distribution**: Localized, regional, generalized
- **Configuration**: Linear, annular, grouped, scattered
- **Symmetry**: Bilateral symmetric vs. asymmetric
- **Morphology**: Primary and secondary lesions

#### Primary Lesions
- **Macule**: Flat, <1cm, color change
- **Patch**: Flat, >1cm, color change
- **Papule**: Elevated, <1cm, solid
- **Plaque**: Elevated, >1cm, flat-topped
- **Nodule**: Elevated, >1cm, deep
- **Vesicle**: Fluid-filled, <1cm
- **Bulla**: Fluid-filled, >1cm
- **Pustule**: Pus-filled
- **Wheal**: Transient edematous papule or plaque

#### Secondary Lesions
- **Scale**: Accumulation of stratum corneum
- **Crust**: Dried serum, blood, or pus
- **Erosion**: Loss of epidermis
- **Ulcer**: Loss of epidermis and dermis
- **Excoriation**: Linear erosion from scratching
- **Fissure**: Linear crack in skin
- **Atrophy**: Thinning of skin
- **Lichenification**: Thickening with accentuated skin markings
- **Scar**: Fibrous tissue replacing normal skin

#### Palpation
- **Texture**: Smooth, rough, verrucous
- **Consistency**: Soft, firm, hard, fluctuant
- **Temperature**: Warm, cool, normal
- **Tenderness**: Painful to touch
- **Mobility**: Fixed vs. mobile

### Dermatologic Descriptors

#### Color
- **Erythema**: Red (vasodilation, inflammation)
- **Purpura**: Purple (extravasated blood)
- **Hyperpigmentation**: Brown (increased melanin)
- **Hypopigmentation**: Light (decreased melanin)
- **Depigmentation**: White (absent melanin)
- **Yellow**: Carotenemia, xanthomas, jaundice
- **Blue**: Cyanosis, venous malformations, melanin in dermis

#### Surface Characteristics
- **Smooth**: Normal texture
- **Scaly**: Desquamation present
- **Crusted**: Dried exudate
- **Verrucous**: Warty, rough texture
- **Umbilicated**: Central depression

## Diagnostic Procedures

### Skin Biopsy

#### Indications
- **Diagnosis**: Uncertain clinical diagnosis
- **Malignancy**: Suspected skin cancer
- **Monitoring**: Treatment response assessment
- **Research**: Investigational studies

#### Techniques
- **Shave biopsy**: Tangential removal, superficial lesions
- **Punch biopsy**: Cylindrical core, 2-8mm diameter
- **Excisional biopsy**: Complete removal with margin
- **Incisional biopsy**: Partial removal of large lesion

#### Processing
- **Fixation**: Formalin for routine histology
- **Special techniques**: Frozen sections, immunofluorescence, electron microscopy
- **Immunohistochemistry**: Antibody staining for specific markers
- **Molecular studies**: PCR, FISH for genetic analysis

### Dermoscopy
- **Device**: Hand-held microscope with 10x magnification
- **Technique**: Contact (with immersion fluid) or non-contact (polarized)
- **Applications**: Melanoma screening, inflammatory diseases, parasitic infections
- **Patterns**: Network, globules, streaks, vascular structures

### Patch Testing
- **Purpose**: Identify contact allergens
- **Technique**: Allergens applied to back under occlusion for 48 hours
- **Reading**: 48 hours and 72-96 hours after application
- **Interpretation**: Erythema, edema, vesiculation graded 0 to 3+

### Tzanck Smear
- **Indication**: Suspected viral blistering (herpes, varicella)
- **Technique**: Scrape base of fresh vesicle, stain with Giemsa or Wright
- **Findings**: Multinucleated giant cells, acantholytic cells
- **Limitations**: Cannot distinguish HSV from VZV

### Potassium Hydroxide (KOH) Preparation
- **Purpose**: Detect fungal elements
- **Technique**: Scrape scale, apply 10-20% KOH, gentle heat
- **Findings**: Hyphae, spores, pseudohyphae
- **Sites**: Skin, nails, hair

### Wood's Lamp Examination
- **Device**: Long-wave UV light (365nm)
- **Applications**: Pigmentary disorders, fungal infections, porphyria
- **Findings**: Coral red (erythrasma), blue-green (Pseudomonas), bright white (vitiligo)

### Diascopy
- **Technique**: Apply pressure with glass slide
- **Purpose**: Distinguish erythema from purpura, assess granulomas
- **Finding**: Erythema blanches, purpura does not
- **Apple-jelly nodules**: Tuberculoid granulomas

## Common Diagnostic Challenges

### Erythematous Scaling Rashes
- **Differential**: Psoriasis, eczema, seborrheic dermatitis, tinea, drug reaction
- **Approach**: Distribution, scale characteristics, associated symptoms
- **Testing**: KOH prep, biopsy if persistent or atypical

### Blistering Disorders
- **Differential**: Autoimmune (pemphigus, pemphigoid), infectious (HSV, impetigo), dermatitis
- **Approach**: Blister characteristics, distribution, immunofluorescence
- **Urgency**: Severe cases may require hospitalization

### Pruritus Without Rash
- **Systemic causes**: Renal failure, cholestasis, thyroid disease, hematologic malignancy
- **Evaluation**: Complete history, physical exam, laboratory testing
- **Management**: Treat underlying cause, symptomatic relief

### Photodistributed Eruptions
- **Differential**: Photoallergic, phototoxic, autoimmune (lupus), porphyria
- **Pattern**: Sun-exposed areas sparing protected sites
- **Testing**: Phototesting, ANA, porphyrin levels

## Treatment Principles

### Topical Therapy

#### Vehicle Selection
- **Creams**: Water-in-oil emulsion, moderate penetration
- **Ointments**: Oil-based, maximal penetration, occlusive
- **Lotions**: Suspension or solution, large or hair-bearing areas
- **Gels**: Alcohol or water-based, drying effect
- **Foams**: Easy application, hair-bearing areas

#### Corticosteroids
- **Potency classes**: 7 classes from superpotent (Class I) to least potent (Class VII)
- **Selection**: Based on location, severity, age
- **Duration**: Short-term use to avoid side effects
- **Side effects**: Atrophy, striae, telangiectasia, perioral dermatitis, HPA suppression

#### Other Topical Agents
- **Calcineurin inhibitors**: Tacrolimus, pimecrolimus for atopic dermatitis
- **Vitamin D analogues**: Calcipotriene for psoriasis
- **Retinoids**: Tretinoin, adapalene for acne
- **Antimicrobials**: Antibiotics, antifungals, antivirals

### Systemic Therapy

#### Oral Medications
- **Antibiotics**: Doxycycline, minocycline for acne, rosacea
- **Antifungals**: Terbinafine, itraconazole for onychomycosis
- **Antivirals**: Acyclovir, valacyclovir for HSV, VZV
- **Immunosuppressants**: Methotrexate, azathioprine, cyclosporine
- **Biologics**: TNF inhibitors, IL-17 inhibitors, IL-23 inhibitors

#### Isotretinoin
- **Indication**: Severe nodulocystic acne
- **Mechanism**: Reduces sebum production, normalizes keratinization
- **Monitoring**: Lipids, liver function, pregnancy testing
- **Teratogenicity**: Absolute contraindication in pregnancy

### Phototherapy
- **Narrowband UVB**: 311-313nm, first-line for psoriasis, vitiligo
- **PUVA**: Psoralen + UVA, severe psoriasis, cutaneous T-cell lymphoma
- **Excimer laser**: Targeted 308nm, localized psoriasis, vitiligo
- **Risks**: Photoaging, skin cancer (cumulative dose-dependent)

### Procedural Dermatology
- **Cryotherapy**: Liquid nitrogen for warts, actinic keratoses, seborrheic keratoses
- **Electrodesiccation and curettage**: Basal cell carcinoma, seborrheic keratoses
- **Chemical peels**: Acne, photoaging, pigmentary disorders
- **Laser therapy**: Hair removal, vascular lesions, pigmented lesions, resurfacing

## Dermatologic Emergencies

### Stevens-Johnson Syndrome/Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis
- **Presentation**: Widespread blistering and skin detachment
- **Etiology**: Drug-induced (allopurinol, anticonvulsants, antibiotics)
- **Mortality**: SJS <10%, TEN >30%
- **Management**: ICU/burn unit, supportive care, discontinue offending drug

### Erythroderma
- **Definition**: Erythema involving >90% body surface area
- **Causes**: Psoriasis, eczema, drug reaction, cutaneous T-cell lymphoma
- **Complications**: Hypothermia, high-output cardiac failure, sepsis
- **Management**: Hospitalization, treat underlying cause, supportive care

### Necrotizing Fasciitis
- **Presentation**: Rapidly progressive cellulitis with severe pain
- **Signs**: Hemorrhagic bullae, crepitus, systemic toxicity
- **Diagnosis**: Clinical + imaging + surgical exploration
- **Management**: Emergent surgical debridement, broad-spectrum antibiotics, ICU care

### Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome
- **Etiology**: Exotoxin-producing Staphylococcus aureus
- **Presentation**: Tender erythema, superficial blistering, positive Nikolsky sign
- **Population**: Primarily infants and young children
- **Management**: Antistaphylococcal antibiotics, supportive care

### Eczema Herpeticum
- **Definition**: Disseminated HSV infection in atopic dermatitis
- **Presentation**: Punched-out erosions, vesicles, crusting, fever
- **Complications**: Bacteremia, meningitis, death
- **Management**: IV acyclovir, antibiotics for secondary infection

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Dermatology
- **Neonatal**: Erythema toxicum, milia, seborrheic dermatitis
- **Infantile**: Atopic dermatitis, diaper dermatitis, infantile hemangiomas
- **Childhood**: Viral exanthems, warts, molluscum contagiosum
- **Considerations**: Different absorption and metabolism of topical medications

### Geriatric Dermatology
- **Skin changes**: Xerosis, purpura, seborrheic keratoses, solar lentigines
- **Malignancy risk**: Cumulative sun exposure, immunosenescence
- **Medication issues**: Polypharmacy, drug reactions
- **Wound healing**: Delayed healing, increased infection risk

### Pregnancy
- **Physiologic changes**: Melasma, striae, spider angiomas
- **Pregnancy-specific dermatoses**: PUPPP, pemphigoid gestationis, intrahepatic cholestasis
- **Medication safety**: Avoid retinoids, many systemic agents
- **Considerations**: Fetal effects, breastfeeding compatibility

### Immunocompromised Patients
- **Infections**: Opportunistic organisms, severe herpes viruses, deep fungal
- **Drug reactions**: Higher frequency and severity
- **Malignancy**: Increased risk of skin cancers
- **Considerations**: Prophylaxis, careful monitoring

## Key Points

- Dermatology encompasses a wide range of conditions affecting the skin, hair, nails, and mucous membranes
- Systematic examination including history, morphology, distribution, and configuration is essential for diagnosis
- Primary lesions (macule, papule, plaque, vesicle, etc.) and secondary lesions (scale, crust, erosion, ulcer) guide diagnosis
- Skin biopsy is the gold standard for definitive diagnosis in many conditions
- Topical therapy requires appropriate vehicle selection and potency matching to location and severity
- Dermatologic emergencies (SJS/TEN, erythroderma, necrotizing fasciitis) require prompt recognition and management
- Special populations (pediatric, geriatric, pregnant, immunocompromised) have unique considerations
- Multidisciplinary approach often needed for systemic diseases with cutaneous manifestations
- Pattern recognition and systematic approach are key skills in dermatologic diagnosis
- Prevention and early detection of skin cancer are major public health priorities
