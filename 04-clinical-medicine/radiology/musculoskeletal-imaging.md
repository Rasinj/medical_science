# Musculoskeletal Imaging

## Overview

Musculoskeletal (MSK) imaging encompasses evaluation of bones, joints, muscles, tendons, ligaments, and soft tissues. Plain radiographs remain the workhorse for initial assessment, while MRI provides unparalleled soft tissue detail for ligament tears, meniscal injuries, and bone marrow pathology. CT excels for complex fractures and surgical planning. Understanding systematic plain film interpretation, recognition of common fracture patterns, arthritis types, and appropriate advanced imaging selection is essential for all clinicians managing musculoskeletal complaints.

### Imaging Modalities
- **Plain radiographs (X-ray)**: First-line, fractures, arthritis, alignment
- **CT**: Complex fractures, surgical planning, bone detail
- **MRI**: Soft tissues (ligaments, tendons, cartilage, marrow, muscles)
- **Ultrasound**: Dynamic tendons, foreign bodies, fluid collections, guided injections
- **Nuclear medicine**: Bone scan for occult fracture, infection, metastases

## Plain Radiograph Interpretation

### Standard Views
- **Two orthogonal views**: Minimum (AP and lateral, or PA and lateral)
- **Additional views**: Obliques, stress views, comparison views (opposite side in children)

### ABCS Approach
- **A**lignment: Normal bone and joint relationships
- **B**one: Cortex, trabecular pattern, density
- **C**artilage/joints: Joint space, alignment
- **S**oft tissues: Swelling, masses, foreign bodies, calcifications

### Alignment
- **Fractures**: Displacement, angulation, rotation
- **Dislocations**: Joint surfaces not congruent
- **Subluxation**: Partial dislocation

### Bone Cortex and Trabecular Pattern
- **Cortex**: Smooth, continuous white line
- **Fracture**: Break in cortical continuity
- **Osteopenia**: Thin cortex, sparse trabeculae
- **Sclerosis**: Increased density (healing fracture, Paget, metastases)

### Soft Tissues
- **Joint effusion**: Distension of joint capsule, fat pads displaced
- **Muscle planes**: Obliterated by hematoma, edema
- **Calcifications**: Vascular, tendon, cartilage, tumoral

## Fractures

### Description
- **Location**: Anatomic site (proximal, mid, distal third; intra-articular vs extra-articular)
- **Orientation**: Transverse, oblique, spiral, comminuted (>2 fragments)
- **Displacement**: Amount and direction
- **Angulation**: Degrees, direction (apex medial, lateral, anterior, posterior)
- **Open vs closed**: Open if communicating wound

### Occult Fractures
- **X-ray negative** initially
- **Clinical**: Persistent pain, inability to bear weight
- **Further imaging**: MRI (most sensitive, immediate), bone scan (2-3 days), repeat X-ray (10-14 days for healing callus)
- **Common sites**: Scaphoid, hip (femoral neck), stress fractures

### Stress Fractures
- **Overuse injury**: Repetitive microtrauma exceeds bone remodeling
- **X-ray**: Often normal initially, later shows periosteal reaction, sclerosis, fracture line
- **MRI**: Bone marrow edema, fracture line
- **Common sites**: Metatarsals (march fracture), tibia, fibula, femur, navicular

### Pathologic Fractures
- **Definition**: Fracture through abnormal bone from minimal trauma
- **Causes**:
  - Metastases (most common): Breast, lung, kidney, thyroid, prostate
  - Multiple myeloma
  - Osteoporosis (vertebral compression fractures, hip)
  - Infection
  - Primary bone tumors
- **Features**: Lucent or sclerotic lesion at fracture site

### Pediatric Fractures

#### Unique Patterns
- **Buckle (torus)**: Compression of one cortex, metaphysis (distal radius common)
- **Greenstick**: Incomplete fracture, one cortex broken, other bent
- **Plastic deformation**: Bowing without fracture line
- **Growth plate (Salter-Harris)**: See below

#### Salter-Harris Classification (Physeal Fractures)
- **Type I**: Through physis only (X-ray may be normal, widened physis)
- **Type II**: Through physis and metaphysis (most common, 75%)
- **Type III**: Through physis and epiphysis (intra-articular)
- **Type IV**: Through metaphysis, physis, and epiphysis (worst prognosis)
- **Type V**: Crush injury to physis (difficult to diagnose, retrospective)

**Mnemonic**: SALTR
- **S**traight across (I)
- **A**bove (II, metaphysis above physis)
- **L**ower (III, epiphysis lower/below)
- **T**hrough (IV)
- **R**uined/cRush (V)

**Prognosis**: Types III, IV, V higher risk of growth arrest

### Common Fracture Sites

#### Hip Fractures
**Femoral neck:**
- **Elderly**: Osteoporosis, fall
- **Garden classification**: I-IV based on displacement
- **Complications**: AVN, non-union (disrupted blood supply)
- **Treatment**: Surgical fixation or replacement

**Intertrochanteric:**
- **Between greater and lesser trochanters**
- **Good blood supply**: Lower AVN risk
- **Treatment**: Intramedullary nail or sliding hip screw

**Subcapital:**
- **Across femoral head-neck junction**
- **Highest AVN risk**

#### Ankle Fractures
**Weber classification (fibula fracture relative to syndesmosis):**
- **Type A**: Below syndesmosis, stable
- **Type B**: At level of syndesmosis, may be unstable
- **Type C**: Above syndesmosis, unstable, syndesmotic injury

**Malleolar fractures:**
- **Unimalleolar**: Lateral or medial malleolus
- **Bimalleolar**: Lateral + medial (unstable)
- **Trimalleolar**: Lateral + medial + posterior malleolus (unstable)

#### Wrist Fractures
**Colles fracture:**
- **Distal radius** with dorsal angulation
- **FOOSH** (fall on outstretched hand)
- **Most common wrist fracture**
- **"Dinner fork" deformity**

**Smith fracture:**
- **Distal radius** with volar (palmar) angulation
- **Reverse Colles** (fall on flexed wrist)
- **Less common**

**Scaphoid fracture:**
- **Most common carpal fracture**
- **FOOSH mechanism**
- **Tenderness**: Anatomic snuffbox
- **X-ray**: Often initially normal
- **Imaging**: Scaphoid views (dedicated), MRI if high suspicion
- **Complications**: AVN (proximal pole, retrograde blood supply), non-union
- **Treatment**: Immobilization (thumb spica), surgical fixation if displaced or proximal

#### Spine Fractures
**Compression fracture:**
- See Neuroimaging section

**Hangman fracture:**
- **C2 pars interarticularis** bilateral fractures
- **Hyperextension injury**

**Odontoid (dens) fracture:**
- **Type I**: Tip of dens (rare)
- **Type II**: Base of dens (common, unstable, non-union risk)
- **Type III**: Extends into C2 body

**Jefferson fracture:**
- **C1 burst fracture** (axial load)
- **Four-part fracture**: Anterior and posterior arches bilaterally

## Arthritis

### Osteoarthritis (OA)

**Radiographic features:**
- **Joint space narrowing**: Asymmetric, focal (load-bearing surfaces)
- **Subchondral sclerosis**: Increased bone density beneath cartilage
- **Osteophytes**: Bony spurs at joint margins
- **Subchondral cysts**: Geodes from synovial fluid intrusion

**Common sites:**
- **Hands**: DIP (Heberden nodes), PIP (Bouchard nodes), thumb CMC
- **Weight-bearing**: Hips, knees, spine, first MTP (hallux rigidus)

**Mnemonic**: LOSS
- **L**oss of joint space
- **O**steophytes
- **S**ubchondral sclerosis
- **S**ubchondral cysts

### Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

**Radiographic features:**
- **Symmetric** polyarticular involvement
- **Erosions**: Marginal (joint margins first), "mouse ears"
- **Periarticular osteopenia**: Juxta-articular bone loss
- **Joint space narrowing**: Uniform (entire joint surface)
- **Soft tissue swelling**: Synovitis
- **Subluxations**: Swan neck, boutonniere, ulnar deviation

**Common sites:**
- **Hands/wrists**: MCP, PIP, wrist (carpal joints)
- **Spares DIP** (unlike OA)
- **Feet**: MTP joints

**Advanced:**
- **Atlantoaxial subluxation**: C1-C2 instability (risk spinal cord compression)
- **Mutilating arthritis** (arthritis mutilans): Severe bone resorption

### Gout

**Radiographic features (chronic):**
- **Erosions**: Well-defined, "punched-out," overhanging edge (rat-bite)
- **Preserved joint space** until late
- **Soft tissue tophi**: Nodular swelling
- **No osteopenia**

**Common sites:**
- **First MTP** (podagra): Classic
- **Ankles, knees, wrists, fingers**

### Psoriatic Arthritis

**Radiographic features:**
- **Erosions**: "Pencil-in-cup" deformity
- **Ankylosis**: Fusion of small joints
- **Sausage digits**: Soft tissue swelling of entire digit
- **DIP involvement** (unlike RA)
- **Sacroiliitis**: Asymmetric

### Ankylosing Spondylitis

**Radiographic features:**
- **Sacroiliitis**: Bilateral symmetric (early finding)
- **Bamboo spine**: Syndesmophytes (vertical ossifications bridging vertebrae)
- **Squaring of vertebrae**: Loss of normal concavity
- **Enthesitis**: Inflammation at tendon/ligament insertion (Achilles, plantar fascia)

**HLA-B27** associated

## MRI Musculoskeletal

### Advantages
- **Soft tissue detail**: Ligaments, tendons, menisci, cartilage, marrow, muscles
- **Multiplanar**: Axial, sagittal, coronal
- **No radiation**
- **Contrast**: Gadolinium enhances inflammation, tumor

### Sequences
- **T1**: Anatomic detail, fat bright, fluid dark, best for marrow
- **T2/FSSFT2**: Fluid-sensitive, water/edema bright
- **STIR/Fat-saturation**: Suppress fat, edema very bright
- **Proton density**: Cartilage detail, menisci
- **Gradient echo**: Hemorrhage, calcium, metal artifact

### Knee MRI

#### Normal Structures
- **Menisci**: Dark (low signal) on all sequences, triangular
- **Ligaments**: Dark, low signal (ACL, PCL, MCL, LCL)
- **Cartilage**: Intermediate signal, smooth articular surfaces
- **Bone marrow**: Bright on T1 (fat), intermediate T2

#### Meniscal Tears
**Grading:**
- **Grade 1**: Intrasubstance signal, does not extend to surface (normal variant/degeneration)
- **Grade 2**: Linear signal, does not extend to surface (degeneration)
- **Grade 3**: Signal extends to articular surface = TEAR (surgical significance)

**Types:**
- **Horizontal**: Cleaves meniscus into superior/inferior
- **Vertical/longitudinal**: Parallel to circumference
- **Radial**: Perpendicular to circumference
- **Bucket-handle**: Displaced fragment (medial meniscus usually)
- **Complex**: Multiple planes

#### ACL Tear
**MRI findings:**
- **Discontinuous fibers**: Complete tear
- **Abnormal orientation**: Horizontal instead of diagonal
- **Bone marrow edema**: Lateral femoral condyle, posterior tibia (pivot shift injury pattern, "kissing contusions")
- **Secondary signs**: Anterior tibial translation, PCL buckling, Segond fracture (lateral tibial rim avulsion)

#### MCL Injury
**Grade:**
- **I**: Edema, intact fibers (sprain)
- **II**: Partial tear
- **III**: Complete tear (discontinuous, gap)

**MRI:**
- T2 bright edema along medial joint line
- Complete tear: Gap, wavy or non-visualized ligament

#### Bone Marrow Edema
- **T1 dark, T2/STIR bright**
- **Causes**:
  - Contusion (trauma)
  - Stress reaction/fracture
  - Osteochondral injury
  - Osteonecrosis (AVN)
  - Infection
  - Tumor

### Shoulder MRI

#### Rotator Cuff
**Muscles:**
- **SITS**: Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus, Teres minor, Subscapularis

**Tears:**
- **Partial thickness**: Articular or bursal surface
- **Full thickness**: Complete discontinuity, fluid extends through
- **Size**: Small (<1 cm), medium (1-3 cm), large (3-5 cm), massive (>5 cm)
- **Retraction**: Degree of tendon retraction
- **Atrophy**: Muscle atrophy (fatty infiltration on T1, Goutallier classification)

**MRI findings:**
- T2 bright signal in tendon
- Gap, retraction
- Fluid in subacromial bursa

#### Labral Tears
**Bankart lesion:**
- **Anteroinferior labrum** tear
- **Anterior dislocation**: Mechanism
- **May have bony Bankart** (glenoid rim fracture)

**SLAP lesion:**
- **Superior Labrum Anterior-Posterior**
- **Biceps anchor** involvement
- **Overhead athletes**

**MR arthrogram:**
- Intra-articular gadolinium contrast
- Extends into labral tear
- More sensitive than routine MRI

### Hip MRI

#### AVN (Avascular Necrosis/Osteonecrosis)
**Causes:**
- Steroids, alcohol, sickle cell, trauma (femoral neck fracture), Gaucher disease

**MRI findings:**
- **T1**: Serpiginous low signal line (demarcates necrotic from viable bone)
- **T2**: "Double line sign" (high signal rim, low signal inner)
- **Location**: Anterosuperior femoral head (weight-bearing)

**Staging** (Ficat, Steinberg)

**Treatment:**
- Early: Core decompression
- Advanced: Total hip arthroplasty

#### Labral Tears
- **Acetabular labrum** tear
- **Femoroacetabular impingement (FAI)**: Cam (femoral head aspherical) or pincer (acetabular overcoverage)
- **MR arthrogram**: Best sensitivity

## Bone Tumors

### Approach
1. **Age**: Pediatric vs adult
2. **Location**: Metaphysis, diaphysis, epiphysis
3. **Lesion pattern**: Lucent (lytic), sclerotic (blastic), mixed
4. **Margins**: Well-defined (benign), ill-defined (aggressive)
5. **Periosteal reaction**: None, solid (benign), interrupted (aggressive)
6. **Soft tissue mass**: Suggests malignancy

### Benign Bone Tumors

#### Osteochondroma
- **Most common benign bone tumor**
- **Metaphysis**, points away from joint
- **Cartilage cap** (<1 cm, if >2 cm consider malignant transformation)
- **Pedunculated or sessile**

#### Enchondroma
- **Cartilaginous tumor**, medullary cavity
- **Hands/feet** most common
- **Well-defined lucent lesion**, "popcorn" chondroid calcifications
- **Multiple**: Ollier disease, Maffucci syndrome

#### Giant Cell Tumor
- **Epiphyseal** (after physeal closure)
- **Eccentric, lytic**, "soap bubble"
- **Abuts articular surface**
- **Aggressive benign**: Recurrence risk, rarely metastasizes

#### Simple Bone Cyst
- **Children/adolescents**, metaphysis (proximal humerus, femur)
- **Well-defined lucent**, "fallen fragment" sign if fractured
- **Benign**, may resolve spontaneously

### Malignant Bone Tumors

#### Osteosarcoma
- **Most common primary malignant bone tumor** (excluding myeloma)
- **Age**: Adolescents (10-20 years), second peak elderly (Paget, radiation)
- **Location**: Metaphysis around knee (distal femur, proximal tibia), proximal humerus
- **X-ray**: Mixed lytic/blastic, aggressive periosteal reaction (sunburst, Codman triangle), soft tissue mass
- **Treatment**: Neoadjuvant chemo + limb-salvage surgery

#### Ewing Sarcoma
- **Age**: Children/adolescents (5-15 years)
- **Location**: Diaphysis, flat bones (pelvis, scapula)
- **X-ray**: Permeative lucency, "onion skin" periosteal reaction, soft tissue mass
- **Treatment**: Chemo + surgery/radiation

#### Multiple Myeloma
- **Most common primary bone malignancy** overall (adults >40)
- **Plasma cell tumor**
- **X-ray**: Multiple "punched out" lytic lesions, osteopenia
- **Skull, spine, ribs, pelvis** common
- **Pathologic fractures**: Vertebral compression fractures
- **Lab**: SPEP (M-spike), urine Bence Jones protein, hypercalcemia

#### Metastases
- **Most common malignant bone lesions** overall
- **Primaries**: Breast, lung, prostate, kidney, thyroid
- **Lytic** (most): Breast, lung, kidney, thyroid
- **Blastic**: Prostate (most common), breast (mixed)
- **Sites**: Spine, pelvis, ribs, proximal long bones (hematogenous spread)

## Infection

### Osteomyelitis

#### Acute Hematogenous (Children)
- **Metaphysis** (rich vasculature, sluggish flow)
- **S. aureus** most common
- **X-ray**: Initially normal (10-14 days for changes)
- **Later**: Periosteal reaction, lucency
- **MRI**: Marrow edema (T1 dark, T2 bright), enhancement, soft tissue edema, abscess

#### Chronic Osteomyelitis
- **Sequestrum**: Dead bone (sclerotic)
- **Involucrum**: New bone formation around sequestrum
- **Cloaca**: Opening in involucrum draining pus

#### Vertebral Osteomyelitis (Adults)
- **Hematogenous** spread
- **S. aureus**, Gram-negatives
- **X-ray**: Disc space narrowing, endplate erosions
- **MRI**: Disc and adjacent vertebral bodies T2 bright, enhancement, may have epidural abscess
- **Key**: Crosses disc space (vs tumor which often spares disc)

### Septic Arthritis
- **Joint space infection**
- **Infants**: Metaphysis penetrates joint (hip, shoulder) â†’ concurrent osteomyelitis
- **X-ray**: Joint effusion, late erosions
- **Ultrasound**: Detect effusion, guide aspiration
- **MRI**: Effusion, synovial enhancement
- **Diagnosis**: Arthrocentesis (WBC >50,000, culture)

## Summary

Musculoskeletal imaging utilizes plain radiographs as first-line for fractures, arthritis, and alignment assessment with systematic ABCS interpretation. Common fracture patterns include femoral neck (elderly, AVN risk), ankle (Weber classification), distal radius (Colles, Smith), and scaphoid (AVN risk). Pediatric fractures have unique patterns including Salter-Harris physeal injuries with growth arrest risk. Arthritis radiographs differentiate osteoarthritis (LOSS: loss of joint space, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis/cysts) from rheumatoid arthritis (marginal erosions, periarticular osteopenia, symmetric MCP/PIP) and other inflammatory arthropathies. MRI provides superior soft tissue detail essential for meniscal tears, ACL injuries, rotator cuff pathology, and labral tears, as well as detecting bone marrow edema from stress fractures, AVN, and infection. Bone tumors are characterized by age, location, margins, and periosteal reaction, with osteosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma, and metastases being the most important malignancies. Infection imaging progresses from initially normal radiographs to MRI showing marrow edema and soft tissue involvement. Appropriate modality selection, systematic interpretation, and clinical correlation optimize musculoskeletal diagnosis and management.
