# Neuroimaging

## Overview

Neuroimaging is essential for diagnosing and managing neurological emergencies, chronic conditions, and guiding neurosurgical interventions. CT remains the workhorse for acute settings due to speed and availability, while MRI provides superior soft tissue detail for non-emergent evaluation. Understanding appropriate imaging selection, systematic interpretation, recognition of acute stroke, traumatic brain injury, common masses, and spinal pathology is critical for all clinicians managing neurological patients.

### Key Modalities
- **CT head (non-contrast)**: First-line acute stroke, trauma, hemorrhage
- **CTA head/neck**: Vascular imaging, aneurysm, dissection, large vessel occlusion
- **MRI brain**: Superior soft tissue resolution, tumor, MS, chronic disease
- **MRA/MRV**: Non-invasive vascular imaging
- **CT/MRI spine**: Spinal cord pathology, degenerative disease, trauma

## CT Head

### Technique
- **Non-contrast**: Standard for acute presentations
- **Contrast**: Tumor evaluation, infection, vascular lesions
- **Bone windows**: Skull fractures, sinuses
- **Soft tissue (brain) windows**: Parenchyma, blood, masses

### Normal Anatomy
- **Gray matter**: Cortex, basal ganglia, thalamus (higher density than white matter)
- **White matter**: Lower density (more water, less cellular)
- **CSF spaces**: Ventricles (lateral, third, fourth), sulci, cisterns (dark/black)
- **Gray-white differentiation**: Loss suggests edema, ischemia
- **Midline**: Falx cerebri should be midline
- **Symmetry**: Hemispheres symmetric

### Systematic Approach
1. **Blood**: Look for bright areas (acute hemorrhage)
2. **Brain parenchyma**: Gray-white differentiation, edema, masses
3. **CSF spaces**: Ventricles (size, shift), sulci (effaced or prominent)
4. **Bone**: Skull fractures (bone windows)
5. **Midline shift**: Falx displacement >5 mm concerning
6. **Extra-axial spaces**: Subdural, epidural, subarachnoid

### Density on CT
- **Hyperdense (bright)**: Acute blood, calcification, contrast, metal
- **Isodense**: Same as brain (subacute blood ~1-2 weeks)
- **Hypodense (dark)**: CSF, edema, chronic blood, fat, air

## Acute Stroke

### Ischemic Stroke

#### CT Head (Non-Contrast)
**Early signs (within 6 hours):**
- **Loss of gray-white differentiation**: Obscuration of basal ganglia, insular ribbon
- **Hyperdense vessel sign**: Bright MCA (thrombus)
- **Sulcal effacement**: Loss of sulci from edema
- **ASPECTS score**: Alberta Stroke Program Early CT Score (0-10), assesses MCA territory involvement

**Late signs (>6 hours):**
- **Hyp odense area**: Established infarct
- **Mass effect**: Edema, midline shift
- **Hemorrhagic transformation**: Blood within infarct

**Limitations:**
- May be normal in first 6-12 hours
- Small posterior circulation strokes difficult to see
- Sensitivity ~60% in first 3-6 hours

#### MRI Brain (DWI-weighted imaging)
- **Gold standard** for detecting acute ischemia
- **DWI bright** within minutes of stroke onset
- **ADC dark** (restricted diffusion)
- **Much more sensitive** than CT in first hours
- **Detects small strokes** CT misses

#### CT Angiography (CTA)
- **Indications**: Suspected large vessel occlusion, intervention candidates
- **Findings**: Vessel cut-off, filling defects, collateral vessels
- **Guides**: Thrombectomy decision-making

#### CT Perfusion (CTP)
- **Core**: Irreversibly infarcted tissue
- **Penumbra**: At-risk tissue, salvageable if reperfused
- **Mismatch**: Large penumbra relative to core suggests benefit from intervention

### Hemorrhagic Stroke

#### Intracerebral Hemorrhage (ICH)
**CT findings:**
- **Hyperdense** (bright) acute blood
- **Mass effect**: Surrounding edema, midline shift
- **Intraventricular extension**: Blood in ventricles (worse prognosis)

**Location patterns:**
- **Basal ganglia/thalamus** (50%): Hypertensive hemorrhage
- **Lobar** (30%): Amyloid angiopathy (elderly), AVM, tumor, coagulopathy
- **Cerebellar** (10%): Hypertensive
- **Brainstem** (10%): Hypertensive, grave prognosis

**Spot sign on CTA:**
- Contrast extravasation within hematoma
- Predicts hematoma expansion

#### Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH)
**CT findings:**
- **Hyperdense blood** in subarachnoid space (cisterns, sulci)
- **Basal cisterns**: Circum mesencephalic, suprasellar, sylvian fissures
- **Sensitivity**: 95% if scanned within 12 hours, decreases with time

**CT negative but high suspicion:**
- Lumbar puncture: Xanthochromia, elevated RBCs

**CTA:**
- Detect aneurysm (85-95% sensitivity)
- Guide neurosurgical/endovascular intervention

**Catheter angiography:**
- Gold standard if CTA negative or multiple aneurysms
- Therapeutic (coiling)

#### Subdural Hematoma (SDH)
**CT findings:**
- **Crescent-shaped** (follows brain contour, crosses sutures)
- **Acute** (<3 days): Hyperdense
- **Subacute** (3-21 days): Isodense (same as brain, difficult to see)
- **Chronic** (>21 days): Hypodense (dark)
- **Mixed density**: Re-bleeding into chronic SDH

**Associations:**
- Elderly, atrophy (bridging veins stretched)
- Trauma (often minimal in elderly)
- Anticoagulation, alcohol abuse
- Bilateral common

#### Epidural Hematoma (EDH)
**CT findings:**
- **Lens/biconvex shape** (does not cross sutures, limited by dural attachments)
- **Hyperdense acute blood**
- **Associated skull fracture** (90%, often temporal, middle meningeal artery)
- **Lucid interval**: Classic history

**Management:**
- Neurosurgical emergency if large or symptomatic
- Small EDH may observe

### Traumatic Brain Injury

#### Concussion
- **CT usually normal**: Diagnosis clinical
- **Imaging if**: LOC, altered mental status, focal deficits, severe mechanism

#### Cerebral Contusion
- **Hemorrhagic bruising** of brain
- **Coup**: At impact site
- **Contre-coup**: Opposite side (brain hits skull)
- **Frontal/temporal** tips common (bony prominences)

#### Diffuse Axonal Injury (DAI)
- **Shearing injury** from rapid acceleration-deceleration
- **CT**: Often normal or subtle scattered petechial hemorrhages
- **MRI**: Multiple small foci (gray-white junction, corpus callosum, brainstem) on gradient echo/SWI
- **Poor prognosis** if severe

#### Skull Fractures
- **Linear**: Most common, often benign
- **Depressed**: Fragment depressed below inner table (surgical if >1 table thickness)
- **Basilar**: Base of skull, CSF leak, Battle sign, raccoon eyes, hemotympanum

## Brain Masses

### Approach to Brain Mass
1. **Location**: Intra-axial (within brain) vs extra-axial (outside brain)
2. **Number**: Solitary vs multiple
3. **Enhancement pattern**: Solid, ring, none
4. **Edema**: Surrounding vasogenic edema
5. **Mass effect**: Midline shift, herniation
6. **Age and history**: Dictate differential

### Extra-Axial Masses

#### Meningioma
- **Most common** extra-axial tumor
- **Dural-based**, broad dural attachment (dural tail)
- **Homogeneous enhancement**
- **May cause hyperostosis** (bone thickening)
- **Usually benign**, slow-growing

#### Schwannoma
- **CN VIII** (vestibular schwannoma/acoustic neuroma) most common
- **Cerebellopontine angle** (CPA) mass
- **Enhancing mass** expanding internal auditory canal
- **Hearing loss**, tinnitus, imbalance

### Intra-Axial Masses

#### Glioblastoma (GBM)
- **Most common** primary malignant brain tumor in adults
- **Thick irregular ring enhancement**
- **Central necrosis**
- **Extensive vasogenic edema**
- **Mass effect**
- **Crosses midline** (butterfly glioma via corpus callosum)
- **Poor prognosis** (~15 months median survival)

#### Metastases
- **Most common** brain tumor overall
- **Multiple** (60%), at gray-white junction (watershed)
- **Ring enhancement**, surrounding edema
- **Primary sources**: Lung, breast, melanoma, RCC, colon
- **Hemorrhagic**: Melanoma, RCC, thyroid, choriocarcinoma

#### Abscess
- **Ring enhancement** (smooth, thin)
- **Restricted diffusion** (bright on DWI, dark on ADC) - key differentiator from tumor
- **Source**: Contiguous (sinusitis, mastoiditis), hematogenous (endocarditis), post-surgical

#### Lymphoma
- **Primary CNS lymphoma**: Immunocompromised (AIDS), elderly
- **Periventricular**, homogeneous enhancement, restricted diffusion
- **AIDS patients**: May have ring enhancement

## Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

### MRI Findings
- **Multiple** T2-bright/FLAIR-bright lesions
- **Locations**: Periventricular (Dawson fingers), juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord
- **Ovoid**, perpendicular to ventricles
- **Active plaques**: Enhance with contrast
- **Chronic plaques**: No enhancement
- **Black holes** on T1: Severe demyelination

### Diagnostic Criteria (McDonald)
- Dissemination in space (multiple locations)
- Dissemination in time (new lesions on follow-up or simultaneous active and inactive lesions)

## Spine Imaging

### Modalities
- **X-ray**: Alignment, fractures, degenerative changes (limited soft tissue)
- **CT**: Bone detail, fractures, canal stenosis
- **MRI**: Soft tissue (cord, discs, ligaments, marrow), modality of choice for most pathology

### Spinal Cord Compression - Emergency

#### Red Flags
- **Back pain** + neurologic symptoms (weakness, numbness, bowel/bladder dysfunction)
- **Upper motor neuron signs**: Hyperreflexia, Babinski, clonus
- **Sensory level**

#### MRI Spine with Contrast
**Causes:**
- **Epidural abscess**: T1 dark, T2 bright, rim enhancement, phlegmon or fluid
- **Metastases**: Vertebral body involvement, epidural extension, enhancement
- **Epidural hematoma**: Acute bright on T1, mass effect on cord
- **Disc herniation**: Disc material in spinal canal

**Management:**
- **Neurosurgical emergency**: Decompression within hours to prevent permanent deficit
- **Steroids**: High-dose dexamethasone if tumor/trauma
- **Antibiotics**: If abscess

### Degenerative Disc Disease

#### Disc Herniation
**Types:**
- **Protrusion**: Broad-based, disc material >50% intact
- **Extrusion**: Narrow base, >50% herniated, may migrate
- **Sequestration**: Fragment separated from disc

**Locations:**
- **Central**: Cord compression (cervical), cauda equina (lumbar)
- **Foraminal/lateral**: Nerve root compression

**Clinical correlation:**
- **Cervical**: Myelopathy (if cord compression), radiculopathy
- **Lumbar**: Sciatica, radiculopathy (L5/S1 most common)

#### Spinal Stenosis
- **Congenital** (narrow canal) or **acquired** (degenerative)
- **Central canal stenosis**: Neurogenic claudication (leg pain with walking, relieved by sitting/flexion)
- **Foraminal stenosis**: Radiculopathy

**MRI findings:**
- Narrowed spinal canal
- Ligamentum flavum hypertrophy
- Facet hypertrophy
- Disc bulging

### Spinal Fractures

#### Compression Fracture
- **Osteoporotic** (elderly, minor trauma)
- **Pathologic** (metastases, multiple myeloma)
- **MRI**: Differentiate benign (T1 normal marrow) vs malignant (T1 dark marrow, enhancement)

#### Burst Fracture
- **High-energy trauma**
- **Retropulsion** of fragments into canal (cord injury risk)
- **CT**: Fracture through anterior and posterior vertebral body
- **Unstable**: Surgical fixation often needed

#### Chance Fracture
- **Flexion-distraction** injury (seat belt)
- **Horizontal fracture** through vertebral body and posterior elements
- **Unstable**

### Cauda Equina Syndrome
- **Massive central disc** or other compressive lesion at L1-L2 or below
- **Saddle anesthesia**, bowel/bladder dysfunction, bilateral leg weakness
- **Neurosurgical emergency**: Decompression within hours

## Cerebrovascular Imaging

### Carotid Stenosis
- **Stroke risk factor**: Atherosclerosis of carotid bifurcation
- **Ultrasound**: First-line screening (velocity measurements)
- **CTA/MRA**: Confirm degree of stenosis
- **NASCET criteria**: % stenosis based on angiography
  - <50%: Medical management
  - 50-69%: CEA (carotid endarterectomy) for symptomatic
  - â‰¥70%: CEA for symptomatic or asymptomatic with good surgical risk

### Vertebral Artery Dissection
- **Trauma** (often trivial, chiropractic manipulation), **spontaneous**
- **CTA/MRA**: Irregular narrowing, intimal flap, double lumen
- **Stroke** in posterior circulation (brainstem, cerebellum, occipital)

### Aneurysm
- **Saccular (berry)**: Most common, anterior circulation (ACA, MCA, PComm), rupture causes SAH
- **Fusiform**: Atherosclerotic, basilar artery
- **Screening**: CTA or MRA for family history (2+ first-degree relatives), polycystic kidney disease
- **Size and rupture risk**: <7 mm low risk, >7 mm higher risk

### AVM (Arteriovenous Malformation)
- **Tangle** of abnormal vessels, nidus
- **Hemorrhage risk**: 2-4% per year
- **CT/MRI**: Enlarged vessels, calcification, prior hemorrhage
- **Catheter angiography**: Define anatomy for treatment planning

## Hydrocephalus

### Types
- **Communicating**: CSF absorption impaired (arachnoid granulations), all ventricles dilated
- **Non-communicating (obstructive)**: CSF flow blocked (tumor, aqueductal stenosis), selective ventricular dilation

### CT/MRI Findings
- **Ventricular enlargement**: Out of proportion to sulcal prominence
- **Periventricular edema**: T2/FLAIR bright (transependymal CSF flow)
- **Cause identification**: Mass, aqueductal stenosis, prior hemorrhage/infection

### Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH)
- **Triad**: Gait apraxia, urinary incontinence, dementia ("wet, wacky, wobbly")
- **Enlarged ventricles** with normal opening pressure on LP
- **Treatment**: Ventriculoperitoneal shunt (may improve symptoms)

## Infections

### Meningitis
- **CT often normal**: Diagnosis by LP
- **Contrast MRI**: Meningeal enhancement
- **Complications**: Hydrocephalus, abscess, venous thrombosis, infarct

### Encephalitis
- **Herpes simplex**: Temporal lobe T2/FLAIR bright, hemorrhage, restricted diffusion
- **Other viral**: Variable patterns
- **MRI > CT**

### Abscess
- See brain masses section above
- **Causes**: Sinusitis, mastoiditis, endocarditis, dental

## Pediatric Considerations

### Shaken Baby Syndrome (Abusive Head Trauma)
- **Subdural hematomas**: Often bilateral, different ages
- **Retinal hemorrhages**
- **Diffuse axonal injury**, cerebral edema
- **Mechanism**: Violent shaking

### Posterior Fossa Tumors (Children)
- **Medulloblastoma**: Midline (vermis), solid, enhances
- **Pilocytic astrocytoma**: Cystic with enhancing nodule
- **Ependymoma**: Fourth ventricle, heterogeneous

## Summary

Neuroimaging is essential for acute and chronic neurological diagnosis. Non-contrast CT head is first-line for stroke, hemorrhage, and trauma due to speed and availability, identifying hyperdense acute blood and early ischemic changes. MRI provides superior soft tissue resolution for tumor characterization, MS, chronic ischemia, and spinal cord pathology, with DWI highly sensitive for acute stroke. CTA rapidly assesses for large vessel occlusion guiding thrombectomy, aneurysm detection, and vascular dissection. Systematic interpretation includes assessment for blood, parenchymal abnormalities, CSF spaces, and mass effect. Common pathologies include ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke, subdural/epidural hematomas, brain masses (glioblastoma, metastases, meningioma), and spinal cord compression. Understanding appropriate modality selection, urgent vs non-urgent indications, and recognition of life-threatening conditions (acute stroke, hemorrhage, cord compression, herniation) is critical for optimal patient care in neurological emergencies and chronic disease management.
