# Abdominal Imaging

## Overview

Abdominal imaging encompasses multiple modalities for evaluating the gastrointestinal tract, solid organs, vasculature, and peritoneal cavity. With the acute abdomen being a common presentation requiring urgent diagnosis, understanding appropriate imaging selection, systematic interpretation, and recognition of key pathologies is essential. This chapter focuses on practical approaches to abdominal X-rays, ultrasound, CT, and MRI, with emphasis on emergency and common conditions encountered in clinical practice.

### Imaging Modalities
- **Abdominal X-ray (KUB)**: Limited role, bowel obstruction, free air
- **Ultrasound**: First-line for biliary, obstetric, pediatric, renal
- **CT abdomen/pelvis**: Workhorse for acute abdomen, trauma, oncology
- **MRI abdomen**: MRCP for biliary, liver lesion characterization, avoid radiation
- **Fluoroscopy**: Upper GI series, barium enema, contrast studies
- **Nuclear medicine**: HIDA scan for cholecystitis, bleeding scan

## Abdominal X-Ray (KUB)

### Technique
- **KUB**: Kidneys, ureters, bladder (misnomer, visualizes bowel gas pattern)
- **Supine**: Standard view
- **Upright**: Detect free air (requires 5-10 minutes upright first)
- **Left lateral decubitus**: Alternative if cannot stand, free air rises to right flank

### Normal Bowel Gas Pattern
- **Stomach**: Air-fluid level normal
- **Small bowel**: 2-3 cm diameter, valvulae conniventes (complete folds)
- **Large bowel**: <6 cm (9 cm cecum), haustra (incomplete folds)
- **Rectum**: Usually some air present

### Bowel Obstruction

#### Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)
**Radiographic findings:**
- Dilated small bowel loops (>3 cm)
- Air-fluid levels on upright (string of pearls)
- Paucity of gas in colon
- Valvulae conniventes visible (crossing entire width)

**Etiologies:**
- Adhesions (60%): Prior surgery
- Hernias (15%): Inguinal, ventral, internal
- Neoplasm (10%): Intrinsic or extrinsic
- Crohn disease: Strictures
- Others: Gallstone ileus, intussusception, volvulus

**CT findings (superior to X-ray):**
- Transition point (dilated proximal, collapsed distal)
- Cause identification (mass, hernia, adhesive band)
- Closed-loop obstruction: C-shaped/U-shaped loop, "whirl sign"
- Ischemia: Bowel wall thickening, pneumatosis, portal venous gas

#### Large Bowel Obstruction (LBO)
**Radiographic findings:**
- Dilated colon (>6 cm, cecum >9 cm)
- Haustra visible
- Collapsed distal to obstruction
- Competent ileocecal valve: Isolated colonic dilation
- Incompetent valve: Small bowel also dilated

**Etiologies:**
- Colon cancer (60%): Stricture, apple-core lesion
- Diverticulitis (20%): Inflammation, abscess
- Volvulus (5%): Sigmoid (coffee bean sign), cecal
- Others: Fecal impaction, stricture, hernia

**Sigmoid volvulus:**
- Coffee bean/omega sign
- Loss of haustra at twist point
- Massively dilated sigmoid extending to upper abdomen

**Cecal volvulus:**
- Dilated cecum in abnormal location (LUQ)
- "Bird's beak" at twist point

#### Paralytic Ileus
**Radiographic findings:**
- Diffuse gaseous distension
- Small and large bowel involved
- No transition point
- No mechanical obstruction

**Causes:**
- Postoperative (most common)
- Medications: Opioids, anticholinergics
- Electrolyte abnormalities: Hypokalemia
- Sepsis, peritonitis
- Ischemia

### Pneumoperitoneum (Free Air)

#### Detection
**Upright CXR (most sensitive):**
- Air under diaphragm (as little as 1-2 mL)
- Bilateral = definite pneumoperitoneum
- Right side more sensitive (liver provides dark background)

**Upright abdominal X-ray:**
- Air under diaphragm
- Rigler sign: Both sides of bowel wall visible (usually only inner)
- Football sign (supine): Large oval lucency (massive free air)

**CT (gold standard):**
- Air in non-dependent peritoneum
- Very sensitive, even tiny amounts

#### Etiologies
- **Perforated viscus**: Peptic ulcer, diverticulitis, appendicitis, ischemic bowel, trauma
- **Iatrogenic**: Post-laparoscopy (normal <3 days), peritoneal dialysis
- **Non-surgical**: Post-endoscopy, pneumatosis with dissection

### Other X-Ray Findings
- **Calcifications**: Kidney stones, gallstones (10-15% radio-opaque), pancreatic calcifications (chronic pancreatitis), vascular (atherosclerosis), appendicolith
- **Organomegaly**: Hepatomegaly (liver edge below right kidney), splenomegaly (spleen tip >11th rib)
- **Masses**: Displacement of bowel gas, soft tissue density

## Ultrasound

### Advantages
- **No radiation**: Safe in pregnancy, children
- **Real-time**: Dynamic assessment, operator-dependent
- **Portable**: Bedside, EFAST in trauma
- **Inexpensive**: Compared to CT/MRI
- **Guidance**: Procedures (biopsy, drainage)

### Limitations
- **Operator-dependent**: Skill and experience critical
- **Body habitus**: Difficult in obesity
- **Bowel gas**: Obscures deep structures
- **Bone**: Cannot penetrate (ribs limit liver dome)

### Right Upper Quadrant Ultrasound

#### Gallbladder and Biliary
**Normal anatomy:**
- Gallbladder: Thin wall (<3 mm), anechoic (dark) lumen
- Common bile duct: <6 mm diameter (<7 mm if post-cholecystectomy, +1 mm per decade >50)

**Cholelithiasis (gallstones):**
- Echogenic (bright) focus
- Posterior acoustic shadow
- Moves with position (gravity-dependent)

**Acute cholecystitis:**
- **Gallstones**: Usually present
- **Sonographic Murphy sign**: Maximal tenderness when transducer over GB
- **Wall thickening**: >3 mm
- **Pericholecystic fluid**: Edema, inflammation
- **Distension**: GB >10 cm length, >5 cm width

**Choledocholithiasis (CBD stone):**
- Echogenic focus in CBD with shadow
- Dilated CBD (>6 mm, adjust for age)
- ERCP or MRCP for confirmation

**Biliary dilation:**
- Intrahepatic: Parallel channel sign (dilated ducts alongside portal veins)
- Extrahepatic: Dilated CBD
- Causes: Stone, stricture, mass (pancreatic head, cholangiocarcinoma)

#### Liver
**Hepatomegaly:**
- Span >15-16 cm
- Extension beyond right kidney

**Hepatic steatosis (fatty liver):**
- Increased echogenicity (bright liver)
- Hepatorenal contrast (liver brighter than kidney)
- Posterior attenuation

**Cirrhosis:**
- Nodular surface contour
- Heterogeneous echotexture
- Enlarged caudate lobe
- Portal hypertension signs: Splenomegaly, ascites, varices

**Focal lesions:**
- Cyst: Anechoic, thin wall, posterior enhancement
- Hemangioma: Hyperechoic, well-defined (CT/MRI for characterization)
- Hepatocellular carcinoma: Variable echogenicity, arterial enhancement on Doppler
- Metastases: Hypoechoic or mixed, multiple often

**Portal vein:**
- Normal: Hepatopetal flow (toward liver)
- Thrombosis: Echogenic thrombus, no flow on Doppler

#### Kidneys
**Normal:**
- Bean-shaped, cortex less echogenic than liver
- Cortex, medulla (pyramids), collecting system

**Hydronephrosis:**
- Dilated collecting system (anechoic)
- Mild: Prominent renal pelvis
- Moderate: Calyces dilated
- Severe: Cortical thinning

**Nephrolithiasis:**
- Echogenic focus, posterior shadow
- May be in calyx, pelvis, or ureter (if visible)
- Often associated with hydronephrosis

**Renal masses:**
- **Simple cyst**: Anechoic, thin wall, posterior enhancement, no septations
- **Complex cyst**: Septations, wall thickening, debris (Bosniak classification)
- **Solid mass**: Further characterization with CT/MRI

### Obstetric Ultrasound
- **First trimester**: Confirm intrauterine pregnancy (IUP), gestational sac (4-5 weeks), fetal pole (5-6 weeks), cardiac activity (6 weeks)
- **Ectopic pregnancy**: Empty uterus with hCG >1500-2000 (discriminatory zone), adnexal mass, free fluid
- **Dating**: Crown-rump length (first trimester), biparietal diameter, femur length (later)

### FAST Exam (Trauma)
**Four views:**
1. Perihepatic (Morison's pouch): Most sensitive for free fluid
2. Perisplenic
3. Pelvic (rectovesical/rectouterine pouch)
4. Pericardial

**Positive**: Free fluid (blood) as anechoic collection
**Limitations**: Misses solid organ injury without significant hemorrhage, retroperitoneal bleeding

## CT Abdomen/Pelvis

### Protocols

#### Non-Contrast
**Indications:**
- Kidney stones (definitive imaging)
- Hemorrhage assessment
- Contraindication to IV contrast

**Findings:**
- Stones (bright on non-contrast)
- Hemorrhage (hyperdense in acute)
- Masses (limited characterization)

#### IV Contrast-Enhanced
**Phases:**
- **Arterial phase**: 25-30 seconds post-injection (vascular anatomy, hypervascular tumors, active bleeding)
- **Portal venous phase**: 65-70 seconds (standard abdominal imaging, liver parenchyma, most pathology)
- **Delayed phase**: 3-5+ minutes (collecting system, urothelial tumors, bladder)

**Most common**: Single phase portal venous for routine abdomen/pelvis

#### Oral Contrast
**Positive contrast** (barium or iodinated):
- Opacifies bowel lumen
- Distinguish bowel from other structures
- Detect leaks (extravasation)

**Water** (neutral):
- Allows mucosal enhancement
- CTA, enterography

**Trend**: Decreasing routine oral contrast use (delays imaging, aspiration risk, adequate without in most cases)

### Systematic Interpretation

#### Windows
- **Soft tissue**: Standard viewing (liver, spleen, kidneys)
- **Lung**: Detect free air, lung bases pathology
- **Bone**: Fractures, lytic/blastic lesions

#### Approach
1. **Patient/study information**: Confirm correct patient, IV contrast phase
2. **Bones**: Spine, ribs, pelvis for fractures, lesions
3. **Lungs**: Lung bases for infiltrate, effusion, masses
4. **Liver**: Size, lesions, ducts
5. **Biliary**: Gallbladder, CBD, stones, dilation
6. **Spleen**: Size, lesions, injury
7. **Pancreas**: Size, enhancement, duct dilation, masses
8. **Kidneys**: Size, lesions, stones, hydronephrosis, enhancement
9. **Adrenals**: Masses, hemorrhage
10. **Bowel**: Wall thickening, obstruction, perforation, inflammation
11. **Vessels**: Aorta, IVC, mesenteric vessels, thrombosis, aneurysm
12. **Peritoneum**: Free fluid, fat stranding, free air
13. **Lymph nodes**: Enlarged (>1 cm short axis)
14. **Pelvis**: Bladder, uterus/ovaries or prostate, rectum
15. **Soft tissues**: Hematoma, abscess, hernia

### Common Acute Conditions

#### Appendicitis
**CT findings:**
- Dilated appendix (>6 mm diameter)
- Wall thickening, enhancement
- Periappendiceal fat stranding
- Appendicolith (30%)
- Fluid/abscess if perforated

**Complications:**
- Perforation: Localized abscess, free air/fluid
- Peritonitis: Diffuse free fluid, peritoneal enhancement

#### Diverticulitis
**CT findings:**
- Colonic wall thickening (usually sigmoid)
- Pericolic fat stranding
- Diverticula (outpouchings)
- Abscess (low-density fluid collection)

**Hinchey classification:**
- Stage 0: Uncomplicated
- Stage Ia: Pericolic abscess
- Stage Ib: Pelvic abscess
- Stage II: Distant abscess (retroperitoneal)
- Stage III: Purulent peritonitis (no fecal matter)
- Stage IV: Fecal peritonitis

**Management implications:**
- Uncomplicated: Antibiotics, possible outpatient
- Abscess <4 cm: Antibiotics
- Abscess >4 cm: Percutaneous drainage
- Perforation/peritonitis: Surgery

#### Pancreatitis
**CT findings (acute):**
- Enlarged, edematous pancreas
- Peripancreatic fat stranding
- Fluid collections
- **Necrotizing pancreatitis**: Lack of enhancement (>30% gland, worse prognosis)

**Balthazar CT Severity Index:**
- Grade A-E based on extent of inflammation and necrosis

**Complications:**
- Pseudocyst: Fluid collection with defined wall, >4 weeks
- Abscess: Infected necrosis/collection
- Vascular: Pseudoaneurysm, thrombosis

**Chronic pancreatitis:**
- Atrophy, calcifications, duct dilation, pseudocysts

#### Small Bowel Obstruction
**CT advantages over X-ray:**
- Identify transition point
- Determine cause (adhesions, hernia, mass, intussusception)
- Detect closed-loop obstruction
- Assess for ischemia

**Ischemic bowel:**
- Bowel wall thickening or thinning
- Absent enhancement (dead bowel)
- Pneumatosis intestinalis (gas in bowel wall)
- Portal venous gas (advanced ischemia, grave sign)
- Mesenteric vessel occlusion
- Ascites

#### Mesenteric Ischemia
**Arterial:**
- **Acute mesenteric ischemia**: Embolus (SMA most common), thrombosis, non-occlusive (shock)
- **CT findings**: Vessel occlusion, bowel wall thinning/pneumatosis, portal venous gas, free fluid
- **Emergency**: High mortality without urgent surgery

**Venous:**
- SMV/portal vein thrombosis
- Hypercoagulable state, portal hypertension, inflammation
- Bowel wall thickening, mesenteric edema, vessel thrombosis

#### Urolithiasis
**Non-contrast CT (gold standard):**
- Hyperdense stone in collecting system, ureter, bladder
- Hydronephrosis (upstream dilation)
- Perinephric fat stranding

**Stone size and location:**
- <5 mm: 90% pass spontaneously
- >10 mm: <10% pass
- Proximal ureter, mid-ureter, ureterovesical junction (common sites)

#### Hepatobiliary Masses

**Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC):**
- **Arterial enhancement**: Bright on arterial phase
- **Washout**: Darker on portal venous/delayed
- **Capsule**: May have enhancing capsule
- **Cirrhosis**: Usually present (risk factor)

**Metastases:**
- Multiple lesions common
- Hypovascular (most): Hypoenhancing
- Hypervascular: Neuroendocrine, RCC, melanoma, thyroid

**Hemangioma:**
- Peripheral nodular enhancement (arterial), central fill-in (delayed)
- Common benign liver lesion

**Focal nodular hyperplasia (FNH):**
- Arterial enhancement, isodense portal venous, central scar

**Hepatic adenoma:**
- Well-circumscribed, heterogeneous, hemorrhage risk

#### Renal Masses
**Bosniak classification (cystic masses):**
- I: Simple cyst, benign
- II: Few thin septae, fine calcification, benign
- IIF: More septae/thickening, follow-up needed, 5% malignant
- III: Thick/irregular walls or septae, 50% malignant (surgery)
- IV: Enhancing soft tissue components, 90% malignant (surgery)

**Solid masses:**
- **Renal cell carcinoma**: Enhancing mass (>15 HU increase with contrast)
- **Angiomyolipoma**: Macroscopic fat (negative HU), benign
- **Oncocytoma**: Cannot differentiate from RCC reliably

#### Trauma
**Liver/spleen injury:**
- Graded I-V based on depth, size
- Lacerations: Linear hypoenhancing
- Hematoma: Hypodense (subcapsular or intraparenchymatic)
- Active bleeding: Contrast extravasation

**Management:**
- Most: Non-operative if hemodynamically stable
- Angioembolization: Active bleeding
- Surgery: Unstable, peritonitis

## MRI Abdomen

### Advantages
- **No radiation**: Pediatrics, young adults, repeated imaging
- **Superior soft tissue contrast**: Liver lesion characterization
- **Functional imaging**: Diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI), perfusion
- **MRCP**: Non-invasive biliary/pancreatic duct imaging

### Disadvantages
- **Time**: 30-60 minutes (vs 5 minutes CT)
- **Cost**: Higher than CT
- **Availability**: Limited
- **Contraindications**: Pacemakers, some implants, claustrophobia

### Sequences
- **T1-weighted**: Anatomic detail, fat bright, fluid dark
- **T2-weighted**: Fluid-sensitive (bile ducts, cysts bright)
- **DWI**: Cellular/hypercellular lesions bright (cancer, abscess)
- **In/out-of-phase**: Detect fat (adenoma, steatosis)
- **Dynamic contrast**: Arterial, portal venous, delayed phases

### MRCP (Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography)
**Indications:**
- Choledocholithiasis (alternative to ERCP)
- Pancreatic duct dilation/obstruction
- PSC (primary sclerosing cholangitis)
- Bile duct strictures

**Advantages:**
- Non-invasive (vs ERCP)
- Visualizes entire biliary tree
- No radiation

### Liver Lesion Characterization
- **Hemangioma**: T2 very bright (lightbulb sign)
- **FNH**: Central scar (dark T1, bright T2)
- **HCC**: Arterial enhancement, washout, DWI bright
- **Metastases**: Variable T1/T2, often DWI bright

## Contrast Agents

### IV Iodinated Contrast (CT)
**Adverse reactions:**
- See Chest Imaging section for details
- **Acute kidney injury**: Risk with CKD (eGFR <30), diabetes, dehydration
- **Prevention**: Hydration (oral or IV), hold nephrotoxic drugs, metformin

### Gadolinium (MRI)
**Adverse reactions:**
- **Allergic**: Less common than iodinated contrast
- **Nephrogenic systemic fibrosis (NSF)**: Rare, severe CKD (eGFR <30), avoid if possible
- **Deposition**: Gadolinium deposition in brain (significance unknown, use judiciously)

## Summary

Abdominal imaging utilizes multiple modalities chosen based on clinical question and patient factors. Plain X-rays have limited but important roles for bowel obstruction and pneumoperitoneum. Ultrasound is first-line for biliary disease, renal colic (in some centers), obstetrics, and pediatric patients, offering real-time imaging without radiation. CT abdomen/pelvis is the workhorse for acute abdomen, trauma, and oncologic staging, providing rapid, detailed assessment with high sensitivity for most pathologies. Key acute conditions include appendicitis, diverticulitis, pancreatitis, bowel obstruction, and urolithiasis, each with characteristic imaging findings guiding management. MRI offers superior soft tissue contrast for liver lesion characterization and biliary imaging (MRCP) without radiation, though with longer acquisition times and higher cost. Systematic interpretation, appropriate modality selection, and understanding contrast safety optimize diagnostic accuracy while minimizing risks and costs in abdominal imaging.
