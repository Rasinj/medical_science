# Chest Imaging

## Overview

Chest imaging is one of the most commonly performed radiologic examinations, with chest X-rays (CXR) accounting for approximately 50% of all radiographs obtained. Chest imaging plays a critical role in diagnosing and monitoring pulmonary, cardiac, pleural, mediastinal, and chest wall pathology. Understanding systematic interpretation, recognizing normal anatomy and variants, identifying common pathologies, and knowing when to advance to cross-sectional imaging are essential skills for all clinicians. This chapter focuses on practical chest X-ray interpretation and the appropriate use of CT for thoracic imaging.

### Epidemiology
- **Most common radiograph**: 50% of all X-rays performed
- **Emergency department**: 20-30% of all ED patients receive CXR
- **Hospitalized patients**: >50% have at least one CXR during admission
- **Screening**: Low-dose CT for lung cancer screening in high-risk populations
- **Yield**: 30-40% of CXRs show clinically significant findings

### Imaging Modalities
- **Chest X-ray (CXR)**: First-line, PA and lateral views standard
- **CT chest**: High-resolution cross-sectional imaging
- **MRI chest**: Limited role (cardiac, vascular, brachial plexus)
- **Ultrasound**: Pleural effusions, pneumothorax assessment
- **Nuclear medicine**: V/Q scan for pulmonary embolism, PET for staging
- **Fluoroscopy**: Diaphragm motion, dynamic studies

## Chest X-Ray Technique

### Standard Views

#### Posteroanterior (PA) View
- **Patient position**: Standing, chest against detector, arms abducted
- **X-ray beam**: Posterior to anterior, 6 feet distance
- **Inspiration**: Deep inspiration (9-10 posterior ribs visible)
- **Advantages**: Heart size accurate (minimizes magnification), scapulae rotated out
- **Standard**: Outpatient, routine chest imaging

#### Lateral View
- **Patient position**: Left side against detector (left lateral)
- **Purpose**: Localize abnormalities, assess retrocardiac/retrodiaphragmatic areas
- **Essential**: Part of complete chest series

#### Anteroposterior (AP) View
- **Patient position**: Supine or upright, detector behind patient
- **X-ray beam**: Anterior to posterior, shorter distance
- **Disadvantages**: Heart magnification (~15%), scapulae not rotated out, often lower inspiration
- **Indications**: Portable (ICU, ED), unable to stand
- **Always label**: "AP" to alert interpreter

### Additional Views
- **Lordotic**: Apices visualization (TB, masses)
- **Lateral decubitus**: Layering pleural effusions, air-fluid levels
- **Expiratory**: Pneumothorax detection, air trapping
- **Oblique**: Rib fractures, focal areas

### Technical Quality Assessment

#### RIPE mnemonic
- **R**otation: Medial clavicle ends equidistant from spinous processes
- **I**nspiration: 9-10 posterior ribs, 5-6 anterior ribs visible above diaphragm
- **P**enetration: Vertebral bodies visible through heart, disc spaces visible
- **E**xposure: Adequate to see peripheral vessels, not overexposed (too dark) or underexposed (too white)

## Systematic Interpretation

### ABCDEFs Approach

#### A - Airway
- **Trachea**: Midline, deviation suggests mass, pneumothorax, atelectasis
- **Carina**: Level of T4-5, angle <100°
- **Main bronchi**: Right more vertical (aspiration common), left more horizontal
- **Abnormal**: Deviation, narrowing, mass

#### B - Bones
- **Ribs**: Count (12 pairs), fractures, lytic/blastic lesions
- **Clavicles**: Fractures, dislocations
- **Spine**: Alignment, height, lytic/blastic lesions
- **Scapulae**: Position, fractures
- **Shoulders**: Glenohumeral joints, degenerative changes

#### C - Cardiac Silhouette
- **Size**: Cardiothoracic ratio <0.5 on PA (0.55 on AP acceptable)
- **Borders**: Right (RA), left (LV apex, LA appendage, pulmonary artery, aortic knob)
- **Enlarged chambers**: Specific patterns
  - Left atrium: Double density behind heart, splaying of carina, elevated left mainstem bronchus
  - Left ventricle: Apex below diaphragm, extends left
  - Right atrium: Enlargement rightward
  - Right ventricle: Lifts apex (boot-shaped heart)
- **Pericardial effusion**: Globular, "water bottle" heart

#### D - Diaphragm
- **Normal position**: Right hemidiaphragm 1-2 cm higher than left (liver)
- **Costophrenic angles**: Sharp, blunting suggests effusion
- **Cardiophrenic angles**: Assess for masses, fat pads
- **Free air**: Lucency below diaphragm (upright film)
- **Flattening**: COPD, hyperinflation
- **Elevation**: Atelectasis, subpulmonic effusion, diaphragm paralysis

#### E - Everything Else (Mediastinum, Soft Tissues)
- **Mediastinal contours**: Aortic knob, aortopulmonary window, azygos vein
- **Widened mediastinum**: Aortic dissection, lymphadenopathy, hemorrhage
- **Hilar regions**: Vascular structures, lymph nodes
- **Soft tissues**: Subcutaneous emphysema, breast shadows, chest wall masses
- **Tubes/lines**: ETT (2-4 cm above carina), NGT (through GE junction), central lines (SVC/RA junction), chest tubes

#### F - Lung Fields
- **Upper zones**: Apices to 2nd anterior rib
- **Mid zones**: 2nd to 4th anterior ribs
- **Lower zones**: Below 4th anterior rib
- **Vascularity**: Vessels taper toward periphery, upper lobe vessels smaller than lower lobe
- **Symmetry**: Compare side to side
- **Abnormalities**: Opacities, lucencies, nodules, masses, consolidation, interstitial patterns

### Silhouette Sign
- **Principle**: Two structures of same density abutting each other lose their border
- **Applications**:
  - Lost right heart border: Right middle lobe or anterior segment right upper lobe pathology
  - Lost left heart border: Lingula pathology
  - Lost diaphragm: Lower lobe pathology
  - Preserved borders: Pathology not adjacent (anterior or posterior)

### Lateral View Interpretation
- **Retrosternal clear space**: 3 cm, abnormal soft tissue suggests anterior mediastinal mass
- **Retrocardiac space**: Should see vasculature, opacity suggests lower lobe pathology
- **Posterior costophrenic sulci**: Deeper than on PA, earliest site for pleural fluid
- **Spine**: Should get darker inferiorly (cervicothoracic sign), white = lower lobe consolidation

## Common Pathologies

### Airspace Disease (Alveolar Filling)

#### Characteristics
- **Air bronchograms**: Air-filled bronchi visible against opacified lung
- **Fluffy margins**: Ill-defined borders
- **Confluence**: Coalesces with adjacent opacities
- **Lobar/segmental distribution**: Anatomic boundaries

#### Pneumonia
- **Lobar pneumonia**: Homogeneous consolidation of entire lobe
  - Streptococcus pneumoniae classic
  - Dense, air bronchograms, volume preserved
- **Bronchopneumonia**: Patchy, multifocal consolidation
  - Staph aureus, Haemophilus, Klebsiella
  - Peribronchovascular distribution
- **Interstitial pneumonia**: Reticular, nodular pattern
  - Viral, atypical organisms (Mycoplasma)
  - Ground-glass opacities

**Complications:**
- Parapneumonic effusion: Blunted costophrenic angle
- Empyema: Loculated fluid, thick walls
- Abscess: Cavity with air-fluid level, thick wall
- Pneumatoceles: Thin-walled cystic spaces (Staph, Pneumocystis)

#### Pulmonary Edema
- **Cardiogenic**: Enlarged heart, cephalization, Kerley B lines (interlobular septal thickening), bilateral symmetric perihilar "bat wing" opacities, pleural effusions
- **Non-cardiogenic (ARDS)**: Normal heart size, bilateral diffuse airspace disease, often peripheral
- **Progression**: Vascular redistribution → interstitial edema (Kerley lines) → alveolar edema

#### Aspiration
- **Location**: Posterior segment RUL or superior segment RLL (supine), basilar segments (upright)
- **Presentation**: Airspace opacity in dependent regions
- **Risk factors**: Altered mental status, dysphagia, elderly

### Interstitial Lung Disease

#### Patterns
- **Reticular**: Lines (intra/interlobular septa, irregular)
  - Chronic fibrosis, lymphangitic carcinomatosis
- **Nodular**: Small nodules (<1 cm)
  - Miliary TB, sarcoidosis, hypersensitivity pneumonitis
- **Reticulonodular**: Combined
  - Viral pneumonia, interstitial edema
- **Ground-glass**: Hazy increased opacity, vessels still visible (better on CT)
  - Hypersensitivity pneumonitis, PCP, early pulmonary edema
- **Honeycombing**: Cystic spaces, end-stage fibrosis (CT finding)

#### Specific Diseases
- **Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF)**: Lower lobe reticular pattern, honeycombing, volume loss
- **Sarcoidosis**: Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy, upper/mid lung reticulonodular pattern
- **Hypersensitivity pneumonitis**: Upper lobe predominance, ground-glass, nodules
- **Pneumoconioses**: Silicosis (upper lobe nodules, eggshell calcifications), asbestosis (lower lobe, pleural plaques)

### Atelectasis

#### Mechanisms
- **Obstructive**: Airway obstruction (mucus plug, tumor, foreign body)
- **Compressive**: External compression (effusion, pneumothorax)
- **Adhesive**: Surfactant deficiency (ARDS)
- **Cicatrization**: Fibrosis/scarring
- **Relaxation**: Pneumothorax

#### Findings
- **Direct signs**: Displaced fissures, crowded vessels/bronchi
- **Indirect signs**:
  - Volume loss: Elevated diaphragm, mediastinal shift toward atelectasis, rib crowding
  - Compensatory hyperinflation: Adjacent lung
  - Silhouette sign: Lost borders
- **Lobar patterns**:
  - RUL: Elevated minor fissure, apex opacity
  - RML: Loss of right heart border, triangular opacity on lateral
  - RLL: Retrocardiac opacity, preserved right heart border
  - LUL: Veil-like opacity, elevation of left hilum
  - LLL: Sail sign, retrocardiac opacity, lost left hemidiaphragm

### Pleural Effusion

#### Detection
- **Upright**: Blunting of costophrenic angle (>50 mL), meniscus sign, tracking up lateral chest wall
- **Lateral**: Posterior costophrenic sulcus blunting (most sensitive, detects ~50 mL)
- **Supine (AP portable)**: Hazy increased opacity, apical cap
- **Massive**: Complete white-out, mediastinal shift to contralateral side
- **Loculated**: Lenticular opacity, does not layer freely

#### Lateral Decubitus View
- **Technique**: Patient lies on affected side
- **Purpose**: Differentiate free-flowing vs loculated
- **Mobile**: Layers along dependent chest wall (>1 cm thickness suggests tappable)
- **Fixed**: Does not move, suggests loculation or pleural thickening

#### Differential Diagnosis
- **Transudative**: CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome
- **Exudative**: Pneumonia (parapneumonic), malignancy, PE, TB, pancreatitis
- **Hemorrhagic**: Trauma, malignancy, anticoagulation
- **Chylous**: Thoracic duct injury, lymphoma

### Pneumothorax

#### Types
- **Simple**: Partial lung collapse, no mediastinal shift
- **Tension**: Mediastinal shift away, hemidiaphragm depression, cardiovascular compromise (emergency)
- **Hydropneumothorax**: Air-fluid level (trauma, bronchopleural fistula, infection)

#### Detection
- **Upright**: White visceral pleural line separated from chest wall by lucent air, absent lung markings peripheral to line
- **Expiratory view**: Accentuates pneumothorax (lung gets smaller, PTX more apparent)
- **Supine (AP portable)**: Deep sulcus sign (deep lateral costophrenic angle), increased lucency, sharper cardiac/diaphragm borders
- **Tension**: Mediastinal shift contralateral, diaphragm depression ipsilateral, compressed IVC/heart

#### Sizing
- **Small**: Apex to cupola distance <3 cm
- **Large**: ≥3 cm or >50% hemithorax
- **Percentage**: Rough estimate, not precise (British Thoracic Society vs American College of Chest Physicians formulas differ)

#### Mimics
- **Skin fold**: Line does not follow pleural contour, vessels visible beyond
- **Scapula**: Superimposed scapula creates lucent area
- **Bullae**: Thin-walled cystic spaces within lung

### Pulmonary Nodule/Mass

#### Definition
- **Nodule**: Rounded opacity ≤3 cm
- **Mass**: Rounded opacity >3 cm (higher malignancy risk)

#### Solitary Pulmonary Nodule (SPN) Evaluation

**Fleischner Society Guidelines (2017):**
- **Solid nodule <6 mm**: No routine follow-up (low risk) or optional 12-month CT (multiple risk factors)
- **6-8 mm**: CT 6-12 months, then 18-24 months
- **>8 mm**: CT 3 months, PET, or biopsy

**Benign features:**
- **Calcification**: Central, diffuse, laminated, popcorn (hamartoma)
- **Stability**: Unchanged for 2 years
- **Smooth margins**: Round, well-defined
- **Fat**: Hamartoma

**Malignant features:**
- **Spiculated margins**: Corona radiata
- **Upper lobe**: Higher risk location
- **Growth**: Increase in size on follow-up
- **Cavitation**: Thick irregular walls
- **Large size**: >3 cm

**Management:**
- Compare to prior imaging (most important)
- Assess for benign calcification
- Determine follow-up per Fleischner guidelines
- Consider PET if >8 mm and risk factors
- Biopsy for high suspicion or PET-avid

### Lung Cancer

#### Types
- **Non-small cell** (80%): Adenocarcinoma (peripheral), squamous cell (central, cavitation), large cell
- **Small cell** (15%): Central, aggressive, mediastinal adenopathy
- **Carcinoid**: Central, well-defined
- **Metastases**: Multiple nodules, lower lobes, "cannonball" metastases

#### Findings
- **Primary tumor**: Mass, nodule, often spiculated
- **Hilar/mediastinal lymphadenopathy**: Bulky lymph nodes
- **Atelectasis**: Post-obstructive
- **Pleural effusion**: Malignant effusion
- **Rib destruction**: Chest wall invasion (Pancoast tumor at apex)
- **Staging**: CT chest/abdomen/pelvis, PET-CT, brain MRI

### Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)

#### Findings
- **Hyperinflation**: Flattened diaphragms (<1.5 cm excursion), >10 posterior ribs visible, increased AP diameter
- **Increased lung lucency**: Diminished vascular markings
- **Bullae**: Lucent thin-walled spaces >1 cm
- **Narrow cardiac silhouette**: Vertical heart
- **Rapid tapering**: Peripheral vessels

#### Complications
- **Pneumothorax**: Secondary spontaneous PTX
- **Pneumonia**: Difficult to assess on CXR
- **Lung cancer**: Increased risk

### Congestive Heart Failure (CHF)

#### Radiographic Stages
1. **Stage 1 (redistribution)**: Cephalization - upper lobe vessels equal or larger than lower lobe (pulmonary venous hypertension)
2. **Stage 2 (interstitial edema)**: Kerley B lines (peripheral horizontal septal lines), peribronchial cuffing, hazy vessels
3. **Stage 3 (alveolar edema)**: Bilateral perihilar "bat wing" airspace opacities, air bronchograms
4. **Pleural effusions**: Bilateral, right > left typically

#### Differentiation from ARDS
- **CHF**: Cardiomegaly, upper lobe redistribution, Kerley lines, central distribution
- **ARDS**: Normal heart size, bilateral diffuse airspace disease, peripheral distribution

### Mediastinal Masses

#### Anterior Mediastinum (4 Ts)
- **Thyroid**: Substernal goiter, continuous with cervical thyroid, displaces trachea
- **Thymus**: Thymoma, thymic hyperplasia (children), associated with myasthenia gravis
- **Teratoma**: Germ cell tumor, may contain fat/teeth/calcium
- **Terrible lymphoma**: Lymphoma (especially Hodgkin), bulky adenopathy

#### Middle Mediastinum
- **Lymphadenopathy**: Sarcoidosis (bilateral symmetric hilar), TB, lymphoma, metastases
- **Vascular**: Aortic aneurysm, dilated pulmonary artery
- **Foregut cysts**: Bronchogenic cyst, esophageal duplication

#### Posterior Mediastinum
- **Neurogenic tumors**: Schwannoma, neurofibroma, neuroblastoma (children)
- **Esophageal**: Achalasia (dilated esophagus, air-fluid level), tumor
- **Vertebral**: Osteomyelitis, metastases

#### Imaging Approach
- **CT chest with contrast**: First-line for characterization
- **MRI**: Vascular lesions, neurogenic tumors, brachial plexus
- **PET-CT**: Lymphoma staging, malignancy assessment

## CT Chest

### Indications
- **Characterize CXR abnormality**: Nodule, mass, mediastinal abnormality
- **Interstitial lung disease**: High-resolution CT (HRCT)
- **Pulmonary embolism**: CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA)
- **Lung cancer screening**: Low-dose CT (LDCT) for high-risk patients
- **Trauma**: Aortic injury, rib fractures, pneumothorax, hemothorax, pulmonary contusion
- **Staging**: Lung cancer, lymphoma, metastatic disease
- **Infection**: Immunocompromised, complications (abscess, empyema)

### Lung Cancer Screening
- **USPSTF 2021 recommendations**:
  - Age 50-80 years
  - 20 pack-year smoking history
  - Current smoker or quit within past 15 years
  - Annual low-dose CT
- **NELSON, NLST trials**: 20-24% reduction in lung cancer mortality

### High-Resolution CT (HRCT)
- **Technique**: Thin slices (1-2 mm), high spatial resolution
- **Indications**: Interstitial lung disease characterization
- **Patterns**:
  - Usual interstitial pneumonia (UIP): Basal, peripheral reticular, honeycombing (IPF)
  - Non-specific interstitial pneumonia (NSIP): Ground-glass, traction bronchiectasis, no honeycombing
  - Organizing pneumonia: Consolidation, peribronchovascular, migratory
  - Hypersensitivity pneumonitis: Ground-glass, centrilobular nodules, mosaic attenuation

### CT Pulmonary Angiography (CTPA)
- **Indication**: Suspected pulmonary embolism
- **Protocol**: IV contrast, timed for pulmonary artery opacification
- **Findings**: Filling defect in pulmonary artery, enlarged RV (strain)
- **Sensitivity/specificity**: >90% for segmental and larger PEs
- **Alternative**: V/Q scan if contrast contraindication, pregnant

## Special Populations

### Pediatric
- **Lower radiation dose**: ALARA protocols
- **Thymus**: Normal anterior mediastinal soft tissue (sail sign), involutes with age/stress
- **Foreign body aspiration**: Expiratory/decubitus films show air trapping
- **Pneumonia**: Viral common (perihilar), bacterial (lobar consolidation)

### Pregnancy
- **Fetal exposure**: CXR <0.001 Gy (minimal risk)
- **Shielding**: Abdomen/pelvis
- **V/Q scan**: Preferred over CTPA for PE (lower fetal dose)
- **Physiologic changes**: Mild hypervolemia, elevated diaphragm (obscures bases)

### Intensive Care
- **AP portable**: Standard, assess ETT, lines, support devices
- **Daily CXR**: Controversial routine practice, directed imaging preferred
- **Common findings**: Atelectasis, edema, pneumonia, ARDS, pneumothorax, line malposition

## Tubes and Lines

### Endotracheal Tube (ETT)
- **Position**: Tip 2-4 cm above carina (T4-5 level)
- **Too high**: Inadvertent extubation risk
- **Too low**: Right mainstem intubation (left lung collapse), carina erosion
- **Remember**: Tube moves with neck (flexion lowers 2 cm, extension raises 2 cm)

### Central Venous Catheter
- **Tip position**: Superior vena cava/right atrial junction
- **Complications**: Pneumothorax, hemothorax, arterial puncture, line kink/fracture
- **Malposition**: Azygos vein, contralateral internal jugular, thyroid vein

### Nasogastric Tube (NGT)
- **Position**: Stomach, below diaphragm
- **Coiling**: Esophagus or mouth (reposition)
- **Bronchial**: Accidental bronchial placement (reposition immediately)

### Chest Tube
- **Position**: Pleural space (for air: apical; for fluid: basilar)
- **Complications**: Malposition (fissure, lung parenchyma), re-expansion pulmonary edema

## Contrast and Radiation

### IV Contrast (Iodinated)
- **Indications**: Vascular assessment (PE, dissection), tumor enhancement, infection
- **Contraindications**: Severe allergic reaction (relative), renal insufficiency (caution)
- **Adverse reactions**:
  - Allergic: Urticaria (mild), bronchospasm/angioedema (moderate), anaphylaxis (severe)
  - Renal: Contrast-induced nephropathy (risk if CrCl <30, diabetes, dehydration)
  - Thyroid: Hyperthyroidism (iodine load)
- **Prophylaxis**: Prednisone 50 mg PO 13, 7, 1 hour before + diphenhydramine 50 mg (if prior reaction)
- **Metformin**: Hold 48 hours after contrast if renal insufficiency

### Radiation Exposure
- **CXR**: 0.02 mSv (2-3 days background)
- **CT chest**: 7 mSv (2+ years background)
- **Risks**: Cancer (stochastic), tissue injury (deterministic, very high doses)
- **ALARA**: Minimize dose, avoid unnecessary imaging, use lowest dose protocols
- **Pregnancy**: Minimize radiation; if essential, shield abdomen; fetal dose usually minimal from chest imaging

## Summary

Chest imaging is fundamental to clinical medicine, with chest X-rays being the most common radiologic study. Systematic interpretation using the ABCDEFs approach ensures comprehensive evaluation. Common pathologies include pneumonia, pulmonary edema, COPD, pneumothorax, pleural effusion, and lung masses. Understanding the silhouette sign helps localize disease. CT chest provides detailed cross-sectional imaging for characterizing abnormalities, staging malignancy, and evaluating complex pathology. High-resolution CT is essential for interstitial lung disease, while CTPA is the standard for pulmonary embolism diagnosis. Lung cancer screening with low-dose CT reduces mortality in high-risk populations. Recognizing normal anatomy, common pathologies, and appropriate indications for imaging advances clinical care while minimizing radiation exposure. Correlation of imaging findings with clinical presentation, prior studies, and evidence-based guidelines optimizes patient outcomes.
