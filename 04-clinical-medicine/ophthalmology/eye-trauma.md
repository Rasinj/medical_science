# Eye Trauma

## Overview

Ocular trauma is a major cause of monocular blindness and visual morbidity worldwide, affecting over 1.6 million people annually with vision loss from eye injuries. Injuries range from minor corneal abrasions to devastating open globe injuries, with mechanisms including blunt trauma, penetrating injuries, chemical burns, and radiation damage. Eye trauma disproportionately affects young males and is often work-related or sports-related. Prompt recognition, appropriate emergency management, and timely referral to ophthalmology are critical for optimizing visual outcomes. Understanding injury mechanisms, classification systems, emergency assessment, and treatment principles is essential for all clinicians who may encounter ocular trauma.

### Epidemiology
- **Incidence**: 2.4 million eye injuries annually in the US
- **Hospitalizations**: ~40,000 per year in the US
- **Blindness**: Leading cause of monocular blindness (traumatic optic neuropathy, severe globe injury)
- **Demographics**: 70% male, peak age 20-30 years
- **Occupational**: 40% work-related (construction, manufacturing, agriculture)
- **Recreational**: Sports, fireworks, assault
- **Pediatric**: High-risk population (BB guns, projectiles, falls)

### Classification
- **Closed globe injury**: Globe wall integrity maintained
  - Contusion, lamellar laceration
- **Open globe injury**: Full-thickness wound of globe wall
  - Rupture, laceration, penetrating, perforating, intraocular foreign body (IOFB)
- **Chemical injury**: Acid or alkali burns
- **Thermal injury**: Heat-related damage
- **Radiation injury**: UV, infrared, ionizing radiation

### Prevention
- **Safety eyewear**: Occupational settings, sports, home projects
- **Polycarbonate lenses**: Impact-resistant
- **Education**: Awareness of hazards
- **Legislation**: Workplace safety regulations

## Initial Assessment and Management

### Primary Survey (Life-Threatening Injuries First)
- **ABC assessment**: If multisystem trauma
- **Cervical spine**: Protect if mechanism suggests injury
- **Head injury**: Associated in 15-30% of facial trauma
- **Orbital fracture**: Rule out other facial fractures

### Ocular History

#### Mechanism of Injury
- **High-velocity projectile**: Hammering, grinding, lawn mowing (IOFB risk)
- **Low-velocity**: Blunt trauma, fall
- **Chemical exposure**: Type of chemical, duration of contact, irrigation
- **Timeline**: Exact time of injury
- **Symptoms**: Pain, vision loss, double vision

#### Tetanus Status
- Update if >5 years since last booster

#### Allergies and Medications
- Anticoagulation status
- Medication allergies

### Initial Examination

#### Visual Acuity
- **Essential baseline**: Document before any intervention
- **Both eyes**: Compare injured to uninjured
- **Method**: Snellen chart, count fingers, hand motion, light perception
- **Pinhole**: If vision reduced (may improve if refractive)
- **Do not delay**: Critical injuries take priority over formal acuity

#### External Examination
- **Eyelids**: Laceration, ptosis, ecchymosis
- **Periorbital tissues**: Edema, subcutaneous emphysema
- **Globe position**: Proptosis, enophthalmos
- **Ocular motility**: Extraocular movements, diplopia
- **Pupil**: Size, shape, reaction, RAPD

#### Globe Integrity Assessment
- **Seidel test**: Aqueous leak from wound (fluorescein streaming)
- **360-degree conjunctival examination**: Lift lids to see entire globe
- **Prolapsed tissue**: Uvea, vitreous (do not manipulate)
- **If open globe suspected**: Stop examination, shield eye, no pressure

#### Anterior Segment
- **Slit-lamp examination**: If globe closed
- **Subconjunctival hemorrhage**: May obscure scleral laceration
- **Cornea**: Abrasion, foreign body, laceration, edema
- **Anterior chamber**: Depth, hyphema, cells/flare
- **Iris**: Traumatic mydriasis, iridodialysis, sphincter tears
- **Lens**: Cataract, subluxation, phacodonesis

#### Intraocular Pressure
- **Only if globe closed**: Do not measure if open globe or suspected open globe
- **Tonometry**: Applanation or Tono-Pen
- **Hypotony**: Suggests open globe or cyclodialysis
- **Elevated**: Hyphema, lens trauma, angle recession

#### Posterior Segment
- **Dilated fundus exam**: If safe (no open globe)
- **Vitreous hemorrhage**: Blood in vitreous cavity
- **Retinal tears/detachment**: Ophthalmoscopy
- **Choroidal rupture**: Linear breaks in choroid
- **Commotio retinae**: Retinal whitening from concussion
- **Optic nerve**: Avulsion, swelling, hemorrhage

#### Imaging

**CT orbit (without contrast):**
- **Indications**: Suspected IOFB, orbital fracture, open globe, globe rupture
- **Thin cuts**: 1-2 mm slices
- **Axial and coronal views**: Essential for surgical planning
- **Detects**: IOFB (especially metal), fractures, hemorrhage, emphysema

**B-scan ultrasound:**
- **Indications**: View obscured by hemorrhage, cataract
- **Contraindication**: Open globe (use gentle technique)
- **Findings**: Retinal detachment, vitreous hemorrhage, IOFB, choroidal detachment

**MRI:**
- **Contraindicated**: If metallic IOFB suspected (magnet)
- **Use**: Selected cases where CT inadequate

**X-ray:**
- **Limited use**: CT superior for orbital evaluation
- **Historical**: Two-view to localize radio-opaque FB

### Emergency Interventions

#### Immediate Actions for Open Globe
1. **Do not manipulate**: No pressure, no further examination
2. **Protective shield**: Rigid shield (Fox shield, cut cup), not patch
3. **NPO**: Nothing by mouth (surgery likely)
4. **Antibiotics**: Broad-spectrum IV (vancomycin + ceftazidime or fluoroquinolone)
5. **Tetanus**: Update if indicated
6. **Antiemetics**: Prevent vomiting (increased IOP)
7. **Pain control**: Avoid excessive straining
8. **Urgent ophthalmology**: Consult immediately

#### Chemical Injury
1. **Immediate copious irrigation**: Before examination
2. **pH testing**: Ensure pH 7.0-7.4
3. **Evert lids**: Remove particulate matter
4. **Continue irrigation**: Until pH normalized (often 30+ minutes)
5. **Ophthalmology consult**: After initial irrigation

## Closed Globe Injuries

### Corneal Abrasion

#### Mechanism
- Scratch, contact lens overwear, foreign body

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Pain**: Severe, foreign body sensation
- **Photophobia**: Light sensitivity
- **Tearing**: Reflex lacrimation
- **Blepharospasm**: Involuntary eyelid closure

#### Diagnosis
- **Fluorescein staining**: Epithelial defect bright green under cobalt blue light
- **Evert upper lid**: Look for foreign body in superior fornix

#### Treatment
- **Topical antibiotic**: Erythromycin ointment or fluoroquinolone drops
- **Cycloplegic**: Cyclopentolate 1% for comfort (ciliary spasm)
- **Pain control**: Oral analgesics (avoid topical anesthetics)
- **No patching**: Delayed healing, infection risk
- **Avoid contact lenses**: Until healed
- **Follow-up**: 24-48 hours or sooner if worsening

#### Complications
- **Recurrent erosion**: Especially if basement membrane damage
- **Infection**: Rare but serious (Pseudomonas in contact lens wearers)

### Subconjunctival Hemorrhage

#### Mechanism
- **Trauma**: Direct injury
- **Spontaneous**: Valsalva, sneezing, hypertension, anticoagulation
- **Posterior limit visible**: Benign
- **Posterior limit not visible**: Concerning for occult globe rupture

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Bright red blood**: Between conjunctiva and sclera
- **Painless**: Usually
- **Vision normal**: Unless other injuries

#### Diagnosis
- **Clinical**: Obvious on examination
- **360-degree exam**: Rule out scleral laceration if traumatic

#### Treatment
- **Observation**: Self-limited, resolves in 1-2 weeks
- **No treatment needed**: Unless underlying pathology
- **Rule out open globe**: If traumatic and 360-degree hemorrhage

### Traumatic Hyphema

#### Definition
Blood in the anterior chamber from iris or ciliary body vessels.

#### Grading
- **Grade 0**: Microhyphema (RBCs in AC, no layering)
- **Grade I**: <1/3 of AC height
- **Grade II**: 1/3 to 1/2 of AC
- **Grade III**: 1/2 to total filling
- **Grade IV**: Total (8-ball hyphema - dark appearance from deoxygenated blood)

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Decreased vision**: Proportional to size
- **Visible blood**: Layered inferiorly
- **Pain**: Variable
- **Photophobia**: Common

#### Complications
- **Rebleeding**: 5-38%, usually days 3-5 (worst prognosis)
- **Elevated IOP**: Blood obstructs trabecular meshwork
- **Corneal blood staining**: If persistent IOP >25 mmHg for days
- **Optic atrophy**: From prolonged elevated IOP
- **Angle recession glaucoma**: Late (years), 5-20% develop glaucoma

#### Diagnosis
- **Slit-lamp**: Visualize blood
- **IOP measurement**: Elevated in 30-50%
- **Gonioscopy**: After resolution, assess angle recession

#### Treatment

**Hospitalization (controversial, selective):**
- Grade III-IV hyphema
- Rebleed
- Uncontrolled IOP
- Poor compliance
- Sickle cell disease

**Medical management:**
- **Bed rest**: Head elevated 30-45° to allow settling
- **Topical steroid**: Prednisolone 1% QID to reduce inflammation
- **Cycloplegic**: Atropine 1% or cyclopentolate for comfort
- **IOP management**: Beta-blocker, alpha-agonist, CAI if elevated
- **Avoid aspirin/NSAIDs**: Increase bleeding risk (steroids controversial)
- **Aminocaproic acid or tranexamic acid**: Antifibrinolytic, reduces rebleed (selective use)

**Activity restrictions:**
- Avoid strenuous activity, straining, reading
- Shield eye to prevent reinjury

**Surgical intervention:**
- **Indications**:
  - Uncontrolled IOP despite maximal medical therapy
  - Corneal blood staining
  - Total hyphema for >5 days
  - Sickle cell disease with IOP >25 mmHg for >24 hours
- **Procedure**: Anterior chamber washout, clot removal

**Sickle cell considerations:**
- Higher risk of complications at lower IOP
- Avoid carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (acidosis worsens sickling)
- Lower threshold for surgical intervention

#### Prognosis
- **Good**: Most resolve without surgery
- **Vision**: Usually returns to baseline
- **Long-term**: Monitor for late glaucoma (angle recession)

### Traumatic Iritis

#### Mechanism
- Blunt trauma causes inflammation

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Pain**: Dull ache
- **Photophobia**: Ciliary spasm
- **Blurred vision**: Inflammatory cells in AC
- **Ciliary flush**: Perilimbal injection

#### Diagnosis
- **Slit-lamp**: Cells and flare in AC, no hyphema

#### Treatment
- **Topical steroid**: Prednisolone 1% q1-4h depending on severity
- **Cycloplegic**: Cyclopentolate 1% or atropine 1% for comfort and prevent synechiae
- **Follow-up**: Monitor for resolution, rule out other injuries

### Angle Recession

#### Mechanism
- Tear in ciliary body between longitudinal and circular muscles

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Acute**: Often asymptomatic unless other findings (hyphema)
- **Chronic**: Late glaucoma (years later)

#### Diagnosis
- **Gonioscopy**: Widened ciliary body band
- **Compare to fellow eye**: Asymmetry

#### Treatment
- **Observation**: Acute injury
- **Long-term monitoring**: Annual IOP checks, glaucoma risk 5-20%
- **Glaucoma treatment**: If develops

### Lens Subluxation/Dislocation

#### Mechanism
- Zonular rupture from blunt trauma

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Phacodonesis**: Lens wobbling with eye movement
- **Iridodonesis**: Iris wobbling (if lens dislocated)
- **Vision loss**: Refractive change, cataract
- **Lens position**: Subluxed (partially displaced) or dislocated (completely displaced into vitreous or AC)

#### Complications
- **Pupillary block glaucoma**: If lens in pupil
- **Cataract**: Traumatic or progressive
- **Corneal decompensation**: If lens in AC
- **Retinal detachment**: Increased risk

#### Treatment
- **Observation**: Mild subluxation without complications
- **Lens removal**: If vision-threatening, glaucoma, or progressive
- **IOL options**: Scleral-fixated, iris-fixated, or ACIOL

### Commotio Retinae (Berlin's Edema)

#### Mechanism
- Retinal concussion from blunt trauma

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Vision loss**: If macula involved
- **Retinal whitening**: Gray-white discoloration

#### Diagnosis
- **Fundus exam**: Whitening of outer retina
- **OCT**: Hyperreflectivity of outer retinal layers

#### Treatment
- **Observation**: No specific treatment
- **Monitor**: For retinal tear, detachment

#### Prognosis
- **Peripheral**: Usually resolves completely
- **Macular**: Variable, may have permanent vision loss

### Choroidal Rupture

#### Mechanism
- Blunt trauma causes break in Bruch membrane, choroid, RPE

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Vision loss**: If through fovea
- **Yellow-white curvilinear streak**: Concentric to optic disc

#### Complications
- **CNV**: Choroidal neovascularization at rupture site (months to years later)

#### Treatment
- **Observation**: Acute
- **Anti-VEGF**: If CNV develops

### Traumatic Optic Neuropathy

#### Mechanism
- **Direct**: Penetrating injury, fracture fragment
- **Indirect**: Transmitted force to optic nerve (most common)

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Vision loss**: Ranges from mild to no light perception
- **RAPD**: Marcus Gunn pupil
- **Optic disc**: Initially normal, later atrophy

#### Diagnosis
- **Clinical**: Vision loss, RAPD, mechanism
- **CT orbit**: Rule out compressive lesion, fracture
- **MRI**: Better nerve visualization (if no IOFB)

#### Treatment (Controversial)
- **Observation**: Many advocate
- **High-dose steroids**: Methylprednisolone (CRASH study suggests harm in head injury)
- **Optic nerve sheath decompression**: Selected cases with hemorrhage
- **Surgical decompression**: Fracture with bone impingement

#### Prognosis
- **Variable**: Some spontaneous improvement
- **Severe initial vision loss**: Poor prognosis

### Orbital Fractures

#### Types

**Orbital floor (blowout fracture):**
- Mechanism: Blunt trauma, hydraulic force
- Complications: Enophthalmos, diplopia, infraorbital nerve hypesthesia
- Muscle entrapment: Inferior rectus, inferior oblique

**Medial wall:**
- Mechanism: Thin lamina papyracea fractures
- Complications: Subcutaneous emphysema, enophthalmos, medial rectus entrapment

**Orbital roof:**
- Mechanism: High-energy trauma
- Associations: Frontal sinus fracture, intracranial injury

**Zygomaticomaxillary complex (ZMC, tripod):**
- Mechanism: Fracture of zygomatic bone
- Involves: Inferior orbital rim, lateral orbital rim, zygomatic arch

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Diplopia**: Especially upgaze (floor), medial gaze (medial wall)
- **Enophthalmos**: Sunken eye (volume loss into sinus)
- **Subcutaneous emphysema**: Air in tissues (nose blowing)
- **Numbness**: Infraorbital nerve (V2) distribution
- **Restricted motility**: Entrapment, edema, hematoma

#### Diagnosis
- **CT orbit**: 1-2 mm cuts, coronal and axial views
- **Forced duction test**: Differentiate restriction from paresis

#### Treatment

**Indications for surgery:**
- **Acute**: Muscle entrapment with oculocardiac reflex (bradycardia, nausea) - pediatric emergency
- **Subacute/delayed**:
  - Persistent diplopia in primary or reading gaze
  - Enophthalmos >2 mm
  - Large fracture (>50% floor)
- **Timing**: Usually 7-14 days (after edema resolves, before fibrosis)

**Conservative management:**
- Avoid nose blowing
- Antibiotics if sinus involvement (controversial)
- Decongestants
- Observation

**Surgical repair:**
- Approach: Transconjunctival, subciliary, or other
- Implant: Various materials (porous polyethylene, titanium mesh, others)
- Goal: Restore orbital volume, release entrapped tissue

## Open Globe Injuries

### Globe Rupture

#### Mechanism
- Blunt trauma causes globe wall to break at weakest points (corneoscleral limbus, old surgical wounds)

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Decreased vision**: Often severe
- **Deep anterior chamber**: Aqueous leak
- **Low IOP**: Hypotony
- **Abnormal pupil**: Peaked toward wound, traumatic mydriasis
- **Subconjunctival hemorrhage**: 360° (hides scleral wound)
- **Prolapsed uveal tissue**: Dark tissue at wound site

#### Diagnosis
- **Clinical suspicion**: Mechanism and findings
- **CT orbit**: May show flattened globe, intraocular air
- **Do not press on globe**: Avoid further extrusion

#### Treatment
1. **Protective shield**: Immediate
2. **NPO, IV antibiotics**: Vancomycin + ceftazidime
3. **Antiemetics**: Prevent vomiting
4. **Urgent surgical repair**: Primary closure within 24 hours
5. **Explore 360°**: Find all wounds
6. **Debride prolapsed tissue**: Minimal excision
7. **Watertight closure**: Scleral and corneal wounds

#### Prognosis
- **Depends on**: Extent of injury, time to repair, associated injuries
- **Poor prognostic factors**: NLP vision, RAPD, extensive posterior injury

### Laceration

#### Definition
- Full-thickness wound from sharp object

#### Classification
- **Corneal**: Anterior to iris plane
- **Corneoscleral**: Involves limbus
- **Scleral**: Posterior to limbus

#### Clinical Presentation
- Similar to rupture but may have linear wound visible

#### Treatment
- Same as rupture: Surgical repair
- **Layered closure**: Restore anatomy
- **Conjunctival peritomy**: Expose scleral wounds

### Penetrating Injury

#### Definition
- Single entrance wound (no exit)

#### Mechanism
- Sharp object enters but does not traverse globe

#### Evaluation
- **IOFB**: Rule out with imaging (CT)
- **Track**: Follow path of injury

#### Treatment
- **Surgical repair**: Entrance wound
- **Vitrectomy**: If posterior segment involvement
- **IOFB removal**: If present

### Perforating Injury

#### Definition
- Entrance and exit wounds

#### Mechanism
- High-velocity projectile traverses globe

#### Treatment
- **Repair both wounds**: Extensive surgery
- **Vitrectomy**: Often required
- **Poor prognosis**: Dual wounds

### Intraocular Foreign Body (IOFB)

#### Mechanism
- Projectile enters globe and remains

#### Risk Factors
- **Occupational**: Hammering metal-on-metal, grinding, drilling
- **No eye protection**: Most cases

#### Clinical Presentation
- **May be occult**: Small entry wound
- **High index of suspicion**: Based on mechanism
- **Variable vision**: Depends on location and composition

#### Diagnosis
- **CT orbit**: Gold standard for detection and localization
- **MRI**: Contraindicated if metallic IOFB
- **B-scan**: Adjunct if non-metallic

#### Complications
- **Endophthalmitis**: 5-10% (higher with soil contamination)
- **Siderosis**: Iron toxicity (rust ring, retinal degeneration)
- **Chalcosis**: Copper toxicity (sunflower cataract, retinal degeneration)
- **Retinal detachment**: Traction, proliferative vitreoretinopathy

#### Treatment
- **Timing**: Depends on material and location
  - **Organic material**: Urgent (high infection risk)
  - **Metallic**: Urgent to prevent toxicity
  - **Inert material**: May delay if posterior
- **Approach**:
  - Anterior chamber: Removal via corneal wound
  - Posterior segment: Pars plana vitrectomy, intraocular magnet or forceps
- **Prophylactic antibiotics**: Intravitreal (vancomycin + ceftazidime)

#### Prognosis
- **Depends on**: Size, material, location, delay to removal, associated injuries
- **Ocular Trauma Score**: Predicts visual outcome

## Chemical Burns

### Alkali Burns (Worse than Acid)

#### Common Agents
- Ammonia (cleaning agents)
- Lye (drain cleaners)
- Lime (cement, plaster)
- Magnesium hydroxide (fireworks, flares)

#### Mechanism
- **Rapid penetration**: Saponification of lipid membranes
- **Deep tissue damage**: Anterior chamber within seconds
- **Liquefactive necrosis**: Ongoing destruction

#### Clinical Presentation (Grading)

**Grade I (mild):**
- Corneal epithelial damage only
- No limbal ischemia
- Good prognosis

**Grade II (moderate):**
- Corneal haze, iris details visible
- <1/3 limbal ischemia
- Good prognosis

**Grade III (moderate-severe):**
- Complete corneal epithelial loss, stromal haze obscures iris
- 1/3 to 1/2 limbal ischemia
- Guarded prognosis

**Grade IV (severe):**
- Opaque cornea, cannot see iris
- >1/2 limbal ischemia
- Poor prognosis

#### Acute Treatment (MINUTES MATTER)
1. **Immediate copious irrigation**: Start before examination
   - Tap water, saline, lactated Ringer's - anything available
   - Duration: 30 minutes minimum, continue until pH 7.0-7.4
   - Morgan lens or IV tubing for continuous irrigation
   - Evert lids, sweep fornices to remove particulate
2. **pH testing**: Litmus paper in inferior fornix
3. **Continue irrigation**: Until pH normalized
4. **Do not delay**: Time is vision

#### Subsequent Management
- **Topical antibiotics**: Prevent infection (epithelium denuded)
- **Cycloplegic**: Comfort, prevent synechiae
- **Topical steroids**: Reduce inflammation (first 7-10 days, then taper - prolonged use impairs healing)
- **IOP control**: Glaucoma may develop
- **Ascorbic acid**: Topical/oral (promotes collagen synthesis)
- **Citrate**: Inhibit collagenase (tetracycline or citrate drops)
- **Amniotic membrane transplant**: Severe cases, promotes epithelialization
- **Limbal stem cell transplantation**: Late, for limbal stem cell deficiency

#### Complications
- **Acute**: Corneal perforation, secondary glaucoma, cataract
- **Chronic**: Corneal scarring, neovascularization, limbal stem cell deficiency, symblepharon (conjunctival scarring), entropion, dry eye
- **Blindness**: Severe burns (grade III-IV)

### Acid Burns (Less Severe)

#### Common Agents
- Sulfuric acid (battery acid)
- Hydrochloric acid (cleaning agents)
- Hydrofluoric acid (glass etching)

#### Mechanism
- **Coagulative necrosis**: Precipitates proteins, limits penetration
- **Less penetration**: Generally better prognosis than alkali

#### Treatment
- Same irrigation and management as alkali
- **Hydrofluoric acid**: Special concern (penetrates more, systemic toxicity - calcium gluconate)

## Radiation Injuries

### Ultraviolet Keratitis (Welder's Flash, Snow Blindness)

#### Mechanism
- UV-B radiation (280-315 nm) damages corneal epithelium

#### Sources
- Welding arc (most common)
- Tanning beds
- Sun reflection off snow/water

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Latency**: 6-12 hours after exposure
- **Pain**: Severe, bilateral
- **Photophobia**: Extreme light sensitivity
- **Tearing, blepharospasm**: Reflex
- **Foreign body sensation**: Epithelial sloughing

#### Diagnosis
- **History**: Exposure without protection
- **Fluorescein**: Punctate epithelial erosions

#### Treatment
- **Topical antibiotic**: Prevent infection
- **Cycloplegic**: Comfort
- **Oral analgesics**: Pain control
- **No patching**: Delays healing
- **Resolution**: 24-48 hours (excellent prognosis)

### Solar Retinopathy

#### Mechanism
- Direct solar viewing (eclipse without protection)
- Photochemical and thermal damage to fovea

#### Clinical Presentation
- **Decreased central vision**: Bilateral
- **Central scotoma**: Permanent in many
- **Metamorphopsia**: Distortion

#### Diagnosis
- **History**: Solar viewing
- **OCT**: Outer retinal defect at fovea
- **FA**: Window defect

#### Treatment
- **Observation**: No effective treatment
- **Prevention**: Never look directly at sun, use certified eclipse glasses

#### Prognosis
- **Variable**: Some improvement over months
- **Often permanent**: Central scotoma

## Sympathetic Ophthalmia

### Definition
Bilateral granulomatous uveitis following penetrating trauma or surgery to one eye.

### Mechanism
- Autoimmune response to uveal antigens
- Inciting event exposes antigens, leading to immune response against both eyes

### Incidence
- Rare: 0.01-0.03% after penetrating trauma
- Higher risk: Delayed primary repair, uveal prolapse

### Clinical Presentation
- **Timing**: 80% within first year (range: 2 weeks to decades)
- **Bilateral uveitis**: Sympathizing eye (uninjured) develops inflammation
- **Symptoms**: Blurred vision, photophobia, pain, redness
- **Examination**: Anterior chamber cells/flare, vitritis, Dalen-Fuchs nodules (yellow-white lesions at RPE level)

### Diagnosis
- **Clinical**: History of penetrating injury, bilateral uveitis
- **Fluorescein angiography**: Multifocal leakage
- **Indocyanine green angiography**: Choroidal inflammation
- **Rule out**: Other causes of bilateral uveitis

### Treatment
- **High-dose systemic corticosteroids**: Prednisone 1-2 mg/kg
- **Immunosuppression**: Steroid-sparing agents (methotrexate, mycophenolate, others) if steroid-dependent
- **Biologic agents**: Adalimumab, infliximab for refractory cases
- **Enucleation of inciting eye**: Not protective once sympathetic ophthalmia develops

### Prognosis
- **Visual outcome**: Variable, depends on severity and control
- **Requires long-term**: Chronic treatment often needed

## Ocular Trauma Score (OTS)

### Purpose
Predict visual outcome after ocular trauma.

### Variables (Assign Points)
**Initial vision:**
- NLP: 60 points
- LP/HM: 70 points
- 1/200 to 19/200: 80 points
- 20/200 to 20/50: 90 points
- ≥20/40: 100 points

**Subtract points for:**
- Globe rupture: -23
- Endophthalmitis: -17
- Perforating injury: -14
- Retinal detachment: -11
- RAPD: -10

### Calculate Raw Score
Sum = Initial vision points - (sum of injury factors)

### Predict Outcome
Probability of final vision ≥20/40 based on score:
- Score 0-44: 1%
- Score 45-49: 2%
- Score 50-59: 11%
- Score 60-69: 26%
- Score 70-79: 59%
- Score 80-89: 82%
- Score 90-100: 92%

## Special Populations

### Pediatric Considerations
- **High-risk activities**: BB guns, fireworks, sports, toys
- **Amblyopia risk**: Unilateral injury in young child
- **White-eyed blowout**: Orbital fracture with minimal external signs but muscle entrapment - pediatric emergency (oculocardiac reflex)
- **Non-accidental trauma**: Consider abuse in young children

### Elderly Considerations
- **Anticoagulation**: Higher bleeding risk
- **Comorbidities**: Systemic diseases complicate management
- **Falls**: Common mechanism

### Athletes
- **Sports-specific**: Basketball, baseball, racquet sports high risk
- **Protective eyewear**: Polycarbonate lenses, face shields
- **Return to play**: After healing, with protection

## Summary

Ocular trauma is a leading cause of monocular blindness, affecting primarily young males in work-related and recreational settings. Injuries range from minor corneal abrasions to sight-threatening open globe injuries. Initial assessment must determine visual acuity and globe integrity, with any suspicion of open globe requiring immediate protective shielding and ophthalmology consultation. Closed globe injuries including hyphema, corneal abrasion, and orbital fractures have varied management strategies from observation to surgery. Open globe injuries require urgent surgical repair with broad-spectrum antibiotics to prevent endophthalmitis. Chemical burns, particularly alkali, demand immediate copious irrigation before any other intervention. Prevention through safety eyewear is paramount. The Ocular Trauma Score helps predict visual prognosis. Understanding mechanisms, recognition of sight-threatening injuries, appropriate emergency management, and timely ophthalmology referral are critical for optimizing outcomes in ocular trauma patients.
