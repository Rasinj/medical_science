# Ophthalmology

## Overview

Ophthalmology is the medical and surgical specialty focused on the diagnosis, treatment, and prevention of diseases affecting the eye and visual system. The eye is a complex organ responsible for vision, one of the most important senses for human function and quality of life. Ophthalmic conditions range from refractive errors requiring simple correction to sight-threatening diseases requiring urgent intervention. Understanding common eye diseases is essential for all healthcare providers, as many systemic conditions manifest with ocular findings.

### Scope of Ophthalmology
- **Comprehensive ophthalmology**: General eye care, refractive errors, cataracts
- **Subspecialties**:
  - Retina: Diabetic retinopathy, macular degeneration, retinal detachment
  - Glaucoma: Medical and surgical management of glaucoma
  - Cornea and external disease**: Infections, corneal transplantation
  - Neuro-ophthalmology: Optic nerve disorders, visual pathway disease
  - Oculoplastics: Eyelid, orbit, and lacrimal system surgery
  - Pediatric ophthalmology and strabismus**: Amblyopia, strabismus, congenital disorders
  - Uveitis: Inflammatory eye disease

### Epidemiology of Vision Loss
- **Global burden**: Leading cause of disability worldwide
- **Age-related conditions**: Cataracts, AMD, glaucoma most common in developed countries
- **Preventable blindness**: Cataracts, refractive error, glaucoma (50% of blindness preventable)
- **Diabetic eye disease**: Leading cause of blindness in working-age adults
- **Economic impact**: Billions in healthcare costs and lost productivity

## Ocular Anatomy and Physiology

### External Eye

#### Eyelids
- **Function**: Protection, tear distribution, drainage
- **Layers**: Skin, orbicularis oculi muscle, tarsus with meibomian glands, conjunctiva
- **Innervation**: CN VII (orbicularis - closure), CN III (levator - opening)
- **Glands**: Meibomian (tarsal), Zeis (sebaceous), Moll (sweat)

#### Conjunctiva
- **Structure**: Thin mucous membrane covering sclera and inner eyelids
- **Zones**: Palpebral (eyelid), bulbar (globe), fornix (cul-de-sac)
- **Function**: Protection, lubrication, immune defense
- **Goblet cells**: Mucin production

#### Lacrimal System
- **Tear production**: Lacrimal gland (main), accessory glands (Krause, Wolfring)
- **Tear film layers**: Lipid (outermost), aqueous (middle), mucin (innermost)
- **Drainage**: Puncta → canaliculi → lacrimal sac → nasolacrimal duct → inferior meatus

### Globe Structure

#### Cornea
- **Transparent**: Refracts and focuses light
- **Avascular**: Nourished by aqueous humor and tears
- **Layers**: Epithelium, Bowman's layer, stroma (90%), Descemet's membrane, endothelium
- **Refractive power**: ~43 diopters (2/3 of eye's focusing power)
- **Innervation**: Dense sensory innervation (CN V1)

#### Anterior Chamber
- **Space**: Between cornea and iris
- **Aqueous humor**: Clear fluid providing nutrition, maintains intraocular pressure
- **Angle**: Iridocorneal angle where aqueous drains through trabecular meshwork

#### Iris
- **Function**: Controls pupil size, regulates light entry
- **Muscles**: Sphincter (parasympathetic, constricts) and dilator (sympathetic, dilates)
- **Color**: Melanin content determines eye color

#### Ciliary Body
- **Functions**: Aqueous humor production, accommodation
- **Ciliary muscle**: Contracts for near vision (parasympathetic)
- **Ciliary processes**: Produce aqueous humor

#### Lens
- **Transparent, biconvex**: Focuses light on retina
- **Accommodation**: Changes shape for near vision
- **Zonules**: Suspensory ligaments attaching lens to ciliary body
- **Avascular**: Nourished by aqueous humor
- **Continues to grow**: Throughout life (basis for presbyopia and cataracts)

#### Vitreous
- **Gel**: 99% water, collagen, hyaluronic acid
- **Volume**: 80% of globe volume
- **Function**: Maintains shape, optical clarity
- **Changes with age**: Liquefaction, posterior vitreous detachment

### Posterior Segment

#### Retina
- **Neural tissue**: Light-sensitive, converts photons to electrical signals
- **Ten layers**: Including photoreceptors, bipolar cells, ganglion cells
- **Photoreceptors**:
  - Rods: 120 million, peripheral vision, low light
  - Cones: 6 million, central vision, color, concentrated in macula
- **Macula**: Central retina, responsible for detailed central vision
- **Fovea**: Center of macula, highest cone density, sharpest vision
- **Optic disc**: Blind spot where optic nerve exits (no photoreceptors)

#### Retinal Pigment Epithelium (RPE)
- **Single layer**: Between photoreceptors and choroid
- **Functions**: Phagocytosis of photoreceptor outer segments, vitamin A metabolism, blood-retina barrier

#### Choroid
- **Vascular layer**: Between sclera and retina
- **Function**: Nourishes outer retina and RPE
- **Highly vascularized**: Highest blood flow per tissue weight in body

#### Sclera
- **"White of eye"**: Dense collagenous tissue
- **Function**: Structural support, maintains globe shape
- **Thickness**: 0.3mm at insertion of extraocular muscles to 1mm at posterior pole

#### Optic Nerve
- **CN II**: Transmits visual information to brain
- **Fibers**: ~1.2 million retinal ganglion cell axons
- **Myelinated**: Behind lamina cribrosa
- **Blood supply**: Central retinal artery, posterior ciliary arteries

### Visual Pathway
- **Retina**: Photoreceptors → bipolar cells → ganglion cells
- **Optic nerve**: Ganglion cell axons exit globe
- **Optic chiasm**: Nasal fibers cross, temporal fibers remain ipsilateral
- **Optic tract**: To lateral geniculate nucleus
- **Optic radiations**: Through parietal and temporal lobes
- **Visual cortex**: Occipital lobe (area 17, V1)

## Ophthalmic Examination

### Visual Acuity
- **Purpose**: Measure clarity of central vision
- **Distance**: Snellen chart at 20 feet (6 meters)
- **Notation**: 20/20 = normal (numerator = test distance, denominator = distance at which normal eye sees)
- **Near**: Near card at 14 inches for presbyopia assessment
- **Pinhole**: Improves vision if refractive error (doesn't help if pathology)

### External Examination
- **Inspection**: Lids, lashes, periorbital area
- **Palpation**: Lacrimal sac, globe tension
- **Alignment**: Corneal light reflex, cover test
- **Motility**: Extraocular movements (six cardinal positions)
- **Pupils**: Size, shape, reactivity, relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD)

### Pupil Examination

#### Normal Response
- **Direct**: Light in eye → pupil constricts
- **Consensual**: Light in one eye → both pupils constrict

#### Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD)
- **"Marcus Gunn pupil"**: Indicates optic nerve or severe retinal disease
- **Swinging flashlight test**: Pupil paradoxically dilates when light swings to affected eye
- **Interpretation**: Decreased afferent input from affected eye

#### Anisocoria
- **Definition**: Unequal pupil size
- **Physiologic**: 20% of population, <1mm difference
- **Pathologic**: Greater in light (abnormal large pupil) vs dark (abnormal small pupil)
- **Horner syndrome**: Miosis, ptosis, anhidrosis (sympathetic denervation)
- **Third nerve palsy**: Mydriasis, ptosis, impaired motility

### Slit Lamp Examination
- **Biomicroscope**: Magnified view of anterior segment
- **Structures examined**: Lids, conjunctiva, cornea, anterior chamber, iris, lens
- **Techniques**: Diffuse, direct, indirect, retroillumination, sclerotic scatter
- **Tonometry**: Intraocular pressure measurement (Goldmann applanation)

### Intraocular Pressure (IOP)
- **Normal range**: 10-21 mmHg
- **Diurnal variation**: 3-6 mmHg, typically higher in morning
- **Methods**:
  - Goldmann applanation: Gold standard
  - Tonopen: Portable
  - Non-contact (air puff): Screening
- **Factors affecting**: Central corneal thickness, time of day, body position

### Gonioscopy
- **Purpose**: Visualize anterior chamber angle
- **Indications**: Glaucoma evaluation, angle assessment
- **Grading**: Shaffer or Scheie classification (grade 0-4)
- **Open vs closed angle**: Determines glaucoma type and treatment

### Dilated Fundus Examination
- **Dilation**: Tropicamide, phenylephrine (mydriasis for 4-6 hours)
- **Methods**:
  - Direct ophthalmoscopy: Monocular, high magnification, small field
  - Indirect ophthalmoscopy: Binocular, stereoscopic, wide field
  - Slit lamp with condensing lens: Detailed posterior segment exam
- **Structures**: Optic disc, macula, vessels, peripheral retina

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)
- **Technology**: Non-invasive cross-sectional imaging
- **Resolution**: 5-7 microns (near-histologic)
- **Uses**:
  - Macular disease: AMD, diabetic macular edema, epiretinal membrane
  - Glaucoma: Optic nerve and retinal nerve fiber layer analysis
  - Vitreomacular interface disorders

### Visual Field Testing
- **Automated perimetry**: Humphrey, Octopus
- **Purpose**: Map visual field, detect defects
- **Indications**: Glaucoma, neurologic disease, monitoring
- **Patterns**:
  - Arcuate scotoma: Glaucoma
  - Bitemporal hemianopia: Chiasmal lesion
  - Homonymous hemianopia: Retrochiasmal lesion

### Fluorescein Angiography (FA)
- **Technique**: IV fluorescein dye, timed photographs
- **Uses**: Retinal vascular disease, macular edema, neovascularization
- **Phases**: Choroidal, arterial, venous, recirculation, late
- **Findings**: Leakage, staining, blockage, window defects

### Optical Coherence Tomography Angiography (OCTA)
- **Non-invasive**: No dye injection (detects blood flow by motion contrast)
- **Uses**: Macular and optic nerve vascular imaging
- **Advantages**: Quick, safe, detailed vascular maps
- **Limitations**: Smaller field of view, motion artifact

## Common Ophthalmic Signs and Symptoms

### Red Eye
**Differential Diagnosis:**
- **Conjunctivitis**: Discharge, no pain, normal vision
- **Keratitis**: Pain, photophobia, decreased vision, corneal opacity
- **Anterior uveitis**: Pain, photophobia, decreased vision, cell and flare
- **Acute angle-closure glaucoma**: Severe pain, halos, markedly elevated IOP, mid-dilated pupil
- **Scleritis/episcleritis**: Deep pain (scleritis), no pain (episcleritis)
- **Subconjunctival hemorrhage**: Blood-red area, no pain, normal vision

**Urgent/Emergent Conditions:**
- Acute angle-closure glaucoma
- Infectious keratitis
- Endophthalmitis
- Corneal ulcer
- Scleritis

### Vision Loss

**Acute Painless:**
- **Retinal artery occlusion**: Sudden, severe, RAPD, cherry-red spot
- **Retinal vein occlusion**: Sudden, variable, retinal hemorrhages
- **Retinal detachment**: Flashes, floaters, visual field defect
- **Vitreous hemorrhage**: Sudden floaters, decreased vision
- **Posterior vitreous detachment**: Flashes, floaters, usually benign
- **Optic neuritis**: Painful eye movements, RAPD, color vision loss

**Acute Painful:**
- **Acute angle-closure glaucoma**: Severe pain, halos, nausea/vomiting
- **Optic neuritis**: Pain with eye movements
- **Endophthalmitis**: Severe pain, vision loss after surgery/trauma

**Gradual:**
- **Cataracts**: Glare, halos, decreased night vision
- **Glaucoma**: Peripheral vision loss, tunnel vision (late)
- **AMD**: Central vision loss, distortion (wet), drusen (dry)
- **Diabetic retinopathy**: Usually asymptomatic until advanced

### Flashes and Floaters
- **Floaters**: Shadows from vitreous opacities
  - Common: Benign, age-related vitreous changes
  - Concerning: New, numerous, with flashes or vision loss
- **Flashes (photopsia)**: Mechanical stimulation of retina
  - Posterior vitreous detachment: Common cause
  - Retinal tear/detachment: Urgent evaluation needed
  - Migraine: Bilateral, lasts 20-30 minutes

### Diplopia (Double Vision)
- **Monocular**: Persists with one eye closed (refractive, cataract, corneal)
- **Binocular**: Resolves with one eye closed (muscle/nerve problem)
  - Cranial nerve palsy (III, IV, VI)
  - Myasthenia gravis
  - Thyroid eye disease
  - Orbital disease

## Ophthalmic Emergencies

### Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma
- **Symptoms**: Severe eye pain, nausea/vomiting, halos around lights
- **Signs**: Mid-dilated, non-reactive pupil, corneal edema, elevated IOP (>40)
- **Management**:
  - IV acetazolamide, topical IOP-lowering drops
  - Topical pilocarpine (after IOP lowered)
  - Laser peripheral iridotomy (definitive)

### Central Retinal Artery Occlusion
- **Symptoms**: Sudden, painless, severe vision loss
- **Signs**: RAPD, cherry-red spot, boxcarring of vessels
- **Management**: Ocular massage, anterior chamber paracentesis, hyperbaric oxygen (limited efficacy)
- **Prognosis**: Poor, permanent vision loss common
- **Workup**: Embolic source, temporal arteritis (ESR, CRP)

### Retinal Detachment
- **Symptoms**: Flashes, floaters, visual field defect ("curtain")
- **Signs**: Elevated, gray retina with folds
- **Management**: Urgent surgical repair (pneumatic retinopexy, scleral buckle, vitrectomy)
- **Macula-off**: Worse prognosis if macula detached

### Endophthalmitis
- **Infection inside eye**: Usually post-operative or post-traumatic
- **Symptoms**: Pain, vision loss, red eye
- **Signs**: Hypopyon, vitritis
- **Management**: Emergent intravitreal antibiotics (vancomycin + ceftazidime), vitrectomy

### Chemical Burns
- **Alkali worse than acid**: Penetrates deeper
- **Management**: Immediate copious irrigation (30 minutes), remove particulate matter
- **Assessment**: Check pH (goal 7.0-7.4)
- **Treatment**: Topical antibiotics, steroids, cycloplegic, ascorbate

### Traumatic Hyphema
- **Blood in anterior chamber**: After blunt trauma
- **Complications**: Re-bleeding, elevated IOP, corneal blood staining
- **Management**: Elevation, cycloplegic, avoid aspirin/NSAIDs, IOP control

## Principles of Ophthalmic Therapeutics

### Topical Medications

#### Administration Technique
- **Proper technique**: Pull down lower lid, look up, drop in fornix, close eye gently
- **Punctal occlusion**: Prevents systemic absorption, increases ocular bioavailability
- **Spacing**: Wait 5 minutes between different drops

#### Classes of Topical Medications
**Antibiotics:**
- Fluoroquinolones (moxifloxacin, gatifloxacin): Broad-spectrum
- Aminoglycosides (tobramycin, gentamicin): Gram-negative
- Macrolides (erythromycin, azithromycin): Chlamydia, blepharitis

**Corticosteroids:**
- Uses: Inflammation, uveitis, post-operative
- Side effects: Elevated IOP, cataract, infection risk
- Monitoring: IOP checks

**Glaucoma Medications:**
- Prostaglandin analogues: Increase outflow (first-line)
- Beta-blockers: Decrease production
- Alpha-agonists: Decrease production, increase outflow
- Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors: Decrease production

**Cycloplegics/Mydriatics:**
- Tropicamide: Short-acting (4-6 hours), dilation for exam
- Cyclopentolate: Longer-acting, cycloplegia for refraction
- Atropine: Long-acting (1-2 weeks), treatment of uveitis

### Systemic Medications
- **Acetazolamide**: Acute IOP elevation
- **Corticosteroids**: Uveitis, optic neuritis, thyroid eye disease
- **Immunosuppressants**: Severe uveitis, scleritis

### Laser Procedures
- **YAG laser capsulotomy**: Posterior capsule opacification after cataract surgery
- **Laser peripheral iridotomy**: Angle-closure glaucoma prevention/treatment
- **Selective laser trabeculoplasty**: Glaucoma, increase aqueous outflow
- **Panretinal photocoagulation**: Proliferative diabetic retinopathy, retinal vein occlusion

### Intravitreal Injections
- **Anti-VEGF agents**: Wet AMD, diabetic macular edema, retinal vein occlusion
  - Bevacizumab (Avastin), ranibizumab (Lucentis), aflibercept (Eylea), brolucizumab (Beovu)
- **Corticosteroids**: Macular edema, uveitis
  - Triamcinolone, dexamethasone implant, fluocinolone implant
- **Antibiotics**: Endophthalmitis

## Key Points

- Ophthalmology encompasses medical and surgical management of eye diseases affecting vision and ocular health
- Comprehensive eye examination includes visual acuity, pupil examination, slit lamp, tonometry, and dilated fundus exam
- Red eye differential includes conjunctivitis (most common), keratitis, uveitis, acute angle-closure glaucoma, and scleritis
- Acute vision loss requires urgent evaluation to differentiate between retinal artery occlusion, retinal detachment, and other sight-threatening conditions
- New onset flashes and floaters warrant dilated fundus exam to rule out retinal tear or detachment
- Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) indicates optic nerve or severe retinal disease
- Optical coherence tomography (OCT) has revolutionized diagnosis and management of macular and optic nerve diseases
- Topical medications are first-line for many conditions; proper administration technique and punctal occlusion improve efficacy
- Intravitreal anti-VEGF injections have transformed treatment of wet AMD, diabetic macular edema, and retinal vein occlusion
- Preventable causes of blindness (cataracts, refractive error, glaucoma) represent half of global blindness
