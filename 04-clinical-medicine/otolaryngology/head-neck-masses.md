# Head and Neck Masses

## Overview

Head and neck masses represent a diverse group of pathologies ranging from benign congenital lesions to life-threatening malignancies. A systematic approach to evaluation is essential, considering patient age, location, duration, and associated symptoms. In children, most neck masses are inflammatory or congenital, while in adults >40 years, malignancy must be excluded. Understanding the differential diagnosis based on anatomic location (midline vs lateral, specific neck levels), appropriate diagnostic workup including imaging and fine needle aspiration, and management principles is critical for all clinicians. Early recognition of malignancy warning signs and timely referral to specialists can significantly impact outcomes.

### Epidemiology
- **Pediatric**: 55% inflammatory, 30% congenital, 15% neoplastic
- **Adults <40 years**: 80% benign
- **Adults >40 years**: 80% malignant (especially with smoking/alcohol history)
- **Most common malignancy**: Squamous cell carcinoma (metastatic from upper aerodigestive tract)
- **Thyroid nodules**: Palpable in 5-7% of adults, mostly benign
- **Salivary gland tumors**: 80% in parotid, 80% benign; 50% in submandibular, 50% benign

### Anatomic Consideration
- **Midline masses**: Thyroid, thyroglossal duct cyst, dermoid cyst, lymph nodes
- **Lateral neck masses**: Lymph nodes, branchial cleft cyst, salivary glands, paraganglioma, lipoma
- **Level-based approach**: Neck divided into levels I-VII for systematic evaluation
- **Triangle-based**: Anterior triangle (digestive contents, carotid sheath) vs posterior triangle (lymph nodes, lipomas)

## Neck Anatomy and Levels

### Anatomic Triangles

#### Anterior Triangle
- **Boundaries**: Midline, mandible, sternocleidomastoid (SCM)
- **Contents**: Submandibular gland, thyroid, parathyroid, lymph nodes, carotid sheath
- **Subdivisions**: Submandibular, submental, carotid, muscular triangles

#### Posterior Triangle
- **Boundaries**: SCM, trapezius, clavicle
- **Contents**: Lymph nodes, spinal accessory nerve (CN XI), brachial plexus, subclavian vessels
- **Floor**: Scalene muscles

### Neck Levels (Lymph Node Classification)

- **Level I**: Submental (Ia) and submandibular (Ib) nodes
- **Level II**: Upper jugular nodes (above hyoid), IIa anterior to CN XI, IIb posterior
- **Level III**: Middle jugular nodes (hyoid to cricoid)
- **Level IV**: Lower jugular nodes (cricoid to clavicle)
- **Level V**: Posterior triangle, Va (spinal accessory) and Vb (transverse cervical)
- **Level VI**: Anterior compartment (hyoid to manubrium): thyroid, parathyroid, paratracheal nodes
- **Level VII**: Superior mediastinal nodes

### Important Structures
- **Carotid sheath**: Carotid artery, internal jugular vein, vagus nerve
- **Thyroid gland**: Level VI
- **Parotid gland**: Anterior to ear, facial nerve runs through
- **Submandibular gland**: Level Ib

## Evaluation Approach

### History

#### Mass Characteristics
- **Duration**: Acute (<2 weeks), subacute (2-6 weeks), chronic (>6 weeks)
- **Onset**: Sudden vs gradual
- **Growth**: Stable, enlarging, fluctuating
- **Consistency**: Soft, firm, hard, fluctuant
- **Mobility**: Mobile vs fixed to skin or deep structures
- **Tenderness**: Painful (inflammation) vs painless (neoplasm typically)
- **Multiplicity**: Solitary vs multiple

#### Associated Symptoms
- **Constitutional**: Fever, night sweats, weight loss (lymphoma, infection, malignancy)
- **Aerodigestive**: Dysphagia, odynophagia, hoarseness, hemoptysis (malignancy)
- **Neurologic**: Vocal cord paralysis, Horner syndrome, shoulder weakness (CN X, sympathetic, CN XI)
- **Ear symptoms**: Otalgia (referred pain), hearing loss, tinnitus
- **Infectious**: Recent URI, dental infection, cat scratch

#### Risk Factors
- **Tobacco**: Smoking, chewing (squamous cell carcinoma)
- **Alcohol**: Synergistic with tobacco
- **HPV**: Oropharyngeal SCC (younger, non-smokers)
- **EBV**: Nasopharyngeal carcinoma, lymphoma
- **Radiation**: Thyroid cancer, salivary tumors
- **Occupational**: Wood dust (sinonasal), leather (nasal), asbestos

### Physical Examination

#### Inspection
- **Size**: Measure in centimeters
- **Location**: Specific level, midline vs lateral
- **Overlying skin**: Normal, erythematous, dimpling, ulceration
- **Visible pulsations**: Vascular lesion

#### Palpation
- **Consistency**: Soft, rubbery, firm, hard
- **Borders**: Well-defined vs indistinct
- **Mobility**: Mobile (benign) vs fixed (malignant, inflammatory)
- **Tenderness**: Tender (inflammatory) vs non-tender
- **Fluctuance**: Abscess, cystic lesion
- **Transillumination**: Cystic lesions light up
- **Compressibility**: Vascular lesions (hemangioma)
- **Movement with swallowing**: Thyroid, thyroglossal duct cyst

#### Complete Head and Neck Examination
- **Oral cavity**: Masses, ulcers, leukoplakia, erythroplakia
- **Oropharynx**: Tonsillar asymmetry, base of tongue
- **Nasopharynx**: Mirror or endoscopic exam
- **Larynx**: Indirect laryngoscopy or flexible fiberoptic
- **Cranial nerves**: Especially VII, IX, X, XI, XII
- **Otoscopy**: Serous effusion (nasopharyngeal mass blocking Eustachian tube)
- **Thyroid**: Palpate for nodules, symmetry
- **Salivary glands**: Parotid, submandibular
- **Skin**: Primary cutaneous malignancy

### Imaging

#### Ultrasound
- **First-line**: Superficial masses, thyroid nodules
- **Advantages**: No radiation, real-time, guides FNA
- **Assesses**: Size, solid vs cystic, vascularity, internal characteristics
- **Thyroid nodules**: TI-RADS classification
- **Lymph nodes**: Size, shape, architecture, vascularity

#### CT Neck with Contrast
- **Indications**: Deep masses, suspected malignancy, surgical planning
- **Advantages**: Bony detail, vascular structures, extent
- **Protocol**: Contrast-enhanced (unless contraindicated)
- **Findings**: Location, size, relation to structures, enhancement pattern

#### MRI Neck with Contrast
- **Indications**: Soft tissue detail, perineural spread, vascular lesions, pediatric (avoid radiation)
- **Advantages**: Superior soft tissue contrast, multiplanar, no radiation
- **T1**: Anatomy
- **T2**: Fluid-sensitive (cysts, edema)
- **T1+Gd**: Enhancement pattern

#### PET-CT
- **Indications**: Unknown primary with metastatic lymph node, staging known malignancy, recurrence surveillance
- **Advantages**: Functional imaging, whole-body survey
- **Limitations**: False positives (inflammation), false negatives (low metabolic tumors)

### Tissue Diagnosis

#### Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA)
- **Gold standard**: Tissue diagnosis of neck mass
- **Technique**: 25-27 gauge needle, multiple passes
- **Ultrasound guidance**: Improved accuracy, especially deep lesions
- **Yield**: Cytology, flow cytometry (lymphoma), molecular testing
- **Sensitivity**: 90-95% for malignancy
- **Complications**: Rare (bleeding, infection, tumor seeding theoretical)

**Indications:**
- Persistent lymph node >2 cm or >2-3 weeks
- Suspicious features (hard, fixed, progressive)
- Adult >40 years with risk factors
- Thyroid nodule meeting criteria

**Contraindications:**
- Suspected vascular lesion (get imaging first)
- Overlying skin infection

#### Core Needle Biopsy
- **Larger sample**: If FNA non-diagnostic or lymphoma suspected
- **Preserves architecture**: Better for lymphoma classification
- **Technique**: 18-20 gauge cutting needle

#### Excisional Biopsy
- **Open surgical removal**: Entire node/mass
- **Indications**: FNA non-diagnostic after repeat, lymphoma diagnosis, definitive treatment of benign lesion
- **Disadvantages**: Disrupts tissue planes (complicates subsequent cancer surgery if malignant), scar

#### Incisional Biopsy
- **Partial removal**: Sample of larger mass
- **Rarely indicated**: FNA/core biopsy usually adequate
- **Risk**: Tumor seeding, complicates definitive surgery

## Differential Diagnosis by Age and Location

### Midline Masses

#### Thyroid Nodule/Goiter
- **Epidemiology**: 5-7% palpable, 50% on ultrasound, 95% benign
- **Moves with swallowing**: Characteristic
- **Workup**: TSH, ultrasound, FNA if indicated (TI-RADS, ATA guidelines)
- **FNA indications**:
  - Nodule >1 cm with high suspicion features
  - Nodule >1.5 cm with intermediate suspicion
  - Nodule >2 cm with low suspicion
  - Any size with suspicious lymph nodes
- **Bethesda classification**: I (non-diagnostic) to VI (malignant)
- **Management**: Depends on cytology and clinical factors

#### Thyroglossal Duct Cyst
- **Most common congenital neck mass**: 70% of congenital midline masses
- **Pathophysiology**: Persistent thyroglossal duct remnant (from foramen cecum to thyroid)
- **Age**: Usually children, can present in adults
- **Location**: Midline, from hyoid to thyroid, moves with swallowing and tongue protrusion
- **Diagnosis**: Clinical, ultrasound shows cystic mass, confirm normal thyroid present
- **Treatment**: Sistrunk procedure (excision with central hyoid bone)
- **Complications**: Infection (10-15%), malignancy (rare, <1%, usually papillary thyroid ca)

#### Dermoid Cyst
- **Pathophysiology**: Ectodermal inclusion cyst
- **Location**: Midline, above hyoid (submental most common neck location)
- **Age**: Children and young adults
- **Characteristics**: Non-tender, doughy, does not move with swallowing
- **Imaging**: CT/MRI shows well-defined cystic lesion with fat density/signal
- **Treatment**: Surgical excision

#### Plunging Ranula
- **Pathophysiology**: Pseudocyst from sublingual gland, extends into neck
- **Location**: Submental, extending from floor of mouth
- **Characteristics**: Fluctuant, transilluminates, may have intraoral component
- **Imaging**: Cystic mass emanating from sublingual space
- **Treatment**: Excision of sublingual gland ± ranula

### Lateral Masses in Children/Young Adults

#### Reactive Lymphadenopathy
- **Most common**: 55% of pediatric neck masses
- **Etiology**: Viral URI, pharyngitis, dental infection, cat scratch disease, atypical mycobacteria
- **Characteristics**: Tender, mobile, soft to rubbery, bilateral or multiple
- **Size**: Usually <2 cm, if >2 cm or persistent >2 weeks warrants workup
- **Associated**: Recent infection, fever, other systemic symptoms
- **Management**: Observation if typical features, workup if persistent or atypical

#### Branchial Cleft Cyst
- **Second most common congenital neck mass**: 30% of congenital neck masses
- **Second branchial cleft** (95%):
  - **Location**: Anterior border of SCM, level II-III, lateral to carotid
  - **Characteristic**: Along line from tonsillar fossa to suprasternal notch
  - **Age**: Usually presents in teens/20s, after URI
- **First branchial cleft** (5%):
  - **Location**: Near parotid, external auditory canal
  - **Risk**: Facial nerve involvement
- **Characteristics**: Fluctuant, painless unless infected, may have opening (sinus)
- **Imaging**: CT/MRI shows cystic mass anterior to SCM, medial to carotid bifurcation
- **Treatment**: Surgical excision (after infection resolved if present)

#### Lymphatic Malformation (Cystic Hygroma)
- **Pathophysiology**: Developmental lymphatic channels that fail to connect to venous system
- **Age**: 90% present by age 2, 50% at birth
- **Location**: Posterior triangle (75%), supraclavicular
- **Characteristics**: Soft, compressible, transilluminates, may enlarge with URI
- **Imaging**: Multiloculated cystic mass, may extend into mediastinum
- **Treatment**: Surgical excision (difficult, high recurrence if incomplete), sclerotherapy

#### Vascular Malformations
- **Hemangioma**: Proliferative phase (infancy), involuting phase (childhood)
  - Bright red, compressible, may have overlying skin changes
  - Most involute spontaneously by age 7
  - Treatment: Observation, propranolol if problematic
- **Venous malformation**: Compressible, blue discoloration, thrombophlebitis
- **Arteriovenous malformation**: Pulsatile, bruit, high-flow

### Lateral Masses in Adults

#### Metastatic Squamous Cell Carcinoma
- **Most common malignant neck mass** in adults >40
- **Primary sites**: Oropharynx, larynx, hypopharynx, oral cavity, nasopharynx, thyroid, skin
- **Characteristics**: Firm to hard, non-tender, fixed or matted nodes, may be cystic (HPV+)
- **Level II-III**: Oropharynx most common
- **Level IV**: Larynx, hypopharynx, thyroid
- **Level V**: Nasopharynx, skin
- **"Unknown primary"**: Thorough head/neck exam, PET-CT, panendoscopy, directed biopsies, tonsillectomy
- **Workup**: FNA, imaging, find primary
- **Treatment**: Depends on primary site and stage (surgery, radiation, chemotherapy)

#### Lymphoma
- **Hodgkin lymphoma**: Bimodal (20s, >50), painless progressive lymphadenopathy, B symptoms
  - Classic Reed-Sternberg cells
  - Cervical lymph nodes involved in 60-80%
- **Non-Hodgkin lymphoma**: Varied types, painless lymphadenopathy
  - May present with extranodal disease
- **Characteristics**: Rubbery, non-tender, may be matted
- **Diagnosis**: Excisional biopsy preferred (architecture important), flow cytometry, immunohistochemistry
- **Staging**: PET-CT, bone marrow biopsy
- **Treatment**: Chemotherapy ± radiation

#### Salivary Gland Tumors

**Parotid masses:**
- **80% of salivary tumors**: 80% benign
- **Benign**: Pleomorphic adenoma (most common), Warthin tumor
- **Malignant**: Mucoepidermoid carcinoma, adenoid cystic carcinoma, acinic cell, carcinoma ex pleomorphic adenoma
- **Presentation**: Painless mass, facial nerve paralysis (malignancy)
- **Workup**: Imaging (MRI or CT), FNA
- **Treatment**: Superficial or total parotidectomy with facial nerve preservation

**Submandibular masses:**
- **10% of salivary tumors**: 50% benign, 50% malignant (higher malignancy rate)
- **Benign**: Pleomorphic adenoma
- **Malignant**: Adenoid cystic carcinoma
- **Treatment**: Submandibular gland excision

#### Paraganglioma (Glomus Tumor)
- **Carotid body tumor** (most common head/neck paraganglioma):
  - **Location**: Carotid bifurcation
  - **Characteristics**: Firm, pulsatile, mobile laterally but not vertically (Fontaine sign)
  - **Imaging**: Intense enhancement, "splaying" of carotid bifurcation
  - **Treatment**: Surgical excision (risk of cranial nerve injury, vascular injury)
- **Vagal paraganglioma**: Along vagus nerve, may cause hoarseness
- **Jugulotympanic**: Jugular bulb, middle ear (pulsatile tinnitus)

#### Lipoma
- **Common benign tumor**: Soft, mobile, non-tender
- **Posterior triangle**: Classic location
- **Diagnosis**: Clinical, ultrasound or MRI if uncertain
- **Treatment**: Observation or excisional biopsy

### Infectious/Inflammatory Masses

#### Cervical Lymphadenitis
- **Bacterial**: Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pyogenes
  - **Presentation**: Tender, enlarged node, overlying erythema
  - **Treatment**: Antibiotics (cephalexin, clindamycin), incision and drainage if abscess

#### Atypical Mycobacteria (NTM)
- **Organisms**: Mycobacterium avium complex, M. scrofulaceum
- **Age**: Children 1-5 years
- **Location**: Submandibular, upper cervical
- **Characteristics**: Painless, may develop violaceous skin discoloration, may drain
- **Diagnosis**: PPD often negative or small, culture (difficult)
- **Treatment**: Surgical excision preferred; medical therapy (clarithromycin + rifampin) if surgery not feasible

#### Cat Scratch Disease (Bartonella henselae)
- **History**: Kitten exposure, scratch/bite
- **Presentation**: Tender lymphadenopathy (usually unilateral), may suppurate
- **Diagnosis**: Serology, history
- **Treatment**: Usually self-limited; azithromycin if severe

#### Tuberculosis (Scrofula)
- **Risk**: Endemic areas, immunocompromised, immigrants
- **Presentation**: Painless, matted nodes, may form abscess ("collar stud"), sinus tracts
- **Diagnosis**: PPD, IGRA, FNA with AFB smear/culture, PCR
- **Treatment**: Multi-drug anti-TB therapy (RIPE: rifampin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, ethambutol)

#### Toxoplasmosis
- **Exposure**: Undercooked meat, cat feces
- **Presentation**: Posterior cervical lymphadenopathy, fever, fatigue
- **Diagnosis**: Serology (IgM and IgG)
- **Treatment**: Usually self-limited in immunocompetent

#### Mononucleosis (EBV)
- **Presentation**: Bilateral posterior cervical lymphadenopathy, pharyngitis, splenomegaly
- **Diagnosis**: Monospot, EBV serology, CBC (lymphocytosis, atypical lymphocytes)
- **Treatment**: Supportive

#### Kikuchi Disease (Histiocytic Necrotizing Lymphadenitis)
- **Presentation**: Tender cervical lymphadenopathy, fever, young adults (Asian, female predominance)
- **Diagnosis**: Excisional biopsy (characteristic histology)
- **Treatment**: Self-limited (2-4 months), NSAIDs

## Red Flags for Malignancy

### Clinical Features
- **Age**: >40 years
- **Duration**: Progressive enlargement, persistent >2-3 weeks
- **Consistency**: Hard, fixed to underlying structures
- **Number**: Solitary dominant node
- **Size**: >2 cm, especially >3 cm
- **Location**: Level IV, V (higher malignancy risk), supraclavicular (Virchow node)
- **Painless**: Most malignancies non-tender
- **Associated**: Hoarseness, dysphagia, odynophagia, weight loss, night sweats

### Risk Factors
- **Tobacco use**: Smoking, chewing
- **Alcohol**: Heavy use
- **HPV**: Risk factor but also better prognosis (oropharyngeal SCC)
- **Occupational**: Specific exposures
- **Prior radiation**: Neck, thyroid radiation
- **Immunosuppression**: HIV, transplant

### Imaging Features
- **Heterogeneous**: Necrosis, irregular borders
- **Invasion**: Adjacent structure involvement
- **PET avidity**: High SUV
- **Multiple matted nodes**: Suggests advanced disease

## Management Principles

### Observation
- **Indications**: Typical reactive lymphadenopathy in children, recent infection
- **Criteria**: <2 cm, mobile, soft, tender, recent infection
- **Duration**: 2-3 weeks
- **Re-examine**: If not improving or enlarging, proceed to workup

### Antibiotic Trial
- **Controversial**: May be appropriate if clinical picture suggests bacterial lymphadenitis
- **Duration**: 10-14 days
- **Follow-up**: If no response, proceed with imaging/FNA
- **Caution**: Do not delay diagnosis of malignancy

### Diagnostic Workup
- **Imaging**: Ultrasound first-line for superficial, CT/MRI for deep/complex
- **FNA**: Preferred initial tissue diagnosis
- **Laboratory**: CBC, monospot, HIV, TB testing as indicated
- **Excisional biopsy**: If FNA non-diagnostic or lymphoma suspected

### Referral to Specialist
- **Otolaryngology**: Persistent mass, suspected malignancy, surgical lesions
- **Oncology**: Confirmed malignancy
- **Infectious disease**: Atypical infections, TB
- **Endocrine surgery**: Thyroid nodules, parathyroid

## Special Considerations

### Pediatric Masses
- **Most common**: Reactive lymphadenopathy (55%), congenital (30%), neoplastic (15%)
- **Approach**: Less aggressive if typical for reactive, but do not delay diagnosis of malignancy
- **FNA**: Appropriate in children if indicated, not routinely for small reactive nodes
- **Imaging**: MRI preferred (no radiation), ultrasound first-line

### Supraclavicular Lymphadenopathy
- **Virchow node** (left): Suggests abdominal/thoracic malignancy
- **Right**: Suggests mediastinal, lung, esophageal primary
- **Workup**: Requires thorough evaluation (CT chest/abdomen/pelvis, upper endoscopy, colonoscopy as indicated)
- **FNA**: Essential

### HIV Patients
- **Differential**: Reactive (persistent generalized lymphadenopathy), lymphoma (higher risk), atypical infections (MAI, TB), Kaposi sarcoma
- **Approach**: Lower threshold for FNA/biopsy

### Pregnancy
- **Imaging**: Ultrasound and MRI without gadolinium safe
- **Avoid**: CT (radiation), PET-CT
- **FNA**: Safe if indicated

## Summary

Head and neck masses require a systematic approach considering patient age, mass characteristics, location, and associated symptoms. In children, most masses are inflammatory or congenital (branchial cleft cyst, thyroglossal duct cyst, lymphatic malformation), while in adults over 40 years, malignancy must be excluded, particularly metastatic squamous cell carcinoma from upper aerodigestive tract primary. The evaluation includes thorough history and physical examination with complete head and neck assessment, appropriate imaging (ultrasound for superficial lesions, CT or MRI for deeper masses), and tissue diagnosis via fine needle aspiration for suspected malignancy. Red flags for malignancy include age >40, hard fixed mass, progressive enlargement, size >2 cm, and associated symptoms such as hoarseness or dysphagia. Thyroid nodules are common and require systematic evaluation with ultrasound and FNA based on TI-RADS criteria. Reactive lymphadenopathy in children can be observed if typical features present, while atypical or persistent masses require workup. Early recognition of malignancy, appropriate diagnostic evaluation, and timely specialist referral are essential for optimal outcomes in patients with head and neck masses.
