# Pediatric Emergency Medicine

## Overview

Pediatric emergency medicine encompasses the evaluation and management of acute illnesses and injuries in infants, children, and adolescents. Children are not simply small adults—they have unique anatomic, physiologic, and psychological characteristics that require specialized approaches to assessment, resuscitation, and treatment. Understanding age-specific presentations, dosing calculations, and communication strategies is essential for providing optimal emergency care to pediatric patients.

**Key Concept**: Rapid recognition and treatment of life-threatening conditions, combined with age-appropriate assessment and family-centered care, are the cornerstones of pediatric emergency medicine.

### Epidemiology
- **ED visits**: Children account for 25-30% of emergency department visits
- **Leading causes**: Injuries, respiratory infections, gastrointestinal illnesses, fever
- **Mortality**: Trauma is leading cause of death in children >1 year
- **Prevention**: Many pediatric emergencies are preventable through safety measures

### Unique Pediatric Considerations

#### Anatomic Differences
- **Airway**: Larger tongue, anterior larynx, narrowest at cricoid (infants)
- **Head**: Proportionally larger, higher risk of head injury
- **Chest wall**: Compliant, may have respiratory compromise without rib fractures
- **Abdomen**: Less muscular protection, more solid organ injuries
- **Skeleton**: Cartilaginous, flexible (more energy transmitted to organs)
- **Surface area**: Higher surface area to volume ratio (heat loss, fluid losses)

#### Physiologic Differences
- **Vital signs**: Age-specific normal ranges
- **Compensation**: Excellent cardiovascular reserve, then sudden decompensation
- **Fluid requirements**: Higher metabolic rate, increased insensible losses
- **Thermoregulation**: Immature, prone to hypothermia
- **Glucose**: Limited glycogen stores, prone to hypoglycemia

#### Psychological Considerations
- **Stranger anxiety**: Peaks 9-12 months
- **Separation anxiety**: Peaks 18-24 months
- **Fear**: Medical procedures, pain, unfamiliar environment
- **Communication**: Age-appropriate explanations
- **Parental involvement**: Family-centered care essential

## Pediatric Assessment

### Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT)

A rapid visual assessment tool to identify severity without touching the child.

#### Appearance
- **Tone**: Muscle tone, position
- **Interactiveness**: Response to environment
- **Consolability**: Ability to be consoled
- **Look/gaze**: Eye contact, following objects
- **Speech/cry**: Quality and strength

#### Work of Breathing
- **Abnormal sounds**: Stridor, grunting, wheezing
- **Positioning**: Tripod, sniffing position
- **Retractions**: Intercostal, subcostal, suprasternal
- **Nasal flaring**: Nostril widening with breathing

#### Circulation to Skin
- **Pallor**: Pale appearance
- **Mottling**: Patchy skin discoloration
- **Cyanosis**: Blue discoloration

#### PAT Categories
- **Stable**: Normal appearance, breathing, circulation
- **Respiratory distress**: Abnormal breathing, normal appearance and circulation
- **Respiratory failure**: Abnormal breathing and appearance, normal circulation
- **Shock**: Abnormal appearance and circulation, variable breathing
- **Cardiopulmonary failure**: All three abnormal

### Primary Survey (ABCDE)

#### Airway
- **Assessment**: Patency, sounds (stridor, gurgling), position
- **Interventions**:
  - Positioning (chin lift, jaw thrust)
  - Suctioning
  - Airway adjuncts (OPA, NPA)
  - Advanced airway (intubation)

#### Breathing
- **Assessment**: Rate, effort, air movement, oxygen saturation
- **Interventions**:
  - Oxygen supplementation
  - Bag-mask ventilation
  - Intubation and mechanical ventilation

#### Circulation
- **Assessment**: Heart rate, pulse quality, capillary refill, blood pressure, skin perfusion
- **Interventions**:
  - Vascular access (IV, IO)
  - Fluid resuscitation
  - Medications (epinephrine, vasopressors)
  - Hemorrhage control

#### Disability (Neurologic)
- **Assessment**: AVPU (Alert, Voice, Pain, Unresponsive) or GCS
- **Pupil assessment**: Size, reactivity
- **Glucose check**: Bedside glucose testing
- **Interventions**: Treat hypoglycemia, avoid hypotension

#### Exposure/Environment
- **Remove clothing**: Full examination
- **Prevent hypothermia**: Warming measures
- **Assessment**: Identify injuries, rashes, signs of abuse

### Age-Specific Vital Signs

#### Normal Heart Rate (beats/min)
- **Newborn (0-3 months)**: 100-150
- **Infant (3-12 months)**: 90-120
- **Toddler (1-3 years)**: 80-110
- **Preschool (3-6 years)**: 70-100
- **School-age (6-12 years)**: 60-90
- **Adolescent (>12 years)**: 55-85

#### Normal Respiratory Rate (breaths/min)
- **Newborn**: 30-60
- **Infant**: 30-50
- **Toddler**: 20-30
- **Preschool**: 20-25
- **School-age**: 15-20
- **Adolescent**: 12-16

#### Normal Blood Pressure (systolic, mmHg)
- **Newborn**: 60-90
- **Infant**: 70-100
- **Toddler**: 80-110
- **Preschool**: 90-110
- **School-age**: 95-120
- **Adolescent**: 100-130

#### Hypotension Definition
- **Infants (1-12 months)**: <70 mmHg
- **Children 1-10 years**: <70 + (2 × age in years) mmHg
- **Children >10 years**: <90 mmHg

## Pediatric Resuscitation (PALS)

### Basic Life Support (BLS)

#### Pediatric BLS Algorithm

##### Check Responsiveness
- **Tap and shout**: Stimulate and assess response
- **No response**: Call for help, get AED/defibrillator

##### Check Breathing and Pulse
- **Breathing**: Look for chest rise, <10 seconds
- **Pulse**: Brachial (infant), carotid (child), <10 seconds
- **No pulse or HR <60 with poor perfusion**: Start CPR

##### Compressions
- **Rate**: 100-120 compressions/minute
- **Depth**:
  - Infant: 1.5 inches (4 cm)
  - Child: 2 inches (5 cm)
- **Location**: Lower half of sternum
- **Technique**:
  - Infant: 2-finger (single rescuer) or 2-thumb-encircling hands (2 rescuers)
  - Child: Heel of 1 or 2 hands
- **Recoil**: Allow complete chest recoil
- **Minimize interruptions**: <10 seconds

##### Airway and Breathing
- **Open airway**: Head tilt-chin lift (if no trauma) or jaw thrust (if trauma)
- **Give breaths**: 2 breaths after 30 compressions (single rescuer)
- **Ratio**: 30:2 (single rescuer), 15:2 (2 rescuers with advanced airway)
- **Each breath**: 1 second, visible chest rise

##### Use AED
- **Apply as soon as available**: Minimize interruption of CPR
- **Pad placement**: Anterior-posterior or anterior-lateral
- **Pediatric pads**: <8 years or <25 kg (if available)
- **Shock advised**: Clear, deliver shock, resume CPR immediately
- **No shock advised**: Resume CPR immediately

### Advanced Life Support (PALS)

#### Cardiac Arrest Rhythms

##### Shockable Rhythms (VF/pVT)
- **Defibrillation**: 2 J/kg initial, 4 J/kg subsequent (maximum 10 J/kg or adult dose)
- **CPR**: 2 minutes between shocks
- **Epinephrine**: After 2nd shock, then every 3-5 minutes
- **Amiodarone**: 5 mg/kg after 3rd shock (can repeat once)

##### Non-Shockable Rhythms (Asystole/PEA)
- **CPR**: High-quality compressions
- **Epinephrine**: As soon as possible, then every 3-5 minutes
- **Reversible causes**: Search for and treat

#### Medications

##### Epinephrine
- **Dose**: 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO (0.1 mL/kg of 1:10,000)
- **Maximum**: 1 mg
- **Interval**: Every 3-5 minutes
- **Indications**: Cardiac arrest, bradycardia with poor perfusion

##### Amiodarone
- **Dose**: 5 mg/kg IV/IO bolus
- **Maximum**: 300 mg initial, 150 mg second dose
- **Indications**: VF/pVT unresponsive to defibrillation

##### Atropine
- **Dose**: 0.02 mg/kg IV/IO
- **Minimum**: 0.1 mg
- **Maximum**: 0.5 mg (child), 1 mg (adolescent)
- **Indications**: Symptomatic bradycardia (not in cardiac arrest)

##### Adenosine
- **Dose**: 0.1 mg/kg IV/IO rapid push (maximum 6 mg), second dose 0.2 mg/kg (maximum 12 mg)
- **Indications**: Supraventricular tachycardia

#### Reversible Causes (Hs and Ts)

##### Hs
- **Hypovolemia**: Fluid bolus
- **Hypoxia**: Oxygenation, ventilation
- **Hydrogen ion (acidosis)**: Ventilation, sodium bicarbonate (rarely)
- **Hypo/hyperkalemia**: ECG changes, treat electrolyte abnormalities
- **Hypothermia**: Rewarm
- **Hypoglycemia**: Dextrose

##### Ts
- **Tension pneumothorax**: Needle decompression
- **Tamponade (cardiac)**: Pericardiocentesis
- **Toxins**: Specific antidotes
- **Thrombosis (coronary)**: Rare in children
- **Thrombosis (pulmonary)**: Rare in children

### Bradycardia

#### Definition
- **Heart rate <60 bpm**: With signs of poor perfusion

#### Management
- **Support airway and breathing**: Oxygenation, ventilation
- **CPR if HR <60**: Despite adequate ventilation
- **Epinephrine**: If bradycardia persists
- **Atropine**: For increased vagal tone or AV block
- **Pacing**: Transcutaneous or transvenous (rarely needed)
- **Treat underlying cause**: Hypoxia, acidosis, toxins

### Tachycardia

#### Distinguish Sinus Tachycardia from SVT

##### Sinus Tachycardia
- **Rate**: Usually <220 infants, <180 children
- **P waves**: Present, normal
- **Variability**: Rate varies with activity/stimulation
- **History**: Fever, pain, dehydration, anxiety

##### Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT)
- **Rate**: Usually >220 infants, >180 children
- **P waves**: Absent or abnormal
- **Onset**: Abrupt
- **Regularity**: Very regular (metronome)
- **History**: May have no identifiable cause

#### Management of SVT

##### Stable (Adequate Perfusion)
- **Vagal maneuvers**: Ice to face (infants), Valsalva (older children)
- **Adenosine**: 0.1 mg/kg rapid IV push (second dose 0.2 mg/kg)
- **Expert consultation**: Consider synchronized cardioversion if refractory

##### Unstable (Poor Perfusion, Shock)
- **Synchronized cardioversion**: 0.5-1 J/kg, increase to 2 J/kg if needed
- **Sedate if possible**: Brief sedation before cardioversion
- **Expert consultation**: Cardiology

## Respiratory Emergencies

### Upper Airway Obstruction

#### Foreign Body Aspiration

##### Clinical Presentation
- **History**: Sudden onset choking, coughing, witnessed event
- **Symptoms**: Dyspnea, stridor, wheezing, cough, aphonia
- **Complete obstruction**: Unable to speak or cough, cyanosis

##### Management

###### Conscious with Effective Cough
- **Encourage coughing**: Do not interfere
- **Monitor closely**: Position of comfort
- **Prepare for deterioration**

###### Conscious with Ineffective Cough
- **Infant**: 5 back blows + 5 chest thrusts
- **Child**: Abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver)
- **Repeat**: Until object expelled or unconscious

###### Unconscious
- **CPR**: Start chest compressions
- **Look in mouth**: Before breaths, remove visible object
- **Do not blind sweep**: May push object deeper

###### Post-Event
- **Bronchoscopy**: If suspected retained foreign body
- **Observation**: Even if object expelled
- **Chest x-ray**: May show radiopaque object, air trapping

### Lower Airway Emergencies

#### Status Asthmaticus

##### Definition
- **Severe asthma exacerbation**: Refractory to initial treatment
- **Life-threatening**: Respiratory failure, altered mental status

##### Clinical Presentation
- **Dyspnea**: Severe respiratory distress
- **Wheezing**: May be absent if severe ("silent chest")
- **Accessory muscle use**: Retractions, nasal flaring
- **Altered mental status**: Agitation or lethargy (ominous sign)
- **Hypoxemia**: Low oxygen saturation

##### Assessment
- **Pulse oximetry**: Monitor oxygenation
- **Peak flow**: If able to perform (>5 years)
- **ABG**: Severe cases (PaCO2 >40 mmHg is concerning)
- **Chest x-ray**: Rule out pneumonia, pneumothorax

##### Management

###### Initial Treatment
- **Oxygen**: To maintain SpO2 ≥90%
- **Albuterol**: Continuous nebulization (10-15 mg/hour)
- **Ipratropium**: Add to first 3 doses of albuterol
- **Corticosteroids**:
  - Oral: Prednisolone 1-2 mg/kg (maximum 60 mg)
  - IV: Methylprednisolone 1-2 mg/kg
- **Reassess frequently**: Every 15-20 minutes

###### Escalation for Severe/Refractory
- **IV magnesium sulfate**: 25-75 mg/kg over 20 minutes (maximum 2 g)
- **Terbutaline**: IV or subcutaneous
- **Heliox**: Helium-oxygen mixture (reduces work of breathing)
- **NIPPV**: Non-invasive positive pressure ventilation
- **Intubation**: Last resort (high risk, optimize first)

###### ICU Admission Criteria
- **Impending respiratory failure**: Altered mental status, severe hypoxemia
- **Refractory to treatment**: Not improving with maximal therapy
- **Need for continuous monitoring**: Unstable vital signs

#### Anaphylaxis

##### Diagnostic Criteria
- **Acute onset (minutes to hours)** with ≥2 of:
  - Skin/mucosal involvement (hives, angioedema)
  - Respiratory compromise (dyspnea, wheeze, stridor)
  - Reduced blood pressure or end-organ dysfunction
  - Gastrointestinal symptoms (vomiting, cramping)

##### Management

###### Immediate (First Minutes)
- **Epinephrine IM**: 0.01 mg/kg (0.01 mL/kg of 1:1,000), maximum 0.5 mg
- **Location**: Anterolateral thigh (vastus lateralis)
- **Repeat**: Every 5-15 minutes if needed
- **Remove allergen**: Stop infusion, remove stinger

###### Supportive Care
- **Airway management**: Oxygen, may need intubation
- **IV fluids**: Bolus 20 mL/kg for hypotension
- **Positioning**: Supine with legs elevated (if hypotensive)
- **Continuous monitoring**: Vital signs, cardiac monitor

###### Adjunct Medications
- **H1 antihistamine**: Diphenhydramine 1 mg/kg IV/PO
- **H2 antihistamine**: Ranitidine or famotidine
- **Corticosteroids**: Methylprednisolone 1-2 mg/kg (prevent biphasic reaction)
- **Albuterol**: If bronchospasm

###### Observation
- **Minimum 4-6 hours**: Even if responds quickly
- **Longer if severe**: 12-24 hours for severe reactions
- **Discharge**: Prescribe epinephrine auto-injector, allergy referral

## Shock

### Types of Shock

#### Hypovolemic Shock
- **Most common in children**
- **Causes**: Gastroenteritis, hemorrhage, burns, diabetic ketoacidosis
- **Pathophysiology**: Decreased intravascular volume

#### Distributive Shock
- **Causes**: Sepsis, anaphylaxis, neurogenic
- **Pathophysiology**: Abnormal vasodilation, increased capillary permeability

#### Cardiogenic Shock
- **Causes**: Myocarditis, cardiomyopathy, post-cardiac surgery, arrhythmia
- **Pathophysiology**: Pump failure

#### Obstructive Shock
- **Causes**: Tension pneumothorax, cardiac tamponade, massive pulmonary embolism
- **Pathophysiology**: Obstruction to flow

### Stages of Shock

#### Compensated Shock
- **Tachycardia**: Early sign
- **Normal blood pressure**: Maintained by compensation
- **Delayed capillary refill**: >2 seconds
- **Cool, mottled extremities**: Poor peripheral perfusion
- **Decreased urine output**: <1 mL/kg/hour
- **Altered mental status**: Anxious, irritable

#### Hypotensive (Decompensated) Shock
- **Hypotension**: Late sign in children (indicates severe shock)
- **Severe tachycardia**: Weak, thready pulses
- **Marked capillary refill delay**: >4 seconds
- **Altered mental status**: Lethargic, unresponsive
- **Minimal urine output**: Oliguria/anuria

#### Irreversible Shock
- **Profound hypotension**: Unresponsive to treatment
- **Bradycardia**: Ominous sign
- **Multi-organ failure**: Cardiac arrest imminent

### Management of Shock

#### Initial Resuscitation
- **Airway and breathing**: Oxygen, ventilation support
- **Vascular access**: Two large-bore IVs or IO
- **Fluid bolus**: 20 mL/kg isotonic crystalloid (NS or LR) over 5-10 minutes
- **Reassess**: After each bolus
- **Repeat boluses**: Up to 60 mL/kg in first hour if needed (except cardiogenic shock)

#### Specific Treatments

##### Hypovolemic Shock
- **Fluid resuscitation**: Multiple boluses often needed
- **Blood products**: If hemorrhagic shock (10 mL/kg PRBC)
- **Address underlying cause**: Control bleeding, treat DKA

##### Septic Shock
- **Early aggressive fluids**: 40-60 mL/kg or more in first hour
- **Antibiotics**: Within 1 hour, broad-spectrum
- **Vasoactive agents**: If fluid-refractory
  - Cold shock: Epinephrine or dopamine
  - Warm shock: Norepinephrine
- **Source control**: Remove infected devices, drain abscesses
- **Stress-dose steroids**: Consider if refractory

##### Cardiogenic Shock
- **Cautious fluids**: 5-10 mL/kg boluses, reassess frequently
- **Inotropes**: Dobutamine, milrinone
- **Afterload reduction**: If hypertensive
- **Treat arrhythmias**: As indicated

##### Anaphylactic Shock
- **Epinephrine IM**: First-line treatment
- **Fluids**: Aggressive (20 mL/kg boluses)
- **Adjunct medications**: Antihistamines, corticosteroids
- **Vasopressors**: If refractory to epinephrine

## Pediatric Trauma

### Trauma Assessment

#### Primary Survey
- **Airway with C-spine protection**: Maintain alignment
- **Breathing**: Assess and support
- **Circulation with hemorrhage control**: Vascular access, fluid resuscitation
- **Disability**: Neurologic assessment (GCS, pupils)
- **Exposure/Environment**: Remove clothing, prevent hypothermia

#### Secondary Survey
- **Head-to-toe examination**: Identify all injuries
- **AMPLE history**:
  - Allergies
  - Medications
  - Past medical history
  - Last meal
  - Events/Environment of injury

### Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

#### Classification (Glasgow Coma Scale)
- **Mild**: GCS 14-15
- **Moderate**: GCS 9-13
- **Severe**: GCS ≤8

#### Pediatric Glasgow Coma Scale

##### Eye Opening (1-4)
- 4: Spontaneous
- 3: To speech
- 2: To pain
- 1: None

##### Verbal Response (1-5)
- **Infant**:
  - 5: Coos, babbles
  - 4: Irritable cry
  - 3: Cries to pain
  - 2: Moans to pain
  - 1: None
- **Child**:
  - 5: Oriented, appropriate
  - 4: Confused
  - 3: Inappropriate words
  - 2: Incomprehensible sounds
  - 1: None

##### Motor Response (1-6)
- 6: Obeys commands (spontaneous movement in infant)
- 5: Localizes pain
- 4: Withdraws from pain
- 3: Flexion to pain
- 2: Extension to pain
- 1: None

#### Management

##### Severe TBI (GCS ≤8)
- **Intubation**: Protect airway
- **Avoid hypotension**: Fluid resuscitation to maintain BP
- **Avoid hypoxia**: Maintain SpO2 >95%
- **Prevent secondary injury**: Avoid hypo/hyperventilation
- **ICP management**:
  - Head of bed 30 degrees
  - Head midline
  - Osmotic therapy (hypertonic saline or mannitol) if signs of herniation
- **Neurosurgical consultation**: Immediate
- **CT imaging**: Identify surgical lesions

##### Mild TBI/Concussion
- **Observation**: Monitor for deterioration
- **CT criteria**: PECARN rules guide imaging decisions
- **Discharge instructions**: Return precautions, concussion management
- **Avoid contact sports**: Until asymptomatic and cleared

#### PECARN Head Injury Rules

##### Children <2 Years (High Risk)
- **GCS <15**: At 2 hours post-injury
- **Altered mental status**: Agitation, somnolence, etc.
- **Palpable skull fracture**

##### Children <2 Years (Moderate Risk)
- **Loss of consciousness**: ≥5 seconds
- **Non-frontal scalp hematoma**
- **Severe mechanism**: MVC, fall >3 feet, etc.
- **Not acting normally per parent**

##### Children ≥2 Years (High Risk)
- **GCS <15**: At 2 hours post-injury
- **Altered mental status**
- **Signs of basilar skull fracture**

##### Children ≥2 Years (Moderate Risk)
- **Loss of consciousness**
- **History of vomiting**
- **Severe mechanism**
- **Severe headache**

### Abdominal Trauma

#### Common Injuries
- **Solid organ injury**: Spleen, liver (most common)
- **Hollow viscus injury**: Intestinal perforation (rare, serious)
- **Renal injury**: Contusion, laceration

#### Assessment
- **Mechanism**: Blunt (MVC, fall) vs. penetrating (rare)
- **Signs/symptoms**:
  - Abdominal pain, tenderness
  - Distension
  - Bruising (seat belt sign)
  - Hemodynamic instability
- **FAST exam**: Ultrasound for free fluid
- **CT abdomen/pelvis**: With IV contrast (if stable)
- **Laboratory**: CBC, liver enzymes, lipase, urinalysis

#### Management
- **Non-operative management**: Preferred for stable blunt solid organ injuries
- **Resuscitation**: Fluids, blood products as needed
- **Serial exams**: Monitor for deterioration
- **Operative intervention**: If unstable or peritonitis
- **Admission**: Most significant abdominal injuries

### Orthopedic Injuries

#### Fracture Patterns Unique to Children

##### Greenstick Fracture
- **Description**: Incomplete fracture, cortex broken on one side
- **Management**: Closed reduction, casting

##### Buckle (Torus) Fracture
- **Description**: Compression fracture, cortex buckles
- **Location**: Metaphysis (distal radius most common)
- **Management**: Immobilization, minimal displacement

##### Plastic Deformation
- **Description**: Bone bends without breaking
- **Location**: Forearm, fibula
- **Management**: May require reduction if significant angulation

#### Physeal (Growth Plate) Injuries

##### Salter-Harris Classification
- **Type I**: Through physis only (often not visible on x-ray)
- **Type II**: Through physis and metaphysis (most common, 75%)
- **Type III**: Through physis and epiphysis (intra-articular)
- **Type IV**: Through metaphysis, physis, and epiphysis
- **Type V**: Compression of physis (rare, often diagnosed retrospectively)

##### Management
- **Type I-II**: Usually closed reduction, casting
- **Type III-IV**: Often require ORIF (intra-articular)
- **Type V**: Supportive, high risk growth arrest
- **Orthopedic follow-up**: Monitor for growth disturbance

#### Supracondylar Humerus Fracture

##### Epidemiology
- **Most common elbow fracture**: In children
- **Age**: 5-7 years
- **Mechanism**: Fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH)

##### Complications
- **Volkmann's ischemic contracture**: Compartment syndrome, vascular injury
- **Nerve injury**: Anterior interosseous nerve (most common)
- **Cubitus varus**: "Gunstock deformity" from malunion

##### Assessment
- **Neurovascular exam**: Critical (radial pulse, hand perfusion, nerve function)
- **X-rays**: AP and lateral elbow
- **Anterior humeral line**: Should bisect capitellum
- **Posterior fat pad**: Indicates effusion/fracture

##### Management
- **Gartland classification**:
  - Type I: Non-displaced - splint
  - Type II: Displaced with intact posterior cortex - closed reduction, pinning
  - Type III: Completely displaced - urgent closed/open reduction, pinning
- **Vascular compromise**: Emergent orthopedic consultation
- **Admission**: Most displaced fractures

## Child Maltreatment

### Types of Abuse
- **Physical abuse**: Non-accidental trauma
- **Sexual abuse**: Sexual exploitation
- **Emotional abuse**: Psychological harm
- **Neglect**: Failure to provide basic needs

### Recognition of Physical Abuse

#### Red Flags in History
- **Inconsistent history**: Story changes or inconsistent with injuries
- **Delay in seeking care**: Unreasonable delay
- **Inappropriate affect**: Lack of concern by caregiver
- **Blaming**: Blame placed on child or sibling
- **Incompatible with development**: Injury doesn't match developmental stage

#### Injury Patterns Concerning for Abuse

##### Bruising
- **TEN-4 FACESp rule**: Bruising in these locations concerning in children who don't cruise:
  - Torso
  - Ear
  - Neck
  - 4: Age <4 months
  - Frenulum
  - Angle of jaw
  - Cheek
  - Eyelid
  - Subconjunctival
  - Patterned
- **Pattern**: Looped cord, handprint, belt, bite mark
- **Multiple ages**: Different colors suggesting different times

##### Burns
- **Immersion pattern**: Stocking-glove distribution, sparing of flexural creases
- **Contact**: Cigarette, curling iron (circular, uniform depth)
- **Patterned**: Grid, brand mark

##### Fractures
- **High specificity**: Rib fractures (especially posterior), metaphyseal corner fractures, multiple fractures at different stages
- **Moderate specificity**: Scapula, spinous process, sternum
- **Age**: Fractures in non-ambulatory infants

##### Head Trauma
- **Abusive head trauma**: Shaken baby syndrome
- **Presentation**: Subdural hematoma, retinal hemorrhages, altered mental status
- **No history of trauma**: Or minor mechanism
- **Age**: Usually <1 year

### Management
- **Ensure child safety**: Immediate priority
- **Thorough documentation**: Photographs, detailed description, quotes
- **Skeletal survey**: Age <2 years with suspected abuse
- **Report to authorities**: Mandatory reporting
- **Multidisciplinary team**: Child protection team, social work, law enforcement
- **Hospitalization**: If safety concerns or medical needs

## Toxicology

### Common Pediatric Ingestions

#### Acetaminophen

##### Toxicity
- **Dose**: >150 mg/kg potentially toxic
- **Mechanism**: Hepatotoxicity from toxic metabolite (NAPQI)

##### Clinical Presentation
- **Stage 1 (0-24 hours)**: Nausea, vomiting, or asymptomatic
- **Stage 2 (24-72 hours)**: RUQ pain, elevated transaminases
- **Stage 3 (72-96 hours)**: Hepatic failure, encephalopathy
- **Stage 4 (>5 days)**: Recovery or death

##### Management
- **Level**: At 4 hours post-ingestion
- **Rumack-Matthew nomogram**: Guides treatment
- **N-acetylcysteine (NAC)**: Antidote
  - Loading dose: 140 mg/kg PO/IV
  - Maintenance: 70 mg/kg every 4 hours × 17 doses (PO) or IV protocol
- **Start NAC if**: >8 hours from ingestion and likely toxic dose

#### Iron

##### Toxicity
- **Dose**: >20 mg/kg elemental iron causes symptoms, >60 mg/kg severe
- **Mechanism**: Direct corrosive effect, cellular toxicity

##### Clinical Presentation
- **Stage 1 (0-6 hours)**: Vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, GI bleeding
- **Stage 2 (6-24 hours)**: Apparent improvement
- **Stage 3 (12-48 hours)**: Shock, metabolic acidosis
- **Stage 4 (2-6 weeks)**: GI scarring, obstruction

##### Diagnosis
- **Serum iron**: Peak at 4-6 hours
- **Total iron-binding capacity**: Calculate free iron
- **Abdominal x-ray**: Pills may be radiopaque

##### Management
- **Whole bowel irrigation**: If visible pills on x-ray
- **Deferoxamine**: Chelation if serum iron >500 mcg/dL or severe symptoms
- **Supportive care**: Fluids, correct acidosis

#### Caustic Ingestion

##### Substances
- **Acids**: Toilet bowl cleaners, drain cleaners
- **Alkalis**: Drain cleaners, disk batteries, detergent pods
- **Mechanism**: Direct tissue injury (alkalis worse - liquefactive necrosis)

##### Clinical Presentation
- **Oropharyngeal burns**: Erythema, ulceration
- **Drooling**: Difficulty swallowing
- **Stridor**: Airway compromise
- **Abdominal pain**: Esophageal or gastric injury
- **Perforation**: Mediastinitis, peritonitis

##### Management
- **DO NOT induce vomiting**: Risk of re-exposure
- **DO NOT neutralize**: Exothermic reaction
- **Airway protection**: If respiratory compromise
- **NPO**: Nothing by mouth
- **Endoscopy**: Within 12-24 hours to assess injury
- **Surgical consultation**: If perforation suspected

## Key Points

- Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT) allows rapid visual assessment without touching the child
- Children compensate well for shock but decompensate suddenly; hypotension is a late sign
- High-quality CPR with minimal interruptions is the foundation of successful resuscitation
- Epinephrine IM is first-line treatment for anaphylaxis; do not delay for IV access
- Status asthmaticus requires aggressive bronchodilator therapy; consider magnesium and NIPPV before intubation
- Abdominal trauma in children is usually managed non-operatively for solid organ injuries
- Supracondylar humerus fractures require careful neurovascular assessment and urgent orthopedic consultation
- PECARN rules guide CT imaging decisions for pediatric head injuries
- Child abuse should be considered when injuries are inconsistent with history or developmental stage
- N-acetylcysteine is the antidote for acetaminophen toxicity; use nomogram to guide treatment
- Caustic ingestions should not be induced to vomit or neutralized; endoscopy assesses injury
- Family-centered care and age-appropriate communication are essential throughout emergency care
