# Parasitology

## Overview

Parasitology is the study of parasites, organisms that live on or within a host and derive nutrients at the host's expense. Medical parasitology focuses on eukaryotic parasites that cause human disease, including protozoa (single-celled organisms), helminths (worms), and ectoparasites (external parasites). Parasitic infections affect billions of people worldwide, predominantly in tropical and subtropical regions, and remain a major cause of morbidity and mortality in resource-limited settings.

Parasites have complex life cycles often involving multiple hosts (definitive and intermediate) and developmental stages. Understanding these life cycles, transmission patterns, pathogenesis mechanisms, and diagnostic approaches is essential for effective treatment and prevention. Many parasitic diseases are neglected tropical diseases (NTDs) requiring global health interventions.

### Key Characteristics

**Parasitic Properties**:
- Eukaryotic organisms with complex cellular structure
- Obligate or facultative parasitism
- Complex life cycles with multiple developmental stages
- Host specificity and tissue tropism
- Immune evasion strategies

**Classification**:
- **Protozoa**: Single-celled organisms (Plasmodium, Toxoplasma, Giardia)
- **Helminths**: Multicellular worms (nematodes, cestodes, trematodes)
- **Ectoparasites**: External parasites (lice, scabies, ticks)

## Classification and Structure

### Protozoa

Single-celled eukaryotic organisms classified by locomotion:

**Amoebae** (movement by pseudopodia):
- Entamoeba histolytica
- Naegleria fowleri, Acanthamoeba

**Flagellates** (movement by flagella):
- Giardia lamblia
- Trichomonas vaginalis
- Trypanosoma species (African and American trypanosomiasis)
- Leishmania species

**Ciliates** (movement by cilia):
- Balantidium coli (rare)

**Apicomplexans** (no specific locomotion, obligate intracellular):
- Plasmodium species (malaria)
- Toxoplasma gondii
- Cryptosporidium species
- Isospora belli
- Babesia species

**Microsporidia**:
- Obligate intracellular, spore-forming
- Enterocytozoon, Encephalitozoon

### Helminths

Multicellular worms with organ systems:

**Nematodes (Roundworms)**:
- Cylindrical, unsegmented body
- Complete digestive system
- **Intestinal**: Ascaris, hookworms, Strongyloides, Enterobius, Trichuris
- **Tissue**: Trichinella, filarial worms (Wuchereria, Onchocerca, Loa loa)

**Cestodes (Tapeworms)**:
- Flat, segmented body (proglottids)
- No digestive system (absorb nutrients through tegument)
- Scolex (head) with attachment organs
- Examples: Taenia, Diphyllobothrium, Echinococcus

**Trematodes (Flukes)**:
- Flat, leaf-shaped, unsegmented body
- Incomplete digestive system
- Oral and ventral suckers
- Examples: Schistosoma, Clonorchis, Paragonimus, Fasciola

### Ectoparasites

**Arthropods**:
- Lice (Pediculus, Phthirus)
- Scabies mite (Sarcoptes scabiei)
- Ticks (Ixodes, Dermacentor)

## Pathophysiology

### Host-Parasite Interactions

**Parasite Entry and Establishment**:
- Oral ingestion (fecal-oral route)
- Vector transmission (mosquitoes, flies, ticks)
- Skin penetration
- Sexual transmission
- Transplacental/congenital

**Tissue Tropism**:
Determined by:
- Surface molecules and receptors
- Host immune status
- Anatomical and physiological factors

**Pathogenic Mechanisms**:
- Direct tissue damage and destruction
- Mechanical obstruction
- Toxin production
- Immune-mediated pathology
- Nutritional competition
- Secondary bacterial infections

### Immune Evasion Strategies

**Antigenic Variation**:
- Trypanosomes change surface glycoproteins
- Plasmodium varies surface antigens

**Immunosuppression**:
- Modulation of host immune responses
- Inhibition of complement activation

**Intracellular Survival**:
- Toxoplasma, Leishmania in macrophages
- Prevention of phagolysosome fusion

**Physical Barriers**:
- Schistosome tegument acquisition of host molecules
- Echinococcus hydatid cyst

**Molecular Mimicry**:
- Expression of host-like molecules
- Reduced immune recognition

### Host Defense Mechanisms

**Innate Immunity**:
- Physical barriers
- Complement activation
- Phagocytosis (limited effectiveness against large parasites)
- Eosinophils (antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity)

**Adaptive Immunity**:
- **Th2 response**: Predominant for helminths (IgE, eosinophils, mast cells)
- **Th1 response**: Important for intracellular protozoa
- Antibody production (protective or pathologic)

## Clinical Presentation

### Intestinal Protozoa

**Entamoeba histolytica**:
- **Transmission**: Fecal-oral (cysts in contaminated food/water)
- **Geographic distribution**: Worldwide, especially tropics

**Clinical Forms**:
1. **Asymptomatic colonization**: Most common (90%)
2. **Intestinal amebiasis**: Bloody diarrhea with mucus ("raspberry jelly" stools), abdominal pain, tenesmus
3. **Amebic liver abscess**: RUQ pain, fever, hepatomegaly; can rupture into pleural/pericardial space

**Diagnosis**:
- Stool microscopy: Trophozoites with ingested RBCs (pathognomonic), cysts
- Antigen detection (distinguishes E. histolytica from E. dispar)
- Serology for invasive disease
- Imaging for liver abscess (single lesion in right lobe)

**Treatment**:
- **Intestinal**: Metronidazole or tinidazole + paromomycin (luminal agent)
- **Liver abscess**: Metronidazole + paromomycin, drainage rarely needed

**Giardia lamblia** (Giardia duodenalis):
- **Transmission**: Fecal-oral (cysts in water - camping, daycare)
- **Geographic distribution**: Worldwide

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Watery, foul-smelling diarrhea
- Bloating, flatulence, steatorrhea
- Chronic malabsorption if untreated
- No blood in stools

**Diagnosis**:
- Stool antigen test (ELISA) - most sensitive
- Microscopy: Trophozoites ("falling leaf" motility), cysts (oval with 4 nuclei)
- String test or duodenal aspirate if negative

**Treatment**:
- Metronidazole or tinidazole
- Nitazoxanide (alternative)

**Cryptosporidium parvum/hominis**:
- **Transmission**: Fecal-oral (resistant oocysts in water)
- **Risk**: Immunocompromised (HIV/AIDS)

**Clinical Presentation**:
- **Immunocompetent**: Self-limited watery diarrhea (1-2 weeks)
- **Immunocompromised**: Severe, chronic, voluminous diarrhea; cholangitis

**Diagnosis**:
- Modified acid-fast stain (oocysts)
- Antigen detection (ELISA, immunofluorescence)
- PCR

**Treatment**:
- **Immunocompetent**: Supportive care, nitazoxanide
- **Immunocompromised**: Antiretroviral therapy for HIV, nitazoxanide (limited efficacy)

**Cyclospora cayetanensis**:
- **Transmission**: Fecal-oral (contaminated produce - berries, herbs)
- **Presentation**: Prolonged watery diarrhea, anorexia, weight loss
- **Diagnosis**: Modified acid-fast stain (larger than Cryptosporidium)
- **Treatment**: TMP-SMX

### Blood and Tissue Protozoa

**Plasmodium species** (Malaria):

**Species**:
- **P. falciparum**: Most severe, cerebral malaria, high mortality
- **P. vivax**: Relapsing (hypnozoites in liver)
- **P. ovale**: Relapsing (hypnozoites)
- **P. malariae**: Chronic infection, nephrotic syndrome
- **P. knowlesi**: Zoonotic, severe

**Transmission**: Female Anopheles mosquito

**Life Cycle**:
- Sporozoites (mosquito saliva) → hepatocytes (exoerythrocytic stage)
- Merozoites → RBCs (erythrocytic stage)
- Gametocytes → mosquito (sexual stage)

**Clinical Presentation**:
- **Paroxysms**: Cyclic fever, chills, sweats (every 48-72 hours depending on species)
- Anemia, splenomegaly, hepatomegaly
- **Complications**:
  - **Cerebral malaria**: Altered consciousness, seizures, coma (P. falciparum)
  - Severe anemia
  - Acute respiratory distress
  - Renal failure ("blackwater fever")
  - Hypoglycemia
  - Splenic rupture

**Diagnosis**:
- **Thick blood smear**: Screening (sensitivity)
- **Thin blood smear**: Species identification, parasitemia quantification
- Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs): Detect parasite antigens (HRP-2, pLDH)
- PCR: Most sensitive

**Treatment**:
- **Uncomplicated P. falciparum** (chloroquine-resistant areas):
  - Artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT): Artemether-lumefantrine, artesunate-amodiaquine
  - Atovaquone-proguanil
  - Quinine + doxycycline/clindamycin

- **Severe malaria**: IV artesunate (preferred) or IV quinidine

- **P. vivax, P. ovale**: Chloroquine (if sensitive) + primaquine (eradicate hypnozoites)
  - G6PD testing required before primaquine

- **P. malariae**: Chloroquine

**Prevention**:
- Chemoprophylaxis: Atovaquone-proguanil, doxycycline, mefloquine
- Insecticide-treated bed nets (ITNs)
- Indoor residual spraying
- Protective clothing, DEET repellent
- RTS,S/AS01 vaccine (limited efficacy)

**Toxoplasma gondii**:
- **Definitive host**: Cats (oocysts in feces)
- **Intermediate hosts**: Humans, livestock (tissue cysts)
- **Transmission**: Ingestion of oocysts (cat feces, contaminated soil), undercooked meat (tissue cysts), transplacental

**Clinical Presentation**:
- **Immunocompetent**: Usually asymptomatic or mild mononucleosis-like illness
- **Immunocompromised** (HIV with CD4 < 100):
  - CNS toxoplasmosis: Encephalitis, focal neurologic deficits, seizures, altered mental status
  - Imaging: Multiple ring-enhancing lesions
- **Congenital toxoplasmosis**: Chorioretinitis, hydrocephalus, intracranial calcifications, intellectual disability

**Diagnosis**:
- Serology: IgM (acute), IgG (chronic/latent)
- PCR (amniotic fluid for congenital)
- Brain biopsy (rarely needed)
- Imaging (MRI/CT)

**Treatment**:
- **Acute/congenital**: Pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + leucovorin
- **CNS in HIV**: Pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + leucovorin
- **Prophylaxis**: TMP-SMX (HIV with CD4 < 100, also covers PCP)
- **Pregnancy**: Spiramycin (prevent transmission)

**Trypanosoma brucei** (African Trypanosomiasis/Sleeping Sickness):
- **T.b. gambiense**: West Africa, chronic (years)
- **T.b. rhodesiense**: East Africa, acute (months)
- **Vector**: Tsetse fly (Glossina)

**Clinical Presentation**:
- **Stage 1** (hemolymphatic): Fever, lymphadenopathy (Winterbottom sign - posterior cervical), hepatosplenomegaly
- **Stage 2** (CNS): Somnolence, personality changes, tremors, ataxia, coma

**Diagnosis**:
- Blood smear (trypomastigotes)
- Lymph node aspiration
- CSF examination for CNS involvement

**Treatment**:
- **Stage 1**: Pentamidine (T.b. gambiense), suramin (T.b. rhodesiense)
- **Stage 2**: Eflornithine + nifurtimox, melarsoprol (toxic)

**Trypanosoma cruzi** (American Trypanosomiasis/Chagas Disease):
- **Vector**: Reduviid bug ("kissing bug") - feces contaminate bite wound
- **Other transmission**: Blood transfusion, congenital, oral (contaminated food)
- **Geographic distribution**: Latin America

**Clinical Presentation**:
- **Acute**: Romaña sign (unilateral periorbital edema), chagoma (skin nodule), fever, lymphadenopathy
- **Chronic indeterminate**: Asymptomatic (years to decades)
- **Chronic symptomatic**:
  - Cardiomyopathy (dilated, arrhythmias, heart block)
  - Megaesophagus, megacolon (achalasia-like)

**Diagnosis**:
- **Acute**: Blood smear (trypomastigotes)
- **Chronic**: Serology (2 different tests required)
- PCR

**Treatment**:
- **Acute/early chronic**: Benznidazole or nifurtimox
- **Chronic with organ damage**: Supportive care, treatment benefit uncertain

**Leishmania species** (Leishmaniasis):
- **Vector**: Sandfly (Phlebotomus, Lutzomyia)
- **Reservoir**: Dogs, rodents
- **Forms**: Cutaneous, mucocutaneous, visceral

**Cutaneous Leishmaniasis** (CL):
- Painless papule → ulcer with raised border
- Self-limited (months) but leaves scar
- Treatment: Local therapy (cryotherapy, intralesional antimony), systemic if extensive

**Mucocutaneous Leishmaniasis** (MCL):
- Progressive destruction of nasal/oral mucosa
- "Tapir nose" deformity
- Treatment: Liposomal amphotericin B, pentavalent antimony

**Visceral Leishmaniasis** (Kala-azar):
- Fever, hepatosplenomegaly, pancytopenia, hyperpigmentation
- Fatal if untreated
- **Diagnosis**: Amastigotes in bone marrow, spleen aspirate; rK39 antigen test
- **Treatment**: Liposomal amphotericin B (first-line), miltefosine

### Intestinal Nematodes

**Enterobius vermicularis** (Pinworm):
- **Transmission**: Fecal-oral, autoinfection
- **Epidemiology**: Most common helminth in US, children

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Perianal itching (nocturnal - female migrates to lay eggs)
- Often asymptomatic

**Diagnosis**:
- Scotch tape test (cellophane tape to perianal area in morning)
- Microscopy shows eggs (flattened on one side)

**Treatment**:
- Albendazole or mebendazole or pyrantel pamoate (single dose, repeat in 2 weeks)
- Treat entire household

**Ascaris lumbricoides** (Roundworm):
- **Transmission**: Fecal-oral (eggs in contaminated soil)
- Largest intestinal nematode (15-35 cm)

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Often asymptomatic
- **Pulmonary phase**: Löffler syndrome (eosinophilic pneumonia) as larvae migrate through lungs
- **Intestinal phase**: Abdominal pain, malnutrition
- **Complications**: Bowel obstruction, biliary obstruction, appendicitis

**Diagnosis**:
- Stool microscopy: Eggs (thick shell with mammillated coat)
- Adult worms may be passed or vomited

**Treatment**:
- Albendazole or mebendazole

**Trichuris trichiura** (Whipworm):
- **Transmission**: Fecal-oral (eggs in soil)
- Anterior end thin, posterior thick (whip-like)

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Light infection: Asymptomatic
- Heavy infection: Bloody diarrhea, rectal prolapse (children), anemia

**Diagnosis**:
- Stool microscopy: Barrel-shaped eggs with polar plugs

**Treatment**:
- Albendazole or mebendazole

**Ancylostoma duodenale** and **Necator americanus** (Hookworms):
- **Transmission**: Larvae penetrate skin (walking barefoot)
- **Distribution**: Tropical/subtropical regions

**Clinical Presentation**:
- **Cutaneous**: "Ground itch" at penetration site
- **Pulmonary**: Cough (larval migration)
- **Intestinal**: Iron deficiency anemia (worms feed on blood), protein malnutrition
- Major cause of anemia in developing countries

**Diagnosis**:
- Stool microscopy: Eggs (thin-shelled, oval)
- Eosinophilia

**Treatment**:
- Albendazole or mebendazole
- Iron supplementation

**Strongyloides stercoralis**:
- **Transmission**: Larvae penetrate skin
- **Unique feature**: Autoinfection (can persist for decades)

**Clinical Presentation**:
- **Cutaneous**: Larva currens (rapidly migrating serpiginous rash)
- **Pulmonary**: Cough
- **GI**: Diarrhea, abdominal pain
- **Hyperinfection syndrome**: Disseminated infection in immunosuppressed (corticosteroids, HTLV-1)
  - Pulmonary infiltrates, gram-negative sepsis (larvae carry bacteria)
  - High mortality

**Diagnosis**:
- Stool microscopy: Rhabditiform larvae (not eggs)
- Serology (sensitive, especially for screening before immunosuppression)
- Eosinophilia (absent in hyperinfection)

**Treatment**:
- Ivermectin (preferred)
- Albendazole (alternative)
- Screen and treat before starting immunosuppression

### Tissue Nematodes

**Trichinella spiralis** (Trichinosis):
- **Transmission**: Ingestion of undercooked meat (pork, bear, walrus)
- Larvae encyst in muscle

**Clinical Presentation**:
- **Early (GI)**: Diarrhea, nausea
- **Late (muscle invasion)**: Myalgia, periorbital edema, fever
- Splinter hemorrhages
- Eosinophilia

**Diagnosis**:
- Serology
- Muscle biopsy (rarely needed)
- Elevated creatine kinase

**Treatment**:
- Albendazole or mebendazole
- Corticosteroids for severe symptoms

**Wuchereria bancrofti** and **Brugia malayi** (Lymphatic Filariasis):
- **Vector**: Mosquitoes
- **Distribution**: Tropical regions

**Clinical Presentation**:
- **Acute**: Lymphangitis, lymphadenitis, fever
- **Chronic**: Lymphedema, elephantiasis (massive limb swelling), hydrocele
- Tropical pulmonary eosinophilia

**Diagnosis**:
- Blood smear at night (nocturnal microfilaremia)
- Antigen detection

**Treatment**:
- Diethylcarbamazine (DEC)
- Mass drug administration programs (DEC + albendazole + ivermectin)

**Onchocerca volvulus** (River Blindness):
- **Vector**: Blackfly (Simulium) near rivers
- **Distribution**: Sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Subcutaneous nodules (onchocercomas)
- Pruritic dermatitis ("leopard skin")
- Ocular disease → blindness (microfilariae in eye)

**Diagnosis**:
- Skin snip microscopy (microfilariae)

**Treatment**:
- Ivermectin (kills microfilariae, annual dosing)
- Doxycycline (kills Wolbachia endosymbiont)
- Nodulectomy

**Loa loa** (Eye Worm):
- **Vector**: Mango fly (Chrysops)
- **Distribution**: Central/West Africa

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Calabar swellings (migratory angioedema)
- Subconjunctival migration (visible worm)

**Diagnosis**:
- Blood smear at midday (diurnal microfilaremia)

**Treatment**:
- Diethylcarbamazine (if low microfilaremia)
- Albendazole
- **Caution**: DEC can cause encephalopathy if high microfilaremia

### Cestodes (Tapeworms)

**Taenia solium** (Pork Tapeworm):

**Taeniasis** (intestinal infection):
- **Transmission**: Undercooked pork (cysticerci)
- Usually asymptomatic
- Proglottids in stool
- **Treatment**: Praziquantel or niclosamide

**Cysticercosis** (larval infection):
- **Transmission**: Fecal-oral (eggs from human feces)
- Cysts in CNS, muscle, eye
- **Neurocysticercosis**: Most common parasitic CNS infection worldwide
  - Seizures (most common presentation)
  - Hydrocephalus, meningitis
- **Diagnosis**: Imaging (CT/MRI) - cysts with scolex, calcifications; serology
- **Treatment**: Albendazole + praziquantel + corticosteroids (for inflammation); antiepileptics

**Taenia saginata** (Beef Tapeworm):
- **Transmission**: Undercooked beef
- Longer than T. solium (up to 25 meters)
- No cysticercosis in humans
- **Treatment**: Praziquantel

**Diphyllobothrium latum** (Fish Tapeworm):
- **Transmission**: Undercooked fish
- Longest tapeworm (up to 30 meters)
- Competes for vitamin B12 → megaloblastic anemia
- **Treatment**: Praziquantel

**Echinococcus granulosus** (Hydatid Disease):
- **Definitive host**: Dogs
- **Intermediate hosts**: Sheep, humans (accidental)

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Slowly growing cysts (years)
- **Liver** (most common): RUQ mass, obstructive jaundice
- **Lung**: Cough, chest pain, hemoptysis
- **Rupture**: Anaphylaxis, secondary echinococcosis

**Diagnosis**:
- Imaging: Cystic lesion, "daughter cysts," calcifications
- Serology (ELISA)
- **Avoid aspiration**: Risk of anaphylaxis and dissemination

**Treatment**:
- Surgical resection or PAIR (puncture, aspiration, injection of hypertonic saline, re-aspiration)
- Albendazole (perioperative, small cysts)

### Trematodes (Flukes)

**Schistosoma species**:
- **S. mansoni**: Africa, South America (intestinal)
- **S. japonicum**: Asia (intestinal)
- **S. haematobium**: Africa, Middle East (urinary)

**Transmission**: Freshwater exposure (cercariae penetrate skin)

**Life Cycle**:
- Cercariae → skin → bloodstream → mature in mesenteric/vesical venules → eggs laid → penetrate tissues → water → miracidia → snails → cercariae

**Clinical Presentation**:
- **Acute (Katayama fever)**: Fever, urticaria, eosinophilia (weeks after exposure)
- **Chronic**:
  - **S. mansoni/japonicum**: Hepatic fibrosis, portal hypertension, splenomegaly
  - **S. haematobium**: Hematuria, bladder fibrosis, bladder cancer (squamous cell)
- Granulomatous inflammation around eggs

**Diagnosis**:
- Stool (S. mansoni/japonicum) or urine (S. haematobium) microscopy for eggs
- Serology
- Eosinophilia

**Treatment**:
- Praziquantel

**Prevention**:
- Avoid freshwater exposure in endemic areas
- Snail control

**Clonorchis sinensis** (Chinese Liver Fluke):
- **Transmission**: Undercooked freshwater fish
- **Distribution**: East Asia

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Often asymptomatic
- Biliary obstruction, cholangitis
- Cholangiocarcinoma (chronic infection)

**Diagnosis**:
- Stool microscopy (small operculated eggs)

**Treatment**:
- Praziquantel

**Paragonimus westermani** (Lung Fluke):
- **Transmission**: Undercooked crustaceans (crabs, crayfish)
- **Distribution**: Asia, Africa, Latin America

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Chronic cough, hemoptysis
- Mimics tuberculosis
- Eosinophilia

**Diagnosis**:
- Sputum or stool microscopy (operculated eggs)
- Imaging: Infiltrates, cavities

**Treatment**:
- Praziquantel

### Ectoparasites

**Sarcoptes scabiei** (Scabies):
- **Transmission**: Direct skin-to-skin contact
- Mite burrows in stratum corneum

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Intense pruritus (worse at night)
- Linear burrows (pathognomonic) - interdigital spaces, wrists, axillae, genitals
- Secondary bacterial infection from scratching
- **Crusted (Norwegian) scabies**: Hyperinfestation in immunocompromised

**Diagnosis**:
- Skin scraping: Mites, eggs, fecal pellets

**Treatment**:
- Permethrin cream (first-line)
- Ivermectin (oral, for crusted scabies)
- Treat close contacts
- Wash bedding/clothing

**Pediculus humanus** (Body/Head Lice) and **Phthirus pubis** (Pubic Lice):
- **Transmission**: Close contact, fomites

**Clinical Presentation**:
- Pruritus
- Nits attached to hair shafts
- Body lice can transmit epidemic typhus, trench fever

**Treatment**:
- Permethrin or malathion lotion
- Manual nit removal
- Wash clothing/bedding

## Diagnosis

### Microscopy

**Stool Examination**:
- **Ova and parasite (O&P) exam**: 3 specimens on different days
- Concentration methods (formalin-ethyl acetate)
- Special stains:
  - Trichrome for protozoa
  - Modified acid-fast for Cryptosporidium, Cyclospora, Isospora

**Blood Smears**:
- **Thick smear**: Screening (malaria, filaria)
- **Thin smear**: Species identification (malaria)
- Timing important (nocturnal vs diurnal microfilaremia)

**Tissue Specimens**:
- Biopsy (liver, muscle, lymph node)
- Special stains (H&E, GMS, trichrome)

### Antigen Detection

**Immunoassays**:
- Giardia and Cryptosporidium antigen (stool)
- Malaria rapid diagnostic tests (blood)
- Schistosoma circulating antigen
- Echinococcus serology

### Molecular Methods

**PCR**:
- High sensitivity and specificity
- Speciation of parasites
- Detection of drug resistance genes (malaria)
- Not widely available for all parasites

### Serology

**Antibody Detection**:
- Useful for tissue parasites (Toxoplasma, Strongyloides, Schistosoma)
- Cannot distinguish active from past infection
- May be negative in immunocompromised

### Imaging

**Radiography/CT/MRI**:
- Neurocysticercosis
- Echinococcus cysts
- Schistosoma fibrosis
- Pneumonitis (Ascaris, Strongyloides)

**Ultrasound**:
- Liver flukes
- Echinococcus cysts
- Schistosoma bladder/liver pathology

### Culture

**Limited Use**:
- Most parasites cannot be cultured
- Entamoeba histolytica, Trichomonas (specialized media)
- Trypanosoma, Leishmania (research/reference labs)

## Treatment

### Antiparasitic Drug Classes

**Benzimidazoles**:

**Albendazole**:
- **Mechanism**: Inhibits microtubule polymerization
- **Spectrum**: Broad (most nematodes, some cestodes, protozoa)
- **Uses**: Ascaris, hookworm, Strongyloides, Trichuris, Enterobius, neurocysticercosis, echinococcosis, cutaneous larva migrans, trichinosis
- **Toxicity**: Generally well-tolerated, teratogenic

**Mebendazole**:
- Similar spectrum to albendazole
- Less well absorbed (better for intestinal helminths)

**Antifolates**:

**Pyrimethamine**:
- Inhibits dihydrofolate reductase
- **Uses**: Toxoplasmosis, malaria (with sulfadiazine or sulfa drugs)
- Always use with leucovorin (folinic acid) to prevent bone marrow toxicity

**Nitroimidazoles**:

**Metronidazole**:
- **Mechanism**: DNA damage via free radicals
- **Uses**: Giardia, Entamoeba, Trichomonas
- **Toxicity**: Metallic taste, disulfiram reaction with alcohol, peripheral neuropathy

**Tinidazole**:
- Similar to metronidazole, longer half-life, better tolerated

**Artemisinin Derivatives**:

**Artesunate, Artemether**:
- **Mechanism**: Free radical damage, inhibits SERCA
- **Uses**: Malaria (always in combination therapy)
- Rapid parasite clearance
- Short half-life (requires partner drug)

**Quinolines**:

**Chloroquine**:
- **Mechanism**: Inhibits heme polymerization in parasite food vacuole
- **Uses**: Malaria (P. vivax, chloroquine-sensitive P. falciparum), amebiasis
- **Resistance**: Widespread in P. falciparum

**Primaquine**:
- **Uses**: P. vivax/ovale radical cure (hypnozoites), PCP prophylaxis
- **Toxicity**: Hemolysis in G6PD deficiency (test required)

**Quinine/Quinidine**:
- **Uses**: Severe malaria (IV quinidine), uncomplicated malaria
- **Toxicity**: Cinchonism (tinnitus, visual disturbances), hypoglycemia, QT prolongation

**Praziquantel**:
- **Mechanism**: Increases calcium permeability → paralysis and tegument damage
- **Uses**: All schistosomes, most cestodes, most trematodes
- **Toxicity**: Minimal (dizziness, headache)
- Contraindicated in neurocysticercosis (can worsen inflammation)

**Ivermectin**:
- **Mechanism**: Hyperpolarization of parasite nerve/muscle cells (GABA agonist)
- **Uses**: Strongyloides, onchocerciasis, lymphatic filariasis, scabies, lice
- **Toxicity**: Minimal, can cause Mazzotti reaction (dead microfilariae release antigens)

**Pentavalent Antimonials**:
- **Sodium stibogluconate, meglumine antimoniate**
- **Uses**: Leishmaniasis
- **Toxicity**: Cardiotoxic, pancreatitis, hepatotoxic

**Amphotericin B**:
- **Uses**: Visceral leishmaniasis (liposomal formulation preferred)

**Miltefosine**:
- **Uses**: Leishmaniasis, free-living amoebae (Naegleria)
- Oral agent
- Teratogenic

**Nitazoxanide**:
- **Uses**: Giardia, Cryptosporidium, various intestinal parasites
- Broad spectrum, well tolerated

**Diethylcarbamazine (DEC)**:
- **Uses**: Lymphatic filariasis, Loa loa
- Kills microfilariae
- **Toxicity**: Allergic reactions to dying parasites

**Permethrin**:
- **Uses**: Scabies, lice (topical)
- Pyrethroid insecticide

## Prevention

### Personal Protective Measures

**Food and Water**:
- Boil or filter water
- Avoid raw/undercooked meat, fish, vegetables
- Peel fruits
- "Boil it, cook it, peel it, or forget it"

**Vector Control**:
- Insecticide-treated bed nets
- DEET repellent
- Protective clothing
- Avoid outdoor exposure at peak vector activity times

**Behavioral Modifications**:
- Avoid freshwater exposure in endemic areas (schistosomiasis)
- Wear shoes (hookworm, Strongyloides)
- Practice safe sex (Trichomonas)

### Chemoprophylaxis

**Malaria**:
- Atovaquone-proguanil (Malarone)
- Doxycycline
- Mefloquine
- Primaquine (terminal prophylaxis for P. vivax/ovale)

**Toxoplasmosis** (HIV with CD4 < 100):
- TMP-SMX

### Public Health Interventions

**Mass Drug Administration**:
- Soil-transmitted helminths: Albendazole/mebendazole
- Lymphatic filariasis: DEC + albendazole + ivermectin
- Schistosomiasis: Praziquantel

**Vector Control Programs**:
- Indoor residual spraying
- Larvicides
- Snail control (schistosomiasis)

**Improved Sanitation**:
- Clean water supply
- Proper sewage disposal
- Latrine construction

**Education**:
- Hygiene practices
- Food safety
- Disease awareness

### Vaccines

**Currently Available**:
- None widely used

**In Development**:
- Malaria vaccines (RTS,S/AS01 - limited efficacy)
- Leishmaniasis vaccines
- Schistosomiasis vaccines

## Key Points

1. Parasites are eukaryotic organisms with complex life cycles often involving multiple hosts
2. Protozoa are single-celled; helminths are multicellular worms
3. Malaria remains a major global health problem; P. falciparum causes most deaths
4. Artemisinin-based combination therapy is first-line for malaria
5. Toxoplasma gondii causes CNS disease in AIDS patients (CD4 < 100)
6. Giardia and Cryptosporidium cause waterborne diarrheal disease
7. Soil-transmitted helminths (Ascaris, hookworm, Trichuris) affect billions worldwide
8. Strongyloides can cause fatal hyperinfection in immunosuppressed patients
9. Schistosomiasis causes chronic liver disease and bladder cancer
10. Neurocysticercosis is the most common parasitic CNS infection
11. Eosinophilia suggests helminthic infection (not protozoa)
12. Prevention focuses on safe food/water, vector control, and mass drug administration
