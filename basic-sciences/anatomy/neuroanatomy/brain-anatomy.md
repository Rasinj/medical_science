# Brain Anatomy

## Introduction

The brain is the most complex organ in the human body, serving as the control center for all bodily functions, consciousness, cognition, emotion, and behavior. Understanding brain anatomy is fundamental to clinical neuroscience, enabling localization of lesions and interpretation of neurological deficits.

### Learning Objectives

1. Identify major brain structures and their anatomical relationships
2. Describe the functional organization of cerebral lobes
3. Understand brainstem anatomy and cranial nerve nuclei
4. Explain cerebellar organization and connections
5. Trace major white matter pathways
6. Correlate anatomical structures with clinical syndromes
7. Understand cerebral vasculature and stroke territories

## Cerebrum

The cerebrum is the largest part of the brain, responsible for higher cognitive functions, voluntary movement, and sensory processing.

### Gross Anatomy

#### External Features

**Hemispheres and Fissures:**
- Two cerebral hemispheres (left and right)
- **Longitudinal fissure**: Separates hemispheres (contains falx cerebri)
- **Lateral sulcus (Sylvian fissure)**: Separates frontal/parietal from temporal lobe
- **Central sulcus (Rolandic fissure)**: Separates frontal from parietal lobe
- **Parieto-occipital sulcus**: Separates parietal from occipital lobe (medial surface)

**Surface Features:**
- **Gyri**: Elevated ridges (increase surface area)
- **Sulci**: Shallow grooves
- **Fissures**: Deep grooves
- Cortical folding increases surface area to ~2,500 cm²

#### Lobes of the Cerebrum

Each hemisphere contains four main lobes:

**1. Frontal Lobe**

*Boundaries:*
- Anterior: Frontal pole
- Posterior: Central sulcus
- Inferior: Lateral sulcus

*Major Gyri:*
- **Precentral gyrus**: Primary motor cortex (M1)
  - Homunculus representation (medial to lateral: lower limb, trunk, upper limb, face)
  - Brodmann area 4
- **Superior frontal gyrus**: Supplementary motor area, prefrontal cortex
- **Middle frontal gyrus**: Frontal eye fields (area 8), prefrontal cortex
- **Inferior frontal gyrus**: Broca's area (areas 44, 45) in dominant hemisphere
  - Opercular part
  - Triangular part
  - Orbital part

*Functions:*
- Motor control and planning
- Executive functions (planning, decision-making, problem-solving)
- Working memory
- Personality and behavior
- Speech production (Broca's area)
- Eye movements (frontal eye fields)
- Olfaction (orbitofrontal cortex)

*Clinical Correlations:*
- **Frontal lobe syndrome**: Personality changes, disinhibition, apathy
- **Broca's aphasia**: Expressive aphasia (non-fluent speech, comprehension intact)
- **Frontal eye field lesion**: Deviation toward lesion side
- **Prefrontal damage**: Executive dysfunction, poor planning

**2. Parietal Lobe**

*Boundaries:*
- Anterior: Central sulcus
- Posterior: Parieto-occipital sulcus (medial), imaginary line (lateral)
- Inferior: Lateral sulcus

*Major Gyri:*
- **Postcentral gyrus**: Primary somatosensory cortex (S1)
  - Homunculus representation
  - Brodmann areas 3, 1, 2
- **Superior parietal lobule**: Spatial orientation, attention
  - Areas 5, 7
- **Inferior parietal lobule**:
  - **Supramarginal gyrus** (area 40): Language processing
  - **Angular gyrus** (area 39): Reading, writing, calculation
- **Intraparietal sulcus**: Separates superior and inferior lobules

*Functions:*
- Somatosensory processing (touch, pain, temperature, proprioception)
- Spatial awareness and orientation
- Visuospatial processing
- Language (dominant hemisphere)
- Calculation (angular gyrus)
- Body awareness

*Clinical Correlations:*
- **Contralateral sensory loss**: Postcentral gyrus lesion
- **Neglect syndrome**: Right (non-dominant) parietal lesion
- **Gerstmann syndrome**: Left angular gyrus lesion
  - Agraphia, acalculia, finger agnosia, left-right disorientation
- **Astereoagnosia**: Inability to identify objects by touch

**3. Temporal Lobe**

*Boundaries:*
- Superior: Lateral sulcus
- Anterior: Temporal pole
- Posterior/Superior: Imaginary line from parieto-occipital sulcus

*Major Gyri:*
- **Superior temporal gyrus**: Primary auditory cortex (areas 41, 42)
  - **Wernicke's area** (area 22, posterior): Language comprehension
  - Heschl's gyrus (transverse temporal gyrus): Primary auditory cortex
- **Middle temporal gyrus**: Visual processing, semantic memory
- **Inferior temporal gyrus**: Visual object recognition
- **Fusiform gyrus** (inferior surface): Face recognition
- **Parahippocampal gyrus** (medial): Memory formation

*Functions:*
- Auditory processing
- Language comprehension (Wernicke's area)
- Memory formation (hippocampus, medial structures)
- Emotion (amygdala)
- Visual object recognition
- Face recognition (fusiform face area)

*Clinical Correlations:*
- **Wernicke's aphasia**: Receptive aphasia (fluent but nonsensical speech, poor comprehension)
- **Temporal lobe epilepsy**: Déjà vu, olfactory/gustatory hallucinations
- **Anterograde amnesia**: Bilateral medial temporal damage (hippocampus)
- **Prosopagnosia**: Face recognition deficit (fusiform gyrus)
- **Auditory agnosia**: Inability to recognize sounds

**4. Occipital Lobe**

*Boundaries:*
- Anterior: Parieto-occipital sulcus (medial), imaginary line (lateral)
- Posterior: Occipital pole

*Major Features:*
- **Calcarine sulcus** (medial surface): Primary visual cortex (V1, area 17)
- **Cuneus**: Above calcarine sulcus
- **Lingual gyrus**: Below calcarine sulcus
- **Visual association areas**: V2-V5 (areas 18, 19)

*Functions:*
- Visual processing
- Color perception
- Motion detection
- Visual spatial processing

*Clinical Correlations:*
- **Homonymous hemianopia**: Visual field defect
  - Contralateral to lesion
  - Macular sparing if posterior cerebral artery stroke (collateral supply)
- **Cortical blindness**: Bilateral occipital damage
- **Visual agnosia**: Inability to recognize visual objects
- **Prosopagnosia**: Bilateral occipito-temporal lesions

#### Medial Surface Structures

**Corpus Callosum:**
- Largest commissural white matter tract
- Connects corresponding areas of hemispheres
- Parts (anterior to posterior):
  - Rostrum
  - Genu
  - Body
  - Splenium
- Allows interhemispheric communication

**Limbic Lobe:**
- **Cingulate gyrus**: Emotion, pain processing
- **Parahippocampal gyrus**: Memory
- **Hippocampus**: Memory consolidation
- **Fornix**: Output pathway from hippocampus to mammillary bodies
- **Mammillary bodies**: Part of Papez circuit (memory)

### Deep Structures

#### Basal Ganglia

A group of subcortical nuclei involved in motor control and other functions.

**Components:**
- **Striatum** (input):
  - **Caudate nucleus**: C-shaped, forms lateral wall of lateral ventricle
  - **Putamen**: Lateral to globus pallidus
  - (Caudate + putamen = dorsal striatum)
  - **Nucleus accumbens**: Ventral striatum (reward, addiction)
- **Globus pallidus** (output):
  - **Internal segment (GPi)**: Major output nucleus
  - **External segment (GPe)**: Indirect pathway
- **Substantia nigra** (midbrain):
  - **Pars compacta**: Dopaminergic neurons (degenerate in Parkinson's)
  - **Pars reticulata**: GABA-ergic output
- **Subthalamic nucleus**: Indirect pathway modulation

**Functional Pathways:**
- **Direct pathway**: Facilitates movement (striatum → GPi → thalamus → cortex)
- **Indirect pathway**: Inhibits movement (striatum → GPe → STN → GPi)
- **Dopamine modulation**: From substantia nigra pars compacta

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Parkinson's disease**: Substantia nigra degeneration
  - Bradykinesia, rigidity, resting tremor, postural instability
- **Huntington's disease**: Striatal degeneration
  - Chorea, cognitive decline, psychiatric symptoms
- **Hemiballismus**: Subthalamic nucleus lesion
  - Violent flinging movements of contralateral limbs
- **Wilson's disease**: Copper deposition in basal ganglia (putamen)

#### Internal Capsule

A major white matter structure containing ascending and descending fibers.

**Parts:**
- **Anterior limb**: Between caudate and lentiform nucleus
  - Frontopontine fibers
  - Anterior thalamic radiations
- **Genu**: "Knee" or bend
  - Corticobulbar fibers
- **Posterior limb**: Between thalamus and lentiform nucleus
  - Corticospinal fibers (motor)
  - Thalamocortical fibers (sensory)
  - Somatotopic organization (face → arm → leg)
- **Retrolenticular part**: Behind lentiform nucleus
  - Optic radiations
  - Parietotemporal fibers
- **Sublenticular part**: Below lentiform nucleus
  - Auditory radiations
  - Some optic radiations

**Clinical Significance:**
- **Lacunar stroke**: Small vessel occlusion
  - Pure motor hemiparesis (posterior limb)
  - Pure sensory stroke (thalamus/posterior limb)
  - Ataxic hemiparesis
- **Large stroke**: Contralateral hemiplegia and hemisensory loss

#### Thalamus

The "gateway to cortex" - major relay station for sensory information.

**Organization:**
- Egg-shaped structure forming lateral wall of third ventricle
- Divided by internal medullary lamina into nuclear groups

**Major Nuclei:**

*Relay Nuclei (specific):*
- **Ventral posterior lateral (VPL)**: Body sensation → S1
  - Receives medial lemniscus, spinothalamic tract
- **Ventral posterior medial (VPM)**: Face sensation → S1
  - Receives trigeminal pathways
- **Ventral lateral (VL)**: Motor information from cerebellum/basal ganglia → M1
- **Ventral anterior (VA)**: Motor information → premotor cortex
- **Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN)**: Vision → V1 (calcarine cortex)
  - Six layers, retinotopic organization
- **Medial geniculate nucleus (MGN)**: Hearing → A1 (Heschl's gyrus)
- **Anterior nucleus**: Memory (part of Papez circuit) → cingulate cortex
  - Receives fornix input

*Association Nuclei:*
- **Mediodorsal (MD)**: Prefrontal cortex (executive function, emotion)
- **Lateral posterior and pulvinar**: Parietal and temporal association areas

*Intralaminar Nuclei:*
- **Centromedian**: Arousal, pain modulation
- Nonspecific projections to cortex

*Reticular Nucleus:*
- Shell around thalamus
- GABAergic, regulates thalamic activity

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Thalamic stroke**: Contralateral hemisensory loss, thalamic pain syndrome
- **Thalamic pain syndrome** (Dejerine-Roussy): Severe, intractable pain
- **Anterior thalamic lesion**: Memory impairment (Papez circuit)

#### Hypothalamus

Small but critical structure controlling homeostasis and endocrine function.

**Location:**
- Forms floor and inferior lateral walls of third ventricle
- Below thalamus (separated by hypothalamic sulcus)

**Regions (anterior to posterior):**
- **Preoptic area**: Temperature regulation, sleep
- **Supraoptic region**: Above optic chiasm
  - Supraoptic nucleus: ADH production
  - Paraventricular nucleus: Oxytocin, ADH, CRH production
  - Suprachiasmatic nucleus: Circadian rhythm (master clock)
- **Tuberal region**: Median eminence, pituitary stalk
  - Arcuate nucleus: GnRH, hunger/satiety signals
  - Ventromedial nucleus: Satiety center
  - Dorsomedial nucleus: Blood pressure, GI function
  - Lateral hypothalamic area: Hunger center (lateral = hunger)
- **Mammillary region**: Posterior
  - Mammillary bodies: Memory (Papez circuit)
  - Posterior nucleus: Heat conservation, sympathetic

**Functions:**
- **Temperature regulation**: Anterior (cooling), posterior (heating)
- **Hunger and satiety**: Lateral (hunger), ventromedial (satiety)
- **Circadian rhythms**: Suprachiasmatic nucleus
- **Endocrine control**: Pituitary hormone regulation
- **Autonomic control**: Sympathetic and parasympathetic
- **Emotional behavior**: Connections with limbic system
- **Water balance**: ADH secretion
- **Sexual behavior**: Preoptic area

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Hypothalamic lesions**: Temperature dysregulation, diabetes insipidus
- **Craniopharyngioma**: Pituitary region tumor affecting hypothalamus
- **Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome**: Mammillary body damage (thiamine deficiency)
  - Confusion, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia, memory impairment
- **SIADH**: Inappropriate ADH secretion (dilutional hyponatremia)

## Brainstem

The brainstem connects the cerebrum to the spinal cord and contains vital centers for cardiovascular and respiratory control.

### General Organization

**Three Main Divisions (superior to inferior):**
1. Midbrain (mesencephalon)
2. Pons
3. Medulla oblongata

**Common Features:**
- Contains cranial nerve nuclei (CN III-XII)
- Contains reticular formation
- Contains ascending and descending tracts
- Organized into:
  - **Tectum**: "Roof" (dorsal)
  - **Tegmentum**: "Covering" (middle)
  - **Base**: Ventral (contains descending tracts)

### Midbrain (Mesencephalon)

**External Features:**

*Dorsal (Posterior) Surface:*
- **Tectum** ("roof"):
  - **Superior colliculi** (pair): Visual reflexes, saccadic eye movements
  - **Inferior colliculi** (pair): Auditory relay to medial geniculate nucleus
- **Cerebral peduncles** (lateral): Large fiber bundles

*Ventral (Anterior) Surface:*
- **Cerebral peduncles**: Contain descending fibers
  - **Crus cerebri** (base): Corticospinal and corticopontine fibers
- **Interpeduncular fossa**: Between cerebral peduncles
  - **Oculomotor nerve (CN III)** emerges from medial aspect
- **Substantia nigra**: Between crus and tegmentum (not visible externally)

**Internal Structures:**

*Tectum:*
- Superior and inferior colliculi (as above)

*Tegmentum:*
- **Red nucleus**: Motor coordination (rubrospinal tract origin)
  - Pink color (high vascularity and iron)
- **Substantia nigra**: Dopaminergic neurons
  - Pars compacta (dopamine)
  - Pars reticulata (GABA)
- **Oculomotor nucleus (CN III)**: Eye movement
- **Edinger-Westphal nucleus**: Parasympathetic to eye (pupil constriction)
- **Trochlear nucleus (CN IV)**: Superior oblique muscle
- **Periaqueductal gray**: Pain modulation, defensive behaviors
- **Ventral tegmental area**: Dopaminergic (reward pathway)

*Base (Crus Cerebri):*
- **Corticospinal tract**: Descending motor fibers
- **Corticobulbar tract**: To cranial nerve nuclei
- **Corticopontine fibers**: To pons

**Cerebral Aqueduct:**
- Connects third and fourth ventricles
- Surrounded by periaqueductal gray

**Clinical Syndromes:**

- **Weber syndrome**: Medial midbrain lesion
  - Ipsilateral CN III palsy (ptosis, lateral eye deviation, mydriasis)
  - Contralateral hemiparesis (crus cerebri)

- **Benedikt syndrome**: Tegmental lesion
  - Ipsilateral CN III palsy
  - Contralateral tremor/ataxia (red nucleus, cerebellar pathways)

- **Parinaud syndrome**: Dorsal midbrain (pineal region tumor)
  - Upward gaze palsy
  - Light-near dissociation (pupils accommodate but don't react to light)
  - Convergence-retraction nystagmus

- **Parkinson's disease**: Substantia nigra degeneration

### Pons

**External Features:**

*Ventral Surface:*
- **Basilar pons**: Large bulge
  - Contains transverse pontine fibers
  - Middle cerebellar peduncles emerge laterally
- **Basilar sulcus**: Midline groove (basilar artery)
- **Trigeminal nerve (CN V)**: Emerges laterally (largest cranial nerve)
- **Abducens nerve (CN VI)**: Emerges at pontomedullary junction
- **Facial nerve (CN VII)**: Emerges at pontomedullary junction (lateral)
- **Vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII)**: Emerges at pontomedullary junction (lateral to VII)

*Dorsal Surface:*
- Forms upper floor of fourth ventricle
- **Superior cerebellar peduncles**: Form lateral walls
- **Facial colliculus**: Elevation over abducens nucleus and facial nerve loop

**Internal Structures:**

*Basilar Pons (Base):*
- **Pontine nuclei**: Relay cortical input to cerebellum
- **Corticopontine fibers**: Descending from cortex
- **Pontocerebellar fibers**: Transverse fibers to cerebellum via middle cerebellar peduncle
- **Corticospinal fibers**: Pass through

*Pontine Tegmentum:*
- **Motor nuclei**:
  - **Trigeminal motor nucleus (CN V)**: Muscles of mastication
  - **Abducens nucleus (CN VI)**: Lateral rectus muscle
  - **Facial motor nucleus (CN VII)**: Facial expression muscles
- **Sensory nuclei**:
  - **Main sensory nucleus of CN V**: Touch, pressure (face)
  - **Spinal nucleus of CN V**: Pain, temperature (face) - extends to medulla
  - **Mesencephalic nucleus of CN V**: Proprioception
  - **Vestibular nuclei**: Balance and equilibrium
  - **Cochlear nuclei**: Hearing (dorsal and ventral)
- **Special nuclei**:
  - **Superior salivatory nucleus**: Parasympathetic (CN VII)
  - **Solitary nucleus**: Taste (CN VII)
- **Reticular formation**: Arousal, respiratory control
- **Medial longitudinal fasciculus (MLF)**: Coordinates eye movements
- **Locus coeruleus**: Noradrenergic neurons (arousal, attention)

**Clinical Syndromes:**

- **Lateral pontine syndrome** (AICA stroke):
  - Ipsilateral facial paralysis (CN VII)
  - Ipsilateral hearing loss (CN VIII)
  - Ipsilateral facial sensory loss (CN V)
  - Ipsilateral ataxia (middle cerebellar peduncle)
  - Contralateral body sensory loss (spinothalamic tract)

- **Medial pontine syndrome**:
  - Ipsilateral lateral rectus palsy (CN VI)
  - Contralateral hemiparesis (corticospinal tract)
  - Contralateral loss of proprioception/vibration (medial lemniscus)

- **Locked-in syndrome**: Basilar pons infarction
  - Quadriplegia and mutism
  - Consciousness preserved
  - Vertical eye movements and blinking preserved

- **Internuclear ophthalmoplegia (INO)**: MLF lesion
  - Impaired adduction of ipsilateral eye
  - Nystagmus of contralateral abducting eye
  - Multiple sclerosis (bilateral), stroke (unilateral)

### Medulla Oblongata

**External Features:**

*Ventral Surface:*
- **Pyramids**: Longitudinal elevations (corticospinal tracts)
- **Pyramidal decussation**: Crossing of corticospinal tracts (lower medulla)
- **Olivary eminence**: Lateral to pyramid (inferior olivary nucleus)
- **Anterolateral sulcus**: CN IX, X, XI roots emerge
- **Hypoglossal nerve (CN XII)**: Emerges between pyramid and olive

*Dorsal Surface:*
- Forms lower floor of fourth ventricle
- **Gracile tubercle** (medial): Nucleus gracilis (lower body sensation)
- **Cuneate tubercle** (lateral): Nucleus cuneatus (upper body sensation)
- **Obex**: Caudal point of fourth ventricle
- **Area postrema**: Lacks blood-brain barrier (vomiting center)

**Internal Structures:**

*Ventral Medulla:*
- **Pyramids**: Corticospinal tracts
  - 90% decussate (lateral corticospinal tract)
  - 10% remain ipsilateral (anterior corticospinal tract)
- **Inferior olivary nucleus**: Projects to cerebellum (motor learning)
- **Medial lemniscus**: Ascends from gracile/cuneate nuclei (after decussation)

*Dorsal Medulla:*
- **Gracile nucleus**: Lower body sensation (legs, lower trunk)
  - Receives gracile fasciculus
- **Cuneate nucleus**: Upper body sensation (arms, upper trunk)
  - Receives cuneate fasciculus
- **Cranial nerve nuclei**:
  - **Hypoglossal nucleus (CN XII)**: Tongue muscles
  - **Dorsal motor nucleus of vagus (CN X)**: Parasympathetic to thoracic/abdominal viscera
  - **Nucleus ambiguus (CN IX, X, XI)**: Motor to pharynx, larynx
  - **Solitary nucleus**: Taste (CN VII, IX, X), visceral sensation
  - **Vestibular nuclei**: Balance
  - **Cochlear nuclei**: Hearing
  - **Spinal trigeminal nucleus**: Pain/temperature from face

*Other Structures:*
- **Inferior cerebellar peduncle**: Connects medulla to cerebellum
- **Reticular formation**: Vital centers
  - Respiratory center
  - Cardiovascular center
  - Vomiting center
- **Raphe nuclei**: Serotonergic (pain modulation, mood)

**Clinical Syndromes:**

- **Lateral medullary syndrome** (Wallenberg, PICA stroke):
  - Ipsilateral facial pain/temperature loss (spinal trigeminal)
  - Ipsilateral Horner syndrome (sympathetic fibers)
  - Ipsilateral ataxia (inferior cerebellar peduncle)
  - Dysphagia, dysarthria, hoarseness (nucleus ambiguus)
  - Contralateral body pain/temperature loss (spinothalamic tract)
  - Vertigo, nystagmus (vestibular nuclei)

- **Medial medullary syndrome**:
  - Ipsilateral tongue deviation toward lesion (CN XII)
  - Contralateral hemiparesis (pyramid)
  - Contralateral loss of proprioception/vibration (medial lemniscus)

- **Central cord syndrome** (syringobulbia):
  - Bilateral loss of pain/temperature (crossing fibers)
  - Spares dorsal columns

## Cerebellum

The cerebellum ("little brain") coordinates movement, maintains balance, and contributes to motor learning.

### Gross Anatomy

**Location:**
- Posterior cranial fossa
- Below tentorium cerebelli
- Posterior to pons and medulla

**External Features:**
- **Two hemispheres**: Left and right
- **Vermis**: Midline structure connecting hemispheres
- **Folia**: Thin, parallel folds (like gyri)
- **Fissures**: Deep grooves separating lobes

**Lobes (Phylogenetic Classification):**
1. **Flocculonodular lobe** (archicerebellum):
   - Oldest evolutionarily
   - Nodulus (vermis) + flocculus (hemispheres)
   - Vestibular connections
   - Balance and eye movements

2. **Anterior lobe** (paleocerebellum):
   - Anterior to primary fissure
   - Spinocerebellar input
   - Limb and trunk coordination

3. **Posterior lobe** (neocerebellum):
   - Between primary and posterolateral fissures
   - Largest lobe
   - Cerebrocerebellar connections
   - Fine motor coordination

**Functional Classification:**
1. **Vestibulocerebellum**: Flocculonodular lobe
   - Balance, equilibrium, eye movements
   - Vestibular input
   - Lesions: Truncal ataxia, nystagmus

2. **Spinocerebellum**: Vermis and intermediate hemisphere
   - Limb and trunk coordination
   - Proprioceptive input
   - Lesions: Gait ataxia, limb ataxia

3. **Cerebrocerebellum**: Lateral hemispheres
   - Motor planning and timing
   - Cognitive functions
   - Corticopontocerebellar pathway
   - Lesions: Intention tremor, dysdiadochokinesia, dysmetria

### Cerebellar Peduncles

Three pairs connect cerebellum to brainstem:

**1. Inferior Cerebellar Peduncle (Restiform Body)**
- Connects to medulla
- **Afferent fibers** (mainly):
  - Dorsal spinocerebellar tract
  - Cuneocerebellar tract
  - Olivocerebellar fibers (from inferior olive)
  - Vestibulocerebellar fibers
- **Efferent fibers**:
  - To vestibular nuclei

**2. Middle Cerebellar Peduncle (Brachium Pontis)**
- Connects to pons
- **Afferent fibers only**:
  - Pontocerebellar fibers (largest peduncle)
  - Corticopontocerebellar pathway

**3. Superior Cerebellar Peduncle (Brachium Conjunctivum)**
- Connects to midbrain
- **Efferent fibers** (mainly):
  - Cerebellothalamic (to VL nucleus → motor cortex)
  - Cerebellorubrospinal
  - Decussates in midbrain
- **Afferent fibers**:
  - Ventral spinocerebellar tract

### Internal Structure

**Three Layers of Cortex:**
1. **Molecular layer** (outer): Parallel fibers, Purkinje dendrites
2. **Purkinje cell layer** (middle): Single row of large Purkinje cells
3. **Granular layer** (inner): Granule cells (most numerous neurons in brain)

**Deep Cerebellar Nuclei** (medial to lateral):
1. **Fastigial nucleus**: Vermis output → vestibular nuclei, reticular formation
2. **Interposed nuclei**: Intermediate hemisphere output
   - Globose nucleus
   - Emboliform nucleus
3. **Dentate nucleus**: Lateral hemisphere output → contralateral thalamus (VL)

### Clinical Correlations

**Cerebellar Signs:**
- **DANISH**:
  - **D**ysdiadochokinesia (rapid alternating movements)
  - **A**taxia (gait and limb)
  - **N**ystagmus
  - **I**ntention tremor
  - **S**peech (scanning, dysarthric)
  - **H**ypotonia

- **Ipsilateral deficits**: Cerebellar lesions cause same-side symptoms
- **Finger-to-nose test**: Intention tremor, dysmetria
- **Heel-to-shin test**: Limb ataxia
- **Romberg test**: Negative in cerebellar disease (positive in dorsal column)

**Cerebellar Stroke:**
- PICA territory: Vermis and inferior cerebellum
- AICA territory: Lateral brainstem and cerebellum
- SCA territory: Superior cerebellum

**Alcoholic Cerebellar Degeneration:**
- Anterior vermis preferentially affected
- Truncal and gait ataxia
- Lower limb ataxia
- Upper limbs relatively spared

## Cranial Nerves

Twelve pairs of nerves arising from brain and brainstem.

### CN I: Olfactory Nerve

**Type**: Sensory (special sense - smell)

**Pathway:**
- Olfactory receptors in nasal mucosa
- Olfactory nerve fibers through cribriform plate
- Synapse in olfactory bulb
- Olfactory tract to:
  - Primary olfactory cortex (piriform cortex, amygdala)
  - Orbitofrontal cortex (conscious smell)

**Testing**: Identify common odors (coffee, peppermint)

**Clinical**:
- **Anosmia**: Loss of smell
  - Head trauma (cribriform plate fracture)
  - Upper respiratory infection
  - Neurodegenerative diseases (Parkinson's, Alzheimer's)
- **Unilateral anosmia**: Often unnoticed unless tested

### CN II: Optic Nerve

**Type**: Sensory (special sense - vision)

**Pathway:**
- Retinal ganglion cells
- Optic nerve (intraorbital, intracanalicular, intracranial)
- Optic chiasm (nasal fibers decussate)
- Optic tract
- Lateral geniculate nucleus (thalamus)
- Optic radiations
- Primary visual cortex (calcarine sulcus, V1)

**Testing**:
- Visual acuity
- Visual fields (confrontation)
- Fundoscopy (optic disc)
- Pupillary light reflex (afferent)

**Clinical**:
- **Optic neuritis**: Demyelination (multiple sclerosis)
  - Painful vision loss
  - Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD)
- **Papilledema**: Optic disc swelling (increased ICP)
- **Visual field defects**: Localize lesions
  - Monocular: Before chiasm
  - Bitemporal hemianopia: Chiasm (pituitary tumor)
  - Homonymous hemianopia: Retrochiasmal

### CN III: Oculomotor Nerve

**Type**: Motor (somatic and parasympathetic)

**Nucleus**: Midbrain (ventral tegmentum)
- Oculomotor nucleus (somatic)
- Edinger-Westphal nucleus (parasympathetic)

**Course**: Exits interpeduncular fossa → cavernous sinus → superior orbital fissure

**Innervation**:
- **Somatic motor**:
  - Superior rectus (up)
  - Inferior rectus (down)
  - Medial rectus (adduction)
  - Inferior oblique (up and out)
  - Levator palpebrae superioris (eyelid elevation)
- **Parasympathetic**:
  - Ciliary muscle (accommodation)
  - Sphincter pupillae (pupil constriction)

**Testing**:
- Eye movements (H-pattern)
- Eyelid position
- Pupil size and reactivity

**Clinical**:
- **Complete CN III palsy**:
  - **"Down and out" eye**: Unopposed lateral rectus (VI) and superior oblique (IV)
  - **Ptosis**: Loss of levator function
  - **Mydriasis** (dilated pupil): Loss of parasympathetic input
- **Pupil involvement**: Suggests compressive lesion (aneurysm, uncal herniation)
  - Parasympathetic fibers run superficially
- **Pupil sparing**: Suggests microvascular ischemia (diabetes)
  - Ischemia affects center (somatic motor) first

### CN IV: Trochlear Nerve

**Type**: Motor

**Nucleus**: Midbrain (caudal, dorsal tegmentum)

**Course**:
- Only cranial nerve to exit dorsally
- Only cranial nerve to decussate completely
- Longest intracranial course (vulnerable to trauma)
- Cavernous sinus → superior orbital fissure

**Innervation**: Superior oblique muscle
- Intorsion, depression (when adducted), abduction

**Testing**:
- **Ask patient to look down and in** (reading position)
- Vertical diplopia on downward gaze
- Head tilt to opposite side (compensatory)

**Clinical**:
- **CN IV palsy**:
  - Vertical diplopia (worse on downgaze)
  - Difficulty reading, descending stairs
  - Head tilt away from affected side
- **Causes**: Trauma (most common), microvascular

### CN V: Trigeminal Nerve

**Type**: Mixed (sensory and motor)

**Nuclei**:
- **Motor nucleus**: Mid-pons (muscles of mastication)
- **Main sensory nucleus**: Pons (touch, pressure)
- **Spinal nucleus**: Pons to upper cervical cord (pain, temperature)
- **Mesencephalic nucleus**: Midbrain (proprioception)

**Three Divisions** (sensory):

**1. V1 - Ophthalmic**:
- **Exit**: Superior orbital fissure
- **Innervation**: Forehead, upper eyelid, cornea, nose (tip)
- **Branches**: Frontal, lacrimal, nasociliary
- **Corneal reflex**: Afferent limb (efferent = VII)

**2. V2 - Maxillary**:
- **Exit**: Foramen rotundum
- **Innervation**: Lower eyelid, upper lip, cheek, maxillary teeth
- **Branches**: Infraorbital, zygomatic

**3. V3 - Mandibular** (mixed):
- **Exit**: Foramen ovale
- **Sensory innervation**: Lower lip, chin, mandibular teeth, anterior 2/3 tongue (general sensation)
- **Motor innervation**: Muscles of mastication
  - Masseter, temporalis, medial and lateral pterygoids
  - Also tensor veli palatini, tensor tympani, mylohyoid, anterior belly of digastric

**Testing**:
- **Sensory**: Light touch, pinprick (three divisions)
- **Motor**: Jaw clenching (masseter), jaw opening (lateral pterygoids)
  - Jaw deviates toward weak side on opening
- **Corneal reflex**: Blink response to corneal touch
- **Jaw jerk reflex**: Masseter stretch reflex (tests pons level)

**Clinical**:
- **Trigeminal neuralgia**: Severe facial pain (V2, V3 distribution)
  - Sharp, lancinating, triggered by touch/chewing
  - Treatment: Carbamazepine, microvascular decompression
- **Herpes zoster ophthalmicus**: V1 distribution
  - Vesicular rash, risk of corneal involvement (Hutchinson's sign if nose tip involved)
- **Lesion localization**:
  - Peripheral: One division affected
  - Central (brainstem): "Onion-skin" pattern (spinal nucleus)

### CN VI: Abducens Nerve

**Type**: Motor

**Nucleus**: Pons (floor of fourth ventricle, beneath facial colliculus)

**Course**:
- Longest intradural course (vulnerable to increased ICP)
- Exits pontomedullary junction
- Cavernous sinus (lateral to internal carotid)
- Superior orbital fissure

**Innervation**: Lateral rectus muscle (eye abduction)

**Testing**: Horizontal eye movements (H-pattern)

**Clinical**:
- **CN VI palsy**:
  - Inability to abduct eye
  - Horizontal diplopia (worse on lateral gaze)
  - Esotropia (eye turned in)
- **"False localizing sign"**: Nonspecific sign of increased ICP
  - Stretching of nerve due to brain shift
- **Causes**: Increased ICP, microvascular, trauma, tumor

### CN VII: Facial Nerve

**Type**: Mixed (motor, sensory, parasympathetic)

**Nuclei**:
- **Motor nucleus**: Pons (facial expression muscles)
- **Superior salivatory nucleus**: Parasympathetic (lacrimal, submandibular, sublingual glands)
- **Solitary nucleus**: Taste (anterior 2/3 tongue)

**Course**:
- Motor root and nervus intermedius (sensory/parasympathetic)
- Exit at pontomedullary junction (cerebellopontine angle)
- Internal acoustic meatus with CN VIII
- Facial canal (temporal bone):
  - Geniculate ganglion (sensory cell bodies)
  - Greater petrosal nerve (lacrimal gland)
  - Nerve to stapedius
  - Chorda tympani (taste, submandibular/sublingual glands)
- Exit: Stylomastoid foramen
- Parotid gland (divides into branches)

**Innervation**:
- **Motor**: Muscles of facial expression
  - Frontalis, orbicularis oculi, orbicularis oris, buccinator, platysma, stapedius
  - Posterior belly of digastric, stylohyoid
- **Sensory**: Taste (anterior 2/3 tongue via chorda tympani)
- **Parasympathetic**:
  - Lacrimal gland (greater petrosal nerve)
  - Submandibular and sublingual glands (chorda tympani)

**Testing**:
- **Motor**: Raise eyebrows, close eyes tightly, smile, puff cheeks
- **Sensory**: Taste (sweet, salty, sour, bitter)
- **Hyperacusis**: Stapedius function (loud sounds)

**Clinical**:
- **Upper motor neuron lesion** (cortical):
  - Contralateral lower face weakness ONLY
  - Forehead spared (bilateral cortical innervation)
- **Lower motor neuron lesion** (peripheral):
  - Ipsilateral complete facial weakness (upper and lower face)
- **Bell's palsy**: Idiopathic peripheral CN VII palsy
  - Acute onset, unilateral
  - May have hyperacusis, loss of taste
  - Treatment: Corticosteroids, eye protection
- **Ramsay Hunt syndrome**: Herpes zoster (geniculate ganglion)
  - Facial weakness + vesicles in ear canal + otalgia
- **Cerebellopontine angle tumor**: CN VII and VIII affected
  - Acoustic neuroma (schwannoma)

### CN VIII: Vestibulocochlear Nerve

**Type**: Sensory (special sense - hearing and balance)

**Two Divisions**:
1. **Cochlear division**: Hearing
2. **Vestibular division**: Balance and equilibrium

**Cochlear Division:**
- Hair cells in organ of Corti (cochlea)
- Spiral ganglion (cell bodies)
- Cochlear nerve
- Cochlear nuclei (pons)
- Lateral lemniscus
- Inferior colliculus
- Medial geniculate nucleus (thalamus)
- Auditory cortex (Heschl's gyrus, superior temporal)

**Vestibular Division:**
- Hair cells in semicircular canals, utricle, saccule
- Vestibular ganglion (Scarpa's ganglion)
- Vestibular nuclei (pons/medulla)
- Projects to:
  - Cerebellum (balance, coordination)
  - MLF (eye movements)
  - Spinal cord (vestibulospinal tract)
  - Thalamus (conscious balance)

**Course**: Internal acoustic meatus with CN VII → cerebellopontine angle

**Testing**:
- **Hearing**: Whisper test, finger rub, tuning forks (Weber, Rinne)
  - **Weber test**: Lateralization (to good ear in sensorineural loss)
  - **Rinne test**: Air vs. bone conduction
- **Vestibular**: Nystagmus, Dix-Hallpike test, gait

**Clinical**:
- **Sensorineural hearing loss**:
  - Cochlear damage or nerve damage
  - Presbycusis (age-related), noise-induced, ototoxicity
- **Conductive hearing loss**:
  - Middle ear pathology
  - Otosclerosis, otitis media
- **Acoustic neuroma** (vestibular schwannoma):
  - Unilateral hearing loss (gradual)
  - Tinnitus
  - Vestibular symptoms
  - May compress CN VII
- **Meniere's disease**: Endolymphatic hydrops
  - Episodic vertigo, tinnitus, hearing loss, ear fullness
- **BPPV** (benign paroxysmal positional vertigo):
  - Positional vertigo (rolling in bed)
  - Dix-Hallpike test positive

### CN IX: Glossopharyngeal Nerve

**Type**: Mixed (motor, sensory, parasympathetic)

**Nuclei**:
- **Nucleus ambiguus**: Motor (stylopharyngeus muscle)
- **Inferior salivatory nucleus**: Parasympathetic (parotid gland)
- **Solitary nucleus**: Taste (posterior 1/3 tongue), visceral sensation (carotid body/sinus)
- **Spinal trigeminal nucleus**: General sensation (ear, tonsils)

**Course**:
- Exits medulla (posterolateral sulcus)
- Jugular foramen (with X, XI)
- Superior and inferior (petrosal) ganglia

**Innervation**:
- **Motor**: Stylopharyngeus (pharyngeal elevation)
- **Sensory**:
  - Taste: Posterior 1/3 tongue
  - General sensation: Posterior 1/3 tongue, tonsils, pharynx, middle ear
  - Special sensation: Carotid body (chemoreceptor), carotid sinus (baroreceptor)
- **Parasympathetic**: Parotid gland (via otic ganglion)

**Testing**:
- Gag reflex (afferent limb; efferent = X)
- Taste (posterior tongue)
- "Say 'ah'" (palate elevation)

**Clinical**:
- **Glossopharyngeal neuralgia**: Rare
  - Severe pain in throat/ear
  - Triggered by swallowing
- **Isolated lesion**: Rare (usually with X)

### CN X: Vagus Nerve

**Type**: Mixed (motor, sensory, parasympathetic)

**Nuclei**:
- **Nucleus ambiguus**: Motor (pharynx, larynx muscles)
- **Dorsal motor nucleus**: Parasympathetic (thoracic and abdominal viscera)
- **Solitary nucleus**: Visceral sensation, taste (epiglottis region)
- **Spinal trigeminal nucleus**: General sensation (ear)

**Course**:
- Exits medulla (posterolateral sulcus)
- Jugular foramen
- Descends in carotid sheath (with carotid artery and internal jugular vein)
- Extensive distribution to neck, thorax, abdomen

**Innervation**:
- **Motor**: All pharyngeal muscles (except stylopharyngeus), all laryngeal muscles (except tensor veli palatini)
  - **Superior laryngeal nerve** (external branch): Cricothyroid
  - **Recurrent laryngeal nerve**: All other laryngeal muscles
    - Right: Loops under right subclavian artery
    - Left: Loops under aortic arch (longer, more vulnerable)
- **Sensory**:
  - Pharynx, larynx
  - Taste: Epiglottis region
  - Visceral sensation: Heart, lungs, GI tract (to splenic flexure)
- **Parasympathetic**: "Rest and digest"
  - Heart: Decreased rate
  - Lungs: Bronchoconstriction
  - GI: Increased motility, secretion (to splenic flexure)

**Testing**:
- Palate elevation ("say 'ah'"): Deviates away from lesion
- Gag reflex (efferent limb; afferent = IX)
- Voice quality (hoarseness)
- Cough

**Clinical**:
- **Unilateral lesion**:
  - Ipsilateral palate droops
  - Uvula deviates away from lesion
  - Hoarseness (vocal cord paralysis)
  - Aspiration risk
- **Recurrent laryngeal nerve injury**:
  - Left more common (thyroid surgery, lung cancer, aortic aneurysm)
  - Hoarseness, aspiration
  - Bilateral: Airway emergency
- **Vagal neuropathy**: Autonomic dysfunction
  - Gastroparesis (diabetes)
  - Cardiac dysrhythmias

### CN XI: Accessory Nerve

**Type**: Motor

**Two Components**:
1. **Cranial part**: From nucleus ambiguus (actually part of vagus)
   - Joins vagus, innervates laryngeal muscles
2. **Spinal part**: From spinal cord (C1-C5)
   - Actual CN XI

**Course**:
- Spinal part: Ascends through foramen magnum
- Exits jugular foramen (with IX, X)
- Descends in neck

**Innervation** (spinal part):
- Sternocleidomastoid: Head turning to opposite side
- Trapezius: Shoulder elevation and retraction

**Testing**:
- Shoulder shrug (trapezius): Weakness on lesion side
- Head turning against resistance (SCM): Weakness turning away from lesion

**Clinical**:
- **CN XI palsy**:
  - Shoulder droop (trapezius weakness)
  - Winging of scapula (differs from serratus anterior/long thoracic)
  - Difficulty turning head
- **Causes**: Neck surgery, trauma, jugular foramen lesion

### CN XII: Hypoglossal Nerve

**Type**: Motor

**Nucleus**: Medulla (floor of fourth ventricle)

**Course**:
- Exits medulla (anterolateral, between pyramid and olive)
- Hypoglossal canal
- Descends in neck

**Innervation**: Intrinsic and extrinsic tongue muscles (all except palatoglossus = CN X)
- Genioglossus (protrusion)
- Hyoglossus, styloglossus
- Intrinsic muscles

**Testing**:
- Tongue protrusion: Deviates toward weak side
- Tongue strength: Push against cheek
- Fasciculations (LMN lesion)

**Clinical**:
- **Lower motor neuron lesion**:
  - Ipsilateral tongue weakness
  - Atrophy, fasciculations
  - Deviation toward lesion
  - Causes: Medullary stroke, neck trauma
- **Upper motor neuron lesion**:
  - Contralateral tongue weakness
  - No atrophy or fasciculations
  - Deviation away from cortical lesion
- **Bilateral UMN lesion**: Pseudobulbar palsy
  - Spastic tongue, dysarthria, dysphagia
  - Emotional lability

## Key Points

1. The cerebrum has four lobes with distinct functions: frontal (motor, executive), parietal (sensory, spatial), temporal (auditory, memory), occipital (visual)

2. Basal ganglia regulate movement; dysfunction causes Parkinson's disease, Huntington's disease, hemiballismus

3. The internal capsule contains descending motor and ascending sensory fibers; strokes cause contralateral deficits

4. The thalamus is the sensory relay station; specific nuclei relay specific modalities to cortex

5. The hypothalamus controls homeostasis, endocrine function, and autonomic regulation

6. The brainstem contains cranial nerve nuclei (CN III-XII) and vital cardiovascular/respiratory centers

7. Midbrain contains oculomotor nuclei (III, IV), substantia nigra, and red nucleus; syndromes include Weber, Benedikt, Parinaud

8. Pons contains CN V-VIII nuclei and pontine nuclei; syndromes include lateral pontine, medial pontine, locked-in, INO

9. Medulla contains CN IX-XII nuclei and pyramidal decussation; syndromes include lateral medullary (Wallenberg), medial medullary

10. The cerebellum coordinates movement and balance; lesions cause ipsilateral ataxia, intention tremor, nystagmus

11. Cranial nerves: I-II (special sense), III-IV-VI (eye movement), V (face sensation/mastication), VII (face movement/taste), VIII (hearing/balance), IX-X (pharynx/larynx), XI (SCM/trapezius), XII (tongue)

12. Cranial nerve lesions can localize to brainstem level based on combinations
