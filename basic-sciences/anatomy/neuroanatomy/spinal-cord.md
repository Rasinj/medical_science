# Spinal Cord Anatomy

## Introduction

The spinal cord is a vital component of the central nervous system, serving as the conduit for communication between the brain and peripheral nervous system. It mediates reflexes, processes sensory information, and transmits motor commands. Understanding spinal cord anatomy is essential for localizing neurological lesions and interpreting clinical deficits.

### Learning Objectives

1. Describe the gross anatomy and organization of the spinal cord
2. Identify internal structures including gray and white matter
3. Understand the functions and pathways of major ascending and descending tracts
4. Explain the organization and clinical significance of spinal reflexes
5. Recognize vascular anatomy and patterns of spinal cord injury
6. Correlate anatomical lesions with specific clinical syndromes

## Gross Anatomy

### External Features

**Location and Extent:**
- Extends from foramen magnum to conus medullaris
- **Adult**: Conus medullaris at L1-L2 vertebral level
- **Newborn**: Conus at L3 level (differential growth)
- **Length**: Approximately 45 cm in adults
- **Diameter**: Approximately 1 cm (varies by level)
- Occupies upper 2/3 of vertebral canal

**Surface Landmarks:**
- **Anterior median fissure**: Deep midline groove (ventral)
  - Contains anterior spinal artery
  - Extends nearly to gray commissure
- **Posterior median sulcus**: Shallow midline groove (dorsal)
  - Continuous with posterior median septum
- **Anterolateral sulcus**: Ventral root exit zone
  - Multiple rootlets emerge
- **Posterolateral sulcus**: Dorsal root entry zone
  - Sensory rootlets enter

**Regional Enlargements:**

**1. Cervical Enlargement (C5-T1)**
- Corresponds to brachial plexus
- Innervation of upper limbs
- Largest diameter (~1.3 cm)
- Most gray matter relative to white matter

**2. Lumbosacral Enlargement (L1-S2)**
- Corresponds to lumbosacral plexus
- Innervation of lower limbs
- Large diameter (~1.2 cm)
- Considerable gray matter

**Terminal Structures:**
- **Conus medullaris**: Tapered terminal end of spinal cord (L1-L2)
- **Cauda equina**: Collection of nerve roots below conus medullaris
  - Contains lumbar, sacral, and coccygeal nerve roots
  - Resembles "horse's tail"
- **Filum terminale**: Fibrous extension from conus to coccyx
  - Anchors spinal cord
  - Composed of pia mater and glial cells
  - **Filum terminale internum**: Within dural sac (to S2)
  - **Filum terminale externum**: Below dural sac (to coccyx)

### Spinal Segments and Vertebral Levels

**31 Spinal Segments:**
- 8 Cervical (C1-C8)
  - Note: C1-C7 nerves exit above corresponding vertebrae
  - C8 nerve exits below C7 vertebra
- 12 Thoracic (T1-T12)
- 5 Lumbar (L1-L5)
- 5 Sacral (S1-S5)
- 1 Coccygeal (Co1)

**Vertebral-Cord Relationship:**
- **Cervical**: Nerve root exits approximately at same level
- **Upper thoracic**: Cord segment 1-2 levels above vertebra
- **Lower thoracic**: Cord segment 2-3 levels above vertebra
- **Lumbar/sacral**: Cord segments in T12-L1 vertebral region

**Clinical Significance:**
- Lesion localization requires knowledge of this relationship
- Vertebral level of injury ≠ spinal cord level
- Important for lumbar puncture (performed below L2 to avoid cord)

### Spinal Meninges

**Three Layers (same as brain):**

**1. Dura Mater (outer)**
- Thick, tough fibrous membrane
- **Epidural space**: Between dura and vertebral periosteum
  - Contains fat and internal vertebral venous plexus
  - Site of epidural anesthesia and hematomas
- **Dural sac**: Extends to S2 vertebral level
- **Denticulate ligaments**: Lateral extensions of pia
  - Attach to dura between nerve roots
  - Stabilize cord within dural sac

**2. Arachnoid Mater (middle)**
- Delicate, avascular membrane
- **Subdural space**: Potential space between dura and arachnoid
  - Normally contains only thin film of fluid
  - Site of subdural hematomas
- **Subarachnoid space**: Between arachnoid and pia
  - Contains cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)
  - Extends to S2 (below conus medullaris)
  - Site of lumbar puncture
  - Contains nerve roots (cauda equina below L2)

**3. Pia Mater (inner)**
- Thin, vascular membrane
- Adherent to spinal cord surface
- Forms denticulate ligaments
- Extends to form filum terminale

### Spinal Nerve Roots

**Formation:**
- **Dorsal (posterior) root**: Sensory afferents
  - Multiple rootlets coalesce
  - **Dorsal root ganglion**: Contains sensory neuron cell bodies
    - Pseudounipolar neurons
    - Located in intervertebral foramen
- **Ventral (anterior) root**: Motor efferents
  - Multiple rootlets coalesce
  - Cell bodies in anterior horn

**Spinal Nerve:**
- Formed by union of dorsal and ventral roots
- Exits through intervertebral foramen
- Very short (mixed nerve)
- Divides into:
  - **Dorsal ramus**: Back muscles and skin
  - **Ventral ramus**: Limbs and anterolateral trunk
    - Forms plexuses (cervical, brachial, lumbar, sacral)
  - **Meningeal branch**: Recurrent, innervates meninges
  - **Rami communicantes** (T1-L2, S2-S4): Autonomic connections

## Internal Organization

### Cross-Sectional Anatomy

The spinal cord has a characteristic butterfly or H-shaped gray matter surrounded by white matter.

### Gray Matter

Composed of neuronal cell bodies, dendrites, unmyelinated axons, and glial cells.

**Organization:**

**1. Anterior (Ventral) Horn**
- Contains motor neurons (alpha and gamma motor neurons)
- **Medial motor neurons**: Axial and proximal limb muscles
- **Lateral motor neurons**: Distal limb muscles
  - Present primarily in enlargements (C5-T1, L1-S2)
- **Somatotopic organization**:
  - Medial: Axial muscles
  - Lateral: Limb muscles
  - Flexors posterior to extensors

**2. Posterior (Dorsal) Horn**
- Receives and processes sensory information
- **Substantia gelatinosa (Lamina II)**: Pain modulation
- **Nucleus dorsalis (Clarke's column)**: C8-L2
  - Gives rise to dorsal spinocerebellar tract
- Receives input from dorsal root ganglia
- **Laminar organization** (Rexed laminae I-VI)

**3. Lateral Horn** (T1-L2, S2-S4 only)
- **Intermediolateral cell column (IML)**
- Contains preganglionic autonomic neurons
- **T1-L2**: Sympathetic (thoracolumbar outflow)
- **S2-S4**: Parasympathetic (sacral outflow)

**4. Gray Commissure**
- Connects left and right gray matter
- Surrounds central canal
- Crossed fibers (e.g., spinothalamic tract crossing)

**5. Central Canal**
- Continuous with fourth ventricle
- Contains CSF
- Lined by ependymal cells
- Usually obliterated in adults

**Rexed Laminae:**
Cytoarchitectonic organization (Laminae I-X):
- **I-IV**: Posterior horn (sensory processing)
- **V-VI**: Intermediate zone
- **VII**: Intermediate gray (includes Clarke's column, IML)
- **VIII**: Medial anterior horn (interneurons)
- **IX**: Lateral anterior horn (motor neurons)
- **X**: Gray commissure around central canal

### White Matter

Composed of myelinated axons organized into funiculi (columns).

**Three Funiculi:**

**1. Anterior (Ventral) Funiculus**
- Between anterior median fissure and anterolateral sulcus
- Contains:
  - Anterior corticospinal tract (descending motor)
  - Vestibulospinal tracts
  - Reticulospinal tracts
  - Tectospinal tract
  - Anterior spinothalamic tract

**2. Lateral Funiculus**
- Between anterolateral and posterolateral sulci
- Contains:
  - Lateral corticospinal tract (descending motor)
  - Rubrospinal tract
  - Lateral spinothalamic tract (pain, temperature)
  - Spinocerebellar tracts (posterior and anterior)

**3. Posterior (Dorsal) Funiculus**
- Between posterior median sulcus and posterolateral sulcus
- Divided by posterior intermediate septum:
  - **Fasciculus gracilis** (medial): Lower body (legs, lower trunk)
    - Present at all levels
    - Fibers from sacral, lumbar, lower thoracic levels
  - **Fasciculus cuneatus** (lateral): Upper body (arms, upper trunk)
    - Present only C1-T6
    - Fibers from upper thoracic and cervical levels
- Conveys touch, vibration, proprioception (conscious)

## Ascending (Sensory) Pathways

### Dorsal Column-Medial Lemniscus Pathway

**Function**: Fine touch, vibration, conscious proprioception

**Pathway:**
1. **First-order neurons**:
   - Cell bodies in dorsal root ganglia
   - Peripheral process: From mechanoreceptors (skin, joints, muscles)
   - Central process: Enters spinal cord via dorsal root
   - **Ascends ipsilaterally** in dorsal columns
     - Lower body → Fasciculus gracilis
     - Upper body → Fasciculus cuneatus

2. **Medulla**:
   - Synapse in nucleus gracilis or nucleus cuneatus
   - **Second-order neurons** cross midline (decussate)
   - Form **medial lemniscus** (contralateral)

3. **Thalamus**:
   - Medial lemniscus ascends through brainstem
   - Synapse in **ventral posterior lateral (VPL)** nucleus

4. **Cortex**:
   - **Third-order neurons** project via thalamocortical radiations
   - Terminate in primary somatosensory cortex (S1, postcentral gyrus)

**Clinical Testing:**
- Vibration sense (tuning fork on bony prominences)
- Proprioception (position sense of toes/fingers)
- Fine touch discrimination (two-point discrimination)

**Lesions:**
- **Ipsilateral loss** below lesion (fibers don't cross until medulla)
- Loss of vibration and proprioception
- Sensory ataxia (impaired joint position sense)
- Positive Romberg sign (visual compensation for proprioception loss)

### Spinothalamic Tract

**Function**: Pain, temperature, crude touch

**Pathway:**
1. **First-order neurons**:
   - Cell bodies in dorsal root ganglia
   - Peripheral process: From nociceptors and thermoreceptors
   - Central process: Enters via dorsal root
   - Synapse in **dorsal horn** (substantia gelatinosa, laminae I-V)

2. **Spinal Cord**:
   - **Second-order neurons** cross midline via **anterior white commissure**
     - Cross within 1-2 segments of entry
   - Ascend **contralaterally** in lateral funiculus
   - **Lateral spinothalamic tract**: Pain and temperature
   - **Anterior spinothalamic tract**: Crude touch (ventral funiculus)
   - Somatotopic organization: Sacral fibers lateral, cervical fibers medial

3. **Thalamus**:
   - Synapse in **VPL** nucleus (lateral body)
   - Also **VPM** for face (trigeminal input)

4. **Cortex**:
   - **Third-order neurons** to S1

**Clinical Testing:**
- Pinprick (pain)
- Hot/cold discrimination (temperature)
- Light touch

**Lesions:**
- **Contralateral loss** below lesion (fibers cross soon after entry)
- Loss of pain and temperature sensation
- Dissociated sensory loss (pain/temp lost, vibration/proprioception preserved)

**Unique Feature:**
- Somatotopic organization allows selective sacral sparing in central cord lesions

### Spinocerebellar Tracts

**Function**: Unconscious proprioception to cerebellum (motor coordination)

**Posterior (Dorsal) Spinocerebellar Tract:**
- **Origin**: Clarke's column (nucleus dorsalis, C8-L2)
- **Function**: Lower limb and lower trunk unconscious proprioception
- **Course**:
  - Ascends **ipsilaterally** in lateral funiculus (lateral)
  - Enters cerebellum via **inferior cerebellar peduncle**
- **Termination**: Vermis and intermediate cerebellum

**Anterior (Ventral) Spinocerebellar Tract:**
- **Origin**: Spinal border cells (lumbosacral cord)
- **Function**: Lower limb unconscious proprioception
- **Course**:
  - **Crosses** in spinal cord (contralateral ascent)
  - Ascends in lateral funiculus (medial to posterior tract)
  - Enters cerebellum via **superior cerebellar peduncle**
  - **Crosses again** in cerebellum (returns to ipsilateral side)
- **Termination**: Vermis and intermediate cerebellum

**Cuneocerebellar Tract:**
- **Origin**: Accessory (lateral) cuneate nucleus (medulla)
- **Function**: Upper limb and upper trunk unconscious proprioception
- **Course**: Similar to posterior spinocerebellar
  - **Ipsilateral**
  - Via inferior cerebellar peduncle

**Rostrospinocerebellar Tract:**
- **Function**: Upper limb (similar to anterior spinocerebellar)
- **Course**: Via superior cerebellar peduncle

**Clinical Significance:**
- Usually not tested clinically (unconscious)
- Lesions manifest as cerebellar signs (ataxia)
- Involved in Friedrich's ataxia (posterior spinocerebellar degeneration)

### Other Ascending Pathways

**Spinoreticular Tract:**
- Poorly defined, multisynaptic
- Arousal and pain awareness
- To reticular formation

**Spinotectal Tract:**
- To superior colliculus
- Visual-auditory-somatic integration
- Reflex head turning to stimuli

**Spinoolivary Tract:**
- To inferior olivary nucleus
- Then to cerebellum (motor learning)

## Descending (Motor) Pathways

### Lateral Corticospinal Tract

**Function**: Voluntary movement of limbs (especially fine motor control)

**Pathway:**
1. **Upper Motor Neurons (UMN)**:
   - Cell bodies in **primary motor cortex** (M1, precentral gyrus, area 4)
   - Also premotor and supplementary motor areas
   - Somatotopic organization (motor homunculus)
   - Descend through:
     - Corona radiata
     - **Internal capsule** (posterior limb)
     - **Cerebral peduncle** (midbrain, middle 2/3)
     - **Basilar pons** (dispersed among pontine nuclei)
     - **Pyramid** (medulla)

2. **Decussation**:
   - **Pyramidal decussation** at lower medulla
   - ~90% of fibers cross to **contralateral** side
   - Form **lateral corticospinal tract**

3. **Spinal Cord**:
   - Descends in **lateral funiculus** (dorsal part)
   - Somatotopic organization:
     - Cervical fibers medial
     - Sacral fibers lateral
   - Synapse on:
     - Anterior horn motor neurons (directly or via interneurons)
     - Primarily **lateral motor neurons** (limb muscles)

4. **Lower Motor Neurons (LMN)**:
   - **Alpha motor neurons** in anterior horn
   - Axons exit via ventral root
   - Innervate skeletal muscle fibers

**Clinical Significance:**

**UMN Lesions** (above decussation):
- **Contralateral** weakness
- Spasticity (increased tone)
- Hyperreflexia
- Positive Babinski sign
- No muscle atrophy (initially)
- No fasciculations

**LMN Lesions** (anterior horn or below):
- **Ipsilateral** weakness (same side as lesion)
- Flaccidity (decreased tone)
- Hyporeflexia/areflexia
- Negative Babinski sign
- Muscle atrophy
- Fasciculations

### Anterior Corticospinal Tract

**Function**: Voluntary movement of axial muscles (trunk, proximal limbs)

**Pathway:**
1. **UMN**: Same origin as lateral tract (motor cortex)
2. **Decussation**: Does NOT cross at pyramidal decussation
   - Descends **ipsilaterally** in anterior funiculus
   - ~10% of corticospinal fibers
3. **Spinal Cord**:
   - Many fibers cross at **segmental level** via anterior white commissure
   - Synapse on medial motor neurons (axial muscles)
   - **Bilateral** innervation pattern

**Clinical Significance:**
- Explains relative sparing of trunk/axial muscles in unilateral lesions
- Bilateral cortical lesions needed for significant axial weakness

### Corticobulbar Tract

**Function**: Voluntary control of cranial nerve motor nuclei

**Pathway:**
1. **UMN**: Motor cortex (lower precentral gyrus)
2. **Descends**: Internal capsule (genu)
3. **Synapses**: Cranial nerve motor nuclei in brainstem
   - CN III, IV, VI (eye movements)
   - CN V (mastication)
   - CN VII (facial expression)
   - CN IX, X, XI (pharynx, larynx)
   - CN XII (tongue)

**Innervation Patterns:**
- **Bilateral** innervation (most cranial nerves):
  - Upper face (CN VII)
  - Most of CN V, IX, X, XI, XII
- **Primarily contralateral** innervation:
  - Lower face (CN VII)
  - Hypoglossal nucleus (CN XII)

**Clinical Significance:**
- **Unilateral UMN lesion**: Contralateral lower face weakness, upper face spared
- **Bilateral UMN lesions**: Pseudobulbar palsy (dysarthria, dysphagia, emotional lability)

### Rubrospinal Tract

**Function**: Facilitates flexor tone (limited in humans)

**Pathway:**
1. **Origin**: Red nucleus (midbrain)
2. **Decussation**: Immediately crosses (ventral tegmental decussation)
3. **Descends**: **Contralaterally** in lateral funiculus (anterior to lateral corticospinal)
4. **Termination**: Lateral anterior horn (flexor motor neurons)

**Clinical Significance:**
- Limited functional importance in humans (corticospinal tract dominant)
- May partially compensate for corticospinal lesions

### Vestibulospinal Tracts

**Function**: Postural control and balance

**Lateral Vestibulospinal Tract:**
- **Origin**: Lateral vestibular nucleus (pons/medulla)
- **Descends**: **Ipsilaterally** in anterior funiculus (lateral part)
- **Function**: Facilitates extensor tone (antigravity muscles)
- **Clinical**: Helps maintain upright posture

**Medial Vestibulospinal Tract:**
- **Origin**: Medial vestibular nucleus
- **Descends**: **Bilaterally** in anterior funiculus (cervical and upper thoracic only)
- **Function**: Head and neck stabilization
- **Via medial longitudinal fasciculus (MLF)**

### Reticulospinal Tracts

**Function**: Posture, locomotion, autonomic modulation

**Pontine (Medial) Reticulospinal Tract:**
- **Origin**: Pontine reticular formation
- **Descends**: **Ipsilaterally and bilaterally** in anterior funiculus
- **Function**: Facilitates extensor tone

**Medullary (Lateral) Reticulospinal Tract:**
- **Origin**: Medullary reticular formation
- **Descends**: **Ipsilaterally** in lateral funiculus
- **Function**: Facilitates flexor tone, inhibits extensor tone

**Clinical Significance:**
- Involved in decerebrate and decorticate posturing
- Modulate spinal reflexes
- Important for locomotion and autonomic functions

### Tectospinal Tract

**Function**: Reflex head movements to visual/auditory stimuli

**Pathway:**
- **Origin**: Superior colliculus (midbrain)
- **Decussation**: Crosses immediately (dorsal tegmental decussation)
- **Descends**: **Contralaterally** in anterior funiculus (cervical cord only)
- **Termination**: Cervical anterior horn (neck muscles)

**Clinical Significance:**
- Limited in humans
- Reflex turning of head toward stimuli

## Spinal Reflexes

Reflexes are rapid, involuntary responses to stimuli mediated by spinal cord circuits.

### Reflex Arc Components

1. **Receptor**: Detects stimulus
2. **Afferent (sensory) neuron**: Conducts to CNS
3. **Integration center**: Spinal cord (synapse)
4. **Efferent (motor) neuron**: Conducts from CNS
5. **Effector**: Muscle or gland responds

### Stretch Reflex (Myotatic Reflex)

**Function**: Maintains muscle tone, resists passive stretch

**Mechanism:**
- **Monosynaptic** (simplest reflex)
- **Receptor**: Muscle spindle (intrafusal fibers)
- **Afferent**: Ia fibers (large, myelinated) from muscle spindle
- **Synapse**: Directly on alpha motor neuron in anterior horn
- **Efferent**: Alpha motor neuron to same muscle (homonymous)
- **Effector**: Muscle contraction (resists stretch)
- **Additional connections**:
  - Reciprocal inhibition of antagonist muscle
  - Golgi tendon organ (Ib) provides negative feedback

**Clinical Testing:**
- **Biceps reflex** (C5-C6)
- **Triceps reflex** (C7-C8)
- **Patellar reflex** (knee jerk, L3-L4)
- **Achilles reflex** (ankle jerk, S1-S2)

**Reflex Grading:**
- 0: Absent
- 1+: Diminished
- 2+: Normal
- 3+: Increased
- 4+: Hyperactive with clonus

**Clinical Significance:**
- **Hyperreflexia**: UMN lesion (above reflex level)
- **Hyporeflexia/areflexia**: LMN lesion (at reflex level)

### Golgi Tendon Organ Reflex (Inverse Myotatic)

**Function**: Prevents excessive muscle tension (protective)

**Mechanism:**
- **Receptor**: Golgi tendon organ (in tendon)
- **Afferent**: Ib fibers (monitors tension)
- **Synapse**: Inhibitory interneuron in spinal cord
- **Efferent**: Inhibits alpha motor neuron to same muscle
- **Effector**: Muscle relaxation (prevents damage)

**Clinical**: Tested indirectly, not routinely assessed

### Flexor (Withdrawal) Reflex

**Function**: Protective withdrawal from painful stimulus

**Mechanism:**
- **Polysynaptic**
- **Receptor**: Nociceptors (pain receptors)
- **Afferent**: A-delta and C fibers
- **Synapse**: Multiple interneurons in dorsal horn
- **Efferent**:
  - **Ipsilateral**: Flexors activated (withdrawal)
  - **Contralateral**: Extensors activated (crossed extensor reflex for support)
- **Effector**: Limb withdrawal

**Clinical Significance:**
- Primitive reflex, present even in spinal cord transection
- Exaggerated in UMN lesions

### Crossed Extensor Reflex

**Function**: Maintain balance during withdrawal reflex

**Mechanism:**
- Occurs simultaneously with flexor reflex
- **Contralateral** to stimulus
- Extensors contract (support body weight)
- Flexors inhibit

### Superficial Reflexes

**Abdominal Reflex:**
- **Stimulus**: Stroking skin of abdomen
- **Response**: Contraction of abdominal muscles, umbilicus moves toward stimulus
- **Levels**:
  - Upper: T7-T9
  - Middle: T9-T11
  - Lower: T11-T12
- **Clinical**: Absent in UMN lesions, obesity, aging

**Cremasteric Reflex:**
- **Stimulus**: Stroking inner thigh
- **Response**: Elevation of ipsilateral testis
- **Level**: L1-L2
- **Clinical**: Absent in UMN lesions, testicular torsion

**Plantar Reflex (Babinski Sign):**
- **Stimulus**: Stroking lateral sole of foot (heel to toe)
- **Normal response** (negative Babinski):
  - Plantar flexion of toes
  - Mediated by S1-S2
- **Abnormal response** (positive Babinski):
  - **Extension (dorsiflexion) of great toe**
  - **Fanning of other toes**
  - Indicates **UMN lesion** (corticospinal tract)
  - Normal in infants <2 years (immature corticospinal tract)

**Clinical Significance:**
- Positive Babinski sign is pathognomonic for UMN lesion
- Most important pathological reflex

### Autonomic Reflexes

**Micturition Reflex:**
- **Level**: S2-S4
- Bladder stretch → parasympathetic activation → detrusor contraction
- Pontine micturition center provides descending control

**Defecation Reflex:**
- **Level**: S2-S4
- Rectal distension → parasympathetic activation → colonic contraction
- Voluntary control via external anal sphincter

**Sexual Reflexes:**
- **Erection**: Parasympathetic (S2-S4) - "Point"
- **Ejaculation**: Sympathetic (T11-L2) - "Shoot"

## Vascular Anatomy

### Arterial Supply

**Longitudinal Arteries:**

**1. Anterior Spinal Artery**
- Formed by union of branches from vertebral arteries
- Descends in anterior median fissure
- Supplies **anterior 2/3 of spinal cord**:
  - Anterior horns (motor neurons)
  - Lateral corticospinal tracts
  - Spinothalamic tracts
  - Anterior and lateral funiculi

**2. Posterior Spinal Arteries (paired)**
- Arise from vertebral or PICA
- Descend on posterolateral surface
- Supply **posterior 1/3 of spinal cord**:
  - Posterior horns
  - Dorsal columns
  - Posterior funiculi

**Segmental Reinforcement:**

**Radicular Arteries:**
- Branch from segmental arteries (vertebral, intercostal, lumbar)
- Enter via intervertebral foramina
- Reinforce longitudinal arteries
- Anterior radicular arteries:
  - **Artery of Adamkiewicz** (arteria radicularis magna)
    - Largest anterior radicular artery
    - Typically arises at **T9-L2 level** (left side, usually)
    - Critical blood supply to lumbar enlargement
    - **Vulnerable during thoracoabdominal aortic surgery**

**Vascular Territories:**
- **Central arteries** (from anterior spinal): Anterior horns, anterior/lateral white matter
- **Circumferential arteries** (from anterior spinal): Superficial anterior and lateral funiculi
- **Posterior arteries**: Dorsal columns, posterior horns

### Venous Drainage

**Spinal Veins:**
- **Anterior spinal vein**: Midline
- **Posterior spinal veins**: Paired
- Drain via radicular veins
- Connect to internal and external vertebral venous plexuses

**Vertebral Venous Plexus (Batson's plexus):**
- Valveless venous network
- Communicates with:
  - Pelvic veins
  - Thoracic/abdominal veins
  - Cranial venous sinuses
- **Clinical**: Route for metastatic cancer spread (prostate, breast) to spine

## Spinal Cord Syndromes

Understanding specific syndromes helps localize lesions.

### Complete Transection

**Etiology**: Trauma, epidural abscess/hematoma

**Acute Phase (Spinal Shock):**
- **Complete loss** of motor and sensory function below lesion
- **Flaccid paralysis** (initially)
- **Areflexia** (initially)
- Loss of bowel/bladder control
- Autonomic dysfunction
- Duration: Days to weeks

**Chronic Phase:**
- **Spastic paralysis** (UMN signs return)
- **Hyperreflexia**
- Positive Babinski sign
- Autonomic dysreflexia (lesions above T6)
- Permanent loss of voluntary motor and sensory function below lesion

**Sensory Level:**
- Complete loss of all sensation below lesion bilaterally

### Brown-Séquard Syndrome (Hemisection)

**Etiology**: Penetrating trauma, demyelination, tumor

**Ipsilateral (Same Side as Lesion):**
- **Motor**: Weakness/paralysis (lateral corticospinal tract)
  - UMN signs below lesion
  - LMN signs at lesion level
- **Dorsal column**: Loss of vibration, proprioception, fine touch
  - Below lesion (ipsilateral fibers)

**Contralateral (Opposite Side):**
- **Spinothalamic**: Loss of pain and temperature
  - 1-2 levels below lesion (fibers cross after entry)

**Bilateral:**
- Band of pain/temperature loss at lesion level (crossing fibers damaged)

**Prognosis**: Best among incomplete syndromes (often good recovery)

### Anterior Cord Syndrome

**Etiology**: Anterior spinal artery occlusion, hyperflexion injury

**Affected:**
- Anterior 2/3 of spinal cord
- Bilateral damage

**Deficits (Bilateral Below Lesion):**
- **Motor**: Weakness/paralysis (corticospinal tracts)
- **Spinothalamic**: Loss of pain and temperature

**Preserved:**
- **Dorsal columns**: Vibration, proprioception, fine touch (posterior 1/3 spared)

**Clinical Recognition:**
- Motor and pain/temperature loss
- Proprioception and vibration intact

**Prognosis**: Poor for motor recovery

### Central Cord Syndrome

**Etiology**: Hyperextension injury (elderly with cervical spondylosis), syringomyelia

**Pathology**: Central gray matter and crossing white matter fibers

**Deficits:**
- **Motor**: Greater weakness in **upper extremities** than lower
  - Somatotopic organization: Cervical (arms) fibers medial, lumbar/sacral (legs) lateral
  - Central damage affects medial (arm) fibers preferentially
- **Sensory**: Cape-like distribution (bilateral loss)
  - Pain and temperature loss (crossing spinothalamic fibers)
  - May have **sacral sparing** (lateral fibers preserved)
- Bladder dysfunction common

**Clinical Recognition:**
- Arms weaker than legs
- Sensory dissociation (pain/temp loss, touch/vibration relatively preserved)

**Prognosis**: Variable; often some recovery, legs > arms

### Posterior Cord Syndrome

**Etiology**: Rare; vitamin B12 deficiency, tabes dorsalis (syphilis), MS

**Affected**: Dorsal columns bilaterally

**Deficits (Bilateral Below Lesion):**
- Loss of vibration, proprioception, fine touch
- **Sensory ataxia** (impaired proprioception)
- Positive Romberg sign (worse with eyes closed)

**Preserved:**
- Motor function
- Pain and temperature sensation

### Conus Medullaris Syndrome

**Level**: L1-L2 (terminal spinal cord)

**Deficits:**
- **Saddle anesthesia** (S3-S5)
- **Bladder/bowel dysfunction** (early and prominent)
  - Areflexic bladder
  - Fecal incontinence
- **Sexual dysfunction**
- Lower limb weakness (variable)
- **Symmetric** deficits
- **Reflexes**: Absent ankle jerks, may have knee jerks

### Cauda Equina Syndrome

**Location**: Nerve roots below conus medullaris (L2 and below)

**Deficits:**
- **Asymmetric** lower limb weakness (radicular pattern)
- **Saddle anesthesia**
- **Bladder/bowel dysfunction** (delayed, less prominent initially)
- **Severe radicular pain** (nerve roots)
- **Reflexes**: Absent ankle jerks

**Etiology**: Disc herniation, tumor, abscess, trauma

**Emergency**: Requires urgent surgical decompression

**Differentiation from Conus:**
- Cauda equina: Asymmetric, severe pain, bowel/bladder dysfunction later
- Conus medullaris: Symmetric, less pain, early bowel/bladder dysfunction

### Syringomyelia

**Pathology**: Fluid-filled cavity (syrinx) in central canal region

**Location**: Cervical cord most common

**Deficits:**
- **"Cape-like" sensory loss** (bilateral arms/shoulders)
- **Dissociated sensory loss**: Pain and temperature lost, touch preserved
  - Crossing spinothalamic fibers damaged
- **Motor**: Weakness, atrophy in hands (anterior horn damage)
  - LMN signs at level
  - UMN signs below level
- **Charcot joints** (painless joint destruction due to sensory loss)
- **Horner syndrome** (if extends to sympathetic fibers)

**Expansion Patterns:**
- Downward: Thoracic sensory level, lower limb weakness
- Upward: Brainstem (syringobulbia) - cranial nerve dysfunction

**Etiology**: Chiari malformation, post-traumatic, idiopathic

### Tabes Dorsalis

**Etiology**: Tertiary syphilis (demyelination of dorsal roots/columns)

**Deficits:**
- Loss of vibration and proprioception
- **Lancinating pains** ("lightning pains")
- **Sensory ataxia** (wide-based gait)
- Positive Romberg sign
- **Argyll Robertson pupils** (accommodate but don't react to light)
- **Charcot joints**
- Absent reflexes (especially ankle jerks)

**Treatment**: Penicillin (prevents progression, doesn't reverse damage)

## Key Points

1. The spinal cord extends from foramen magnum to conus medullaris (L1-L2 in adults)

2. Gray matter (butterfly-shaped) contains neuronal cell bodies; white matter contains myelinated tracts

3. Anterior horn contains motor neurons; posterior horn processes sensory information; lateral horn (T1-L2, S2-S4) contains autonomic neurons

4. Dorsal column-medial lemniscus pathway: Fine touch, vibration, proprioception; ascends ipsilaterally, crosses in medulla

5. Spinothalamic tract: Pain, temperature, crude touch; crosses within 1-2 segments, ascends contralaterally

6. Lateral corticospinal tract: Voluntary motor; crosses at pyramidal decussation (90%), descends contralaterally

7. Anterior spinal artery supplies anterior 2/3 of cord (motor, pain/temperature); posterior arteries supply posterior 1/3 (dorsal columns)

8. Spinal reflexes: Stretch reflex (monosynaptic), withdrawal reflex (polysynaptic), Babinski sign (UMN lesion indicator)

9. Complete transection: Bilateral motor and sensory loss below lesion; spinal shock initially

10. Brown-Séquard: Ipsilateral motor and dorsal column loss, contralateral pain/temperature loss

11. Anterior cord: Bilateral motor and pain/temperature loss, dorsal columns spared

12. Central cord: Arms weaker than legs, dissociated sensory loss, sacral sparing

13. Syringomyelia: Cape-like dissociated sensory loss, hand weakness

14. Conus medullaris vs. cauda equina: Symmetric vs. asymmetric, early vs. late bowel/bladder dysfunction
