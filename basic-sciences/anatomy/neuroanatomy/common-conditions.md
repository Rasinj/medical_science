# Common Neuroanatomical Conditions

## Introduction

This section correlates neuroanatomical knowledge with clinical conditions, focusing on how lesions at specific locations produce characteristic deficits. Understanding these anatomical-clinical correlations is essential for diagnosis, localization, and management of neurological disorders.

### Learning Objectives

1. Recognize characteristic clinical presentations of cerebrovascular accidents
2. Localize lesions based on patterns of neurological deficits
3. Understand the anatomical basis of common neurodegenerative diseases
4. Identify cranial nerve lesion patterns and their localizing value
5. Correlate traumatic injuries with anatomical damage patterns
6. Apply neuroanatomical knowledge to interpret clinical vignettes

## Cerebrovascular Disorders (Stroke)

Stroke is the most common neurological emergency, classified as ischemic (87%) or hemorrhagic (13%).

### Anterior Circulation Strokes

#### Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) Stroke

**Vascular Territory:**
- Lateral convexity of hemisphere
- Most commonly affected artery in stroke
- Supplies:
  - Primary motor cortex (arm, face areas)
  - Primary sensory cortex (arm, face areas)
  - Broca's area (dominant hemisphere)
  - Wernicke's area (dominant hemisphere)

**Main Trunk Occlusion (Large MCA Stroke):**
- **Contralateral hemiplegia** and **hemisensory loss**
  - Arm and face > leg (leg is ACA territory)
- **Contralateral homonymous hemianopia**
  - Optic radiations affected
- **Aphasia** (dominant hemisphere, usually left):
  - **Global aphasia**: Broca's + Wernicke's areas affected
  - Nonfluent speech, poor comprehension
- **Neglect syndrome** (nondominant hemisphere, usually right):
  - Left-sided neglect
  - Anosognosia (unawareness of deficits)
  - Constructional apraxia
- **Gaze deviation toward lesion**
  - Frontal eye fields damaged
- **Contralateral lower facial droop** (UMN pattern)

**Superior Division MCA:**
- **Motor/sensory deficits** (arm and face)
- **Broca's aphasia** (if dominant hemisphere):
  - Nonfluent, effortful speech
  - Comprehension relatively preserved
  - Aware of deficits (frustrated)

**Inferior Division MCA:**
- **Wernicke's aphasia** (if dominant hemisphere):
  - Fluent but nonsensical speech (word salad)
  - Poor comprehension
  - Unaware of deficits
- **Homonymous hemianopia**
- **Neglect** (if nondominant hemisphere)

**Clinical Pearl:**
- MCA stroke is most common
- "Arm > leg" weakness suggests MCA
- Think MCA if aphasia + hemiparesis

#### Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA) Stroke

**Vascular Territory:**
- Medial aspect of hemisphere
- Supplies:
  - Primary motor cortex (leg area)
  - Primary sensory cortex (leg area)
  - Supplementary motor area

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **Contralateral leg weakness and sensory loss**
  - Leg >> arm (opposite of MCA)
- **Urinary incontinence** (paracentral lobule)
- **Abulia** (lack of motivation)
- **Primitive reflexes** (grasp, suck)
- **Transcortical motor aphasia** (if dominant)
  - Nonfluent, good repetition

**Bilateral ACA Occlusion:**
- Bilateral leg weakness
- Akinetic mutism
- Severe abulia

**Clinical Pearl:**
- "Leg > arm" weakness suggests ACA
- Rare compared to MCA stroke

#### Internal Carotid Artery Occlusion

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Combination of MCA and ACA territory deficits
- May be less severe if Circle of Willis intact
- **Ipsilateral monocular vision loss** (ophthalmic artery)
  - "Amaurosis fugax" (transient)
- Severe strokes with large areas of infarction

#### Anterior Choroidal Artery

**Territory:**
- Internal capsule (posterior limb)
- Optic tract
- Cerebral peduncle

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **Contralateral hemiplegia**
- **Contralateral hemisensory loss**
- **Contralateral homonymous hemianopia**
- Classic triad

### Posterior Circulation Strokes

#### Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA) Stroke

**Vascular Territory:**
- Occipital lobe
- Inferior temporal lobe
- Thalamus (some branches)
- Midbrain (some branches)

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **Contralateral homonymous hemianopia** (most common)
  - With **macular sparing** (collateral from MCA)
- **Visual agnosia** (can't recognize objects)
- **Prosopagnosia** (can't recognize faces, bilateral)
- **Alexia without agraphia** (dominant PCA + splenium of corpus callosum)
  - Can write but can't read
- **Memory impairment** (medial temporal lobe)

**Bilateral PCA:**
- **Cortical blindness**
- **Anton syndrome**: Denial of blindness

**Clinical Pearl:**
- Isolated homonymous hemianopia suggests PCA
- Pupillary light reflex preserved (cortical blindness)

#### Vertebrobasilar Insufficiency

**Territory:**
- Brainstem
- Cerebellum
- Occipital lobes

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **"5 Ds"**: Dizziness, Diplopia, Dysarthria, Dysphagia, Drop attacks
- Vertigo
- Nystagmus
- Ataxia
- Crossed signs (ipsilateral cranial nerve, contralateral body)

**Basilar Artery Occlusion:**
- **Locked-in syndrome** (basis pontis)
  - Quadriplegia
  - Mutism
  - Preserved consciousness
  - Vertical eye movements and blinking preserved
- High mortality

#### Posterior Inferior Cerebellar Artery (PICA) Stroke

**Lateral Medullary Syndrome (Wallenberg):**

**Ipsilateral Deficits:**
- **Facial pain/temperature loss** (spinal trigeminal nucleus/tract)
- **Ataxia** (inferior cerebellar peduncle, cerebellum)
- **Horner syndrome** (descending sympathetic fibers)
  - Ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis
- **Dysphagia, dysarthria, hoarseness** (nucleus ambiguus)
  - Ipsilateral palate droop
  - Decreased gag reflex
- **Nystagmus, vertigo** (vestibular nuclei)

**Contralateral Deficits:**
- **Body pain/temperature loss** (spinothalamic tract)
  - Spares face

**Preserved:**
- Motor strength (pyramids ventral)
- Facial sensation to touch (medial lemniscus ventral)

**Clinical Pearl:**
- Most common brainstem stroke syndrome
- Crossed sensory findings (face ipsilateral, body contralateral)

#### Anterior Inferior Cerebellar Artery (AICA) Stroke

**Lateral Pontine Syndrome:**

**Ipsilateral Deficits:**
- **Facial paralysis** (CN VII nucleus)
- **Hearing loss, vertigo** (CN VIII)
- **Facial sensory loss** (CN V)
- **Horner syndrome**
- **Ataxia** (middle cerebellar peduncle)

**Contralateral Deficits:**
- **Body pain/temperature loss** (spinothalamic tract)

**Clinical Pearl:**
- CN VII and VIII involvement distinguishes from PICA
- "AICA = VII, VIII"

#### Superior Cerebellar Artery (SCA) Stroke

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **Ipsilateral cerebellar ataxia**
- **Contralateral pain/temperature loss** (body)
- May involve midbrain (ipsilateral CN III palsy)
- **Horner syndrome** possible

**Clinical Pearl:**
- Pure cerebellar signs more common than PICA/AICA

### Lacunar Strokes

Small vessel disease affecting penetrating arterioles.

**Risk Factors:**
- Hypertension
- Diabetes mellitus

**Locations:**
- Internal capsule (most common)
- Basal ganglia
- Pons
- Thalamus

**Five Classic Lacunar Syndromes:**

**1. Pure Motor Hemiparesis**
- **Location**: Posterior limb of internal capsule or basis pontis
- **Presentation**: Contralateral face, arm, leg weakness
- **No sensory deficits, no cortical signs**
- Most common lacunar syndrome

**2. Pure Sensory Stroke**
- **Location**: Thalamus (VPL nucleus)
- **Presentation**: Contralateral hemisensory loss (all modalities)
- **No motor deficits**

**3. Ataxic Hemiparesis**
- **Location**: Pons or internal capsule
- **Presentation**: Contralateral weakness + ipsilateral ataxia
- Combination of corticospinal and corticopontocerebellar fiber involvement

**4. Dysarthria-Clumsy Hand Syndrome**
- **Location**: Pons (basis)
- **Presentation**: Facial weakness, dysarthria, hand clumsiness
- Mild symptoms

**5. Mixed Sensorimotor Stroke**
- **Location**: Thalamus and internal capsule
- **Presentation**: Contralateral motor and sensory deficits

**Clinical Pearl:**
- No cortical signs (no aphasia, neglect, hemianopia)
- Small infarcts on imaging
- Better prognosis than large vessel strokes

### Hemorrhagic Stroke

#### Intracerebral Hemorrhage (ICH)

**Risk Factors:**
- Hypertension (most common)
- Amyloid angiopathy (elderly)
- Anticoagulation
- Vascular malformations

**Common Locations:**

**1. Basal Ganglia/Internal Capsule (Most Common)**
- **Putamen** (35-50% of ICH)
- **Presentation**: Contralateral hemiparesis, hemisensory loss, gaze deviation
- May extend into ventricles

**2. Thalamic**
- **Presentation**: Contralateral sensory loss, aphasia (dominant), neglect (nondominant)
- Small pupils (damage to descending sympathetic)
- Downward/medial gaze deviation

**3. Lobar**
- **Presentation**: Depends on lobe
- Think amyloid angiopathy in elderly
- Seizures more common than deep hemorrhages

**4. Pontine**
- **Presentation**: Quadriplegia, coma, pinpoint pupils
- High mortality
- "Locked-in" if ventral pons

**5. Cerebellar**
- **Presentation**: Ataxia, nausea/vomiting, headache, vertigo
- May cause obstructive hydrocephalus
- **Surgical emergency** if large or deteriorating

**General ICH Features:**
- **Sudden onset** (unlike ischemic)
- **Headache** (more common than ischemic)
- **Nausea/vomiting** (increased ICP)
- **Decreased consciousness** (mass effect)

#### Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH)

**Etiology:**
- **Ruptured aneurysm** (85%)
- Trauma
- AVM

**Common Aneurysm Locations:**
- **Anterior communicating artery** (30%)
  - May cause visual field defects, hypothalamic dysfunction
- **Posterior communicating artery** (25%)
  - CN III palsy (dilated pupil, ptosis, "down and out" eye)
- **Middle cerebral artery bifurcation** (20%)
- **Basilar tip**

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **"Worst headache of my life"** (sudden, severe, thunderclap)
- **Nuchal rigidity** (meningeal irritation)
- **Photophobia**
- **Nausea/vomiting**
- **Loss of consciousness** (may be brief)
- **Focal neurological deficits** (depend on location)

**Diagnosis:**
- **Non-contrast CT**: Hyperdense blood in subarachnoid space
- **Lumbar puncture**: Xanthochromia (if CT negative, high suspicion)
- **CT/CTA**: Identify aneurysm

**Complications:**
- **Rebleeding** (highest risk in first 24 hours)
- **Vasospasm** (days 4-14, peak day 7)
  - Delayed cerebral ischemia
  - "Triple H therapy": Hypertension, Hypervolemia, Hemodilution
- **Hydrocephalus** (acute or delayed)
- **Hyponatremia** (SIADH or cerebral salt wasting)

**Grading:**
- **Hunt-Hess Scale**: Grades I-V (clinical severity)
- **Fisher Scale**: Grades 1-4 (blood on CT, predicts vasospasm)

#### Subdural Hematoma

**Mechanism**: Tearing of **bridging veins** (between cortex and dural sinuses)

**Risk Factors:**
- Elderly (brain atrophy → stretched bridging veins)
- Alcoholism
- Anticoagulation
- Trauma (may be minor)

**Types:**

**Acute Subdural Hematoma:**
- Within 24-48 hours of trauma
- **Hyperdense (bright)** on CT
- Symptoms develop rapidly
- Associated with underlying brain injury

**Chronic Subdural Hematoma:**
- Weeks after trauma (often forgotten)
- **Hypodense (dark)** on CT
- Gradual onset of symptoms:
  - Headache
  - Confusion
  - Gait instability
  - Fluctuating consciousness
- **Crescent-shaped** (follows brain contour, crosses suture lines)

**Treatment:**
- Large/symptomatic: Surgical drainage
- Small/asymptomatic: Observation

#### Epidural Hematoma

**Mechanism**: Rupture of **middle meningeal artery** (typically)

**Risk Factors:**
- Temporal bone fracture

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **"Lucid interval"** (classic):
  - Brief loss of consciousness
  - Temporary improvement
  - Rapid deterioration (expanding hematoma)
- Ipsilateral pupil dilation (uncal herniation, CN III compression)
- Contralateral hemiparesis

**Imaging:**
- **Biconvex (lens-shaped)** on CT
- **Hyperdense**
- Does NOT cross suture lines (dura adherent to skull)

**Treatment:**
- **Surgical emergency** (craniotomy, hematoma evacuation)
- Excellent prognosis if treated promptly

## Neurodegenerative Diseases

### Alzheimer's Disease

**Pathology:**
- **Neurofibrillary tangles** (tau protein)
- **Amyloid plaques** (beta-amyloid)
- Neuronal loss

**Anatomical Distribution:**
- **Hippocampus** and **medial temporal lobe** (early)
  - Memory impairment
- **Parietal and temporal cortex**
  - Language, visuospatial deficits
- **Frontal cortex** (later)
  - Executive dysfunction

**Clinical Progression:**
- **Early**: Anterograde amnesia (can't form new memories)
- **Middle**: Aphasia, apraxia, agnosia
- **Late**: Severe cognitive impairment, behavioral changes

**Imaging:**
- **MRI**: Hippocampal atrophy, cortical atrophy
- **PET**: Hypometabolism in temporal and parietal lobes

**Neurotransmitter:**
- **Decreased acetylcholine** (nucleus basalis of Meynert degeneration)
- Treatment: Cholinesterase inhibitors

### Parkinson's Disease

**Pathology:**
- **Substantia nigra pars compacta** degeneration (dopaminergic neurons)
- **Lewy bodies** (alpha-synuclein aggregates)

**Anatomical Basis:**
- Loss of dopamine → basal ganglia dysfunction
- **Direct pathway** underactive (can't facilitate movement)
- **Indirect pathway** overactive (excessive inhibition)

**Clinical Features (TRAP):**
- **Tremor**: Resting, "pill-rolling"
- **Rigidity**: Lead-pipe or cogwheel
- **Akinesia/Bradykinesia**: Slow movements, masked facies
- **Postural instability**: Falls

**Additional Features:**
- Shuffling gait
- Micrographia (small handwriting)
- Hypophonia (soft voice)
- Dementia (late)

**Treatment:**
- **Levodopa** (dopamine precursor)
- **Dopamine agonists**
- **MAO-B inhibitors**
- **Deep brain stimulation** (subthalamic nucleus or GPi)

### Huntington's Disease

**Pathology:**
- **Striatum degeneration** (caudate and putamen)
  - GABAergic medium spiny neurons lost
- **CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion** (huntingtin gene)
- Autosomal dominant

**Anatomical Basis:**
- Caudate atrophy → enlarged lateral ventricles ("boxcar ventricles")
- Loss of indirect pathway inhibition → excessive movement

**Clinical Features:**
- **Chorea**: Involuntary, jerky, dance-like movements
- **Cognitive decline**: Frontal-subcortical dementia
- **Psychiatric symptoms**: Depression, psychosis, personality changes

**Imaging:**
- Caudate atrophy
- Enlarged frontal horns of lateral ventricles

**Prognosis:**
- Progressive, fatal
- Onset typically 30s-40s
- Death 10-20 years after symptom onset

### Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)

**Pathology:**
- **Upper motor neurons** (motor cortex, corticospinal tract)
- **Lower motor neurons** (anterior horn cells, brainstem motor nuclei)
- Progressive degeneration

**Clinical Features:**

**UMN Signs:**
- Spasticity
- Hyperreflexia
- Positive Babinski sign

**LMN Signs:**
- Weakness
- Muscle atrophy
- Fasciculations
- Hyporeflexia (may be masked by UMN involvement)

**Distribution:**
- **Limbs**: Asymmetric weakness progressing to symmetric
- **Bulbar**: Dysarthria, dysphagia (cranial nerves IX, X, XII)

**Preserved:**
- **Sensation** (purely motor disease)
- **Extraocular movements** (CN III, IV, VI)
- **Sphincters** (initially)
- **Cognition** (usually, some develop frontotemporal dementia)

**Prognosis:**
- Progressive, fatal
- Death typically from respiratory failure
- Median survival 2-5 years

**Treatment:**
- Riluzole (modestly prolongs survival)
- Supportive care

### Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

**Pathology:**
- **Demyelination** of CNS (oligodendrocyte damage)
- **Plaques**: Areas of inflammation and demyelination
- Autoimmune

**Common Locations:**
- **Periventricular white matter**
- **Optic nerves**
- **Brainstem**
- **Spinal cord**
- **Cerebellum**

**Clinical Features:**
- **"Disseminated in time and space"**
- Symptoms depend on plaque location

**Common Presentations:**
- **Optic neuritis**: Painful vision loss, RAPD
- **Internuclear ophthalmoplegia (INO)**: MLF lesion
  - Impaired adduction ipsilateral
  - Nystagmus contralateral abducting eye
  - Bilateral INO highly suggestive of MS
- **Sensory symptoms**: Numbness, paresthesias, Lhermitte's sign
  - **Lhermitte's sign**: Electric shock sensation down spine with neck flexion (posterior column)
- **Motor symptoms**: Weakness, spasticity
- **Cerebellar symptoms**: Ataxia, intention tremor
- **Bladder/bowel dysfunction**
- **Fatigue**
- **Cognitive impairment** (later)

**Imaging:**
- **MRI**: Multiple T2 hyperintense lesions
  - Periventricular ("Dawson fingers")
  - Gadolinium enhancement (active lesions)

**Diagnosis:**
- Clinical + MRI (McDonald criteria)
- CSF: Oligoclonal bands, elevated IgG index

**Treatment:**
- Acute: Corticosteroids
- Disease-modifying therapies: Interferon-beta, glatiramer, natalizumab, fingolimod, others

## Cranial Nerve Disorders

### Optic Neuritis (CN II)

**Etiology:**
- **Multiple sclerosis** (most common in young adults)
- Infection
- Autoimmune

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **Painful vision loss** (unilateral, subacute)
  - Pain worse with eye movement
- **Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD)**
  - "Marcus Gunn pupil"
- **Decreased color vision** (red desaturation)
- **Central scotoma**

**Fundoscopy:**
- May be normal (retrobulbar neuritis)
- Or optic disc swelling (papillitis)

**Prognosis:**
- Usually recovers (weeks to months)
- 50% develop MS within 15 years

### Horner Syndrome

**Anatomy**: Disruption of **sympathetic pathway** to eye and face

**Pathway:**
1. **First-order neuron**: Hypothalamus → brainstem → spinal cord (C8-T2)
2. **Second-order neuron**: Spinal cord → superior cervical ganglion
3. **Third-order neuron**: Superior cervical ganglion → eye (along carotid artery)

**Classic Triad:**
- **Ptosis** (mild, Müller's muscle)
- **Miosis** (constricted pupil)
- **Anhidrosis** (no sweating)
  - Distribution depends on lesion level
  - Face: Central or preganglionic
  - None: Postganglionic (along internal carotid)

**Localization:**

**Central (First-Order):**
- **Locations**: Hypothalamus, brainstem, spinal cord
- **Causes**: Stroke (lateral medullary), demyelination, tumor
- **Anhidrosis**: Entire half of body

**Preganglionic (Second-Order):**
- **Locations**: Spinal cord exit to superior cervical ganglion
- **Causes**:
  - **Pancoast tumor** (lung apex)
  - Trauma, surgery
  - Cervical lymphadenopathy
- **Anhidrosis**: Entire face

**Postganglionic (Third-Order):**
- **Locations**: Superior cervical ganglion to eye
- **Causes**:
  - Carotid dissection (painful)
  - Cavernous sinus lesion
  - Cluster headache
- **Anhidrosis**: None or minimal

**Diagnosis:**
- **Cocaine test**: Fails to dilate Horner pupil
- **Apraclonidine test**: Reverses anisocoria in Horner's
- **Hydroxyamphetamine test**: Localizes to pre- vs. postganglionic

**Clinical Pearl:**
- Painful Horner's: Think **carotid dissection** (emergency!)

### Argyll Robertson Pupil

**Etiology**: **Neurosyphilis** (tertiary)

**Location**: Midbrain (pretectal area, Edinger-Westphal nucleus region)

**Clinical Features:**
- **"Accommodation-light dissociation"**
  - Pupils **accommodate** (constrict with near vision)
  - Pupils do **NOT react to light**
- **Bilateral**
- **Small, irregular pupils**
- **"Prostitute pupil"**: Accommodates but doesn't react

**Differential:**
- Diabetes (can cause similar pupil)
- Parinaud syndrome (dorsal midbrain, also light-near dissociation)

### Bell's Palsy (CN VII)

**Definition**: Idiopathic peripheral facial nerve palsy

**Etiology:**
- Often **viral** (HSV-1 reactivation hypothesized)
- Inflammation and edema in facial canal

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **Acute onset** (hours to days)
- **Complete facial weakness** (ipsilateral)
  - Upper AND lower face
  - Cannot raise eyebrow, close eye, smile
- **Hyperacusis** (stapedius muscle)
- **Decreased taste** (anterior 2/3 tongue)
- **Decreased lacrimation**
- May have ear pain

**Complications:**
- **Corneal abrasion** (can't close eye)
  - Eye protection essential!
- **Synkinesis** (aberrant reinnervation)

**Diagnosis:**
- Clinical (rule out stroke, Lyme, tumor)
- **Forehead involved** (distinguishes from UMN lesion)

**Treatment:**
- **Corticosteroids** (within 72 hours)
- **Eye protection**: Artificial tears, eye patch at night
- Most recover within 3-6 months (70-80%)

**Clinical Pearl:**
- Forehead involvement = peripheral (Bell's, can't raise eyebrow)
- Forehead spared = central (stroke, can raise eyebrow)

### Trigeminal Neuralgia (CN V)

**Definition**: Severe, lancinating facial pain in trigeminal distribution

**Epidemiology:**
- More common in elderly
- Female > male

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **Sudden, severe, electric shock-like pain**
- **Unilateral** (usually)
- Distribution:
  - **V2 (maxillary)** or **V3 (mandibular)** most common
  - V1 less common
- **Triggered** by:
  - Touch (light touch, not deep pressure)
  - Chewing
  - Talking
  - Cold air
  - Brushing teeth
- **Brief** (seconds to minutes)
- **No sensory loss** (pain only)

**Etiology:**
- **Primary (idiopathic)**: Vascular compression of nerve root (superior cerebellar artery)
- **Secondary**: Tumor, MS, aneurysm

**Diagnosis:**
- Clinical
- **MRI**: Rule out secondary causes

**Treatment:**
- **Medical**: Carbamazepine (first-line), oxcarbazepine, gabapentin
- **Surgical**: Microvascular decompression (if medical fails)

**Clinical Pearl:**
- If bilateral or in young patient, think **multiple sclerosis**

### Meniere's Disease (CN VIII)

**Definition**: Endolymphatic hydrops (excess endolymph in inner ear)

**Clinical Features (Classic Triad):**
- **Episodic vertigo** (minutes to hours)
  - Severe, spinning
  - Nausea, vomiting
- **Tinnitus** (low-pitched, roaring)
- **Hearing loss** (fluctuating initially, progressive)
  - Sensorineural, low-frequency
- **Aural fullness** (ear pressure)

**Diagnosis:**
- Clinical
- Audiometry: Sensorineural hearing loss

**Treatment:**
- Acute: Antiemetics, benzodiazepines
- Chronic: Low-salt diet, diuretics
- Refractory: Intratympanic gentamicin, surgery

**Prognosis:**
- Attacks decrease over time
- Hearing loss may be permanent

## Traumatic Injuries

### Diffuse Axonal Injury (DAI)

**Mechanism**: Acceleration-deceleration forces → axonal shearing

**Risk Factors:**
- High-speed motor vehicle accidents
- Shaken baby syndrome

**Pathology:**
- Widespread axonal damage
- **Corpus callosum**, **dorsolateral brainstem**, **gray-white junction**

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **Immediate coma** (no lucid interval)
- Prolonged unconsciousness
- Variable recovery

**Imaging:**
- **CT**: May be normal initially
- **MRI**: Multiple small hemorrhages (gradient echo/SWI sequences)

**Prognosis:**
- Poor, depends on severity
- Major cause of vegetative state

### Spinal Cord Injury Without Radiographic Abnormality (SCIWORA)

**Definition**: Spinal cord injury with no fracture/dislocation on imaging

**Population**: Children (more flexible spines)

**Mechanism**: Transient vertebral column deformation

**Presentation**: Spinal cord deficits (variable severity)

**Imaging:**
- X-ray, CT: Normal
- **MRI**: May show cord edema, hemorrhage

**Prognosis**: Variable

### Brachial Plexus Injuries

**Erb-Duchenne Palsy (Upper Trunk, C5-C6):**

**Mechanism**: Excessive lateral neck flexion (shoulder dystocia, motorcycle)

**Affected Muscles:**
- Deltoid, supraspinatus, infraspinatus (shoulder abduction, external rotation)
- Biceps, brachialis (elbow flexion)

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **"Waiter's tip" position**:
  - Arm adducted, internally rotated
  - Elbow extended
  - Forearm pronated
  - Wrist flexed
- Loss of biceps reflex

**Klumpke Palsy (Lower Trunk, C8-T1):**

**Mechanism**: Upward traction on arm (grabbing to break fall)

**Affected Muscles:**
- Intrinsic hand muscles
- Wrist and finger flexors

**Clinical Presentation:**
- **"Claw hand"**
  - Hyperextension at MCP joints
  - Flexion at IP joints
- Sensory loss: Medial hand and forearm
- **Horner syndrome** (if T1 root avulsed)

**Total Plexus Injury:**
- Complete arm paralysis
- Flail limb
- Sensory loss

## Congenital and Developmental Abnormalities

### Chiari Malformations

**Type I:**
- **Cerebellar tonsil herniation** through foramen magnum (>5 mm)
- Usually asymptomatic
- May present in adulthood

**Clinical Features:**
- Headache (worse with Valsalva)
- Neck pain
- **Syringomyelia** (50%)
  - Cape-like sensory loss
  - Weakness, atrophy

**Imaging**: MRI (cerebellar tonsil descent)

**Treatment**: Posterior fossa decompression (if symptomatic)

**Type II (Arnold-Chiari):**
- Cerebellar vermis + medulla herniation
- **Associated with myelomeningocele**
- Presents in infancy

**Clinical Features:**
- Hydrocephalus
- Brainstem dysfunction
- Apnea

### Spina Bifida

**Spina Bifida Occulta:**
- **Incomplete vertebral arch fusion** (posterior)
- No herniation
- Usually asymptomatic
- Skin findings: Tuft of hair, dimple, lipoma

**Meningocele:**
- Meninges herniate through defect
- CSF-filled sac
- No neural elements

**Myelomeningocele:**
- **Meninges + spinal cord** herniate
- Most severe
- Neurological deficits below lesion
- Bowel/bladder dysfunction
- Associated with Chiari II, hydrocephalus

**Prevention**: Folic acid supplementation (periconceptional)

### Hydrocephalus

**Definition**: Abnormal accumulation of CSF

**Types:**

**Obstructive (Noncommunicating):**
- **Blockage** of CSF flow within ventricular system
- Causes:
  - **Aqueductal stenosis** (congenital, most common)
  - Tumors (posterior fossa, pineal region)
  - Infection (aqueductal scarring)
  - Chiari malformation

**Communicating:**
- Impaired **reabsorption** at arachnoid granulations
- Causes:
  - Post-hemorrhagic (SAH, IVH)
  - Post-infectious (meningitis)
  - Tumor (impairs CSF flow over convexities)

**Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH):**
- **Classic triad (Wacky, Wobbly, Wet)**:
  - **Cognitive impairment** (dementia)
  - **Gait apraxia** (magnetic gait)
  - **Urinary incontinence**
- Enlarged ventricles, normal opening pressure on LP
- **Reversible** with shunt

**Clinical Features (Infants):**
- Enlarged head circumference
- Bulging fontanelle
- "Setting sun" sign (eyes deviate down)
- Developmental delay

**Clinical Features (Adults):**
- Headache (worse in morning)
- Nausea, vomiting
- Papilledema
- Gait instability
- Cognitive changes

**Treatment:**
- **Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) shunt**
- **Endoscopic third ventriculostomy** (ETV, for obstructive)

## Key Points

1. **MCA stroke**: Most common; contralateral hemiplegia with arm/face > leg, aphasia (dominant), neglect (nondominant)

2. **ACA stroke**: Contralateral leg > arm weakness, urinary incontinence, abulia

3. **PCA stroke**: Contralateral homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing, visual agnosia

4. **Lateral medullary syndrome (PICA)**: Crossed sensory findings, ipsilateral Horner's, ataxia, dysphagia

5. **Lacunar strokes**: Small vessel disease; no cortical signs; pure motor, pure sensory, ataxic hemiparesis

6. **Epidural hematoma**: Lucid interval, lens-shaped on CT, middle meningeal artery, surgical emergency

7. **Subdural hematoma**: Bridging veins, crescent-shaped, crosses sutures; acute (bright) vs. chronic (dark)

8. **SAH**: Thunderclap headache, nuchal rigidity; complications include rebleeding, vasospasm, hydrocephalus

9. **Alzheimer's disease**: Hippocampal atrophy, neurofibrillary tangles, amyloid plaques, progressive dementia

10. **Parkinson's disease**: Substantia nigra degeneration, resting tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, postural instability

11. **Huntington's disease**: Caudate atrophy, chorea, cognitive decline, psychiatric symptoms, autosomal dominant

12. **ALS**: UMN + LMN signs, progressive weakness, sensation preserved, fatal

13. **Multiple sclerosis**: Demyelination, optic neuritis, INO, Lhermitte's sign, relapsing-remitting course

14. **Bell's palsy**: Peripheral CN VII palsy, forehead involved, eye protection essential, steroids within 72 hours

15. **Horner syndrome**: Ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis; painful = carotid dissection

16. **Trigeminal neuralgia**: Lancinating facial pain, triggered, carbamazepine first-line

17. **Chiari malformation**: Cerebellar tonsillar herniation, associated with syringomyelia

18. **Hydrocephalus**: NPH triad (dementia, gait apraxia, incontinence); reversible with shunt
