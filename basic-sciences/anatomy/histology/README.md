# Histology

## Overview

Histology is the study of tissues at the microscopic level. Understanding tissue organization and cellular composition is fundamental to comprehending organ structure, normal physiology, and disease processes. This subsection provides comprehensive coverage of the four basic tissue types and their clinical correlations.

## Purpose and Scope

This subsection covers:
- **Microscopy Techniques**: Light microscopy, staining methods, tissue preparation
- **Epithelial Tissue**: Covering and lining epithelia, glandular epithelia
- **Connective Tissue**: Loose, dense, specialized (cartilage, bone, blood)
- **Muscle Tissue**: Skeletal, cardiac, smooth muscle
- **Nervous Tissue**: Neurons, glial cells, organization

## Key Histological Concepts

### Four Basic Tissue Types

**1. Epithelial Tissue:**
- Covers body surfaces, lines cavities
- Forms glands
- Functions: Protection, secretion, absorption, sensation
- Little extracellular matrix
- Avascular (no blood vessels)
- High regeneration capacity

**2. Connective Tissue:**
- Supports and connects other tissues
- Abundant extracellular matrix
- Functions: Support, protection, transport, storage
- Variable vascularity
- Derived from mesoderm (mostly)

**3. Muscle Tissue:**
- Specialized for contraction
- Functions: Movement, heat production
- Types: Skeletal, cardiac, smooth
- High metabolic activity

**4. Nervous Tissue:**
- Specialized for communication
- Functions: Reception, processing, transmission of information
- Composed of neurons and glial cells
- Limited regeneration in CNS

### Microscopy Techniques

**Light Microscopy:**
- Standard histological technique
- Magnification up to ~1000x
- Requires tissue fixation and staining

**Common Stains:**
- **Hematoxylin and Eosin (H&E)**: Most common
  - Hematoxylin: Basophilic structures (nuclei, ribosomes) → blue/purple
  - Eosin: Acidophilic structures (cytoplasm, collagen) → pink/red
- **Periodic Acid-Schiff (PAS)**: Carbohydrates, glycogen → magenta
- **Masson's Trichrome**: Collagen → blue/green, muscle → red
- **Elastic stain (Verhoeff-van Gieson)**: Elastic fibers → black
- **Silver stain**: Reticular fibers, neurons → black

**Special Techniques:**
- **Immunohistochemistry**: Antibody-based detection of specific proteins
- **Electron microscopy**: Ultrastructural details (up to 1,000,000x)
- **Confocal microscopy**: Fluorescence imaging with optical sectioning

### Tissue Preparation

**Steps:**
1. **Fixation**: Preserve tissue (formalin most common)
2. **Dehydration**: Remove water (graded alcohols)
3. **Clearing**: Replace alcohol with xylene (miscible with paraffin)
4. **Embedding**: Infiltrate with paraffin wax
5. **Sectioning**: Cut thin sections (5-10 μm) with microtome
6. **Staining**: Apply stains (H&E, special stains)
7. **Mounting**: Cover with coverslip for microscopy
