# Nervous Tissue

## Introduction

Nervous tissue is specialized for communication, enabling rapid transmission of information throughout the body. It consists of two main cell types: neurons (excitable cells that transmit electrical signals) and glial cells (supporting cells). Nervous tissue forms the nervous system, including the brain, spinal cord (CNS), and nerves (PNS).

### Learning Objectives

1. Describe the structure and classification of neurons
2. Identify different types of glial cells and their functions
3. Understand synaptic transmission and neuronal communication
4. Differentiate CNS and PNS glial cells
5. Recognize the organization of nervous tissue in gray and white matter
6. Correlate nervous tissue structure with clinical conditions

## General Characteristics

**Functions:**
- **Reception**: Detect stimuli (sensory)
- **Integration**: Process and interpret information
- **Transmission**: Conduct signals rapidly over long distances
- **Response**: Generate appropriate motor or secretory responses

**Components:**
- **Neurons**: Functional units (excitable cells)
  - 100 billion in human brain
  - Transmit action potentials
- **Glial cells** (Neuroglia): Support cells
  - Outnumber neurons 10:1 to 50:1
  - Do NOT generate action potentials
  - Essential for neuronal function

**Characteristics:**
- **High metabolic rate**: Dependent on glucose and oxygen
- **Limited regeneration**: Especially in CNS
- **Electrical excitability**: Neurons can generate and conduct action potentials
- **Complex connectivity**: Each neuron may synapse with thousands of others

## Neurons

### Structure

**Cell Body (Soma, Perikaryon):**
- Contains nucleus and organelles
- **Nucleus**: Large, pale (euchromatic), prominent nucleolus
- **Nissl substance** (Nissl bodies):
  - Basophilic clumps (H&E: blue/purple)
  - Composed of rough endoplasmic reticulum (RER) and free ribosomes
  - Abundant protein synthesis (neurotransmitters, membrane proteins)
  - Absent in axon hillock and axon
- **Golgi apparatus**: Extensive
- **Mitochondria**: Abundant (high energy demand)
- **Neurofilaments and microtubules**: Cytoskeleton
- **Lipofuscin**: Yellow-brown pigment (aging pigment, accumulates with age)
- **Melanin**: In substantia nigra, locus coeruleus

**Dendrites:**
- **Multiple** branching processes extending from cell body
- **Receive** synaptic inputs (afferent signals)
- Contain:
  - Nissl substance (proximal dendrites)
  - Mitochondria
  - Ribosomes (local protein synthesis)
  - Dendritic spines: Small protrusions, increase surface area for synapses
- Taper distally
- **Function**: Increase receptive surface area

**Axon:**
- **Single** elongated process (may branch: collaterals)
- **Transmits** action potentials (efferent signals) away from cell body
- Originates at **axon hillock** (cone-shaped elevation on cell body)
  - Site of action potential initiation (high density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels)
  - No Nissl substance (distinguishing feature)
- **Axoplasm**: Cytoplasm of axon
- Contains:
  - Microtubules (axonal transport)
  - Neurofilaments
  - Mitochondria
  - Vesicles
  - NO Nissl substance, NO ribosomes (protein synthesis in cell body only)
- **Axon terminals** (synaptic boutons, terminal buttons):
  - Swellings at distal end
  - Contain **synaptic vesicles** (neurotransmitters)
  - Form synapses with target cells

**Axonal Transport:**
- **Anterograde** (cell body → terminal):
  - **Fast**: 400 mm/day (vesicles, organelles) via kinesin
  - **Slow**: 0.1-3 mm/day (cytoskeletal proteins, enzymes)
- **Retrograde** (terminal → cell body):
  - **Fast**: 200 mm/day via dynein
  - Recycles membrane, brings trophic factors
  - **Clinical**: Rabies virus, tetanus toxin, herpes virus exploit retrograde transport

### Classification of Neurons

**By Structure (Number of Processes):**

**1. Unipolar (Pseudounipolar):**
- Single process divides into two branches (T-shaped)
- **Central process**: To CNS
- **Peripheral process**: From sensory receptor
- **Function**: Sensory
- **Example**: **Dorsal root ganglion neurons** (most common)

**2. Bipolar:**
- Two processes: One dendrite, one axon
- Elongated cell body
- **Function**: Sensory (special senses)
- **Examples**:
  - **Retina** (bipolar cells)
  - **Olfactory epithelium**
  - **Vestibular ganglion** (balance)
  - **Spiral ganglion** (hearing)

**3. Multipolar:**
- **Most common** type
- Multiple dendrites, one axon
- **Examples**:
  - **Motor neurons** (anterior horn cells, cranial nerve motor nuclei)
  - **Pyramidal cells** (cerebral cortex)
  - **Purkinje cells** (cerebellar cortex)
  - **Interneurons** (CNS)

**By Function:**

**1. Sensory (Afferent):**
- Convey information from receptors to CNS
- Cell bodies in ganglia (dorsal root ganglia, cranial nerve ganglia)
- Usually pseudounipolar

**2. Motor (Efferent):**
- Convey commands from CNS to effectors (muscles, glands)
- Cell bodies in CNS (anterior horn, brainstem motor nuclei)
- Multipolar

**3. Interneurons:**
- Connect neurons within CNS
- Most abundant (~99% of neurons)
- Multipolar
- Integrate and process information

**By Neurotransmitter:**
- **Cholinergic**: Acetylcholine (motor neurons, parasympathetic)
- **Adrenergic**: Norepinephrine (sympathetic postganglionic)
- **Dopaminergic**: Dopamine (substantia nigra, ventral tegmental area)
- **Serotonergic**: Serotonin (raphe nuclei)
- **GABAergic**: GABA (inhibitory interneurons)
- **Glutamatergic**: Glutamate (excitatory, most common in CNS)

**By Axon Length:**
- **Golgi Type I**: Long axons (projection neurons)
  - Motor neurons, pyramidal cells
- **Golgi Type II**: Short axons (local circuit neurons)
  - Interneurons

### Synapses

**Definition**: Specialized junctions for communication between neurons or between neuron and effector.

**Types:**

**Chemical Synapses** (Most common):
- **Presynaptic terminal**: Axon terminal of transmitting neuron
  - Contains synaptic vesicles (neurotransmitters)
  - Mitochondria (energy for synthesis, release)
  - Presynaptic membrane (release sites)
- **Synaptic cleft**: 20-30 nm gap
- **Postsynaptic membrane**: Dendrite, cell body, or axon of receiving neuron
  - Contains neurotransmitter receptors
  - May have postsynaptic density (electron-dense area, contains receptors and signaling proteins)

**Transmission:**
1. Action potential arrives at presynaptic terminal
2. Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ influx
3. Synaptic vesicles fuse with presynaptic membrane (exocytosis)
4. Neurotransmitter released into synaptic cleft
5. Neurotransmitter binds receptors on postsynaptic membrane
6. **Ionotropic receptors**: Ligand-gated ion channels (fast)
   - Examples: Nicotinic ACh receptors, GABA-A, NMDA, AMPA
7. **Metabotropic receptors**: G-protein coupled (slower, modulatory)
   - Examples: Muscarinic ACh receptors, adrenergic receptors
8. Postsynaptic response: EPSP (excitatory) or IPSP (inhibitory)
9. Neurotransmitter removed:
   - Enzymatic degradation (e.g., AChE)
   - Reuptake (e.g., serotonin, dopamine)
   - Diffusion

**Electrical Synapses:**
- **Gap junctions** (connexons) directly connect neurons
- Allow direct passage of ions and small molecules
- **Bidirectional** communication
- **Very fast** (no synaptic delay)
- **Locations**: Retina, cardiac muscle, smooth muscle, some CNS neurons

**Synapse Types by Location:**
- **Axodendritic**: Axon → Dendrite (most common)
- **Axosomatic**: Axon → Cell body
- **Axoaxonic**: Axon → Axon (presynaptic modulation)
- **Dendrodendritic**: Dendrite ↔ Dendrite (rare, olfactory bulb)

### Neurotransmitters

**Small Molecule Neurotransmitters:**
- **Acetylcholine (ACh)**: NMJ, parasympathetic, basal forebrain
- **Catecholamines**:
  - Dopamine: Substantia nigra, reward pathways
  - Norepinephrine: Locus coeruleus, sympathetic postganglionic
  - Epinephrine: Adrenal medulla
- **Serotonin (5-HT)**: Raphe nuclei, mood, sleep
- **Amino acids**:
  - **Glutamate**: Main excitatory neurotransmitter in CNS
  - **GABA**: Main inhibitory neurotransmitter in CNS
  - Glycine: Inhibitory (spinal cord)
- **Histamine**: Hypothalamus, arousal
- **ATP**: Co-transmitter, some neurons

**Neuropeptides** (synthesized in cell body):
- Substance P: Pain transmission
- Endorphins, enkephalins: Pain modulation
- Neuropeptide Y, somatostatin, cholecystokinin, etc.

**Gaseous Neurotransmitters:**
- Nitric oxide (NO): Retrograde messenger, vasodilation
- Carbon monoxide (CO)

## Glial Cells (Neuroglia)

**Functions:**
- Structural support
- Metabolic support (nutrients, waste removal)
- Insulation (myelin)
- Immune defense
- Regulate extracellular environment
- Modulate synaptic transmission

**Types**: Different in CNS vs. PNS

### CNS Glial Cells

#### Astrocytes

**Most abundant glial cells in CNS.**

**Types:**
- **Protoplasmic astrocytes**: Gray matter
  - Short, highly branched processes
- **Fibrous astrocytes**: White matter
  - Fewer, longer processes

**Structure:**
- Star-shaped
- Multiple processes
- **End-feet** (perivascular processes): Contact blood vessels and pia mater
- **GFAP (Glial Fibrillary Acidic Protein)**: Intermediate filament, marker for astrocytes

**Functions:**
1. **Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)**:
   - Astrocyte end-feet induce tight junctions between endothelial cells
   - Regulate permeability
2. **Metabolic support**:
   - Provide glucose and lactate to neurons
   - Regulate extracellular K⁺ concentration (prevent excessive depolarization)
   - Buffer neurotransmitters (glutamate, GABA uptake)
3. **Structural support**:
   - Framework for CNS
4. **Scar formation** (gliosis):
   - Proliferate after CNS injury
   - Form glial scar (astrocytes + ECM)
   - Inhibits axon regeneration
5. **Synaptic modulation**:
   - Tripartite synapse (presynaptic, postsynaptic, astrocyte)
   - Release gliotransmitters (glutamate, ATP, D-serine)
6. **Glycogen storage**: Energy reserve

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Gliosis**: Reactive astrocytosis (response to injury, infection, neurodegeneration)
  - Seen in Alzheimer's, multiple sclerosis, stroke
- **Astrocytoma**: Tumor of astrocytes
  - **Glioblastoma multiforme** (GBM): Most aggressive (WHO grade IV)
  - GFAP-positive
- **Alexander disease**: Mutation in GFAP gene
- **Hepatic encephalopathy**: Astrocyte dysfunction (ammonia accumulation)

#### Oligodendrocytes

**Form myelin in CNS.**

**Structure:**
- Small cell body, few processes
- **One oligodendrocyte myelinates multiple axons** (up to 50)
- Processes wrap around axons

**Function:**
- **Myelination**: Electrical insulation of axons
  - Increases conduction velocity (saltatory conduction)
  - Myelin: Lipid-rich (80% lipid, 20% protein)
    - White appearance (white matter)
  - **Nodes of Ranvier**: Gaps between myelin segments
    - High density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels
    - Action potential "jumps" from node to node (saltatory conduction)
- Metabolic support for axons

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Multiple Sclerosis (MS)**:
  - Autoimmune demyelination in CNS
  - Oligodendrocyte damage
  - Demyelinating plaques (periventricular white matter)
  - Symptoms: Vision loss, weakness, sensory disturbances, ataxia
  - Relapsing-remitting course
- **Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy (PML)**:
  - JC virus infection of oligodendrocytes
  - Demyelination
  - Seen in immunocompromised (HIV/AIDS)
- **Leukodystrophies**: Genetic myelin disorders
  - Krabbe disease, metachromatic leukodystrophy

#### Microglia

**CNS resident immune cells.**

**Origin**: Mesoderm (monocyte lineage) - only CNS glial cells NOT from neuroectoderm

**Structure:**
- Small, elongated cell body
- Short, branched processes
- **Ramified (resting) state**: Processes survey environment
- **Activated state**: Retract processes, become amoeboid, phagocytic

**Functions:**
1. **Immune surveillance**: Monitor CNS for pathogens, damage
2. **Phagocytosis**: Remove debris, dead cells, pathogens
3. **Antigen presentation**: To T cells
4. **Cytokine secretion**: Inflammatory mediators
5. **Synaptic pruning**: During development and plasticity

**Markers**: CD11b, Iba1 (ionized calcium-binding adapter molecule 1)

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Neuroinflammation**: Activated microglia in Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, MS
- **HIV encephalitis**: Microglial activation and nodules
- **Prion diseases**: Microglial activation
- **Excessive activation**: Neurotoxicity (release of reactive oxygen species, cytokines)

#### Ependymal Cells

**Line ventricles and central canal.**

**Structure:**
- **Cuboidal to columnar** epithelium
- **Ciliated** apical surface (some have microvilli)
- Tight junctions (incomplete, leaky)

**Functions:**
1. **Facilitate CSF circulation**: Cilia beat to move CSF
2. **Barrier** (limited): Between CSF and brain parenchyma
3. **CSF production** (specialized ependymal cells in choroid plexus):
   - **Choroid plexus**: Vascularized, ependymal cells cover capillaries
   - Secrete CSF into ventricles

**Types:**
- **Ependymocytes**: Line ventricles and central canal
- **Tanycytes**: Specialized ependymal cells (hypothalamus, median eminence)
- **Choroid plexus epithelium**: Modified ependymal cells (CSF production)

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Ependymoma**: Tumor of ependymal cells (often in 4th ventricle, children)
- **Hydrocephalus**: CSF accumulation (choroid plexus overproduction or obstruction)

### PNS Glial Cells

#### Schwann Cells

**Form myelin in PNS.**

**Structure:**
- Flat cell wraps around axon
- **One Schwann cell myelinates ONE axon segment**
- Nucleus and cytoplasm pushed to periphery (forming **neurilemma**)

**Types:**

**Myelinating Schwann Cells:**
- Wrap around single axon segment (internode)
- Form myelin sheath
- **Nodes of Ranvier**: Gaps between adjacent Schwann cells
- **Schmidt-Lanterman incisors**: Cytoplasmic channels in myelin (metabolic support)

**Non-myelinating Schwann Cells:**
- Ensheath multiple small unmyelinated axons
- **Remak bundles**: Groups of unmyelinated axons in single Schwann cell

**Functions:**
1. **Myelination**: Increase conduction velocity (large axons)
2. **Axonal support**: Trophic factors, metabolic support
3. **Axon regeneration**: Guide regrowing axons after injury
   - Form **bands of Büngner** (proliferating Schwann cells in endoneurial tubes)
   - Secrete growth factors

**Clinical Correlations:**
- **Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS)**:
  - Autoimmune demyelination in PNS
  - Acute inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (AIDP)
  - Ascending paralysis, areflexia
  - Often post-infection (Campylobacter jejuni)
  - CSF: Elevated protein, normal WBC ("albuminocytologic dissociation")
  - Recovery possible (Schwann cells regenerate)
- **Charcot-Marie-Tooth Disease (CMT)**:
  - Hereditary motor and sensory neuropathy
  - Demyelination (Type 1) or axonal loss (Type 2)
  - Distal weakness, foot drop, pes cavus
- **Schwannoma**:
  - Benign tumor of Schwann cells
  - **Acoustic neuroma** (vestibular schwannoma): CN VIII, cerebellopontine angle

#### Satellite Cells

**Surround neuronal cell bodies in ganglia.**

**Location:**
- **Dorsal root ganglia** (sensory)
- **Autonomic ganglia** (sympathetic, parasympathetic)

**Structure:**
- Flattened cells
- Surround neuron cell body (like satellites)

**Functions:**
- **Metabolic support**: Regulate microenvironment
- **Nutrient exchange**
- Similar to astrocytes in CNS

**Clinical**: Less prominent role in disease compared to other glia

## Organization of Nervous Tissue

### Gray Matter

**Composition:**
- **Neuronal cell bodies**
- Dendrites
- Unmyelinated axons
- Glial cells
- Synapses
- Capillaries

**Locations:**
- **CNS**:
  - Cerebral cortex (outer layer of cerebrum)
  - Deep nuclei (basal ganglia, thalamus)
  - Cerebellar cortex
  - Spinal cord (central, butterfly/H-shape)
- **PNS**:
  - Ganglia (dorsal root ganglia, autonomic ganglia)

**Appearance**: Gray (lack of myelin)

**Function**: Information processing, integration

### White Matter

**Composition:**
- **Myelinated axons** (predominantly)
- Oligodendrocytes (CNS) or Schwann cells (PNS)
- Astrocytes (CNS)

**Locations:**
- **CNS**:
  - Deep to cerebral cortex
  - Corpus callosum, internal capsule, white matter tracts
  - Cerebellar white matter (arbor vitae)
  - Spinal cord (peripheral)
- **PNS**:
  - Nerves

**Appearance**: White (myelin is lipid-rich, white)

**Function**: Transmission of information (axonal tracts)

**Tracts**:
- **Ascending**: Sensory pathways (to brain)
- **Descending**: Motor pathways (from brain)
- **Association**: Connect regions within same hemisphere
- **Commissural**: Connect left and right hemispheres (corpus callosum)

### Peripheral Nerves

**Connective Tissue Sheaths:**
- **Endoneurium**: Surrounds individual axons (and Schwann cells)
  - Delicate connective tissue, capillaries
- **Perineurium**: Surrounds fascicles (bundles of axons)
  - Dense irregular connective tissue
  - Forms blood-nerve barrier
- **Epineurium**: Surrounds entire nerve
  - Dense irregular connective tissue, adipose
  - Contains blood vessels (vasa nervorum)

**Comparison to Muscle:**
| Nerve | Muscle |
|-------|--------|
| Endoneurium | Endomysium |
| Perineurium | Perimysium |
| Epineurium | Epimysium |

## Nerve Regeneration

**CNS:**
- **Limited regeneration**
- Barriers:
  - Glial scar (astrocytes)
  - Inhibitory molecules (myelin-associated inhibitors: Nogo, MAG)
  - Lack of neurotrophic support
- Neuronal cell bodies survive → axons may sprout but don't regenerate long distances

**PNS:**
- **Better regeneration potential** (if cell body survives and endoneurial tube intact)

**Process (Wallerian Degeneration and Regeneration):**
1. **Injury**: Axon severed
2. **Wallerian degeneration** (distal to injury):
   - Axon and myelin degenerate (distal to injury)
   - Macrophages and Schwann cells phagocytose debris
3. **Schwann cell proliferation**:
   - Form **bands of Büngner** (columns in endoneurial tubes)
   - Secrete neurotrophic factors (NGF, BDNF)
4. **Axonal sprouting**:
   - Proximal axon sprouts grow down endoneurial tubes
   - Guided by Schwann cells
5. **Remyelination**:
   - Schwann cells remyelinate regenerating axon
6. **Reinnervation**:
   - Axon reaches target (if successful)

**Factors Affecting Regeneration:**
- **Cell body survival**: Essential
- **Endoneurial tube integrity**: Guides regeneration
- **Distance to target**: Longer distances reduce success
- **Age**: Better in young
- **Time to repair**: Delays reduce success

**Clinical**:
- **Nerve transection**: Surgical repair improves outcomes
- **Crush injury**: Better prognosis than transection (endoneurial tubes intact)

## Clinical Correlations

**Neurodegenerative Diseases:**
- **Alzheimer's Disease**: Neuronal loss (hippocampus, cortex), plaques, tangles, gliosis
- **Parkinson's Disease**: Loss of dopaminergic neurons (substantia nigra pars compacta)
- **ALS**: Motor neuron degeneration (anterior horn cells, corticospinal tracts)
- **Huntington's Disease**: Striatal neuron loss (caudate, putamen)

**Demyelinating Diseases:**
- **Multiple Sclerosis**: CNS (oligodendrocytes)
- **Guillain-Barré Syndrome**: PNS (Schwann cells)

**Peripheral Neuropathy:**
- Diabetes, alcoholism, toxins, hereditary (CMT)
- Axonal or demyelinating

**Tumors:**
- **CNS**: Gliomas (astrocytoma, oligodendroglioma, ependymoma, GBM)
- **PNS**: Schwannoma, neurofibroma

## Key Points

1. Nervous tissue: Neurons (functional units) and glial cells (support)

2. Neuron structure: Cell body (Nissl substance), dendrites (receive), axon (transmit)

3. Neuron classification: Unipolar (sensory), bipolar (special senses), multipolar (motor, interneurons)

4. Synapses: Chemical (most common, neurotransmitter) or electrical (gap junctions)

5. Astrocytes: Most abundant CNS glia; BBB formation, metabolic support, scar formation (gliosis), GFAP-positive

6. Oligodendrocytes: Myelinate CNS axons; one cell myelinates multiple (up to 50) axons

7. Microglia: CNS immune cells; phagocytosis, mesodermal origin

8. Ependymal cells: Line ventricles; ciliated, facilitate CSF circulation

9. Schwann cells: Myelinate PNS axons; one cell per axon segment; facilitate regeneration

10. Satellite cells: Surround neuronal cell bodies in ganglia

11. Gray matter: Neuronal cell bodies, dendrites, unmyelinated axons; processing centers

12. White matter: Myelinated axons; transmission pathways

13. CNS regeneration: Limited (glial scar, inhibitory molecules)

14. PNS regeneration: Better (Schwann cells guide, form bands of Büngner)

15. Clinical: MS (oligodendrocyte), GBS (Schwann cell), Alzheimer's (neuronal loss), tumors (gliomas, schwannomas)
